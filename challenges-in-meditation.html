Why the Transcendental State Is So Powerful

Why go beyond the mind at all? Because of where the leverage is.

Most substances change you only while present: an SSRI, a benzo, alcohol. When the chemical clears, the effect goes too — that's why you take the pill every day. Psychedelics are different, and the first hint science has given us into this deeper layer. In psychedelic-assisted therapy you don't need the drug present to maintain the change — a single experience produces a lasting shift. Something happened at the level of experience, not chemistry.

"We're not operating at the level of the body — that's where an SSRI works. We're not even operating at the level of thoughts. We are going in and editing the source code of yourself."

You can logically tell someone "only the present moment exists" and they'll nod and change nothing. Have that realization inside a transcendental state and it rewires how you live. The yogic framing is that ordinary reality is Maya — illusion. We sit in the theater believing the movie is real; transcendental consciousness is walking onto the set, where you can change the scenes.

There's even partial scientific cover. Color doesn't exist out there — only light waves your brain transmutes into color. Sound is just vibration your eardrum converts into an image. Add cognitive biases and it's clear: you never perceive reality as it is, only a filtered rendering. When you act from the transcendental level, you're editing the rendering itself. This is why the same mantra is weak from ordinary consciousness but carries enormous "manifestation power" from a transcendental state.

Why the Transcendental State Is So Powerful

Why bother going beyond the mind at all? Because of where the leverage is.

Consider how most substances change you: an SSRI, a benzo, alcohol, marijuana. As long as the chemical is floating in your brain, it produces an effect. When it clears, the effect goes too. That's why you take the pill every day.

Psychedelics are different, and they're the first hint science has given us into this deeper layer. In psychedelic-assisted therapy, you don't need the drug present to maintain the change — a single experience can produce a relatively lasting shift. Something happened at the level of experience, not chemistry.

"We're not operating at the level of the body — that's where an SSRI works. We're not even operating at the level of thoughts. We are going in and editing the source code of yourself."

This is the whole point. You can logically tell someone "only the present moment exists" and they'll nod and change nothing. But have that realization inside a transcendental state and it rewires how you live. The yogic framing is that ordinary reality is Maya — illusion. We sit in the theater believing the movie is real. Transcendental consciousness is walking onto the set, where you can actually understand how the film is made and change the scenes.

There's even partial scientific cover for the "Maya" idea. Color doesn't exist out there — only absorption spectrums and light waves, which your brain transmutes into the experience of color. Sound is just vibration your eardrum converts into an image in your auditory cortex, the way a computer renders pixels from binary. Add cognitive biases on top and it's clear: you never perceive reality as it is, only a heavily filtered rendering.

So when you act from the transcendental level, you're editing the rendering itself. This is why the same practice yields wildly different results depending on the state you do it from. A mantra repeated from ordinary consciousness is weak; the same mantra from a transcendental state has enormous "manifestation power."
