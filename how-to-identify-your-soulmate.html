Red Flags Aren't Always Red Lights

One counterintuitive sign you're actually in love: an early red flag can be a good thing.

If you dump anyone who shows a problem, you never learn the most important thing — how they handle a problem when you raise it. But if you fall for someone, discover a flaw, and say "I love you, but this is a problem for me" — and they repair it — that repair is the single best predictor of a healthy long-term relationship.

"The ability to repair a red flag — this is what Eros love gives us. This is why soulmates are forever."

Because your risk-assessment is dimmed early on, you fall first and discover the flaw later. Since nothing was hidden, it doesn't feel like betrayal — and navigating it together is what makes the bond durable.

You build a life together — a home, trips, kids — and the original "we were meant to be" ripples outward into a co-authored life. This is the bridge from infatuation to a partnership that keeps growing instead of plateauing.

  • A soulmate connection is about your pull toward them. No fantasy. The hallmark is that the person feels wrong for you — wrong type, terrifying logistics — and you can't stay away anyway.

An unreciprocated connection can still be real — and still doesn't obligate action. You can honor it and let it go: "Not in this life."

The Neuroscience: Why Love Makes You Stupid (On Purpose)

In the Eros phase, a partner's mere presence secretes dopamine in your brain. They don't have to do anything — just being near them produces pleasure, craving, and the urge to seek them out again. Red Flags Aren't Always Red Lights

One counterintuitive sign you're actually in love: an early red flag can be a good thing.

The Self-Expansion Model: From "Me" to "We"

Once Eros stabilizes, lasting passion shifts onto a different engine: the self-expansion model. Humans love to level up — student, then professional, then expert. Soulmates Aren't Always Forever — And Limerence Isn't a Soulmate

Two important clarifications:

A soulmate doesn't have to be your life partner for life. The karmic debt may be small. You can be someone's soulmate for a season, settle the karma, and amicably part ways. Soulmate ≠ limerence. This is the cleanest distinction in the talk:

  • A soulmate connection is about your pull toward them. There's no fantasy. In fact, the hallmark is that the person feels wrong for you — wrong type, wrong religion, terrifying logistics — and you can't stay away anyway.
  • Limerence is about wanting them to want you. It's built on fantasy and a constructed image, and it carries heavy cognitive and emotional baggage. "I want you, but more than that, I want you to want me."

"The whole problem with soulmates is the connection is so profound and so deep that they're not what you want, and you can't stay away anyway."

A connection that isn't reciprocated today can still be real. Karmic debts aren't always even — an unrequited love now may be the settling of an unrequited love you carried in another life.   • Mistaking compatibility for love. Shared interests build friendships (storge), not necessarily Eros.
  • Trauma dumping. Spiking intimacy too fast reads as "I don't know this person" and drives people away.

  3. Protect the slope of intimacy. When you meet someone promising, resist both extremes: don't dump your whole history at once, and don't clam up. Let what you share accelerate gradually.
  4. Build a separate emotional outlet. If you tend to over-share when you feel connected, create a pressure valve elsewhere — a journal, a friend, a therapist — so you can be open with a partner without flooding them.
