Summary

TL;DR — Surya Namaskara (Sun Salutation) isn't about nailing perfect postures — it's a repeatable flow that lets you drop deeper into your body each round. Work at your own level, sync movement with breath, and use the calm, embodied state it builds as a doorway into self-awareness that carries off the mat.

Done as a ritual — the same sequence again and again — the practice stops being a checklist of techniques and becomes a moving meditation. You stop fussing over "am I doing this right?" and start noticing what's happening in your muscles, breath, mind, and emotions. It's taught in three tiers — beginner, regular, advanced — so any body has a version that challenges without harming.

Safety first: this is a physical practice. With blood pressure issues, a chronic condition, or no movement experience, consult a professional and go gently. Stop if something feels wrong.

Ground, Warm Up, Then Move

Ground first. Before a single muscle moves, sit cross-legged, close your eyes, and "wash your awareness from the top of your head to the soles of your feet." Scan three layers — body (sensations, breath), mind (thoughts), emotion (name it: anger, peace, boredom). You arrive carrying something; naming it means you practice with yourself rather than steamrolling your state. Optionally set an intention — an attitude (patience, curiosity) or a focus of attention.

Warm up the specific areas the salutations tax: child's pose with side stretches, neck rolls and shoulder rolls (never past a seven or eight out of ten stretch), hip windshield-wipers, and bridges (work from abs, glutes, quads — never the lower back), finishing with a knee hug.

The Three Tiers

The point of tiers: there's no shame in working at your own pace. Make the practice yours without a thought for anyone else's mat.

The skeleton is shared. From Samasthiti (mountain — engaged like a martial artist, not stiff): inhale to Urdhva Hastasana (arms up), exhale to Uttanasana (forward fold), inhale to Ashwa Sanchalanasana (low lunge), hold for Chaturanga (plank), lower to Cobra/Sphinx, exhale to Adho Mukha Svanasana (down dog), lunge the other side, and rise back to mountain. Three rounds; pause and feel what changed after each.

  • Beginner: bent knees, hands on hips or shins, blocks under hands in the lunge, knees-down plank, Sphinx instead of cobra, bent-knee down dog. "Passive" poses still demand abs and glutes.
  • Regular (Surya Namaskar A): supports come off — straight legs, full plank, Ashtanga Namaskara (eight-limbed salute) into full cobra, straight-knee down dog. Aim for one breath, one movement.
  • Advanced: layer on bound hands, Lizard pose, three-legged plank/dog, surfing into upward dog, hip openers, fingertip work. If a tier is too much, drop back — doing what's right for your body is the whole point.

Breath and the Mind-Body Connection

Two skills elevate this from exercise to practice.

Synchronize breath with movement — inhale to rise and open, exhale to fold and lower. Linking them, you "fall into the flow" toward a meditative state. Long holds are also fine; they get you deeply into the body.

Experiment with it yourself and find what's right for you in your practice.

Tune in — the mind-body element commercial studios often skip. Notice sensations in hip flexors, calves, shoulders (especially the back leg, which gets ignored), and notice the mind and emotions too. This inward attention is what turns the same movements into self-awareness, not just flexibility. The ritual is why it works: knowing the sequence frees you to drop deeper each round (traditionally 108 rounds). Do as many as you can while staying present with good form — quality of attention beats reps.

Off the Mat: Cross-Train and Carry Embodiment

Doing only yoga can create weaknesses (e.g. weak knees). Strength work builds the muscle to go deeper into poses — and do yoga after hard workouts, not before.

Sometimes my knees were getting quite weak from doing yoga every single day, and so going to the gym has been really helping build my muscles so that I can continue doing more yoga.

The embodiment you build is portable — bring the same internal attention to lifting, running, or sport.

The Ego Meditation: "Who Am I?"

Close with self-inquiry (Ahamkara meditation). Ground, lengthen the spine, pick an anchor (breath, hands, teeth). Then drop in the question "Who am I?" and watch what bubbles up unforced — verbal (labels, roles) or non-verbal (images, memories, sensation patterns). Thank your psyche after each answer, then ask again: "Who am I, really?" Notice both what identities appear and how you react to them. You're not resolving anything — just seeing what you're made of. Then release and return to your anchor.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing the full pose. Folding flat with tight hamstrings just compromises your back — go as far as a straight spine allows.
  • Letting the lower back work. Bridges, cobra, back bends engage abs and glutes; lower-back strain means modify.
  • Treating Sphinx/cobra as rest — they demand real engagement.
  • Moving on autopilot. The tuning-in is the practice.
  • Ego. A stretch is a seven or eight, not a ten; refusing blocks out of pride hurts alignment and breath.
  • Skipping the warm-up or ignoring sharp warning-sign pain.

  5. Tune in every round — pause and notice what changed.
  6. Use blocks as alignment tools wherever reaching the floor crunches your shoulders.
  7. Experiment: alternate the leading leg, hold poses longer, try ambient music if you drift.
  8. Cross-train — add strength work; do yoga after hard workouts.
  9. Carry embodiment off the mat into one other activity this week.

Warming Up the Body

Sun Salutation gets intensive fast, so prepare the specific areas it will demand. A short, targeted warm-up is the difference between opening up and straining.

  • Child's pose (Balasana) with side stretches. Knees wide, sit back on your heels, crawl your fingers forward, forehead toward the mat. Keeping the chest parallel to the ground, walk your hands right to stretch the left side, breathe, return, then left. Roll up vertebra by vertebra, neck last.
  • Neck rolls (Vajrasana). Sync with breath: exhale chin down, inhale right ear to right shoulder, hold as the head tilts slightly back (not all the way), exhale to the left. Attend closely to the neck.
  • Shoulder rolls. Fingertips on shoulders, inhale elbows forward and up, exhale back and down. Never push past a seven or eight out of ten stretch.
  • Hip prep on your back. Windshield-wiper the knees side to side. Then bridges: contract abs, squeeze glutes, push through the feet and lift. The work is done by abs, glutes, and quads — not the lower back. There should be no pressure in the lower back.
  • Knee hug. Squeeze knees to chest, flex the feet, then roll to one side and come to standing.

This primes the neck, shoulders, wrists, hips, and core engagement the salutations will tax.

The Three Tiers of Surya Namaskara

The genius of teaching it in tiers is that there's no shame in working at your own pace. If you're new, watch the first round, then follow along. If the beginner or regular versions challenge you enough, stay there while others go advanced. Make the practice yours.

The Beginner Sequence

Start in Samasthiti / Sthiti (mountain pose) — feet and legs together, muscles gently engaged from soles to neck. Not stiff like a soldier; engaged like a martial artist, ready to move.

  1. Inhale → Urdhva Hastasana (upward hands): arms up, palms facing, biceps by the ears, look forward.
  2. Exhale → Uttanasana (forward fold), modified: feet hip-width, knees bent, hands on hips, then freefall the upper body with the neck aligned to the spine. Bend the knees deeply, belly toward thighs, grab behind the calves. Too much? Do Ardha Uttanasana (half-lift), hands on shins, back flat.
  3. Inhale → Ashwa Sanchalanasana (low lunge), right side: right foot forward between the hands, left knee and foot down, right knee at 90°. Use blocks under your hands if needed.
  4. Hold breath → modified Chaturanga (knees-down plank): straight line head to knees, abs and glutes engaged.
  5. Lower → Bhujangasana (cobra) or beginner Sphinx: forearms down, pelvis pressed to the floor, abs and glutes holding, feet pushing. Looks passive, full of engagement.
  6. Exhale → Adho Mukha Svanasana (downward dog), knees bent: hands wide, spine long, gaze between the feet. "Walk the dog" to warm calves and hamstrings.
  7. Inhale → Ashwa Sanchalanasana, left side. Then exhale → Uttanasana, roll up, inhale → Urdhva Hastasana, exhale → Samasthiti with hands at the chest (Anjali Mudra).

Three rounds. After each, pause and feel what's changed.

The Regular Sequence (Surya Namaskar A)

Same structure, supports off, spine working harder.

  • In upward hands, look up between your palms, drawing the ribs up.
  • In forward fold, legs stay straight, feet together, hinging from the hips. Tight hamstrings? Go as far as a straight back and neck allow — folding to a full "sandwich" is for the flexible, not the goal for everyone.
  • Chaturanga becomes full plank: shoulders over wrists, abs and glutes on, straight line from heels to head.
  • Instead of dropping straight down, do Ashtanga Namaskara (eight-limbed salute): knees, chest, chin to the mat, elbows hugging the ribs, pelvis tilted up — then lower into full Cobra.
  • Downward dog now has straight knees.

By the third round, aim for one breath, one movement — flowing at the natural pace of your breathing.

The Advanced Modifications

These layer strength, mobility, and hip opening onto the same skeleton.

  • Urdhva Baddha Hastasana (upward bound hands): interlace fingers, push palms up, gaze up, abs engaged. Add a gentle upper-back bend — bending only from the mid-back up, never the lower back.
  • Low lunge → Lizard pose: keep the back knee lifted, or bring the front leg outside the same-side shoulder; press the inner front thigh out with your hand to open the hip flexor.
  • Three-legged plank, dog, and upward dog: lift one leg in each to deepen stretch and challenge stability — keep the body centered.
  • Surfing into Upward Dog: glide from plank down low (without touching the floor) and roll up into upward-facing dog — shoulders back, hips off the mat — then press to down dog.
  • Hip openers from down dog: draw a knee forward into a "chicken-wing" and open the hip while the chest stays forward.

The principle throughout: if a tier is too much, drop back a level. Doing what's right for your body is the whole point.

Breath, Flow, and the Mind-Body Connection

Two ideas elevate this from exercise to practice.

First, synchronize breath with movement. Inhale to rise and open, exhale to fold and lower. Linked, you "fall into the flow" instead of getting tangled in technique. Holding poses longer is also fine — long holds get you deeply into the body. There's no single rule:

Experiment with it yourself and find what's right for you in your practice.

A tip if you hold poses for 25–30 seconds: catch the natural breath of the sequence as you transition out, so movement and breath stay aligned.

Second, and most important, is the mind-body connection — often missing from commercial studios:

When you go to a lot of studios, they sometimes are not too concerned with the mind-body element and really tuning into your body and how you're responding to it.

Throughout every round, keep tuning in. Notice the hip flexors, calves, shoulders, and especially the back leg, which gets ignored while attention pools in the front. Notice the mind — is it racing? Notice the emotions — relief, satisfaction, a craving for more. This ongoing inward attention is what turns the same movements into self-awareness rather than just flexibility.

Make the Practice Your Own

These recurring principles are the operating system underneath the poses.

  • Why it's so valued. Its power is the ritual element: knowing the sequence, you stop wrestling technique and drop deeper into each pose. Repetition is the feature — traditionally people do 108 rounds in one sitting.
  • How many to do. As many as you can while staying present with good technique. The moment form or presence breaks, pause. Quality beats quantity.
  • When to use blocks. If reaching the floor in a lunge crunches your shoulders and constricts your chest, blocks open you up — better alignment, open chest, easier breathing, better circulation. A tool, not weakness.
  • Right side first? Traditionally the right leg leads, then the left — but alternating which side leads is a fine way to balance your flexibility.
  • As a meditation warm-up. Yes — preparing body and mind for meditation is a core purpose (see the eight limbs of yoga: asana sets up pranayama and meditation).
  • On waking, or with music. Fine on waking if you warm up first. Ambient music isn't traditional but gives a wandering mind somewhere to return from, back into the body.

Cross-Training and Carrying Embodiment Off the Mat

A counterintuitive point: balance yoga with other movement. Doing only yoga can create weaknesses — knees getting weak from daily practice, for instance. Strength work builds the muscle that lets you go deeper into poses.

Sometimes my knees were getting quite weak from doing yoga every single day, and so going to the gym has been really helping build my muscles so that I can continue doing more yoga.

(Sequencing note: do yoga after a workout, not before — beforehand you can feel too loose and "wonky" to train with control.)

The bigger principle: the embodiment you cultivate on the mat is portable. The same internal attention can go into lifting, running, dancing with a partner, or playing a sport — balancing awareness of yourself with awareness of your surroundings. The point of these practices is ultimately their effect on everything off the mat.

The Ego Meditation: Asking "Who Am I?"

The session closes with self-inquiry (also called Ahamkara meditation).

First, ground. Sit comfortably, lengthen the spine as if the crown reaches the ceiling, tuck the chin slightly, roll the shoulders back. Pick an anchor — breath at the belly, sensations in hands or feet, the contact of your teeth — and let it rest in the background.

Then, with the anchor steady, drop a question into your awareness: "Who am I?" Don't force answers; watch what bubbles up on its own. Answers can be:

  • Verbal — words, labels, roles.
  • Non-verbal — images, memories, or patterns of sensation (if you've lived with anxiety, you may be identified with a familiar pattern in your body).

After each answer, thank your psyche — "thank you very much" — and ask again: "Who am I, really?" This is meditative questing: searching to see what comes, not racing to a conclusion.

Two things matter equally: what identities appear (including ones you didn't know you held), and how you react to them. You're not resolving anything — just noticing what you're made of and how you relate to it. When done, release the answers and your reactions, and return to the anchor in your body.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing the full expression of a pose. Folding flat, looking up, going advanced — not the goal. Forcing a sandwich fold with tight hamstrings compromises the back. Go as far as a straight spine allows.
  • Letting the lower back do the work. In bridges, cobra, sphinx, and back bends, engagement comes from abs and glutes (and bending from the mid-back up). Lower-back strain means modify.
  • Treating "passive" poses as rest. Sphinx and cobra demand real abdominal and glute engagement plus support through hands and feet.
  • Moving without checking in. Cranking reps on autopilot wastes the practice — the mind-body tuning is the practice.
  • Pushing past your edge for ego. A stretch should be a seven or eight, not a ten. Comparing yourself to others; refusing blocks out of pride.
  • Ignoring warning signs. Sharp, concerning discomfort means stop or modify. With any chronic condition, get informed guidance.
  • Skipping the warm-up. Cold joints invite injury.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ground before you move. Scan body, mind, and emotion — name what you find. Optionally set one intention.
  2. Do the targeted warm-up: child's pose with side stretches, neck rolls, shoulder rolls, hip windshield-wipers, bridges (abs/glutes/quads, not lower back), knee hug.
  3. Pick your tier honestly. Start with three rounds of the beginner sequence. Move to regular only when genuinely manageable. Treat advanced as optional layering.
  4. Build toward one breath, one movement. Let your breath set the pace — inhale to open and rise, exhale to fold and lower.
  5. Tune in every round. After each, pause and notice what's changed in body, breath, mind, and emotions.
  6. Use blocks as alignment tools wherever reaching the floor crunches your shoulders or rounds your spine.
  7. Experiment to find your version: alternate which leg leads, hold poses longer some days, try ambient music if you struggle to stay present.
  8. Cross-train. Add strength or other movement work — and do yoga after hard workouts, not before.
  9. Carry embodiment off the mat. Bring the same internal attention into one other activity this week — a walk, a lift, a sport.
  10. Try the ego meditation. Ground with an anchor, repeatedly ask "Who am I?", watch what arises, thank your psyche, notice your reaction, and ask again — without forcing an answer.
  11. Work up gradually toward longer sets. The traditional target is 108 rounds — but only to the point where technique and presence hold.

  • Why Surya Namaskara is so valued. Its power is the ritual element. Because you know the sequence, you stop wrestling the technique and start dropping deeper into each pose, noticing subtle shifts in sensation and awareness round after round. Repetition is the feature, not the boredom — traditionally people do 108 rounds in one sitting.
  • How many to do. Do as many as you can while still being present and keeping good technique. The moment your form or your presence breaks down, pause. Quality of attention beats quantity of reps.
  • When to use blocks. Build body intuition. If drawing your hands to the floor in a lunge crunches your shoulders and constricts your chest, blocks open you up — better spinal alignment, an open chest, easier breathing, and better circulation. It's a tool for alignment, not a sign of weakness.
  • Right side first? Traditionally the right leg leads, then the left. But if you're not bound by a ritual reason, alternating which side leads is a perfectly good way to balance your flexibility.
  • As a meditation warm-up. Yes — preparing the body and mind for meditation is one of its core purposes. (Look up the eight limbs of yoga to see how asana is meant to set up pranayama and meditation.)
  • Doing it on waking. Fine, as long as you give yourself a light warm-up first — the warm-up sequence above exists precisely to ready the areas the salutations stress.
  • With music. Not traditional, but a genuinely useful tool, especially if you struggle to stay in your body. Ambient music gives the wandering mind somewhere to return from, back into the body.

Cross-Training and Carrying Embodiment Off the Mat

A counterintuitive but valuable point: balance yoga with other movement. Doing only yoga can actually create weaknesses — for instance, knees getting weak from daily practice. Adding strength work in the gym builds the muscle that lets you go deeper into the poses. Cross-training — gym, running, swimming, martial arts, even dance — rounds out what any single discipline neglects.

Sometimes my knees were getting quite weak from doing yoga every single day, and so going to the gym has been really helping build my muscles so that I can continue doing more yoga.

(One sequencing note: doing yoga after a workout or sport tends to work better than before it — afterward you can feel too loose and "wonky" to train with control.)

The bigger principle is that the embodiment you cultivate on the mat is portable. The same internal attention you practice in a sun salutation can be brought to lifting weights, running, dancing with a partner, or playing a sport — balancing awareness of yourself with awareness of the music, your partner, or your surroundings. The more you intersect what you learn in yoga and meditation with the rest of your life, the deeper the practice gets — because the point of these practices is ultimately their effect on everything off the mat.

The Ego Meditation: Asking "Who Am I?"

The session closes with a self-inquiry practice (also called Ahamkara meditation).

First, ground. Sit comfortably, lengthen the spine as if the crown reaches for the ceiling, tuck the chin slightly, roll the shoulders back to open the chest. Pick an anchor — the breath at the belly, sensations in the hands or feet, the contact of your teeth — and let it rest quietly in the background.

Then, with that anchor steady, drop a question into your awareness: "Who am I?" You don't try to think of answers or force them. You watch what bubbles up on its own. Answers can be:

  • Verbal — words, labels, roles.
  • Non-verbal — images, memories, or patterns of sensation (for example, if you've lived with anxiety a long time, you may be identified with a familiar pattern in your body).

After each answer surfaces, thank your psyche — "thank you very much" — and ask again: "Who am I, really?" One way to frame it is meditative questing: you're searching to see what comes, not racing toward a conclusion.

Two things matter equally:

  1. What identities appear — including ones you didn't know you were holding.
  2. How you react to them — some will feel surprising, some will trigger a visceral response.

You're not trying to resolve anything. You're noticing what you're made of and how you relate to it. When you're done, release the answers (and any lack of answers), let go of your reactions, and return to the anchor in your body.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing the full expression of a pose. Folding flat into your legs, looking up, going advanced — these are not the goal. Forcing a sandwich fold with tight hamstrings just compromises your back. Go as far as a straight spine allows.
  • Letting the lower back do the work. In bridges, cobra, sphinx, and back bends, the engagement comes from abs and glutes (and bending from the mid-back up). If you feel it straining your lower back, you're doing it wrong — come lower or modify.
  • Treating "passive" poses as rest. Sphinx and cobra look relaxed but demand real abdominal and glute engagement plus support through hands and feet.
  • Moving without checking in. Cranking through reps on autopilot wastes the practice. The mind-body tuning is the practice.
  • Pushing past your edge for ego. A stretch should be a seven or eight, not a ten. Comparing yourself to others on their mats. Refusing blocks out of pride when they'd improve your alignment and breathing.
  • Ignoring warning signs. A normal stretch is fine; sharp, concerning discomfort means stop or modify. With any chronic condition, get informed guidance rather than forcing a standard sequence.
  • Skipping the warm-up. The sequence is intensive; cold joints invite injury.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ground before you move. Sit for a minute and scan body, mind, and emotion — name what you find. Optionally set one intention (an attitude or a focus of attention).
  2. Do the targeted warm-up: child's pose with side stretches, neck rolls, shoulder rolls, hip windshield-wipers, bridges (abs/glutes/quads, not lower back), knee hug.
  3. Pick your tier honestly. Start with three rounds of the beginner sequence. Move to regular only when it's genuinely manageable. Treat advanced as optional layering, dropping back any time.
  4. Build toward one breath, one movement. On a later round, let your breath set the pace — inhale to open and rise, exhale to fold and lower.
  5. Tune in every round. After each one, pause and notice what's changed in your body, your breath, your mind, and your emotions.
  6. Use blocks as alignment tools wherever reaching the floor crunches your shoulders or rounds your spine — no shame in it.
  7. Experiment to find your version: try alternating which leg leads, hold poses longer some days, try practicing to ambient music if you struggle to stay present.
  8. Cross-train. Add strength or other movement work to support the flexibility-heavy demands of yoga — and do yoga after hard workouts, not before.
  9. Carry embodiment off the mat. Deliberately bring the same internal attention into one other activity this week — a walk, a lift, a sport.
  10. Try the ego meditation. Ground with an anchor, then repeatedly ask "Who am I?" Watch what arises (words, images, sensations), thank your psyche after each, notice your reaction, and ask again — without trying to reach an answer.
  11. Work up gradually toward longer sets. The traditional target is 108 rounds — but only ever to the point where your technique and presence hold.
