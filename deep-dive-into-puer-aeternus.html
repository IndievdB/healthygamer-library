Pathway One: The Evidence-Based Approach

These map onto ADHD (often the biggest contributor), avoidant attachment, trauma/CPTSD (avoidance coping, low distress tolerance), and sometimes mood disorders.

To amplify results, build EQ: self-awareness, emotional regulation, distress tolerance, identity development. This is the most reliable path, and the puer concept never has to enter the room.

Pathway Two: The Classic (Shadow Work) Approach

To work within the puer language, find the shadow — the polarized energy you're not aware of. The shadow forms when one thing splits in two: half in the light, half in darkness. Interrogate each surface feature by asking where it was learned.

1. Fear of lost potential. You avoid commitment because deep down you don't believe you can handle it. You tell yourself you can't do dreary work, so you never test yourself, so you never gather data, so you stay trapped in hypotheticals. Three learned experiences recur: fear you won't live up to potential; disbelief you can manage when things go wrong; once losing potential and being punished brutally. This is where parentification surfaces — kids made responsible for an ill, addicted, or abusive parent, learning it's never enough, I can't handle things when they fall apart.

"Write out the rules of potential as if I were an alien. Where does it come from? How do you lose it? How do you live up to it?"

2. The relationship with time. The puer's youthful exuberance is a cover for panic about time. The cycle is brutal: the more time you "run out of," the more important the right choice feels; but you can't see the future, so you freeze; another year passes, desperation rises, paralysis deepens. "I'm 35, this is my last shot." Ask: when did time become your enemy?

3. Failure to commit. Sometimes you fear commitment because you've never committed. But sometimes you fear it because you committed once and it genuinely blew up. That's a learning, not a neurosis. Distinguish the two.

4. Loathing of ordinary work. ADHD and dopamine explain a lot, combined with an upbringing that never taught the satisfaction of work for its own sake. The puer is stuck on what's it worth? — never on the action itself being rewarding.

The cure: substitute experience for thinking

"What alters your thinking more than anything else is experience. It's not thinking."

The puer needs to start living. Yes, committing does cost potential — and that's okay. You're losing potential anyway: every day passes and is gone; there's no conserving it. You can major in one thing and still spend 20–30 hours a week on the side thing. Two truths the puer hasn't felt:

  • We are extraordinarily resilient. People asked whether they'd rather die or lose both arms overwhelmingly choose death. Ask people who actually lost their arms a year later — they're glad to be alive. The fear of the thing is more dangerous than the thing itself.
  • Failing after giving your all is devastating — but picking yourself back up is wonderful. The puer sees only the fall, never the recovery.

Once you've lived through it, it's no longer something to fear. Then the archetype constellates: your creativity, playfulness, and healthy attention to opportunity cost all integrate. The archetype was never the enemy — it was just out of balance.

Pathway Three: The Spiritual Approach — Ego and Time

The yogic tradition does something radically different. Medicine adjusts your conscious surface; Jung dives into the unconscious; the yogis ignore the content of the mind entirely and go beyond mind. Their logic: it doesn't matter what kind of broken ego you have. "Ego is ego." Don't fix problems one at a time — purify the whole instrument. This is why meditation works across nearly every mental illness: it works on the root, not the branch.

Two root tattvas are the whole game:

Ahamkara — the ego, the "I"

Puer language drowns in I: I want to be this. I am this. The decisive insight:

"Your dream of traveling the world is not about traveling the world. It's about you. You want to be the person who travels the world."

That's why you're paralyzed. If an action won't deliver the image of yourself you're attached to, you won't do it. The distinction to train: Do you want to be in shape, or to be someone who is in shape? Do you want to help the person who dropped their groceries — or to be a person who helps people? One discharges and is gone; the other you carry as identity and it activates the whole puer machinery (comparison, the need to be seen, collapse when the self-image isn't fast enough to materialize). Trace ego-changing desires back and you'll find an insecurity: "I am ugly down here, therefore I must be beautiful up there." Dissolve the ego, and the puer is gone.

Kala — time

The puer doesn't just say "this is unpleasant" — they say "this is unpleasant times infinity."

"Unpleasant times infinity equals pain that's too great — which turns into inaction."

Remove that variable — it's only unpleasant for a while — and action becomes possible. (This is attachment to time, not ignoring time as a real planning dimension.) Ahamkara and kala are braided: the idea of progression comes from time; the dissatisfaction with it comes from ego. Together they paralyze you.

Gross vs. Subtle

People ask "so what do I DO to fix puer? Just grind forever?" No. There's sthula (gross, the level of action) and sukshma (subtle, the level of mind and attitude).

"Puer is not in the dimension of action. Fixing it is in the dimension of the subtle. It is the internal psychic attitude that determines whether the action will work."

Any action can be hijacked by the puer. Go to the gym to fix your self-image, and the dreariness will defeat you because it was all for your ego. The shift is in how you act, not what you do. "There's a huge difference between working toward peace and wanting to be someone who is peaceful."

The Throat Chakra and the So Hum Practice

The experimental frontier: archetypes correlate with chakras and seed sounds, so working a chakra directly may resolve the archetype downstream. Puer — a problem of potential and manifestation — maps onto Vishuddha, the throat chakra, the seat of bringing inner energy (shakti) into the world. Words shape reality; pyramids got built because someone spoke. The puer is stuck precisely at "I have all this potential and can't make it manifest."

Two tools:

1. Ujjayi Pranayama ("Darth Vader breath"). Sit straight — neck, back, head aligned. Gently contract the throat and breathe so it softly hisses on inhale and exhale.

2. The So Hum mantra. So Hum means "I am that which I am." No potential, no more, no less. This is ego-dissolution that leaves you intact. A pure emptiness practice leaves you with nothing; So Hum strips away all the potential ego and leaves the real you, free to become what you're actually meant to be.

"I am that which I am. Not more, not less. Ahamkara always has a value judgment — better, worse, comparison. So Hum has none."

As you chant, you'll notice that impulses and cravings are not you — they're "barnacles." "You're not really an addict. You're just a human who has desires."

How to practice: Listen for so on the inhale, hum on the exhale (Ujjayi makes it audible), or chant actively. Use a mala: start with 108 repetitions, work up over time toward 9 then 12 malas a day. Attention is everything — a mala done with a wandering mind doesn't count. Focused attention is a laser; diffuse attention is a lightbulb.

Timeframe: three to seven years of diligent practice before judging. And critically — don't commit. That's the kala trap again. Just do it today. The practice itself trains you out of the time-attachment that makes you ask "how long?"

Common Pitfalls

  • Identifying as a puer. "That's just a part of your psychology." Treating it as fixed destiny is the horoscope trap.
  • Asking "what action fixes this?" The fix lives in the subtle (attitude), not the gross (action). Any action can be hijacked if your underlying attitude is unchanged.
  • Letting your ego hijack the cure. "I'll become a tantric practitioner and it'll cure my puer" is pure ego — a new self-image to chase. Do the practice; don't get attached to becoming someone through it.
  • Confusing imagination with perception in meditation. When you strain to "hear" So Hum, you may manufacture it. The cure is time: trust what shows up consistently, not one-off experiences.
  • Wrong dosage, then declaring failure. "I did this a month and still have problems" isn't a fair test. The minimum effective dose is measured in focused attention over years.
  • Chasing flashy experiences. Visions, "ego death" moments — let them come and go, then return to the practice.
  • Sloppy posture. How you sit is a multiplier.
  • Forgetting potential is lost either way. Committing feels like conserving potential. You're not. Every day passes regardless.

  1. Pick your pathway by temperament. Reliability → evidence-based (get assessed for ADHD, attachment, trauma, mood disorders). Depth → classic shadow work with a trained therapist. Spiritual → begin So Hum. You don't need all three.

  6. Substitute experience for thinking. Commit to one thing you've been circling and live through a hard patch — to gather data on your resilience and on how good picking yourself back up feels.
  7. Learn Ujjayi breath. Sit straight, contract the throat gently, breathe so it softly hisses. Daily until natural.
  8. Begin So Hum with a mala. 108 repetitions with full attention; so on the inhale, hum on the exhale. Work up gradually toward 9 then 12 malas a day.
  9. Don't commit — just do today. Refuse to ask "how long until it works?" Practice today; reassess tomorrow. Let the not-committing dismantle your attachment to time.
  10. Give it a real dose before judging. Three to seven years of attentive practice. Treat flashy experiences as scenery, not destinations.

Pathway Three: The Spiritual Approach — Ego and Time

The yogic tradition does something radically different from both medicine and Jung. Medicine adjusts your conscious surface (textbook CBT). Jung dives into the unconscious to resolve conflicts and integrate shadows. The yogis ignore the content of the mind entirely and go beyond mind altogether.

Their logic: it doesn't matter what kind of broken ego you have. Low self-esteem about your body, your career, your relationships — the yogi doesn't care about the flavor. "Ego is ego." Don't fix one problem at a time; purify the whole instrument. This is why meditation and mindfulness are effective across nearly every mental illness — they work on the root, not the branch.

Applied to the puer, two root tattvas (fundamental qualities) are the whole game:

Ahamkara — the ego, the "I"

Look closely at puer language and you'll find it drowning in I: I want to be this. I am this. I want to live up to my potential. Here's the subtle, decisive insight:

"Your dream of traveling the world is not about traveling the world. It's about you. You don't actually want to travel the world — you want to be the person who travels the world."

That's why you're paralyzed. If an action won't deliver the image of yourself you're attached to, you won't do it. Take away the need to live up to a self-image, and suddenly you could do anything.

The practical distinction to train: Do you want to be in shape, or do you want to be someone who is in shape? Do you want to help the person who dropped their groceries — or to be a person who helps people? One discharges and is gone; the other you carry as identity. One is free of ego; the other activates the whole puer machinery (comparison, the need to be seen, collapse when the self-image isn't fast enough to materialize).

So: watch your desires. Some are clean and time-limited (eating a hamburger). Others, when gratified, change your ego — and if you trace those back, you'll find an insecurity at the root. "I am ugly down here, therefore I must be beautiful up there" — that polarization is the puer engine. Dissolve the ego, and the puer is, in his words, "gg'd out."

Kala — time

The second root is attachment to time. The puer doesn't just say "this is unpleasant" — they say "this is unpleasant times infinity."

"The reason you can't act is because there's an infinite time applied to whatever unpleasantness. Unpleasant times infinity equals pain that's too great — which turns into inaction."

Remove that variable — it's only unpleasant for a while — and action becomes possible again. (Note: this is attachment to time, not ignoring time as a real planning dimension.)

These two, ahamkara and kala, are braided together. The idea of progression comes from time; the dissatisfaction with your progression comes from ego. Together they paralyze you, and your mind will twist endlessly around them. Watch how much weight you actually feel from time, and how much wanting comes from "I."

Gross vs. Subtle: Why You Can't "Just Do" Your Way Out

A core trap: people ask "so what do I actually DO to fix puer? Just grind forever?" No.

In the yogic frame there's sthula (gross, the level of action) and sukshma (subtle, the level of mind and attitude).

"Puer is not in the dimension of action. Fixing it is in the dimension of the subtle. It is the internal psychic attitude that determines whether the action will work or not."

Any action you take can be hijacked by the puer. Go to the gym to fix your self-image, and the dreariness will defeat you because the whole thing was for your ego. The shift is in how you act, not what you do. Can you do something for the joy of it? Can you study hard not to be top of the class but because you genuinely want to master the thing? It's subtle — "there's a huge difference between working toward peace and wanting to be someone who is peaceful" — but night and day.

The Throat Chakra and the So Hum Practice

Here's the experimental frontier. The claim: archetypes correlate with tattvas, chakras, and seed sounds — so working a chakra directly may resolve the archetype downstream, bypassing the psychology entirely.

Puer, being a problem of potential and manifestation, maps onto Vishuddha, the throat chakra — the seat of manifestation, of bringing your inner energy (shakti) out into the world. The pen is mightier than the sword; pyramids got built because someone spoke; words are how we shape reality. The puer is stuck precisely at "I have all this potential and can't make it manifest." Work Vishuddha, and the puer complex should start to slough off.

Two tools:

1. Ujjayi Pranayama ("Darth Vader breath"). Sit straight — neck, back, head aligned. Gently contract the throat and breathe through that contraction, producing a soft hissing sound on the inhale and exhale.

2. The So Hum mantra. So Hum means "I am that which I am." No potential, no more, no less — just you. This is ego-dissolution that leaves you intact. A pure emptiness practice (shunya) leaves you with nothing; So Hum strips away all the potential ego and leaves the real you, grounded, free to move "dharmically and karmically" — to become what you're actually meant to be, not the inflated self-image.

"I am that which I am. Not more, not less. There's no value judgment. Ahamkara always has a value judgment — better, worse, comparison. So Hum has none."

As you chant, you'll notice that impulses, habits, and cravings are not you — they're "barnacles" attached to your being. "You're not really an addict. You're just a human who has desires." Your self-image simplifies, in a good way.

How to practice: - Listen for So Hum inside the breath itself — a soft "so" on the inhale, "hum" on the exhale. (Ujjayi makes it audible.) Or chant it actively. - Use a mala: start with 108 repetitions, work up over time to 9 malas, eventually up to 12 a day. - Attention is everything. A mala done with a wandering mind doesn't count. Tantric practice is not completionist — it's about focused attention. Diffuse attention is a lightbulb; focused attention is a laser.

Timeframe: three to seven years of diligent practice before you should judge whether it works. And critically — don't commit. That would be the kala trap all over again. Just do it today. What does today cost? Do it tomorrow. The practice itself trains you out of the time-attachment that makes you ask "how long?"

Common Pitfalls

  • Identifying as a puer. "You're not a puer eternus. That's just a part of your psychology." Treating it as your fixed identity — "I'm doomed to drift" — is the horoscope trap. The archetype is a slice of you that's out of balance, not your destiny.
  • Asking "what action fixes this?" The fix lives in the subtle (attitude), not the gross (action). Any action can be hijacked by the puer if your underlying attitude is unchanged.
  • Letting your ego hijack the cure itself. "I'll do tantric practice and become a tantric practitioner, and it'll cure my puer." That's pure ego — a new self-image to chase. Do the practice; don't get attached to becoming someone through it.
  • Confusing imagination with perception in meditation. When you strain to "hear" So Hum, you may manufacture it. The cure is time: perception solidifies with practice, imagination whiffs. Don't trust one-off experiences — trust what shows up consistently.
  • Wrong dosage, then declaring failure. "I did this for a month and still have problems" isn't a fair test. There's a minimum effective dose for spiritual practice, and it's measured in focused attention over years, not reps mindlessly logged.
  • Chasing flashy meditation experiences. Visions, "ego death" moments — let them come and go, learn what you can, return to the practice. Don't get tripped up or think you're done.
  • Practicing in sloppy posture. Try a mudra lying curled on your side versus sitting with a straight spine — you'll feel the difference. How you sit is a multiplier.
  • Forgetting potential is lost either way. "If I commit to this, I lose that" feels like conserving potential. You're not. Every day passes regardless. There is no saving it up.
