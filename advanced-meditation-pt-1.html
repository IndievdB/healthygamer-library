TL;DR — Advanced meditation isn't a fancier technique; it's training your awareness to become subtle enough to move away from the objects of experience (body, thoughts, emotions) and toward the bare experiencer itself — and that requires patient preparation, not greed for results.

A technique is only an ingredient. What determines progress is the refinement of your awareness — your ability to detect tiny things inside yourself — built slowly over years. The map is simple: you normally live in the material world, attention hooked outward through five senses. Beneath body and mind sits an experiencer, the one constant that has witnessed your whole life. You Are the Experiencer

Science extends our reach into matter (telescopes, fMRI), but it can measure only physical correlates — never a thought or the felt experience of being alive. That whole dimension of subjective experience is where advanced meditation operates.

Borrowing from Advaita Vedanta, peel yourself apart. You are not your body — your grammar gives it away (my body, my headache); the body changes endlessly while you remain you. You are not your mind — the mind isn't a thing but activity, thoughts and emotions arising and passing like ripples (vrittis) in a pond. What remains is the constant witness.

"The one thing that is constant in you is that you are the experiencer of your life."

Picture an axis: body (gross matter) at the bottom, mind in the middle, experiencer at the top. Advanced meditation moves up this axis toward conscious experience without an object — samadhi. That destination has no qualities and no wants (nirgun); all pains and cravings belong to body and mind, not to the neutral capacity for experience itself.

Patanjali's Ladder

Each rung stands on the one below:

  1. Pratyahara — withdrawing attention from the outside world. Brahmari Pranayam in Shanmukhi Mudra (sealing the senses, humming) floods awareness inward.
  2. Dharana — sustained concentration on one object (e.g., candle-gazing, trataka). Only possible because you did pratyahara first.
  3. Dhyana — meditation proper, where you and the object merge (the flow state is a cousin).
  4. Samadhi — within dhyana, spontaneously, the object drops away and pure awareness remains. It can't be forced.

"An advanced technique won't advance you further unless you have done the necessary preparation."

Subtlety and Preparation Are the Practice

The texts distinguish gross (coarse, material) from subtle (fine, refined). Subtle awareness is the ability to feel the tiniest fluctuation within yourself and follow it upward — the same faculty separating a master chef or champion from the rest. This is why identical guided meditations land wildly differently: the variable is the subtlety of your awareness and the cleanliness of your prana.

Patanjali starts not with meditation but with yama, niyama, asana, pranayama — ethics, posture, breathwork — preparing the instrument. Like an Olympic runner, the technique is trivial; the diet, sleep, and recovery around it make the difference. These are the 20% multipliers that compound over years.

The Puer Practice (Muladhara ↔ Vishuddha)

"Any power or capacity we have that is not used well will turn into toxicity."

Strengthen the link between Muladhara (root — primal craving, dopamine) and Vishuddha (throat — making vision real).

  • Locate both: clench to find the perineum point; a Darth Vader breath finds the throat. Don't force precision.
  • Map the mantras: chant Lum and Hum, noticing each vibration's direction and quality.
  • Move the breath: sit upright, jnana mudra. Inhale up the front (root → throat), exhale down the spine.
  • Zoom out: hold the whole circuit at once — front and back. This "stepping back" is itself the movement toward the experiencer.
  • Add mantra internally: mentally repeat Lum up, Hum down. Mantra escalates in power as it goes inward (aloud = 1, whisper = 10, mental = 100, spontaneous = 1,000).

Zooming Out Explains Suffering

Fully zoomed in on a thought, you lose perspective and it becomes total reality ("my life is over"). This is the mechanism of attachment and suffering. Belief in your thoughts forms a spectrum: 100% → delusion, then panic, anxiety, normal, tranquility, meditation, enlightenment. CBT calls this thought diffusion; we say "take a step back."

"Where are you stepping from, and where are you stepping to? You are moving from the object toward the witness."

Bliss (samadhi feels literally orgasmic) is not the goal — just what that state is made of. The aim is to un-forget that you already are the witness. And motivation isn't fuel to burn: it's your default state, buried by processed food and doomscrolling. The practice removes blocks; motivation flows on its own.

Common Pitfalls

  • Skipping preparation to chase advanced techniques. Simple practices done well beat fancy ones done unprepared.
  • Expecting fast results. This is a years-to-decades path; rough heuristic is one "level up" per seven years.
  • Fixating on bliss/samadhi. Bliss is a byproduct; keep practicing.
  • Practicing intensely while emotionally unwell. During grief or instability, stick to grounded, validated practices.
  • Ignoring warning signs — insomnia, nightmares, palpitations. Stop; see a doctor.
  • Over-interpreting sensations. What you feel depends on your subtlety and prana; don't decode early.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the puer practice for 28 days — 20 minutes once daily minimum; twice is exponentially better.
  2. Locate your two centers (perineum, throat) without forcing precision.
  3. Map Lum and Hum aloud, noticing each vibration's direction and quality.
  4. Do the circuit: inhale up the front, exhale down the spine, mentally repeating Lum up / Hum down.
  5. Practice zooming out — hold the whole loop at once; apply "step back" in daily life.
  6. Try Brahmari in Shanmukhi Mudra as a standalone pratyahara drill.
  7. Build supporting buffs: Surya Namaskar, Nadi Shuddhi, Kapalbhati.
  8. Clean the instrument: move diet toward fresh, sattvic food; notice cravings drop naturally.
  9. Guard your attention — cut hours of external input; reconnect to the inner source of motivation.
  10. Stay safe: if a session brings distress, return to basic practices. There is no rush.

Preparation Is the Practice (The Olympic Principle)

"Advanced practice is doing an advanced technique and also setting yourself up to succeed."

A telling, unforced pattern: serious meditators tend to spontaneously cut back on meat after a couple of years — not from preaching, but because meat dulls subtle awareness. The diet, breathwork, and postures are the 20% multipliers that, compounded over years, make the difference between standing still and arriving.

The Puer Practice: Linking Muladhara and Vishuddha

A concrete advanced practice. Its underlying logic is powerful on its own:

"Any power or capacity that we have as human beings that is not used well will turn into toxicity."

Unrealized potential isn't a neutral empty lot — that energy will manifest, and if not channeled into building, it becomes lust, addiction, substance use. This is why realizing potential and conquering addiction move like a seesaw. The practice strengthens two energy centers:

  • Muladhara (root, perineum) — primal impulses: hunger, thirst, lust, craving, dopamine.
  • Vishuddha (throat) — your capacity to take a vision and make it real.

Step 1 — Locate them. Clench as in a "butt-clenching moment"; the spot between anus and pubic front (not the anus) is muladhara. Make a Darth Vader breath; the throat part you contract is vishuddha. Don't force precision.

Step 2 — Learn the seed mantras. Chant "Lum" and "Hum" and notice the direction and quality of each vibration. Go loud first to feel it, then quiet to catch subtlety.

Step 3 — Move the breath. Sit upright (spine and neck straight, head tilted very slightly back so it truly relaxes), hands in jnana mudra. Inhale, drawing awareness up the front from muladhara to throat. Exhale, sending it down the spine to the root. A "banana shape" up the front, a tube down the back.

Step 4 — Zoom out. Normally attention sits only on the most present feeling. The leap is to hold the whole circuit at once — front and back, the whole racetrack simultaneously. That "stepping back" from any single point is the very movement toward the experiencer.

Step 5 — Add the mantra internally. Mentally repeat Lum up, Hum down. Mantra escalates in power: aloud is one unit (gross, broadcast into matter); whispering is ten; mental is a hundred; spontaneous, automatic is a thousand. The more internal, the closer it works to consciousness. (Beginners still chant aloud because the senses are the anchor we have before we can control the mind.)

Zooming Out: Attachment, Suffering, and Taking a Step Back

The "zoom" idea is the most portable lesson, and it explains suffering itself.

When you are fully zoomed in on a thought, you lose perspective and the thought becomes total reality: "My life is over. I'll be alone forever." This is the mechanism of attachment, and of suffering.

It maps onto a clean spectrum of belief in your own thoughts:

  • Believe a thought 100% → delusion / psychosis
  • Less → panic → anxiety → normal (you can doubt your anxiety)
  • Less still → tranquility, resilience
  • Keep stepping back → meditation, eventually enlightenment

Psychology knows this: CBT calls it thought diffusion; everyday language says "take a step back." Notice the geometry:

"Take a step back. Where are you stepping from, and where are you stepping to?"

You're literally moving from total absorption in an object toward the witness. This is also why stacked failures crush people — each fuses with the previous until you're managing four at once "without processing each one." Zooming in fuses; zooming out separates and frees. The same move that ends a panic spiral is the one that, taken far enough, ends in liberation.

Timescale, Bliss, and the Quality-less Witness

Be honest about time. This works on the scale of years — a rough heuristic of one genuine "level up" every seven years of diligent practice, ideally guided by a teacher who knows which practice will bear fruit for you. But you're not stuck while you wait: every session refines subtle awareness and clears prana, compounding invisibly like savings.

On bliss and samadhi: the felt quality of samadhi is described literally as sustained orgasmic bliss. This is why an enlightened being lives in bliss. But bliss is not the goal and not a side effect — it's simply what that state is made of. The real aim is to recognize that you already are that — to un-forget it.

The destination has no qualities at all — nirgun. Your capacity for experience (not its contents) is spacious, neutral, "kind of gray," present, limited by nothing. All dualities — goal and side-effect, pleasure and pain — belong to the objects witnessed, not the witness. Brahman can't be "explored," because exploration implies mapped and unmapped territory; it is at once an infinite space and a dimensionless point. You don't reason your way in. You abide in the witness.

Motivation Is Your Default State, Not Fuel

Do not use these practices as fuel for motivation — that framing is wrong.

"Motivation arises naturally within you. You don't need fuel because you're the sun."

A healthy human is intrinsically motivated — think of the late-night surge to create, build, do. That energy is the default state. It doesn't run out; it gets buried and extinguished — by processed food, doomscrolling, and six hours a day letting "society" tell you who to be. The puer practice doesn't supply fuel; it removes the blocks, and motivation flows on its own.

Sustainable motivation only arises from inside — borrowed inspiration from content fades. As the warning goes, paraphrasing Krishnamurti: being well-adjusted to a sick society is not health.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing advanced techniques while skipping preparation. The technique is the ingredient, not the meal. Simple practices done well beat fancy ones done unprepared.
  • Expecting fast results. This is a years-to-decades path; judging it on a one-week timescale guarantees quitting.
  • Getting greedy or fixated on bliss/samadhi. Bliss is a byproduct; samadhi is a decade-plus away. Just keep practicing.
  • Doing intense practice while emotionally unwell. During heavy grief or instability, stick to grounded, validated practices (Surya Namaskar, Nadi Shuddhi, basic pranayama). Advanced meditation should come from stability.
  • Ignoring warning signs. Insomnia, nightmares, palpitations, or discomfort mean stop — and see a doctor if serious. Meditation often uncovers health problems because you become more attuned to your body.
  • Treating the practice as motivational fuel. It removes blocks; it isn't a battery to drain.
  • Over-interpreting your sensations. What you feel depends on subtlety, prana cleanliness, and where you're clogged. Don't get lost decoding it early; precision comes with repetition, not analysis.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the puer practice for 28 days (one lunar cycle). Twenty minutes once a day minimum; twice a day is exponentially better. Morning cleanses daily "debuffs"; the second session banks real growth.
  2. Locate your two centers. Clench for muladhara; Darth Vader breath for vishuddha. Don't force precision.
  3. Map the mantras. Chant Lum and Hum aloud, noticing direction and quality. Start loud, then quiet.
  4. Do the circuit. Sit upright, jnana mudra, eyes closed. Inhale up the front, exhale down the spine. Mentally repeat Lum up, Hum down.
  5. Practice zooming out. Hold the whole loop at once — front and back together. Apply the same "step back" when a thought feels like total reality.
  6. Try Brahmari in Shanmukhi Mudra as a standalone pratyahara drill: seal the senses, hum, and feel attention flood inward.
  7. Build your supporting buffs. Add ~15 min of Surya Namaskar, plus basic Nadi Shuddhi and Kapalbhati. These 20% multipliers compound enormously.
  8. Clean the instrument. Move your diet toward freshness (fresh-cooked grains; sprouted lentils, chickpeas, mung beans; neem and turmeric). Notice — without forcing — whether cravings for processed food and meat naturally drop.
  9. Guard your attention. Cut the hours of "society" you let in daily; reconnect to the internal source of motivation rather than borrowing it from content.
  10. Stay safe. If a session brings distress, stop and return to basic, validated practices — see a doctor if serious. There is no rush.

The gap is so large that you have a 0% chance of reaching even 50% of their performance — unless you do those supporting things.

"Advanced practice in meditation is doing an advanced technique and also setting yourself up to succeed."

A telling, unforced pattern: serious meditators who practice diligently for a couple of years tend to spontaneously cut back on meat — not because anyone preaches morality at them, but because meat dulls subtle awareness. You simply stop being able to feel things as finely. The diet, the breathwork, the postures aren't side quests; they're the 20% multipliers that, compounded over years, make the difference between standing still and arriving.

The Puer Practice: Linking Muladhara and Vishuddha

Here is a concrete advanced practice, built on the puer (eternal-adolescent) theme. The logic underneath it is powerful on its own:

"Any power or capacity that we have as human beings that is not used well will turn into toxicity."

Unrealized potential isn't a neutral empty lot. That energy will manifest as something — and if it isn't channeled into building, it gets transmuted into lust, addiction, pornography, substance use, an unhealthy lifestyle. This is why realizing your potential and conquering addiction move together like a seesaw. The practice strengthens the relationship between two energy centers:

  • Muladhara (root, at the perineum) — governs primal impulses: hunger, thirst, lust, craving, dopamine.
  • Vishuddha (throat) — governs your capacity to take a vision and make it real in the world.

Step 1 — Locate them. Clench as if there's a "butt-clenching moment" in a video game; the spot that contracts between the anus and the front of the pubic area (not the anus itself) is the muladhara. Make a Darth Vader breath; the part of the throat you contract is the vishuddha. Don't force precision — the exact spot reveals itself as your awareness sharpens.

Step 2 — Learn the two seed mantras. Chant "Lum" and "Hum" and notice, as best your awareness allows, the direction and quality of the vibration each one produces. Go a little loud at first to feel it, then quiet down to catch the subtlety.

Step 3 — Move the breath. Sit upright (spine, neck straight, head tilted very slightly back so it truly relaxes), hands in jnana mudra (thumb and forefinger joined, palms up on knees). As you inhale, draw awareness up the front from muladhara to the throat. As you exhale, send it back down the spine to the root. A "banana shape" up the front, a tube down the back, an energy that balls up at the base.

Step 4 — Zoom out. Normally your attention sits only on the most present feeling — the rising in front, then the throat, then the descent. The leap is to hold the whole circuit at once — feel the front and the back, the whole racetrack, simultaneously. This forces you backward, away from being absorbed in any single point. That "stepping back" is the very movement toward the experiencer.

Step 5 — Add the mantra internally. Mentally repeat Lum on the way up, Hum on the way down. This matters because of how mantra escalates in power: chanting aloud is one unit of energy (it's gross, broadcast into the material world); whispering is ten; mental repetition is a hundred; spontaneous, automatic repetition is a thousand. The louder and more external, the more it stays in matter; the more internal, the closer it works to consciousness. (Beginners still chant aloud because the senses are the anchor we have when we can't yet control the mind.)

A more advanced layer (not for beginners) adds visualizing the yantras of each chakra while breathing, feeling location, and repeating the mantra — all at once. The crowded petals and symbols of a yantra are deliberate: holding all of it forces you to zoom out, which is the whole point. You learn to visualize a yantra by gazing at it (trataka), then closing your eyes to see its afterimage, repeated over roughly a thousand days.

Zooming Out: Attachment, Suffering, and Taking a Step Back

The "zoom" idea is the most portable lesson here, and it explains suffering itself.

When you are fully zoomed in on a thought, you lose all perspective and the thought becomes total reality: "My life is over. I'll be alone forever. This is a fact, there's no hope." The more absorbed you are in one object, the more you lose perspective. This is the mechanism of attachment, and of suffering.

It maps onto a clean spectrum of belief in your own thoughts:

  • Believe a thought 100% → delusion / psychosis
  • A little less → panic
  • A little less → anxiety
  • A little less → normal (you can doubt your anxiety)
  • Less still → tranquility, resilience
  • Keep stepping back → meditation, and eventually enlightenment

Psychology already knows this. Cognitive behavioral therapy calls it thought diffusion; everyday language calls it "take a step back." But notice the geometry of that phrase:

"Take a step back. Where are you stepping from, and where are you stepping to? You are moving from here into here."

You're literally moving from total absorption in an object toward the witness. This is also why stacked failures crush people: each fresh failure gets fused with all the previous ones until you're not managing one failure but four, "without processing each one." Zooming in fuses; zooming out separates and frees. The same move that ends a panic spiral is the move that, taken far enough, ends in liberation.

The Timescale, Bliss, and the Quality-less Witness

Be honest about time. The modern world wants results in minutes; this works on the scale of years. A rough heuristic: a genuine "level up" once every seven years of diligent practice, ideally guided by a teacher who knows which practice will bear fruit for you. Don't expect anything in a week, a month, even three months. But you're not stuck while you wait — every session refines your subtle awareness and clears your prana; the leveling is happening invisibly, compounding like savings.

About bliss and samadhi: the felt quality of samadhi is described — literally, not metaphorically — as something like sustained orgasmic bliss, "coming and coming for 45 minutes." This is why an enlightened being is said to live in bliss, and why such states are rarely talked about: they're too far from ordinary experience to be conveyed. But bliss is not the goal and not a side effect — it's simply what that state is made of. The real aim is to recognize that you already are that — to un-forget it.

And the destination has no qualities at all — nirgun. Ask what your capacity for experience is like (not its contents): it's spacious, neutral, "kind of gray," present, limited by nothing. All the dualities — effect and side-effect, exploration, goal, pleasure and pain — belong to the objects witnessed, not to the witness. Brahman can't be "explored," because exploration implies mapped and unmapped territory; it is at once an infinite space and a dimensionless point. The Buddha left certain questions unanswered for exactly this reason: any verbal answer breeds misunderstanding. You don't reason your way in. You abide in the witness — and that's it.

Motivation Is Your Default State, Not Fuel

A closing reframe that stands on its own: do not use these practices as fuel for motivation, because that framing is wrong.

"Motivation arises naturally within you. You don't need fuel because you're the sun. The sun is full of fuel."

We treat motivation as a limited resource that burns down. But a healthy human is intrinsically motivated — think of the late-night surge where you suddenly want to create, build, travel, do. That energy is the default state, not a temporary one. It doesn't run out; it gets buried and extinguished — by highly processed, high-glycemic, preservative-heavy food, by doomscrolling, by spending six hours a day letting "society" tell you who to be. The puer practice doesn't supply fuel; it removes the blocks, and motivation flows on its own.

The deeper damage of constant external input is that it pulls your attention away from yourself. When inspiration comes from a piece of content, it's borrowed and it fades. Sustainable motivation only arises from inside — and the work is simply to stay connected to that inner source instead of disconnecting from it after every bad day at work or breakup. As the warning goes, paraphrasing Krishnamurti: being well-adjusted to a sick society is not health.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing advanced techniques while skipping preparation. The technique is the ingredient, not the meal. Without pratyahara, asana, pranayama, and clean prana, advanced practice yields almost nothing. Simple practices done well beat fancy ones done unprepared.
  • Expecting fast results. This is a years-to-decades path. Judging it on a one-week or one-month timescale guarantees disappointment and quitting.
  • Getting greedy or fixated on bliss/samadhi. Bliss is a byproduct, samadhi is a decade-plus away. Worrying now about "falling into samadhi" or "riding the ox backward" is misplaced — keep practicing; the fear sorts itself out.
  • Doing intense spiritual practice while emotionally unwell. During heavy grief or instability, stick to physiologically grounded, validated practices (Surya Namaskar, Nadi Shuddhi, basic pranayama). Advanced meditation should only be done from a place of stability. Deal with the negative thing directly, then return.
  • Ignoring warning signs. Insomnia, nightmares, palpitations, or discomfort mean stop and, if serious, see a doctor — meditation often uncovers health problems because you become more attuned to your body.
  • Treating the practice as motivational fuel. It removes blocks; it isn't a battery to drain for productivity.
  • Over-interpreting your sensations. What you feel depends on your subtlety, prana cleanliness, and where you're "clogged." Whether your "Hum" resists or your "Lum" flows, the practice is the same. Don't get lost decoding it early on.
  • Fixating on whether your experience is "right." Early on, you're both right and wrong; precision comes with repetition, not analysis.
