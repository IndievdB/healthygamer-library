Where Validation Should Come From

The trap: you do hard work, your boss says "good job," you feel good — until your boss says nothing, and now you feel bad. At that point your boss's words control how you feel about yourself.

The sequence to move toward:

  • You do good work, and you know it. You feel good because you're proud.
  • If the boss says "good job" — great, there's concordance; your value is recognized.
  • If the boss says nothing — you still feel good, and now you respond intelligently: "I'm not valued here." You polish your resume.

As long as you're doing a good job and you feel really good about what you do, the rest of the world will see your value.

This isn't "never expect appreciation." It's that the more your sense of value comes from inside, the freer you are to decide clearly whether to stay or go. Validation that depends on the outside world makes you a hostage to it.

How to Actually Land a Mentor

Ask what mentors want and you'll hear: potential, willingness to learn, earnestness. Real — but checkbox stuff. Necessary, not decisive.

The single most powerful thing that makes you a desirable mentee: you are already on your own professional trajectory, and you've done real work. A good mentor is usually only a couple steps ahead and must choose between several people wanting their time. Put yourself in their shoes — who do you say yes to?

  • "I'm passionate about psychology, will you mentor me?" → The mentor has nothing to grab onto.
  • "I'm a mental health content creator in my last year of residency with 70,000 followers — here's my work, will you help me get better?" → Now there's something to work with.

There's a huge difference between "I want to start a business, will you help me?" and "I started a business, this is how far I've come, I don't know what comes next, will you help me?"

Want a writing mentor? Write the bad 80,000-word novel first, then bring it. Accomplishment doesn't mean awards — it means you've done something rather than merely intending to. Bonus: when you actually start doing the work, allies come out of the woodwork. Initiative attracts help.

Growth Is Not Advancement

We constantly fuse two different things:

  • Growth is you leveling up — skills, resilience. It's internal.
  • Advancement is title, pay, organizational recognition. It's external.

They often move together, so our brains glue them — but the frustration of careers lives in the gap. You get certified, build a competency, then expect a promotion that never comes. Why? Because you're being paid to do a particular thing, not extra things. Your new skill may not be a need they have.

Sometimes growth and advancement are linked by design — a bank funding your MBA if you return. But that's the company aligning your growth with their needs.

The consequence: treat growth as your own project, separate from your job. Decide what skills you want, get them, then find the place where they're rewarded — which may not be where you are. This is why switching jobs every couple years raises average pay: not mechanical hopping, but always looking for where your particular level-up gets recognized. One client learned coaching, was told "your job is Excel," and found a VC firm that valued both spreadsheets and coaching. She's thrived ever since. The skill didn't matter at the old zone; it mattered enormously at the new one.

Empathy for Your Employer

The uncomfortable hot take: if you want to advance, the most valuable thing you can have is empathy for your employer.

Empathy here isn't compassion — it's genuinely seeing what they need. People stuck in their own perspective do "extra" — birthday parties, morale, extra shifts — then feel betrayed when no raise comes. Meanwhile the person who planned zero parties but raised sales 6% and cut costs 4% gets promoted.

Test it: if you pay someone to clean your house, what earns them 20% more? Cute towel animals, or actually cleaning the garage too? When the tables turn, the answer is obvious.

Empathy also dissolves burnout-inducing confusion:

  • "Unrealistic expectations for entry-level." From the employer's side, "entry-level" is often a tactic to lowball you. See it, and stop accepting it — come back with six comparable listings paying far more and a firm number.
  • "Hard work isn't rewarded." In a clinic, the MBA evaluating you isn't looking at patient outcomes — they're looking at throughput. Given patients 10% happier or revenue 10% higher, a business picks revenue. Harsh, but knowing it is power.

Remove all the bias from your mind and look at things objectively. If you want to advance, you need to provide what the employer wants.

And when you deliver and still don't advance, be empathic there too. Sometimes it's just slow — budgeting can mean a 12–18 month lag. Sometimes it reveals a place that keeps people underpaid by design. If so, find somewhere your work is rewarded — because if you're competent and actually try, you're rare. Most workers are average. You don't need heroic effort to stand out; just clearly better than average.

Reverse-Engineering the Career You Want

When you're scared and broke, you think one step ahead: "welding or butchery?" That short-term thinking keeps you perpetually one step from doom.

Instead, think big first, then reverse-engineer backward.

  1. Picture the actual dream.
  2. Ask what's the step right before that.
  3. What's before that?
  4. Keep walking backward — look at real listings, see they want five years, ask "how do I get one year?", "what job gives me that?", "what resume gets that job?"
  5. Message people already in these roles on LinkedIn; notice patterns ("everyone wants illustrators" → take a course, build a portfolio).

This is real, doable, frontal-lobe work. One client wanted Riot Games; reverse-engineering took ~five years, and they got there. A welding certification isn't the end of the chain — it's a funded step. Figure out the full number you need to live the life you want, then every shift feels good, because it's funding the life you're actually building.

Fulfillment and the Nemic Effect

Fulfillment literally means filling something empty. It comes from wanting something and getting it after sweat and tears. The same "soulless" job can drain or fulfill depending on whether it aligns with goals that are genuinely yours. Give your effort direction toward a real vision and it feels good even while it's hard.

But many steps are boring or miserable. The tool: the nemic effect — framing a shortcoming as a step toward the thing you want. The old feedback sandwich (compliment, criticism, compliment) doesn't work — it's a croissant with a log in the middle. What works is connecting the hard task to the outcome the person cares about:

If you want to be the doctor you want to be, you have to check the vitals every morning at 7:30. This must be done.

Apply it to yourself. You loathe LinkedIn? Frame it: this miserable step is a necessary move toward what I actually want. The drudgery doesn't become fun, but it becomes meaningful — enough to keep going.

The fatal error is tying those feelings to the external situation. If you only feel overwhelmed because there are a thousand jobs to apply to, you've handed your inner state to a number. While circumstances dictate your inner weather, you're trapped — even success becomes fragile.

Lean on the basics:

  • Treat the job search like a job. When you don't have one, finding one is the job. Put in your hours — resume, reach out to 30 people, apply to 40 roles. It's not fun. It's the price of admission.
  • One day at a time. What's the task today?
  • Process rejections. "So be it — I have 39 more in the pipeline." The cruel part: the further behind you fall, the more effort to push forward. Recognizing this is half the battle.
  • Build real work-life balance. Burnout is accumulated fatigue, so keep every day balanced enough that nothing piles up.

Every time you take the right action despite feeling weak, your resilience levels up. You take HP damage, yes — but the reps build mental fortitude.

Also reframe effort itself. Effort is not a coin in a vending machine. Each failure can be one debt being cleared before the positive arrives. Whether or not you take the spiritual version literally, the internal version is real: each setback builds the capacity you'll need when the opening comes.

How to Actually Land a Mentor

Ask people what mentors want and they'll say: potential, willingness to learn, open-mindedness, earnestness. All of that is real — but it's just the checkbox stuff. It's necessary, not decisive.

The single most powerful thing that makes you a desirable mentee is this: you are already on your own professional trajectory, and you've done real work. A good mentor is usually only a couple of steps ahead of you, and they have to choose between several people who want their time. So put yourself in their shoes — who do you say yes to?

Compare these two approaches:

  • "I'm really passionate about psychology, it excites me, will you mentor me?" → The mentor has no idea what to do with you. The "mentoring" would just be talking you into getting started.
  • "I'm a mental health content creator. I'm in my last year of residency, I have 70,000 followers, here's the work I've made — will you help me get better?" → Now there's something to grab onto.

There's a huge difference between "I want to start a business, will you help me?" and "I started a business, this is how far I've come, I don't know what comes next, will you help me?" The engagement you get will be night and day.

So if you want a writing mentor, write the 80,000-word novel first — even if it's bad — then bring it. If you want to break into game design, build the campaign setting, assemble the portfolio, then reach out. Accomplishment doesn't mean awards. It means you've done something rather than merely intending to.

And there's a bonus: when you actually start doing the work, people come out of the woodwork to help. The first few people who showed up to build Healthy Gamer appeared because the work had started — three of them are still around years later. Initiative attracts allies.

Growth Is Not Advancement

We constantly fuse two different things:

  • Growth is you leveling up — acquiring skills, getting better, building resilience. It's internal.
  • Advancement is the title, the pay, the way the organization recognizes you. It's external.

They often move together, which is why our brains glue them. But they're separate, and the frustration of careers lives in that gap. You go get certified, you build a new competency, you take the HR training — and then you march into work expecting a promotion, and it doesn't come. Why? Because you're being paid to do a particular thing, not extra things. Your new skill may simply not be a need they have.

Sometimes growth and advancement are linked by design — an investment bank that pays for your MBA on the condition you return, or a job that gives you professional development funds. But that's the company aligning your growth with their needs.

The practical consequence: treat growth as your own project, separate from your job. Decide what skills you want, and go get them. Then look for the place where that growth can actually be rewarded — which may not be where you currently are. This is why "switch jobs every couple of years" shows up as a way to earn more on average. The lesson isn't to hop jobs mechanically; it's to always be looking for the place where your particular level-up gets recognized.

One client learned coaching skills and asked to use them at her spreadsheet job. Her employer said no — your job is Excel. So she kept building the competency and found a VC firm that valued both the spreadsheets and the coaching. She's thrived and been steadily promoted there ever since. The skill didn't matter at the old zone; it mattered enormously at the new one.

Empathy for Your Employer

This is the hot take, and it's uncomfortable: if you want to advance, the most valuable thing you can have is empathy for your employer.

Empathy here does not mean compassion or kindness. It means the ability to genuinely put yourself in their shoes and see what they experience and need. Most people stuck in their own perspective do all kinds of "extra" — organizing birthday parties, building morale, working extra shifts — then feel betrayed when no raise comes. Meanwhile the person who planned zero parties but raised sales 6% and cut costs 4% gets promoted.

Run the test on yourself. If you pay someone to clean your house, what would make you pay them 20% more? Cute towel animals because they love art? Or actually cleaning the garage too — delivering more of what you need? When the tables turn, the answer is obvious.

Empathy also dissolves a lot of the burnout-inducing confusion:

  • "Unrealistic expectations for entry-level positions." From the employer's side, calling something "entry-level" is often a deliberate tactic to lowball your starting offer. Once you see that, you stop accepting it. You come back with six comparable listings paying far more and a firm number: "I'll do great work, and I want 80k, not 30k."
  • "Hard work isn't rewarded." In a psychiatry clinic, the MBA evaluating you isn't looking at patient outcomes or reviews — they're looking at throughput, the volume you generate. Given a choice between patients 10% happier or revenue 10% higher, a business picks revenue. Harsh, but knowing it is power.

Remove all the bias from your mind and look at things objectively. If you want to advance, you need to provide what the employer wants — and provide it as much as possible.

And when you do deliver and still don't advance? Be empathic there too. Sometimes it's just slow — budgeting and forecasting can mean a 12–18 month lag even for great employees. Sometimes it tells you the place keeps people permanently underpaid by design. If so, don't get consumed by how unfair it is. Find somewhere your work is rewarded — because if you're competent and you actually try, you're rare. Most workers are average. You don't need heroic effort to stand out; you just need to be clearly better than average.

Reverse-Engineering the Career You Want

When you're scared and broke, you think one step ahead: "Should I learn welding or butchery?" That short-term, one-problem-at-a-time thinking is exactly what keeps you perpetually one step from doom.

Instead, think big first, then reverse-engineer backward.

  1. Picture the actual dream — fantasy author, game designer, your own 5e campaign setting, whatever it genuinely is.
  2. Ask: what's the step right before that? (Publish a setting, or get hired at the publisher.)
  3. What's before that? (Build a setting; understand the publication process.)
  4. Keep walking it backward — look at real job listings, see they want five years of experience, ask "how do I get one year?", "what job gives me that?", "what resume gets that job?"
  5. Reach out to people who already hold these roles on LinkedIn and ask how they got there. Notice patterns — "everyone wants illustrators" becomes "I'll take an illustration course and build a portfolio."

This is real, doable, frontal-lobe work. One client wanted a job at Riot Games; reverse-engineering the path took about five years, and they got there. A welding certification isn't the end of the chain — it's a funded step. Figure out the full number you need to live the life you want, then ask how far a trade gets you and what doors it opens next. Every shift at the welding job then feels good, because it's no longer a dead end — it's the thing funding the life you're actually building.

The Nature of Fulfillment, and the Nemic Effect

Fulfillment literally means filling something empty. It comes from wanting something and getting it after the sweat, blood, and tears. That's why the same "soulless" job can be either draining or fulfilling — it depends on whether it aligns with goals that are genuinely yours. People feel empty because they chose jobs to pay bills, not because the work connected to anything they actually wanted. Give your effort direction toward a real vision and it starts to feel good even while it's hard.

But many steps along that path are boring or miserable. Here's the tool for that: the nemic effect — framing a shortcoming as a step toward the positive thing you want.

The old "feedback sandwich" (compliment, criticism, compliment) doesn't work — it's a warm croissant with a log in the middle. What works is connecting the hard, unglamorous task to the outcome the person cares about:

You clearly love this work, and you're great with patients. But being a good doctor isn't just doing the important things right — it's not fumbling the basic things. If you want to be the doctor you want to be, you have to check the vitals every morning at 7:30. This must be done.

Apply it to yourself. You loathe updating your resume. You loathe LinkedIn. Fine — frame it: this miserable step is a necessary move toward the thing I actually want. The drudgery doesn't become fun, but it becomes meaningful, which is enough to keep going.

Doing the Internal Work During a Job Search

The path is full of things you'll hate — networking, rejection, applications, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, the feeling of being overwhelmed. The fatal error is tying those feelings to the external situation. If you only feel overwhelmed because there are a thousand jobs to apply to, you've handed control of your inner state to a number. As long as your external circumstances dictate your internal weather, you're trapped — even success becomes fragile, because then a bad week can knock you back down.

So lean on the basics:

  • Treat the job search like a job. When you don't have one, finding one is the job. Put in your hours — polish the resume, reach out to 30 people, apply to 40 roles, do the networking. That's the day. It is not fun. It's the price of admission.
  • Take it one day at a time. You don't have to apply to a thousand jobs today. What's the task today?
  • Process the rejections. "This is bad, this isn't what I wanted, but so be it — I have 39 more in the pipeline." The cruel part is that the further behind you fall, the more effort it takes to push from 81 rejections to 120. Recognizing this is half the battle.
  • Build in real work-life balance. Two desks: one for the grind, one for the reward. Do the work, then close the laptop and play your game for as long as you like — but pick a life that doesn't let one activity swallow everything. Burnout is accumulated fatigue, so every day should be balanced enough that nothing piles up.

Every time you take the right action despite feeling weak and tired, your resilience is leveling up. You take HP damage, yes — but you're your own main opponent here, and the reps are building mental fortitude.

Common Pitfalls

  • Outsourcing fulfillment, growth, and validation to your employer. A job's job is to pay you. Everything else is yours to take responsibility for.
  • Letting your boss's words (or silence) determine your self-worth. Know your own work is good first; treat external recognition as confirmation, not a verdict.
  • Confusing growth with advancement. Leveling up your skills does not entitle you to a promotion if the new skill isn't a need your employer has.
  • Approaching mentors with interest instead of work. "I want to do X" gets you a pep talk; "I've done X, here's how far I've come" gets you real mentorship.
  • Having zero empathy for your employer, then feeling betrayed when invisible "extra" effort goes unrewarded while bottom-line impact gets the raise.
  • Thinking one step ahead instead of big-then-backward, which leaves you perpetually one crisis from disaster.
  • Tying your anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → to external numbers ("I'll feel okay once I'm not facing 1,000 jobs"), so your peace depends on circumstances you can't control.
  • Reading effort as a vending machine and collapsing into despair when it doesn't instantly pay out — instead of seeing setbacks as debts being cleared.
  • Mistaking the internet's incomplete, rage-baiting picture for the truth and getting quietly radicalized into hopelessness.
