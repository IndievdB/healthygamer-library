The Three Tools: Mantra, Yantra, Yagna

The Rishis built reusable tools — inventing a jackhammer is hard, using one is easy.

Mantra — the dimension of sound. A precise string of syllables, best understood as a magic spell: the right sounds in the right order produce a specific manifestation. Beneath all mantras are 12 bija (seed) mantras, the fundamental building blocks of the universe. The key idea is a transcendent essence with many manifestations. Love shows up as the letters L-O-V-E, a father and son, a cat on your chest, a poem — none is love, yet all express one essence. Likewise, the essence of existence itself — pure is-ness — expressed as sound is Om, the fundamental oscillation of the universe. A second essence is duality: listen to your breath, or the ocean, or the wind, and you hear the same something — inhale/exhale, light/dark. String the right bijas together and you activate specific universal energies, just as arranging atoms produces a reaction. Mantra is that, one layer up: arranging consciousness.

Yantra — the dimension of form. The same essence expressed as a precise geometric arrangement of symbols, with specific pigments and metals. A Kali yantra differs from a Tara yantra because each encodes a different essence. Vibration and form are different dimensions, like the music and the guitar.

Yagna — ritual. Complex fire ceremonies and deity evocation — the most advanced and least practical for a beginner, so largely set aside.

What the Rishis Actually Did

Western science is built entirely on the senses. We could see things with our eyes, so we built telescopes to make far things visible and microscopes to make tiny things visible. Then we developed X-rays to see through solid objects, then IV contrast to reveal structures inside the body that were invisible before. Each new instrument let us detect more of the material world — including things that are completely undetectable to the naked eye.

The Rishis — the word means seer — ran exactly the same process, but pointed in the opposite direction: into the non-material world. A thought, for instance, doesn't exist materially. You can't touch it, photograph it, or weigh it. So they built the equivalent of microscopes, X-rays, and contrast dye for the mind — a set of spiritual technologies that let them perceive layers of reality our ordinary senses can't reach.

This is the crux of why science struggles here. Science can't even detect the first rung — a thought as a non-material object — so it has no instruments to perceive anything further up. You're free to take a biological-reductionist view and dismiss all of it. But the tantric claim is simply that there are instruments you haven't looked through yet.

"This is where we are with Tantra. We don't understand because we don't have the technologies to perceive things the way that the Rishis perceived them."

The story of the public-health professor in Africa captures it perfectly. Villagers kept getting sick from wells contaminated by nearby latrines. When he told them to build cemented wells, they asked why. Because of microorganisms. Show us one. I can't — they're too small to see. Then how do you know? Because there's an instrument called a microscope. From the inside, the explanation sounds like magic. It isn't. It's just a layer of reality you don't yet have the tool to see.

The Three Tools: Mantra, Yantra, Yagna

The Rishis didn't just perceive reality — they built reusable tools, the way inventing a jackhammer is hard but using one is easy. Three tools matter:

Mantra — the dimension of sound

A mantra is a precise string of syllables, best understood as a magic spell: assemble the right sounds in the right order and you produce a specific manifestation. Underneath all mantras are 12 bija (seed) mantras — the fundamental building blocks of the universe.

The idea of a transcendent essence with many manifestations is the key. Consider love: it shows up as the letters L-O-V-E, as a father and son, as a cat on your chest at night, as a poem, as the birds and the bees. None of those is love by itself, yet all of them express one transcendent essence. In the same way, the essence of existence itself — pure is-ness — when expressed as sound, is Om, the fundamental oscillation of the universe.

There's a second essence: duality. Listen closely to your own breath and you'll hear a sound. Go to the ocean and you'll hear the same sound — though one is air and one is water. You'll hear it in the wind, everywhere. The Rishis investigated this shared "something" and found that one of reality's bedrock attributes is duality — up/down, light/dark, inhale/exhale, wave in/wave out. First there is oneness; then oneness splits in two.

There are 12 such fundamental qualities — existence, duality, attraction, repulsion, fusion, transformation, and so on. String the right bija mantras together and you activate specific energies of the universe, just as arranging atoms in a precise way produces a chemical or nuclear reaction. Mantra is that, one layer up: arranging consciousness.

Yantra — the dimension of form

A yantra is the same essence expressed not as sound but as form: a precise geometric arrangement of symbols, often with specific pigments, metals, and even depth. A Kali yantra differs from a Tara yantra in shape and color because each encodes a different essence. Vibration and arrangement are two different dimensions — a guitar (form) and the music played on it (energy) are not the same thing.

Yagna — ritual

The third tool, yagna (or tantra proper), is complex ritual — fire ceremonies, evoking deities, and so on. It's the most advanced and least practical for a beginner, so it's largely set aside here.

Precision Is Everything

These tools only work if executed with near-perfect precision. A building whose pillars are off by a hair loses its stability. A sailing course off by one degree lands you on a different continent. A mantra must be chanted with exactly the right tone, inflection, length, and conscious focus — otherwise nothing happens.

This is also why so many people "meditate" and get nowhere: they're mechanically reciting, not editing the source code.

  1. Chant aloud — you create a physical vibration in the material dimension.
  2. Drop to a whisper — less energy escapes into the physical; you move into the energetic dimension.
  3. Go silent and internal — the body is still, the mind is still, and you become nothing but consciousness. Activate the mantra from there, and it manifests with enormous power.

The flow state is just the first, minimal step along this path — it pulls you out of the purely physical and makes manifestation easier. The Deities Are Forces, Not Idols

Tantra is often called goddess worship, but the deities aren't objects of blind faith. They're better understood as laws of physics with a personality — or as immensely powerful beings whose lifespans run on the scale of the birth and death of universes. To them we're like ants. They aren't waiting on us; they don't particularly care. But you can earnestly ask for help, and if your devotion is genuine and your state of consciousness high enough, they answer.

Your ishta devata is your personal chosen deity, the manifestation you feel drawn to. Different deities govern different essences:

  • Kali — the goddess of time and space (kala = time); she sets the cosmos in motion.   • Lakshmi / Tara / Devi — concerned with material manifestation; pray to them for worldly results.
  • Bhairava — fierce, terrifying. A cautionary tale: drawn to Bhairava's badass image and assuming it was "just a metaphor," one can invoke him and get wrecked. To pray to Bhairava is to say, show me what is truly real, strip away the illusion. So he does — by tearing away your attachments to this world, one after another, until only the divine remains. Terrifying only if you still cling to this world.
