When a coach or therapist sits with you in your shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → without becoming hopeless themselves, something important happens. They're not just offering techniques. They're modeling a response. They're demonstrating that it's possible to be in the presence of this heavy, impenetrable feeling without being destroyed by it.

"If the therapist can sit with that hopelessness and not feel hopeless in turn, you're going to show the person how to do that."

Human beings learn by observation. The way people walk, talk, and carry themselves in different places is absorbed through proximity, not instruction. The same is true for self-dialogue. You can't just decide to talk to yourself with compassion — but if someone consistently treats you with compassion when you fail, your brain gradually learns a different way to talk to itself.

Common Pitfalls

Treating partial progress as no progress. Gaining emotional vocabulary and EQ after eight months of therapy is progress. Dismissing it because the central issue isn't resolved yet is the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → talking — it applies a binary standard (fixed vs. broken) that doesn't match how real change works.

Interpreting a therapist's uncertainty as confirmation you're unfixable. When a therapist seems unsure what to try next, it doesn't mean you're beyond help. It may mean they've fallen into the desperation cycle alongside you — which is a dynamic problem, not a verdict about you.

Trying more things faster. When nothing feels like it's working, the instinct is to escalate — new therapist, new modality, add coaching, try something else. But this can reinforce the nocebo loop. Each new attempt that doesn't produce immediate results adds to the mental ledger of "proof that I can't be helped."

Thinking you need hope before you can start. You don't. It's the coach's or therapist's job to hold the hope until you can build some of your own. You showing up is enough to start — belief can come later.

Confusing knowing the problem with having solved it. Understanding intellectually that shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → is the root obstacle doesn't bypass the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. Cognitive insight and emotional healing are different tracks. Knowing what's wrong doesn't mean the body and nervous system have caught up.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Don't quit therapy yet — but change what you're trying to do in the room. Instead of pushing for a breakthrough on the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → itself, bring the experience of having an unsolvable problem into the session. Talk about what it feels like to be stuck. That is the work, not a detour from it.

  2. Notice the nocebo loop in yourself. Before your next session, notice whether you walk in already expecting it not to help. You don't have to force optimism — just observe the expectation. Name it out loud to your therapist if you can: "I walked in today already feeling like this won't work."

  3. Practice the externalization exercise. When shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → or a "can't" thought shows up, try narrating it as a separate thing: "There's the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → again" rather than "I'm ashamed." This is awkward at first. Do it anyway. The language gap between I am and I have matters more than it sounds.

  4. Watch how your therapist or coach responds to your failures. Don't just absorb the content of what they say — notice the tone. Are they modeling that it's possible to be with difficulty without panicking? If yes, let that land. If no, that's worth naming in session, or it's a signal to find someone who can hold steadiness for you.

  5. Slow down the treatment timeline in your head. If the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → has been running for years, give the process at least a year before concluding it's hopeless. Set a more realistic internal benchmark — not "am I fixed?" but "am I slightly more able to sit with this than I was three months ago?"

  6. If you're working with a coach, let them carry the hope for now. You don't have to believe it will work. Tell them you don't believe it. That's an honest place to start, and it's their job to stay hopeful while you find your footing.
