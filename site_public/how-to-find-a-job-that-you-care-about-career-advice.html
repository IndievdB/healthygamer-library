Summary

TL;DR — Stop searching for a career and start searching for what you actually care about. The career comes second.

Most people who can't find a satisfying career aren't failing because they haven't tried enough options — they're failing because they're asking the wrong question. The question "what career should I have?" is already too narrow. It funnels your thinking toward whatever jobs happen to be visible in your immediate environment, and it skips the one step that actually matters: figuring out what you genuinely enjoy, care about, or want to have an impact on.

The Hidden Problem With "Finding a Career"

When most people think about picking a career, they're not actually surveying all the options available to them. They're reaching for the most immediately visible ones. Someone who grew up around auto mechanics thinks about working at an auto shop. Someone whose parents were doctors goes pre-med. Someone who has worked in restaurants and clinics circles back to restaurants and clinics.

This is just how human decision-making tends to work by default — a kind of mental "monkey see, monkey do." The options you consider are strongly shaped by the options already in front of you, not by any real accounting of everything that's possible.

The same thing happens when you think about a "career" as the unit of decision. The word itself nudges you toward conventional, legible job categories — call centers, food service, office work. It quietly rules out huge swaths of things you could actually get paid to do, before you've even thought about them.

"The problem is that when you think about career, then you start thinking about things like call center, billing department, food service — you're reaching for the simplest, most available thing instead of considering all of your options."

A better model comes from medicine: differential diagnosis. Before a doctor commits to a single explanation for a patient's symptoms, they're trained to deliberately generate all the plausible possibilities first. Good career thinking works the same way — open the search wide before you narrow it down.

Start With Passion, Not Practicality

The first real move is to step back from the idea of a career entirely and ask different questions:

  • What do I genuinely enjoy doing?
  • What was the last thing that had me intellectually engaged?
  • What problem in the world would I actually like to work on?
  • When was the last time I had real fun doing something?

This feels uncomfortable because your mind will immediately start offering objections. I care about equal rights, but I have no qualifications. I love tabletop roleplaying, but that's not a real job. That rapid shut-down is what's called premature closure — dismissing a possibility before you've actually investigated it.

Here's what makes premature closure worth resisting: there are professional dungeon masters in New York City charging groups of investment bankers $400 a week to run tabletop sessions. That's not a hypothetical — it's a real supply meeting real demand. It exists because going out for dinner and drinks in New York costs $200 a night minimum, and a private DM session is better entertainment for the money. Most people will never consider this as a livable income source because nothing in their social environment makes it visible to them.

The world is full of niches like this. You can only find them if you start from what you care about and work outward — not the other way around.

The Competing Interest Principle

Even if you figure out what you're passionate about, you still have to grind through difficult steps to get there — courses you don't want to take, applications, rejections, learning new skills. What makes that grind survivable?

The answer is a competing interest: something you care about enough that it outweighs the friction.

The clearest illustration of this comes from addiction psychiatry. Getting a smoker to quit doesn't work by giving them information — you'll get lung cancer, you'll get heart disease, you'll have complications — because information alone doesn't change behavior. What actually works is finding something the person cares about deeply enough to compete with the biological pull of nicotine.

One patient kept smoking through every warning until one conversation landed differently: "When you walk your daughter down the aisle one day, do you want to be wheeling an oxygen tank?" That image connected to something real — and over the next six to eight months, he quit.

The same principle applies to careers. "This job pays well" or "this is a logical choice" is not a competing interest. It's a weak reason that collapses the moment the work gets hard or boring. But when you have a genuine passion as your destination, even the parts of the path you'd normally avoid become tolerable — because they're in service of something you actually want.

"Once you have a good competing interest and you're like, hey, this is something that I actually care about — then you align your career behind your passions. It's not how do I find a career I can enjoy. You start by thinking about what you care about, and then you try to do a systematic process of making it work."

Turning Obstacles Into a Roadmap

When your mind tells you that you can't pursue what you care about because of some obstacle, that obstacle is actually your next step.

You can't do that because you haven't graduated college. → You need to graduate college.
You can't graduate without getting in. → You need to apply.
You can't apply without writing an essay. → You need to write an essay.

Each objection your brain raises is really just a hidden instruction about what to do next. This is more useful than it sounds, because it turns a vague impossibility into a sequence of concrete, completable tasks.

There's also an unexpected benefit to having a genuine passion before you apply to school: your application becomes distinctive. A college essay that says "I want to study creative writing to become a professional DM and I want formal training in constructing adventures and storytelling" is memorable. Admissions readers see thousands of generic career-minded applications. Honest enthusiasm for something unusual stands out.

What to Do If You've Lost Interest in Everything

Sometimes the problem isn't that you've never found something you love — it's that you used to have interests and they've gone quiet. That's a different situation, and it deserves a different response.

Interest doesn't disappear. It gets buried. The distinction matters because something buried can be uncovered; something gone is just gone.

Interests tend to get buried by things like exhaustion, depression, physical health problems, or extended periods of just surviving. When you have COVID, you don't want to play video games. When you're chronically sleep-deprived or vitamin D deficient, motivation flatlines. When you've been grinding through soul-crushing work for years, the parts of yourself that used to get excited about things go quiet from disuse.

That means part of the work is investigative — figuring out when and how the interest got buried, and what was happening at that time. Another part is physical: sleep, movement, nutrition, and seeing a doctor to rule out biological factors (thyroid issues, vitamin deficiencies, etc.) can meaningfully restore baseline motivation before any career thinking is even possible.

Common Pitfalls

Treating "I've tried many things and hated them" as evidence that nothing will work. Switching majors eight times or trying multiple jobs doesn't mean you're broken. It means you've been searching using the wrong method — chasing available options rather than starting from genuine interest.

Dismissing a passion the moment it seems impractical. The mind moves fast from "I love this" to "but I can't do that because..." — and most people accept that second thought without questioning it. That jump is premature closure, and it cuts off the investigation before it starts.

Expecting logical reasons to sustain effort. "This career has good job security" is not a competing interest. It won't carry you through years of hard work. Passions do. Logic alone doesn't.

Confusing lost interest with no interest. If you genuinely can't identify anything you care about right now, that's worth taking seriously as a possible signal about your current physical or mental state — not as a permanent truth about who you are.

Assuming a career has to look like a career. The category itself is the trap. Livable income from something you love is the actual goal. Those can take forms that don't have a job title.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run a differential diagnosis on your interests. Set aside the question of what you can get paid for entirely. Write down everything you find genuinely interesting, enjoyable, or worth caring about — including things that seem impractical or weird. Don't filter yet. The goal is breadth.

  2. Notice premature closure and write it down. Every time your mind says "I can't do X because Y," write down both X and Y separately. X is a clue about what you actually want. Y is your next obstacle to work through.

  3. Investigate the niches. Pick one or two things from your list and spend a few hours genuinely researching whether anyone, anywhere, makes money doing something related to it. You're looking for existence proofs — evidence that the thing is possible, not a complete plan.

  4. Work backward from obstacles. Take one "I can't do this because..." and turn it into a sequence: what would you need to do to remove that obstacle? Break it into the smallest possible first step.

  5. If nothing sounds interesting right now, address the physical layer first. Get consistent sleep. Move your body. Eat reasonably well. Get a checkup — ask about vitamin D, thyroid, and anything else your doctor thinks relevant. Give your nervous system a chance to recover before concluding you have no passions.

  6. Ask the buried-interest question. Think back to a time when you were genuinely excited about something — even if it was years ago, even if it seems childish now. What was it? What was happening in your life at that time? What changed?
