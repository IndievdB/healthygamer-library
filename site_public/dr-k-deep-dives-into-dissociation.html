What Dissociation Actually Is (And Why It's Not Rare Anymore)

Dissociation has historically been treated as a fringe psychiatric topic — conditions like Dissociative Identity Disorder affect roughly 1 in 1,000 to 1 in 10,000 people, so why spend much time on it? That framing has become badly outdated.

A study on problematic internet use among online roleplaying game players found that 47% of participants showed high prevalence of attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → disorganization, and 13.5% scored above clinical cut-offs for dissociative experiences. That's not 1 in 10,000 — that's more than 1 in 10. And those are just the people whose dissociation crossed a measurable threshold.

The formal definition is worth sitting with: dissociation is a disruption or discontinuity in the normal subjective integration of one or more aspects of psychological functioning — including memory, identity, consciousness, perception, and motor control. In plain terms, the self is not one solid thing. It's a collection of parts that normally work together: your internal body sense, your emotions, your memories, your sense of being a person moving through the world. Dissociation is what happens when those parts stop talking to each other.

Two things happen simultaneously when you dissociate — and both matter:

  1. Numbing (over-modulation): Emotional signal gets suppressed. You feel flat, detached, unreal.
  2. Intrusion (under-modulation): The stuff that got walled off doesn't disappear. It breaks through later as flashbacks, triggered emotional floods, intrusive daydreams, or disproportionate reactions to small stressors.

This is why people with dissociation say they feel nothing, yet their heart rate is elevated, their pupils are dilated, and their galvanic skin response is spiking. The body is stressed. The conscious experience has just been disconnected from that stress.

The Brain Science: Why This Happens

The self isn't stored in one brain region — it's a network. Several structures work together to create your sense of being you:

In dissociation, insula activity is suppressed, which is why people lose the felt sense of themselves.

The Freeze Response: Dissociation as Survival

This whole mechanism isn't a malfunction — it's ancient biology. Animals under extreme threat, when they can neither fight nor flee, play dead. In that state, they develop analgesia (inability to feel pain), behavioral shutdown, and what appears to be an altered state of consciousness. Predators often avoid animals that appear dead because dead animals can carry disease.

The same neurological mechanism exists in humans. When pain or fear exceeds a threshold, the brain literally unplugs from the internal experience of what's happening. Bessel van der Kolk's research documented that in dissociative states, communication across the corpus callosumThe corpus callosum is the band of neural tissue sitting in the middle of the brain that connects the left and right hemispheres, acting as the communication highway between them.Full definition → — the bridge between brain hemispheres — becomes disrupted. The brain stops talking to itself in its normal integrated way.

The problem is that this ancient survival circuit doesn't turn off cleanly. The compartmentalized experience sits in storage, and later, minor triggers crack the container open.

The Cost: Losing Salience, Identity, and Motivation

Here's where the clinical picture connects directly to everyday life.

Salience is the brain's capacity to make things feel important. It's not a cognitive judgment — it's a felt sense driven by the same limbic circuits that dissociation suppresses. You can know intellectually that your resume matters, that your health matters, that your relationships matter — and feel absolutely nothing about any of it. That emptiness isn't laziness. It's the loss of the neural signal that makes things feel worth pursuing.

Identity is built from the narrative integration of emotional experiences. You know who you are because you remember not just what happened, but how it felt — the relationship you had, the failure that changed you, the moment you stood up for yourself. When emotion gets walled off, the emotional content of memories goes with it, and the narrative falls apart. The result: I don't know who I am. I don't know what I want. I don't know what matters.

The paralysis of initiation — a term from Judith Herman's early PTSD research — describes what follows. People with trauma histories often become goalies rather than forwards. Their entire neural architecture has been trained to defend against incoming threats, not to initiate and pursue. They're not incapable — they're wired for a different game, one where survival was the only objective.

The future dimension atrophies too. When your whole operating mode is "get through today," the brain circuits involved in future planning and long-term motivation genuinely weaken from disuse.

Technology as a Dissociation Amplifier

This is where modern life intersects with everything above in a way that's rarely discussed clearly.

The virtual world works so well for dissociators because it's safe. In the game, you don't need to suppress your emotions, because nothing in there can really hurt you the way the real world can. Salience circuits activate. Things feel important. Identity coheres around a role — the guild leader, the healer, the expert. The paralysis of initiation dissolves; you know exactly what to do next.

Meanwhile, in the real world, the same circuits remain offline.

"When I ask you, when you are playing the game, does it feel important? Your logical mind will say it is not important — but does it feel important? You say yes. You are connected to your salience when you're in the virtual world because it feels safe."

Social media adds another layer. Filters create a version of you that your brain prefers. When you look in the mirror afterward and see the unfiltered face, the shame circuits activate. Shame triggers dissociation. Dissociation kills salience and motivation. So you don't go to the gym or change anything — you log back in, and the cycle repeats.

The psychic pit model from the research describes this clearly: childhood experiences of loss, neglect, or abuse → unresolved attachment disorganization → absorption into the internet to protect the self from traumatic memories → problematic internet use. As the addictive potency of technology increases, the threshold of prior damage required to fall into this pattern drops. You don't need a severe trauma history anymore. The product will do enough of the work.

Ketamine, Depression, and the Dissociation Spectrum

One of the more surprising implications of this framework: if dissociation represents a hypoactive default mode network (the self is suppressed, things don't feel real or important), major depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → represents the opposite — a hyperactive default mode network where self-referential ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → runs unchecked (it's my fault, I'm worthless, my family would be better without me).

This explains why ketamine — a dissociative anesthetic — works so rapidly for treatment-resistant depression. It temporarily depresses the overactive self-referential circuitry and gives relief. The challenge is that it doesn't change whatever drove the depression; when the drug wears off, the underlying patterns remain. It's also a drug of abuse for exactly the same reason it works — the dissociative state feels like relief to people who are in pain.

Common Pitfalls

"I feel nothing, so nothing is wrong with me." The physiological data says otherwise. Emotional numbness coexists with a body running stress responses. The numbness is not resolution — it's suppression, and the suppressed material will intrude.

"I'm just lazy/unmotivated." Amotivation and inability to initiate are not character flaws in this context. They're predictable outputs of suppressed salience and limbic circuitry. Understanding the mechanism doesn't excuse inaction, but it does change the approach.

"Gaming/the internet caused my dissociation." It didn't — it provides an environment where dissociation is less necessary because the perceived threat level is lower. The underlying fragmentation preceded the gaming. The gaming is a symptom, not a cause.

"MeditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → will fix this." Open-awareness meditation techniques (observe your thoughts, let them flow) can be actively harmful for people with unprocessed trauma. The suppressed material isn't the kind you can observe neutrally — it can overwhelm. Trauma-focused interventions (EMDR, DBT, grounding-based practices) are specifically designed to avoid retraumatization. If you have a diagnosable dissociative disorder, this is not something to self-treat with generic mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition → apps.

"If I accept where I am, I'm giving up on changing." Action Items & Exercises

  1. Seek professional evaluation if symptoms are significant. If you experience ongoing dissociative episodes, depersonalization, or recognize yourself in descriptions of BPD, DID, or complex PTSD, get a clinical evaluation before trying to self-navigate. EMDR specifically has a strong evidence base for trauma-related dissociation.

  2. Practice grounding-based meditation, not open-awareness meditation. If you have a trauma history, focus on sensory anchoring — feeling your feet on the floor, noticing the temperature of your breath, attending to the physical sensation of your body right now. This builds insula activity and interoceptive awareness without opening the door to overwhelming intrusive material.

  3. Do embodied movement — yoga, tai chi, or similar. Bessel van der Kolk's work specifically highlights yoga as therapeutic for trauma because it rebuilds the felt sense of being in your body. Even basic yoga builds interoceptive awareness, which is exactly what dissociation erodes.

  4. Build a narrative of your life. Write it out. The goal isn't to produce something polished — it's to practice connecting the emotional highlights and lowlights of your experience into a coherent story of who you are. Identity is narrative integration of emotional memory. This is what therapy helps with, and you can begin it informally.

  5. Re-engage with at least one real-world relationship. The research on attachment and dissociation is consistent: human emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → developed to be a shared process. You are not designed to contain all your own emotions alone. Pick one person and practice being slightly more honest with them about how you're doing.

  6. Notice the salience gap. When you feel motivated in a game or online and dead in real life, don't judge it — get curious. Ask yourself: what makes this space feel safe enough that the salience circuits turn on? What threat does the real-world context represent? The answer tells you what you're actually afraid of.

  7. Stop treating the virtual identity as separate. The energy, care, and investment you put into your online self is real — it's yours. The work is to gradually bring that same engagement into real-world contexts, not to eliminate the virtual life. Integration, not amputation.

  8. Collect experience rather than optimizing for the right answer. If you're stuck oscillating between over-intellectualizing and over-emoting, the path forward is neither — it's more data. The balance comes through practice and repeated exposure, not through figuring it out first. Put yourself in more situations and let your brain adapt.
