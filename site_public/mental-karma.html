How the Mind Grooves Itself

The mind works like a block of ice with water poured over it. The first pour cuts a shallow channel. The second pour deepens it. Eventually water flows that way automatically, without effort.

Every thought you repeat makes it easier to think that thought again. Every pattern you run through becomes more automatic. This isn't a flaw — it's just how neurons wire. The practical implication cuts both ways: grooves that take you somewhere good get easier with practice, and grooves that make you miserable also get easier with practice. You are always, whether you intend to or not, training your mind toward something.

Attachment: The Suffering You Generate Yourself

When something goes wrong, your mind doesn't just register what happened. It immediately projects forward: what this means, what others will think, how badly this will go. Klishthas: The Colorings That Distort Reality

A klishta is the layer of meaning your mind automatically paints onto everything it perceives. Objects and events exist as they are. But the moment you perceive something, your mind stamps it: good, bad, threatening, desirable, insulting, confirming.

You don't get a text back. That's the reality. But the mind immediately colors it: they're annoyed with me, I said something wrong, they've moved on. None of those interpretations are in the unreturned text itself. They're in the coloring your mind applied.

The more klishthas you accumulate, the further your inner experience drifts from what's actually happening. This is especially visible in traumaTrauma is what happens when an experience generates more emotion than you can process in a single moment — the overwhelm itself is the defining feature, not just the event.Full definition → — where even neutral stimuli get coated in old, painful meanings and the person can no longer see the situation as it is. But it happens to everyone, constantly, in subtler ways.

Uncoloring a klishta starts with observation: noticing that your reaction to something is generated by you, not by the thing itself. When you can see the coloring as coloring, you regain the ability to consider other explanations, other possibilities. The text might mean many things. Your mind's first answer isn't the only one.
