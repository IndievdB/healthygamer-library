Through one person's story — a 27-year-old content creator and investor who grew up as an immigrant kid, moved constantly through school, and never had a safety net — a clear picture emerges of how a mind learns to protect itself and then can't stop.

The Making Sure Monster

At the center of everything is what gets called "the making sure monster" — an inner voice that never lets you rest.

It sounds like productivity. It tells you: - You could be working on that project right now - You could have gotten this done faster - You're wasting time

But it isn't drive. It's fear wearing the costume of discipline. The goal isn't achievement — it's control over outcomes that feel dangerous to leave to chance.

The telltale sign: it doesn't feel good to succeed. You just get a brief reprieve before the voice starts scanning for the next threat. Sitting still for fifteen minutes doesn't feel peaceful — it feels like a lava sensation rising in the chest, a near-panic, because your brain has learned that stillness equals exposure.

"There's never a point where your brain will allow you to stop and be yourself. It's always: you can be doing something."

How the Pattern Forms

This kind of thinking doesn't appear from nowhere. It gets built — usually early, usually in response to real circumstances that genuinely were out of your control.

In sixth grade, a kid who had moved between countries because of his father's job returned to school and was told he might have to repeat the grade. His parents came in. Nobody blamed him out loud, but his brain filled in the gap: only an idiot has to repeat sixth grade. He spent his entire March break doing six months of curriculum work — two book reports, multiple projects — alone, so he could move forward with his class.

He succeeded. And his mind drew a lesson:

  1. The world will put you in dangerous situations through no fault of your own.
  2. No one is going to save you.
  3. If you sacrifice yourself completely, you can alter the course of fate.
  4. The price is just yourself.

That lesson worked. It got him through high school (while moving between countries), through the decision to leave university, into a career. The problem is that a rule that was useful at eleven years old keeps running at twenty-seven — and the cost keeps compounding.

"Better Dead Than Weak"

This is the unspoken house motto. It doesn't feel dramatic from the inside — it just feels obvious. Suffering is fine. Needing help is not. You keep things inside because sharing them means:

  • Appearing weak
  • Burdening other people
  • Losing control of how others perceive you

So when someone asks "are you okay?" the automatic answer is "I'm great." Even to a partner. Even to parents. Even to yourself. Not because you're dishonest — but because you have spent years learning that expressing difficulty costs more than suppressing it.

"It's better to be dead than weak. You've literally hit my life. That is a mantra I could live by."

"Bro — you have been living your life by it."

The cruel irony: bottling everything up feels like strength and functions like slow self-destruction. The person in this conversation could identify, clearly and logically, that he couldn't keep doing this for another decade without something breaking. He just didn't know a way out.

The Cost Nobody Accounts For

The making sure monster doesn't only eat your rest. It eats your relationships.

When you can't be vulnerable with a romantic partner, the relationship stays shallow — not because you don't care, but because intimacy requires exactly the kind of exposure the monster forbids. When someone you love is going through something serious — illness, grief, struggle — and you feel the need to put on an "extra smile" to avoid burdening them, you're denying them the chance to be close to you. You end up both performing for each other.

There's something important here that goes against instinct: for someone who is terminally ill, or in deep pain, one of the most loving things you can do is let them take care of you sometimes. Not because you need rescuing, but because love is mutual. Allowing someone to comfort you is a gift to them.

The same applies to aging parents. "Making sure" they never feel guilty about the hard parts of your childhood doesn't protect them — it just keeps a real conversation from ever happening. And it keeps you from the connection you actually want.

The Cycle and How It Passes On

The making sure monster is inheritable.

A father who worked across two countries, redid medical school from scratch, drove insane distances to provide — he wasn't teaching weakness. He was modeling survival. And his son absorbed the lesson perfectly: you've only got yourself.

Now that son, thinking about having children of his own, already knows what he would tell them: no one is coming for you, dig your heels in or die.

The same lesson. The same boxing ring. Passed forward.

"I think he's trying, in his own way, to tell you: don't be like me."

The father's question — do you even know if you'll be alive at forty? — isn't random. It's a man who has lived the cost and is trying to open a door. The tragedy is that both of them have learned, from the same family culture, not to walk through it.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking control for safety. The making sure monster feels like it's keeping you safe. It's actually keeping you isolated, exhausted, and unable to receive help — which leaves you more fragile, not less.

Thinking vulnerability means weakness. The assumption underneath all of this is that if you show people the difficulty, they'll think less of you. In practice, the people closest to you already sense something is wrong. What the silence actually does is prevent closeness.

Trying to fix anxiety by managing it harder. If you notice yourself thinking "how do I make sure I'm mentally stable six months from now," that's the monster rebranding itself. You're still making sure — just with your emotional health as the new variable to optimize. The goal isn't to control your mental bank account more efficiently; it's to stop needing everything to be controlled.

Pathologizing the pattern without understanding it. The making sure monster isn't a character flaw. It was genuinely adaptive once. It protected a kid who had no safety net. Treating it like a moral failure just adds shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → to the load; understanding where it came from is what actually gives you leverage over it.

Assuming the next generation will just turn out differently. If you don't do something with this, you will hand it forward — not out of cruelty, but because it's the water you swim in. The lesson you were taught is the lesson you'll teach, unless something interrupts the pattern.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the voice. When you feel the anxiety spike during rest, or the urge to work instead of breathe, try labeling it: that's the making sure monster. You don't have to fight it. Just notice it. Naming it creates a small gap between you and it — and that gap is where change happens.

  2. Have the dinner with your dad. Not a confrontation. Not a blame session. Start by asking him about his life at your age: what was running through his head, what sacrifices he made, what he worried about. Ask him to explain what he meant when he said he didn't know if you'd be alive at forty. Let the conversation go where it goes. You don't have to perform strength or weakness — just curiosity.

  3. Practice tolerating one small uncertainty per day. Pick something low-stakes where you'd normally "make sure" — a work email you'd usually triple-check, a plan you'd normally over-prepare for — and let it be slightly less certain than you'd like. The goal isn't recklessness; it's building the muscle that says I can handle not knowing.

  4. If rest feels unbearable, investigate the feeling instead of fighting it. Next time you sit down to rest and your brain says you're wasting time, don't immediately pick up your phone or start a task. Sit with it for two more minutes and ask: what am I afraid will happen if I don't do something right now? Get specific. The answer is usually more informative than the anxiety itself.

  5. Let one person see that you're not okay. It doesn't have to be a big disclosure. It can be as small as telling someone your day was rough and meaning it. Notice what your brain predicts will happen — and then notice what actually happens.

  6. Consider working with a therapist or coach on this material. The insight from one conversation can only go so far. The pattern took decades to build; it benefits from ongoing, consistent work with someone trained to help you through it.
