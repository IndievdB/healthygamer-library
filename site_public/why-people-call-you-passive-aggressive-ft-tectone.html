Summary

TL;DR — When you never learned the unspoken rules of healthy relationships, you can do everything "right" by your own rulebook and still drive people away. The path forward isn't blame — it's building awareness of what was never taught to you.

If you've been told you're passive aggressive but can't figure out what you're actually doing wrong, the answer might not be in your behavior at all. It might be in something that was never installed in the first place: the intuitive, internalized sense of how relationships actually work — including the simple habit of asking other people what they think and feel.

This is a conversation about what happens when someone grows up in an environment where healthy relationship modeling was absent, and how that gap quietly shapes every friendship, argument, and connection in adult life.

The Difference Between Rules and Understanding

There are two ways to know how to play a game. The first is to have grown up around it — to absorb it so naturally that the principles feel instinctive. The second is to reverse-engineer it, building up a set of if-then rules from observation and logic.

Most people who grew up in stable, functional homes learned relationships the first way. They didn't consciously learn "ask how someone is feeling" or "check in before assuming" — they just internalized it by watching it happen thousands of times.

If that wasn't your childhood, you do what any smart person does: you figure it out. You build rules. If I end a text with a period, people think I'm angry, so I add an emoji. If someone crosses a line, I note it and adjust. You get surprisingly good at navigating social situations this way — for a while.

The problem is the same as trying to approximate lane pressure in a strategy game through pure if-then logic. You can get close, but the underlying principle — the actual equation — never gets learned. And when you hit a situation your rulebook doesn't cover, you lose, and you often don't know why.

"You're engaging with people where you're following all the rules as you've learned them... and then what happens is you're not violating any of the rules, but something is rubbing someone the wrong way to where they're like, I can't point to anything wrong that he's doing, but I just don't like him."

That's where the label passive aggressive often comes from. It's not a precise diagnosis. It's what people say when something feels off but they can't point to a specific violation. You haven't broken any rule they can name — because you're playing by a different rulebook entirely.

Your Intolerance of Their Pain Is Still About You

One of the subtler traps in this kind of upbringing is the belief that you're constantly prioritizing other people's feelings — stepping in when a friend is getting hurt, pushing back when someone you love is making a bad decision, inserting yourself into situations because you care.

But there's a question worth sitting with: when you feel compelled to act on someone else's pain, whose feelings are you actually serving in that moment?

If the honest answer is that you can't stand watching them hurt — that their pain is intolerable to you — then the emotional driver is your discomfort, not their wellbeing. That's not a moral failure. It's a very human thing, and it usually comes from somewhere real.

"It's actually your intolerance of their pain that drives you to act, which means you're not placing their feelings above your own — even though it's done with love."

This matters because it changes what you do next. If a friend goes back to someone who cheated on them, there's an entire interior world you're not seeing: reasons, fears, attachments, history. When you react from your own discomfort rather than genuine curiosity about them, you skip over all of that and land on a conclusion that probably feels abrasive to them — even if it's technically correct.

Being right and being helpful are not the same thing.

The Single Habit That Changes Everything

If you don't understand something in a relationship, try asking the other person.

This sounds almost embarrassingly simple. But for someone whose early environment didn't model it, this is a genuinely novel idea — not laziness, not stupidity. Just an absence.

The impulse that fills the gap instead is usually internal: google it, reason it out, make your best inference. You treat the other person like an NPC — their behavior is data to be parsed rather than a perspective to be invited in.

"When you don't know something in a relationship, maybe try asking the other person."

The practical shift this requires is moving from single-player mode to co-op mode in conversation. That means:

  • Accepting that you're not fully responsible for how the conversation goes
  • Letting the other person carry some of the weight
  • Asking open-ended questions instead of presenting conclusions
  • Listening for their answer instead of building your rebuttal

When you dominate a conversation — even brilliantly, even entertainingly — you're still the only person really present. The other person becomes an audience. That's fun, but it's not connection, and eventually the distance becomes impossible to ignore.

All-In or All-Out: The Problem With No Acquaintances

A natural side effect of this relational style is that relationships tend to snap between two states: complete intimacy or total distance. You're best friends or strangers. There's no middle ground, no comfortable acquaintance zone, no gradual building of trust.

This pattern makes sense as a survival strategy. In an environment where people were clearly either safe or dangerous, reliably good or reliably harmful, fast binary judgment was adaptive. It kept you from getting hurt by people who'd already proven themselves untrustworthy.

But most adult relationships live in the gray zone — and if you don't know how to navigate there, you'll keep hitting the same wall. You go all-in fast, the other person can't match that intensity or eventually does something that doesn't fit your rulebook, and then they're gone. The cycle repeats.

The antidote isn't to care less. It's to make room for the other person's reality — including the possibility that their logic, their choices, and their perceptions are just as valid as yours, even when they don't match.

"Their perceptions are just as valid as yours. The reality is both bars combined, not exclusively either one."

Why Asking Questions Feels Foreign — And Why That's Not Your Fault

Growing up in chaos teaches specific lessons about other people: that they can't be relied on, that trusting them leads to getting hurt, that the only safe strategy is to handle things yourself. These aren't irrational conclusions. They're accurate observations about the specific environment you were raised in.

The problem is that the brain doesn't automatically update the rulebook when the environment changes. You carry those lessons into adulthood, into friendships, into relationships — and they fire automatically, before you've even had a chance to question them.

This is why "just ask them how they feel" can be genuinely revolutionary rather than obvious. It's not that you lack the intelligence to do it. It's that some part of your threat-detection system has been quietly classifying that kind of openness as dangerous for decades.

Being compassionate with yourself about this isn't the same as excusing it. It's what makes change actually possible — because if you pile on shame and self-blame every time you notice a pattern, you'll be too overwhelmed to do anything different.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing responsibility with blame. Recognizing that your behavior contributes to relational problems is not the same as declaring yourself a bad person. One is useful; the other is just painful and unhelpful.

Assuming you know what someone is feeling or thinking without asking. Even if your inference is usually accurate, the act of inferring rather than asking removes the other person from the equation entirely. You're managing a model of them, not actually connecting with them.

Laughing or smiling when angry or frustrated. This is a masking behavior — a way of managing your own emotional experience at the expense of the other person feeling heard or taken seriously. If it's something you do, name it to people rather than just hoping they won't notice.

Issuing permission instead of asking questions. "It's okay to be angry at me" is generous, but it keeps all the power on one side. Asking "can you help me understand why you see it that way?" actually invites them in.

Conflating your opinion being logical with it being correct. Two people can both believe they're being logical and one of them can still simply be wrong. Social rank — how much a person actually understands relationships — functions like game rank. Perceived logical superiority doesn't override it.

Taking full responsibility for how conversations go. If you're running every exchange like a single-player game, making sure everything lands perfectly, you're both exhausting yourself and starving the other person of any real role in the relationship.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice asking instead of inferring. The next time you're confused about why someone did something or how they're feeling, resist the impulse to reason it out internally. Ask them directly. Start small — low-stakes situations with people you trust.

  2. Notice when you're in single-player mode during a conversation. Are you tracking what you want to say next while they're still talking? Are you constructing the "correct" response? That's the signal. When you catch it, bring your attention back to actually listening.

  3. Try open-ended questions in place of conclusions. Instead of "you should leave, she cheated on you twice," try "what do you see in her that makes you want to try again?" You don't have to agree with the answer — just receive it.

  4. When you feel you've screwed up, don't just apologize for what you feel bad about — ask how they experienced it first. "Can you help me understand how that landed for you?" gives them space to be actually present in the repair, not just an audience for your remorse.

  5. Practice the co-op heartbeat meditation. Sit up straight, close your eyes, and put on headphones to dampen external sound. First, focus entirely on the sound of your breath. Then, listen carefully for your heartbeat. When you find it, try to hear both components of it — the two-part sound, the lub-dub. This is a practice in noticing duality rather than just one signal. Do it regularly. The goal isn't to achieve anything quickly — it's to train your awareness to perceive two things at once instead of collapsing everything into one.

  6. Go back and notice your language when you describe conflicts. Watch for phrases like "in reality, it was actually..." or "they perceived it as X, but what I meant was Y." Your intentions are real — and so is their experience. Both are part of the same reality.

  7. Be patient with yourself through the transition. Understanding the problem intellectually does not immediately rewire decades of conditioning. Expect discomfort when you try to trust someone else to carry part of a conversation, or when you resist the urge to fix a friend's situation. That discomfort is the actual work.
