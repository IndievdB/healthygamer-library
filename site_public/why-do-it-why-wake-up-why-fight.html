Summary

TL;DR — You don't need a reason to keep going. You already have one: the pull itself is the reason.

There's a question that can feel almost paralyzing when you sit with it long enough: Why bother? Why get up? Why keep fighting? The answer isn't found in a philosophy book or a perfect goal. It's already operating inside you right now — you just have to learn to trust it instead of overriding it with your plans.

The Gravitational Pull That Already Exists in You

Everything that exists seems to move toward something. Water flows toward the ocean. Fire reaches upward. These aren't perfect scientific analogies, but they point at something real: all things strive toward their source.

Human beings have their own version of this. When you're discontent, something in you reaches toward peace. When your life feels empty, something in you reaches toward meaning. When you're unhealthy, something in you reaches toward vitality. You don't manufacture this pull — it's already there. The ache you feel when things aren't right is the pull.

"You have this gravitational pull to become happy and peaceful and content. That is actually really, really important."

This is why you do it. This is why you get up. Not because of a goal you've set, not because of a philosophy you've chosen, not because someone convinced you it was worth it — but because something in you keeps pointing in a direction. That's the reason. The pull is the reason.

Why the "One Thing" Never Works

Here's where most people go wrong: they locate that pull, and then they attach it to a specific destination. Once I get that job, I'll be happy. Once I lose that weight, I'll feel good about myself. Once I get to that level, things will finally click.

The problem is well-documented in human experience. People at the top of their field — managing directors, executives, people who fought their way to exactly where they said they wanted to be — don't arrive at peace. They arrive at the next milestone. Two divorces behind them, no closer to contentment than when they started.

This is the hedonistic treadmill: the pattern where achieving one thing just reveals the next thing you think you need. The finish line keeps moving because the finish line was never the real point.

"There is no particular thing that, if you get that thing, it's going to make you happy."

The pull inside you is real. The specific destination you've assigned it to is a guess — and often a bad one.

Direction Over Destination

If the specific goal isn't the answer, what is? The answer is simpler and harder at the same time: move in the direction you're being pulled, without clinging to a particular endpoint.

If you're hungry, eat something — don't wait until you can have the exact meal you're craving. If you want to get healthier, take one small step today — drink water instead of soda, eat one piece of fruit, do one thing — rather than waiting until you have the perfect plan or the perfect body to start with.

The direction is trustworthy. The precise destination is a fantasy you invented with the least amount of information you'll ever have about the journey.

This connects to something called the planning fallacy: we make our plans at the very beginning of a process, which is exactly when we know the least. So the plan you're most attached to — the one you've decided is the only acceptable outcome — is statistically your worst plan. It was made before you learned anything.

Move first. Let the plan update as you go.

"Happiness Comes From Within" Actually Means Something

You've seen this phrase on every wellness account and inspirational poster. It's been hollowed out by overuse. But it points at something precise and useful.

It doesn't mean happiness is magically inside you waiting to be unlocked. It means the way you perceive and engage with your life matters more than the specific circumstances of your life. Two people can have identical external conditions — same job, same income, same relationships — and have completely different inner experiences, because what's happening inside them is different.

When you keep betting everything on external outcomes — on the goal, the milestone, the achievement — you're betting on the wrong variable. The outer conditions are downstream of the inner ones, not the other way around.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until conditions are perfect to start. "I'll go to the gym once I'm already in shape so I don't embarrass myself." There's a part of you that genuinely feels this way — and it makes a twisted kind of sense emotionally. But look at what it's actually saying. It's not protecting you; it's ensuring you never start.

Treating the pull as optional. Some people feel the yearning — for something more, something better, something different — and dismiss it as wishful thinking or self-indulgence. That pull is not a distraction from your real life. It is information about your real life. Take it seriously.

Confusing the direction with a specific destination. The pull toward health isn't a pull toward a particular body. The pull toward meaning isn't a pull toward a particular career. When you collapse the direction into one specific outcome, you make that outcome load-bearing for your entire wellbeing — and almost nothing can hold that weight.

Holding out for the perfect solution. The more convinced you are that you've finally found the one thing that will fix everything, the more suspicious you should be. Perfect certainty at the beginning of a journey is usually a sign you're attached to a plan, not responding to reality. Take the silver medal. Take the bronze. Just move.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the direction, not the destination. Write down one area of your life where you feel the pull — where something in you is reaching toward something better. Describe it as a direction ("toward feeling more at ease in my body," "toward work that feels meaningful") rather than a fixed goal ("weigh X pounds," "get a job at Y company"). Keep it open.

  2. Take one step today that costs almost nothing. Choose the smallest possible action that moves you in that direction. Drink water instead of a soda. Send one message. Step outside. Do one thing. The size of the step doesn't matter — only the direction. This is how momentum starts.

  3. Notice when you're waiting for perfect conditions. Catch yourself the next time you think "I'll start when..." and ask: what would the minimum viable version of starting look like right now, with things as they are?

  4. Audit your milestones. If you're chasing a specific goal because you believe it will finally make you happy, ask yourself: do I know anyone who has reached this goal and is actually, lastingly content? If the answer is complicated, that's useful information.

  5. When you feel discontent, sit with it for a moment before reacting. Instead of immediately reaching for a distraction or a fix, ask: what direction is this discomfort pointing me in? The ache is a compass reading, not just noise to suppress.
