Summary

TL;DR — Toxic masculinity doesn't just hurt people who are excluded from it; it hollows out the people who embody it too. And the antidote isn't theory — it's learning to sit with someone without trying to fix them.

This is a live group conversation between five men with very different relationships to masculinity — one who was deep inside hypermasculine culture (powerlifting, steroids, emotional suppression), two who were bullied and rejected by it, and two who sit somewhere in between. What starts as a discussion about defining toxic masculinity turns into something more useful: a real-time demonstration of how men can actually show up for each other emotionally, and why they usually don't.

What Toxic Masculinity Actually Does to People

The conversation opens with attempts to define toxic masculinity abstractly — "being stronger than emotions," "socially regressive behavior." But the more useful definition comes from Shay, the person in the room who most embodied the masculine ideal:

"It's when you take a masculine trait and that becomes greater than the person itself — it's like the dehumanization of some men where something that could just be seen as masculine becomes the ideal above them being human."

From the outside, the hypermasculine guy seems like he has everything. From the inside, Shay describes something different: a slow process of being cut off from himself, from his own emotions, from any real joy.

"There's this hyper masculine idea of like you're a man, don't feel emotions, don't feel pain, just man up — and it just slowly kills you on the inside and you just turn numb."

The men who were bullied by masculine culture look at guys like Shay and feel envy, resentment, or despair — assuming those guys have everything easy. But the two experiences are more similar than they look. Both leave you disconnected. One disconnects you from your own inner life. The other disconnects you from belonging and acceptanceAcceptance is the clear-eyed acknowledgment of your present circumstances — not a surrender to them, but the necessary starting point for doing anything about them.Full definition →. Either way, you're isolated.

The Hidden Cost of Being "Fine"

Shay traces his emotional numbness to a single moment: at ten years old, his mother told him that girls don't like sensitive guys. From that point forward, sensitivity was something to bury.

"Emotional vulnerability was not accepted in my household. You'd make a joke about something, that was what you were allowed to do, and that was that."

The result wasn't just emotional suppression. It was that no one ever stopped to check on him — because he looked like he had it all together. He was performing the role well enough that he became invisible as a person.

"I'm just angry at the world that no one [ __ ] stopped me at any point in time and said 'hey man, I think you need some help.' I was a kid. I was 10 years old, and you're telling me to suppress my emotions for girls."

The anger Shay expresses isn't the destructive kind. It's protective — grief on behalf of the kid nobody checked on. Sam notices this distinction: there's a lash-out kind of anger, and there's a protective kind. The second one is actually healthy.

Why Men Freeze When Someone Is Vulnerable

When Shay opens up, the group goes quiet. Nobody knows what to say. This is the central practical lesson of the conversation: most men aren't bad at emotional support because they're callous — they're inexperienced. Nobody taught them.

"It sounds really dumb, but if you asked women this, it wouldn't be silence. We're just completely useless."

The instinct in the room is to either fix the problem or say nothing. But what Shay actually needs is simpler: acknowledgment that what he's feeling is real, and permission to keep feeling it. His mother's message was I don't want to see that. The opposite of that isn't advice — it's just I see it, and I'm okay with it.

"We don't have to be competent. We just have to be willing. That's all he needs from us."

When the group admits they feel inept — when they stop pretending to have answers — something shifts in Shay's face. That moment of shared vulnerability does more than any advice could have. He doesn't need them to be good at this. He needs them to try.

The Elephant You're Afraid to Name

Ed describes a pattern many recognize: seeing other men and automatically assuming they're better — more attractive, more capable, more deserving. This comparison corroded a relationship for him and poisoned casual friendships.

Quinton describes a quiet hatred he carries toward athletic-looking men he passes at work, calling them names in his head. It's jealousy he hasn't fully owned.

And there's an unspoken question underneath all of it when Shay is in the room: Were you one of the people who hurt me?

That question doesn't get asked directly — until it does. And when it does, Shay answers clearly: he wasn't. He was actually protective of people who got targeted. The relief in Quinton is immediate.

"When we have an unspoken question that we don't ask because we're afraid of hurting someone's feelings, it's like an elephant in the room. By asking the question and giving the person a chance to answer, we can put it to rest and move forward together."

Even if Shay had answered differently — even if he'd said yes, I was one of those guys — the relief would still have come. Because connection doesn't require a perfect answer. It requires honesty and the willingness to face the question at all.

Luck Isn't the Only Variable

Ed describes his turning point: a friend named Luke who sat with him on Discord for hours during a breakdown, asking how he felt, listening without trying to fix anything. It was the first time at 18 that Ed had experienced that kind of intimacy with another man.

"The level of intimacy was almost like there was a part of me that wanted to push it away — like, what the [ __ ] is this? I've had tens of conversations with this guy about really deep, hurtful stuff, and I've just become a better person every single time."

Common Pitfalls

Reassuring instead of witnessing. When someone says they feel like a bad person, the instinct is to say "no you're not, you're great." This actually disconnects them. It tells them their self-assessment is wrong before you've even understood it. What works instead is staying with them in the experience — asking what the guilt feels like, not rushing to dissolve it.

Treating emotional support as a fix-it problem. Men are trained to solve things. But Luke didn't fix any of Ed's problems. He just sat there. The impulse to offer advice or action is often a way of avoiding the discomfort of sitting with someone's pain. Sometimes the most validating thing you can say is that you have no idea how to solve it — because then at least the person isn't alone in their helplessness.

Assuming the people who had it "easy" are okay. The men who were bullied assumed the masculine guys had everything figured out. They didn't. The front that looks strong is often the thing that's doing the most damage — precisely because no one checks behind it.

Mistaking inexperience for incompetence. Most men can't do this stuff, but the reason isn't that they're bad at emotions. It's that they were never taught. The gap is experience, not capacity.

Over-intellectualizing. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ask the question you've been avoiding. If there's something you've wanted to ask someone but haven't because it might be offensive or uncomfortable, say it. Give the other person a chance to answer. The elephant in the room prevents connection more than the actual answer will.

  2. Practice the "sit with it" response. Next time a friend shares something difficult, resist the urge to fix, reassure, or offer solutions. Instead: acknowledge what you heard, name what emotion you're seeing, and ask them to say more. You don't need to be competent — you just need to try.

  3. Let someone help you with something you could handle yourself. Eat the Play-Doh sandwich. Ask for help you don't strictly need. Allow someone the experience of being useful to you. This is a gift you give them, not a weakness you're displaying.

  4. Practice sitting with an unpleasant sensation deliberately. Once a day, choose the slightly less appealing option — the food you like less, the seat that's less comfortable. When the unpleasantness shows up, don't push it away. Observe it from a slight distance: wow, that's really unpleasant for me right now. Notice that you are the one observing it, not the one trapped inside it. Practice this until the unpleasantness loses some of its power over your choices.

  5. When you're tempted to tell someone they're a good person, stop. Ask instead: Are you scared that this is who you really are? Let them answer. Follow wherever the answer leads.

  6. Identify your "Luke" — and then try to be one. Think about whether there's someone in your life who has been that person for you. If there is, tell them. If there isn't, recognize that you can practice becoming that for someone else — even badly, even imperfectly — starting now.
