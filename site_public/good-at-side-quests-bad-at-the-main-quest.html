Summary

TL;DR — Your "side quests" might actually be your main quest. The traditional life path isn't the only valid one, and skills the world undervalues can be worth serious money to the right person.

A 25-year-old with a classics degree is unemployed, living at home, good at daily chores and errands, bad at "adulting" in the conventional sense — career, finances, a plan. They spend their days walking in the woods, reading at the park, applying to dead-end jobs out of obligation. The shame is real, but the diagnosis of "something is wrong with this person" may be completely off.

The deeper issue isn't a lack of ability. It's a mismatch between who this person actually is and the life script they feel pressured to follow.

The Problem Isn't the Person — It's the Template

There's a specific kind of suffering that comes not from dysfunction but from comparison to an arbitrary standard. This person isn't in crisis. They're contemplative, detail-oriented, capable of executing small tasks without complaint, and genuinely curious about the world. None of that is broken.

What's broken is the assumption that everyone's main quest looks the same: prestigious career, fast upward trajectory, aggressive goal-setting. That template was not written with every kind of person in mind.

Some people are naturally wired to move slowly and thoughtfully through the world. They're not lazy — they're operating on a different register. They notice details others miss, they get satisfaction from completing small tasks well, and they tend to think more than they act. In a culture that rewards hustle and relentless ambition, this personality type reads as a failure. It isn't.

"Don't adopt a main quest just because other people are adopting it. The whole point of the main quest is that it's spawn-specific — your main quest is different from other people's main quests."

Your Skills Have a Real Market — Just Not Where You've Been Looking

The person in this scenario keeps applying to dead-end jobs unrelated to their interests, probably because those feel "safe" and "realistic." This is a trap. It keeps them underemployed and disconnected from what they're actually good at.

What are they actually good at? Running errands. Managing small logistics. Executing on detail-oriented tasks without being overwhelmed or annoyed by them. Keeping track of things that would slip through the cracks for a busier, more chaotic person.

That skill set has a name in the professional world: executive assistant or personal assistant. And it is dramatically undervalued in the popular imagination.

A skilled executive assistant can earn anywhere from a solid living to genuinely high compensation, depending on who they work for. The reason is straightforward: people who generate significant income are often terrible at the small logistical details of their own lives. They forget to renew registrations, miss appointments, let inboxes spiral out of control. Someone who can calmly handle all of that for them — and actually enjoys doing it — is worth real money.

The exchange is clean: you do the things that are genuinely easy for you, for someone who finds those same things nearly impossible. Everyone wins.

The path to this isn't perfectly mapped out. There's no degree program called "executive assistant to a high-net-worth individual." But the role exists, the demand is real, and the best entry point is simply to start looking for those openings and keep your radar up.

Decouple Your Degree From Your Income

One of the most common sources of paralysis for people with humanities or arts degrees is the belief that their career must justify their education. If you studied classics, you should be working in classics. Otherwise, why did you go?

This belief is worth letting go of entirely.

Your degree and your income source can be completely separate things. Your education shapes how you think, what you're curious about, and what kind of person you're becoming — it doesn't have to be the engine that pays your rent. Plenty of people build careers that have nothing to do with what they studied, and the knowledge they accumulated doesn't go to waste; it just expresses itself differently.

The contemplative, philosophical mind that studies classical civilizations doesn't stop being useful because you're picking up someone's dry cleaning. It's still running in the background. It's still forming ideas, making connections, noticing things. The trick is to find a way to capture that and let it develop on its own timeline, independently of how you pay your bills.

The Translation Problem: Thoughts Are Not Enough

There's a specific failure mode common to thoughtful, curious people: consuming endlessly, reflecting deeply, and producing nothing.

Ideas that stay inside your head have zero value to anyone else. This isn't a moral failing — it's just a mechanical truth. A great thought that never leaves your skull cannot help another person, cannot connect you to a community, cannot grow into something larger. The value only unlocks when it gets translated into some external form.

That translation is where most people get stuck. Not because they lack the ideas, but because the step from "thinking about something" to "making something" feels enormous, or unnecessary, or presumptuous.

It doesn't have to be grand. The minimum viable version is just: write it down. Keep a notebook. Type thoughts into a document. Start capturing what's already moving through your mind. You're not writing for an audience yet — you're just practicing the act of externalizing what's inside.

"You may have great taste in music, but at some point you've got to make it."

From there, if it ever feels right, you can share it. Start a channel. Post something. Show your thinking to the world in some form. The creators who eventually reach large audiences almost universally describe the same beginning: they just started, it was small and awkward for a while, and then one day it wasn't.

The contemplative person walking through the woods thinking about ancient civilizations has something worth saying. But it has to come out of the woods first.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking shame for signal. Feeling ashamed about not following the conventional path doesn't mean the conventional path is right for you. Shame is a social emotion — it tells you what others might judge, not what's actually wrong or missing.

Applying to jobs that are wrong for you out of obligation. Firing off applications to dead-end jobs every day creates the feeling of effort without any real movement. It also reinforces the belief that you're not qualified for anything better. Stop applying to jobs that have nothing to do with your strengths and start looking for roles that actually match how your mind works.

Waiting until the creative output is "good enough" to start. The notebook doesn't need to be a masterpiece. The first YouTube video doesn't need to go viral. Starting badly is not a failure condition — it's the only way the process begins.

Assuming your skills are ordinary because they feel easy. Things that come naturally to you feel like nothing special. But ease and value are not the same thing. The fact that managing logistics feels effortless to you is precisely why someone who finds it agonizing will pay well for your help.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Stop applying to dead-end jobs. Instead, search specifically for personal assistant and executive assistant openings. Look for roles supporting busy professionals, founders, or executives. The compensation ceiling is much higher than most people assume.

  2. Start carrying a notebook. Any time you're doing a routine task — an errand, a walk, a commute — and an interesting thought surfaces, write it down. Don't filter it. Don't judge whether it's "worth" capturing. Just get it out of your head and onto a page.

  3. Pick one interest and make one thing. It doesn't need to be public. Write a short piece about something in classical history that you find fascinating. Record yourself talking about an idea. Draft an essay you'll never publish. The goal is to practice the act of converting thought into artifact.

  4. Audit what you're actually good at — not what you think you "should" be good at. Make a list of the tasks you execute easily that other people struggle with. Logistics, errands, follow-through, detail-tracking — whatever applies. That list is a job description. Use it to search for roles rather than applying broadly.

  5. Give yourself permission to let your income and your intellectual life run on separate tracks. You don't have to find a job that honors your degree. Find a job that fits your natural skill set, and let your curiosity develop in its own time, in its own direction.
