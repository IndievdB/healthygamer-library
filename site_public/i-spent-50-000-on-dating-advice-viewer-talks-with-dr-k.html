The Treadmill of Fixing Yourself

Ish described a pattern that will feel familiar to a lot of people in self-improvement spaces. Every solved problem simply revealed the next one on the list:

  • Social anxiety → gym → income → business → dating coaches → pick-up bootcamps

Each item was attacked with genuine effort and real results. And each result produced... nothing, emotionally. The gym felt like a chore done for women's approval, not for himself. The money felt like fuel to buy the next fix. Getting laid felt like "masturbating with another person."

The brutal irony is structural: every win gets converted into evidence that more work is needed. Crossing something off the list doesn't register as pride — it just exposes the next deficit. Taking the W's in life and turning them into L's, over and over.

"I wake up every day and I'm just like, what can I fix today?"

This isn't laziness or stupidity. It's a learned response. When the one moment of pride Ish could remember from childhood came from winning a national competition — and it was his father who felt proud — he learned a very specific equation: achievement = worthiness of love. Not being, not existing. Achieving. And only the best achievement counts, because second place was never enough.

Once you learn that equation in childhood, no amount of adult accomplishment can satisfy it. The goalposts are inside you.

Why Positive Emotions Feel Like Danger

Several times, Ish was asked what he was feeling in the moment, and he kept saying "I don't know." This wasn't evasion — it was genuinely confusing to him.

Here's what was actually happening: when something positive broke through — a flash of pride, being told someone was proud of him — his face showed it briefly before he shut it down. Positive emotion landed like a threat. He literally used the word Terror to describe what it felt like to be proud of himself.

This makes complete sense given the history. When you're raised in an environment of emotional, verbal, and physical abuse, hypervigilance becomes the default setting. You learn to track danger constantly. Negative emotions become familiar and navigable; positive ones become unfamiliar and therefore suspect. When pride or joy shows up, the alarm goes off: something is wrong, this won't last, I'm being tricked.

So the positive emotion gets shut down fast — with a "thanks," a deflection, a pivot back to what still needs fixing.

"It's unfamiliar. Unknown. Danger."

The result is that Ish had to carve every positive feeling out of stone. Nothing came easily or naturally. And the effort required to sustain good feelings (the five-morning-routine items, the meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →, the journaling) meant that any slip in the routine sent him back to baseline self-loathing. The good states felt like bypassing the real problem rather than healing it.

The Mask Problem and What Pick-Up Coaching Actually Did

The dating coaching world promised a formula: do X, get Y. Ish applied it. It technically worked — he attracted women, slept with more of them. And it made him feel more lonely.

Because what the pick-up approach required was constructing a mask: projected confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →, scripted escalation, performed bravado. Women responded to the mask. Which taught him the exact opposite of what he needed to learn — it taught him that the real him is unattractive, and only the performance works.

"I learned that the real me, whoever that is, is unattractive."

Then he tried to correct in the other direction — lower the mask, be vulnerable — and went too far the other way, traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →-bonding on first dates, which scared people off for different reasons.

He was oscillating between two performed versions of himself, neither of which was actually him. And he genuinely doesn't know who "him" is, because he grew up as a social chameleon, becoming whoever the room needed.

The Unconscious Protection Hypothesis

Here's the most counterintuitive idea in the conversation: Ish is extremely competent. He learns things, applies them, gets results. That competence has worked in every domain of his life. So why not dating?

One possibility: maybe it has worked, and he's ending up exactly where some part of him is steering.

The more he connects with someone on a date — the more he actually likes them — the more a quiet alarm sounds: she'd be better off without me. Not a conscious thought. An unconscious one, rooted in genuinely caring about people and genuinely believing, at a deep level, that he is too much, too dark, too broken for someone to handle.

So the connection gets subtly sabotaged. He waits for her to text instead of texting. He shares childhood trauma on a first date. He goes into a mode that closes the connection off just as it starts to form.

"I wonder if some of this is just that the reason you have difficulty getting second and third dates is because you're a very effective communicator and you kind of know how to keep that from happening."

This isn't a certain diagnosis. But it's worth sitting with — especially if you've noticed that the closer you get to something you actually want, the more likely things are to fall apart.

You Can't Fix What Someone Else Broke

This is probably the hardest thing in the conversation, and the most important.

Ish was not born feeling broken. The feeling was installed by his environment — abuse, impossible standards, conditional love, the message that his face was wrong, that silence meant defectiveness, that friends couldn't be trusted.

The biggest lie in self-improvement culture is that because someone else damaged you, you can fix it. Through enough effort, enough courses, enough therapy techniques, enough morning routines.

Some of that helps. Ish has done all of it. He's done his piece.

"If someone else taught you how to hate yourself, someone else really has to teach you how to love yourself."

That's terrifying because it means the healing is partially out of your control. It's also liberating for exactly the same reason — you can stop waking up every morning trying to outwork a wound you didn't create.

The path forward isn't another fix. It's interdependence — actually letting other people in, letting them carry some of the weight, letting them be proud of you without immediately shutting it down with a "thanks."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking action for healing. Going to the gym, meditating, journaling — these can be genuine self-care or they can be another way of running from the uncomfortable core. The test: does stopping the routine for a week send you back to zero? If so, you're managing, not healing.

Treating the symptom (loneliness) as the problem. The dating issue is downstream of something older. Solving it at the level of tactics — better eye contact, less interview-style conversation — won't touch what's actually driving the cycle.

Oscillating between masks. Going from pick-up artist performance to radical oversharing is still performing. Neither is actually being yourself. The goal isn't to find the right amount of vulnerability to dispense; it's to be present enough that what you share is organic.

Converting accomplishments into proof of inadequacy. If every win just shows you the next deficit, the game is unwinnable by design. Notice when this is happening.

Shutting down positive emotion with politeness. "Thanks" is a door closing. It feels socially appropriate and actually blocks the thing you need most.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the "thanks" reflex. The next time someone says something genuinely kind about you, pause before responding. Don't say thanks automatically. If you can, ask: "Can you help me understand that? I'm not sure I see what you're seeing." Then actually listen to their answer without immediately refuting it internally.

  2. Stop watching the replay. If you have a vulnerable conversation — a date, a therapy session, an honest talk with a friend — resist the urge to go back and analyze what you said wrong. Give it at least a few weeks before revisiting anything recorded.

  3. Distinguish doing from fixing. When you exercise, meditate, or journal, ask yourself: Am I doing this because it feels good to take care of myself, or because I'll feel worthless if I skip it? The first is self-care. The second is the treadmill in a different outfit.

  4. Let the routine be fragile sometimes. If the five-morning-things slip for a day, notice what the self-talk sounds like. That self-talk — the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, the "I'm back to zero" feeling — is the thing worth working on in therapy. The routine is not the cure; it's a clue pointing at the wound.

  5. Practice receiving, not just giving. Ish described being emotionally over-available — ready to be someone's support — while being unable to receive the same. Deliberately practice letting people do things for you: carry something, pay, give advice, express care. Notice the discomfort and sit with it rather than deflecting.

  6. Date without an outcome in mind. Before the next date, drop the question "How do I get them to like me?" and replace it with "What do I actually notice about this person? How do I feel around them?" The shift in attention is small; the shift in experience can be significant.

  7. Work the trauma with professional support. This is not optional or a nice-to-have. Complex PTSD, emotional flashbacks, hypervigilance — these respond to specific therapeutic approaches. The solo toolkit has limits. Let someone else help carry it.
