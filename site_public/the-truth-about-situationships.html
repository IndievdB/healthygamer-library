Summary

TL;DR — Situationships are a rational adaptation to a broken dating environment, but evolution doesn't care about your agreement. Understanding the neuroscience of how people fall in love — and why that process keeps getting short-circuited — is what lets you navigate these relationships with less damage.

Situationships are everywhere, and dismissing them as laziness or emotional immaturity misses the point entirely. They emerge from a genuinely difficult environment: fractured attention, exhausted dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → systems, and a real fear of being the one who falls while the other person keeps their options open. At the same time, biology has its own agenda that no verbal agreement fully overrides. Understanding both sides of that tension — the rational and the biological — is what actually helps you make better decisions.

Why Falling in Love Is a Neurochemical Process

Lust and love come from completely different systems, and conflating them causes a lot of confusion.

Lust is governed by sex hormones — testosterone and estrogen, produced by the gonads. More testosterone generally means more libido, regardless of gender. People who are asexual but still capable of falling in love tend to show differences in this hormonal system, not in the system responsible for love. This matters because your drive to have sex can remain fully intact even when your capacity to fall in love is impaired.

Love is governed by the dopaminergic system — specifically the nucleus accumbensA brain region at the center of the dopamine system that drives pleasure, craving, and behavioral reinforcement.Full definition → and the mesolimbic circuit. This is the same machinery that drives addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →. Three things come out of it:

  • PleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition → — just being near someone you're falling for releases dopamine
  • Craving — when they're gone, you want them back intensely
  • Behavioral reinforcementThe brain's mechanism for repeating actions that produced pleasure or relief — when a behavior leads to a dopamine release (or suppresses negative emotion), the nucleus accumbens signals "do that again," building…Full definition → — you keep seeking them out

One key feature of this system in the context of love is kindling: the opposite of tolerance. Where tolerance means you need more of a substance to get the same effect, kindling means each exposure increases your sensitivity. Spend one hour with someone and feel something small; spend two hours and feel four times as much. This is why falling in love has that runaway, accelerating quality — you're not imagining it, you're experiencing a sensitization loop.

The rate-of-change-in-intimacy model formalizes this: passion is highest when intimacy is rising quickly. When two people are actively learning new things about each other, sharing new experiences, showing affection — passion ignites. When intimacy plateaus, passion fades. This is why early love feels so electric and why long-term couples need different models to sustain connection.

There's also a self-expansion model: humans are motivated to grow, and one of the central pathways to growth is incorporating another person into your identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →. Becoming a partner, a spouse, a parent — these aren't just social roles, they represent genuine expansions of self. The problem is that this model requires trust and stability, and those feel very scarce right now.

The Prisoners' Dilemma of Modern Dating

These neurological processes require one specific ingredient: sustained, mutual attention. You cannot kindle passion if your attention is split between six people. The sensitization loop never gets started.

Here's what's actually happening in dating today:

  1. Everyone hedges by talking to multiple people simultaneously
  2. Divided attention prevents the ignition of genuine passion
  3. People try harder — more apps, more matches — which divides attention further
  4. The rare person who does fall in love often falls for someone whose attention is elsewhere, leading to rejection and pain
  5. The lesson everyone learns: falling in love is dangerous

This is a classic prisoners' dilemma. If you open up emotionally and the other person doesn't, you get hurt. If both of you stay guarded, you both stay lonely. The "safe" strategy that emerges from this logic — for everyone, simultaneously — is the situationship.

On top of that, dopaminergic circuits are being degraded. Social media, doom scrolling, pornography, and video games all run on the same circuitry that love depends on. When that system develops tolerance or becomes numbed from overstimulation, the capacity to fall in love diminishes. You can be out there dating and genuinely not feel much — not because something is wrong with you as a person, but because the underlying hardware is worn down.

Add to this a broader climate of radical uncertainty: people don't know where they'll be living in two years, can't afford homes, aren't sure about careers. The self-expansion model — tying your life to another person's — sounds terrifying when your own path feels unstable. A situationship protects individual autonomy while still providing connection and sex.

Why Biology Doesn't Honor Your Agreement

Here's the uncomfortable part: you can decide rationally to keep things casual. Your oxytocinA hormone central to emotional bonding, released during physical contact — hugging, cuddling, skin-to-skin touch, breastfeeding, and sexual activity.Full definition → system doesn't get the memo.

Oxytocin is released through physical contact, cuddling, and sex. It creates emotional bonding. Evolution shaped this precisely to counteract post-sex clarity — the sobering moment after desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → fades when, without any other mechanism, you might just leave. Oxytocin is the evolutionary patch that kept early humans around long enough to raise offspring together. It works whether you want it to or not.

Beyond oxytocin, even kissing alters your gut microbiome. The bacterial exchange influences neurotransmitter precursor production — serotoninA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition → building blocks, monoamines. Breakups have been shown to alter gut microbiome composition and affect mental health. The biological entanglement of physical intimacy goes deeper than most people — including most practitioners — fully understand.

The result: people create rules (no cuddling, no sleepovers, don't catch feelings) without knowing all the mechanisms they're trying to override. And research on Friends with Benefits relationships from 2009 found that 51–60% of college students had been in one — with women showing more dissatisfaction over time and being less likely to enter similar arrangements in the future. One consistent pattern is that one partner tends to catch feelings and the situation destabilizes.

There's also a longer-term risk: staying in a situationship for an extended period may require actively conditioning yourself to suppress emotional signals. Some people who have done this — including those who've worked in emotionally demanding sexual contexts — find it genuinely hard to undo that conditioning when they later want a committed relationship. The emotional numbness, the reflex to not attach, can persist and complicate future partnerships.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming the rules you agreed to will hold: Your verbal agreement doesn't govern your limbic systemThe limbic system is an emotional circuit — several interconnected regions of the brain working together to produce the full range of human emotions.Full definition →. "We said no feelings" is not a reliable predictor of whether feelings will arise.

  • Being dishonest in the first conversation: The opening negotiation of a situationship is often the most dishonest moment. One or both people suppress real feelings to avoid awkwardness, preserve the friendship, or not seem too eager. This early deception creates compounding problems later.

  • Thinking more options means more chance of connection: The instinct to talk to more people to find the right spark actually destroys the conditions under which sparks form. Sustained, focused attentionThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → is a prerequisite for passion, not a luxury.

  • Believing that less commitment means less baggage: Situationships create their own emotional residue — conditioning, jealousy, changed gut microbiome, altered relational patterns. "Keeping it casual" doesn't mean "keeping it clean."

  • Conflating lust with love: Your sex drive is fine. That doesn't mean you're capable of falling in love right now, especially if your dopaminergic system is chronically overstimulated by other sources.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your attention before entering anything: If you are currently talking to multiple people romantically at the same time, recognize that you're making it structurally impossible for passion to develop with any of them. Ask honestly whether the hedging is worth the cost.

  2. Have the explicit conversation before anything physical happens — not after. Cover: Is this exclusive? What does contact look like (texting frequency, seeing each other)? Is this secret or public? What happens when one of us catches feelings? What's the exit strategy?

  3. Be honest in that first conversation, even if it's uncomfortable. If you have even a flicker of romantic interest in the person, say so. "I have some feelings and I'm not sure what I want" is more protective of both people than performing certainty you don't have.

  4. Set a tentative time horizon. You don't need a contract. But "until one of us starts dating someone else" or "let's check in after three months" gives the situation a structure that prevents indefinite drift.

  5. Honestly assess your dopaminergic load. If you're doom-scrolling, gaming heavily, or using pornography regularly, your capacity for the kind of emotional sensitivity that love requires is likely blunted. Reducing that load isn't just good for your general wellbeing — it's relevant to your romantic life.

  6. Plan for emotional emergence, not just emotional rules. Rather than "we won't catch feelings," plan for what you'll do when feelings arise. What's the conversation? What changes? Having a protocol in advance makes it far easier to handle when it actually happens.

  7. If you've been in multiple situationships or have been conditioning yourself to detach, consider that this pattern may need deliberate attention before your next relationship attempt — not because you're broken, but because practiced suppression takes practiced reversal.
