Summary

TL;DR — Empathy is not straightforwardly good. High levels of affective empathyThe ability to literally feel what another person is feeling, rather than simply understanding it intellectually.Full definition → are linked to burnout, being taken advantage of, and even sabotaging your own relationships.

Most conversations about empathy treat it as something you should cultivate without limit — the more you have, the better. The reality is more complicated. Empathy is a psychological tool, and like any tool, it can work against you when it's operating outside your control. Understanding the specific mechanisms by which empathy causes harm gives you a way to protect yourself without becoming cold or indifferent.

Two Kinds of Empathy

Before getting into the problems, it helps to know what we're actually talking about.

  • Cognitive empathyUnderstanding something intellectually rather than feeling it — that's cognitive empathy.Full definition → is the ability to understand what another person thinks or believes — a kind of intellectual perspective-taking.
  • Affective empathy is the ability to feel what another person feels — the emotional resonance most people picture when they hear the word "empathy."

Affective empathy is where most of the danger lies.

Emotional Contagion: Why Feeling Their Pain Hurts You

When you empathize with someone who is suffering, your brain's empathic circuits create a kind of emotional connection — and in doing so, you absorb some of their negativity. Researchers literally call this emotional contagion, and the analogy is apt: just like an infection, catching someone else's emotional state doesn't reduce the amount they're carrying. You both end up sick.

This is not a minor effect. Studies on social workers — people whose job centers on empathy — find that between 50 and 75% of them are burned out. The same pattern appears in caregiver burnout, originally studied in spouses of cancer patients and now well-documented in people supporting friends, partners, or family members through mental health struggles.

At the extreme end, repeated exposure to someone else's traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → can produce vicarious trauma — you begin exhibiting symptoms of traumatic stress yourself, not because anything happened to you directly, but because your empathic circuits processed it as though it had. Therapists are trained to guard against this. Most people are not.

This same dynamic is now playing out through social media. The more you care, the more you expose yourself to footage of suffering, violence, and tragedy — because you want to stay informed, spread awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, do something. The kinder you are, the more you absorb. There is growing evidence that this constitutes a form of vicarious trauma delivered through a screen.

Empathy Makes You Easier to Exploit

There's a classic economics experiment: one person is given $100 and must propose a split with a second person. If the second person accepts, both keep their shares. If the second person rejects, nobody gets anything.

The rational move for the person making the offer is to propose something unfair — say, $90 for them, $10 for you — because $10 is better than nothing, and a rational actor should accept it. What determines whether someone accepts an unfair offer? Their level of empathy. The more empathic the person on the receiving end, the more likely they are to take the bad deal.

Researchers took this further: when participants were given SSRIsA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition → (medications that increase serotonin, which is associated with empathy), they became more likely to accept unfair trades. This wasn't a personality difference — it was chemically induced. The mechanism is real and measurable.

The more empathic you are, the more likely you are to be screwed by other people.

This shows up in everyday life as the double standard many empathic people recognize: you understand your friend's pain when they're struggling, so you show up for them. But when you need support, they're suddenly unavailable. They set limits with you but resent it when you set limits with them. Your empathy makes their behavior legible and forgivable to you in a way that your behavior never becomes legible or forgivable to them.

In the most severe cases, this dynamic produces Stockholm syndrome — where someone who has been controlled or abused develops positive feelings toward their abuser — which appears to be mediated by empathy. The more you can see things from your abuser's perspective, the more you excuse their behavior, and the more trapped you become.

Empathizing With Cruelty Changes You

There's a study worth knowing about. Participants played a video game as either Superman or the Joker, then were tested afterward. People who played as the Joker were:

  • More likely to endorse violent solutions to problems
  • More likely to interpret neutral facial expressions as hostile
  • Less likely to donate to a good cause

Playing a character who operates without empathy for others actually shifted how people perceived the world and what they considered acceptable. The implication: if you're spending a lot of time in the company of people who are callous or cruel, or if you're practicing empathy toward those people, it can gradually reshape your own moral perception.

How High Empathy Sabotages Early Relationships

This one is counterintuitive. You might assume that being highly attuned to your partner's feelings would strengthen a relationship. Early on, it actually does the opposite.

At the start of a relationship, everyone is ambivalent to some degree. Attraction is mixed with uncertainty. A partner might feel some dissatisfaction, think about someone they used to date, or find someone else attractive — not because they're a bad partner, but because that's just how early relationships work.

A highly empathic person picks up on all of this accurately. And because of emotional contagion, they then feel it. Now both people are experiencing the partner's early dissatisfaction — which is more than twice as destabilizing as one person quietly working through their feelings.

Studies show that a relationship with one unhappy person and one happy person is more likely to survive than a relationship where one person's unhappiness spreads to both.

Someone with less emotional attunement might simply not notice their partner's early doubts, giving that partner time and space to work through them. The empathic person notices, absorbs it, and the relationship collapses under the weight of amplified uncertainty.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming the solution is to feel less and care less. The goal isn't indifference — it's shifting from affective empathy to compassion. Compassion means acting with kindness toward someone without fusing with their emotional state. You can help someone without becoming them. A good therapist does this constantly: caring deeply about a patient's wellbeing without being destabilized by the patient's pain.

Taking responsibility for outcomes instead of efforts. There's a difference between being responsible for helping someone and being responsible for their problems. When you make their problems yours, two things happen: you burn out trying to control something you can't control, and they stop taking responsibility for their own outcomes. This is the engine behind weaponized incompetence — someone can only stay incompetent if someone else keeps compensating for it.

Thinking self-sacrifice equals virtue. High empathy combined with self-criticism is a particularly destructive combination. Feeling someone else's pain activates your own guilt and ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition →. You feel bad for them, then blame yourself for not doing enough, then feel guilty about having limits at all. The empathy isn't the core problem here — the self-criticism is the accelerant.

Accepting other people's framing of your obligations. Empathic people are prone to adopting the perspective of whoever is in front of them. If someone presents their dissatisfaction as your responsibility to fix, you'll feel that framing as though it were true. Getting out of this requires recognizing when the framing itself is the problem.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice compassion instead of empathy. When someone shares something painful with you, consciously practice staying on your side of the emotional boundary. You can say "that sounds really hard" and mean it without absorbing it. Notice the difference between feeling for someone and feeling with them.

  2. Separate being responsible for help from being responsible for outcomes. Before helping someone, get clear on what you can actually control. You can offer your time, presence, or a resource. You cannot control whether they use it, feel better, or change. Helping is your job; the outcome is theirs.

  3. Audit your self-criticism. If empathy is hitting you especially hard — you feel burned out, guilty, or like a bad person for having limits — ask whether self-critical thinking is amplifying the impact. Work on the self-criticism directly, not just the empathy.

  4. Identify your internal conflicts. When you feel trapped between helping someone and protecting yourself, write out the conflict explicitly. Ask: whose problem is this actually? What did I agree to, and what was never mine to carry? Resolving the internal conflict is often more effective than trying to set external limits under guilt.

  5. Limit passive empathic exposure. If you're spending significant time consuming traumatic content on social media out of a sense of moral obligation, recognize that emotional contagion operates through screens too. Staying informed does not require absorbing everything. Set deliberate limits on this exposure — not out of indifference, but out of self-preservation.

  6. Notice when you're accepting an unfair trade. When a relationship or arrangement feels consistently one-sided, ask honestly whether your empathy for the other person's circumstances is leading you to rationalize it. Understanding why someone behaves badly is not the same as that behavior being acceptable.
