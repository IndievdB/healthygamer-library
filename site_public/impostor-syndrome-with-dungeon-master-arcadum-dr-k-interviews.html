Summary

TL;DR — Impostor syndrome isn't primarily a thinking problem; it's an emotional one rooted in old wounds, and beating the voice into silence only makes it louder. This is a deep conversation between a psychiatrist and a professional Dungeon Master who has built a career out of creating safe spaces for other people — while quietly denying himself that same safety.

Jeremy (Arcadum) grew up in poverty and chaos in rural Arkansas, survived abuse of every kind, discovered D&D at 13 as a genuine lifeline, and built a professional career around giving other people the escape he once desperately needed. By the time of this conversation he is running nine campaigns for roughly forty players, managing a Discord MMO, and watching his audience grow — yet the more successful he becomes, the louder an internal voice gets telling him he's not good enough. That paradox is the heart of what follows.

Why Impostor Syndrome Gets Worse as You Succeed

The common assumption is that success should silence self-doubt. The opposite tends to happen. Impostor syndrome arises most powerfully when your external sense of worth — what the world reflects back at you — far outpaces your internal sense of worth. One day you're applying to medical school; the next you're in. Your circumstances changed in 24 hours. Your sense of self didn't.

The cruelest feature of impostor syndrome is that it is adaptive. The internal voice that says "you're not good enough" drives you to work harder, and you do get better. The voice quiets for a while. Then it comes back, louder, because now the stakes are higher. You farm the XP, you level up — but the portal that keeps spawning those thoughts is still open. Attacking the thoughts one by one never closes the portal.

"Imposter syndrome arises when our internal sense of worth is far outweighed by our external sense of worth. The more successful you are, the more vulnerable you are to it."

The Wound That Leveling Up Doesn't Heal

Jeremy's father would praise him in one breath and send his little sister to check his work in the next — a constant, quiet demolition of confidence. When a difficult player in an early campaign said "I knew you couldn't do it," Jeremy didn't hear that player. He heard his dad.

That is the mechanism worth understanding: a wound from childhood becomes a voice, and that voice gets attached to every future moment of vulnerability. You can gain eight hit points every time you level up. The original damage is still there. Leveling up does not automatically heal it.

"I hear a guy who was running around at half health who's been leveling up a lot, but at the end of the day never healed that initial wound."

The therapeutic work — or the internal work — is not about acquiring more skill or more evidence of competence. It's about going back and treating the wound itself.

The Dice Are Not Yours to Control

Jeremy carries an enormous felt sense of responsibility: for his players' fun, for his community's wellbeing, for whether D&D actually functions as an escape for people who are suffering. He frames this as a moral obligation — if I can help someone, I should, and if I don't, I've failed them.

The flaw in that framing is a quiet but consequential one: he has accepted responsibility not just for showing up and doing the work, but for the outcome.

"You are entitled to your actions but not the fruits of your actions. It's my choice whether I choose to swing my sword. It's not my choice whether I critical hit, critical fail, or hit — that's determined by the dice."

A dungeon master creates the conditions for an adventure. He cannot guarantee the adventure. A fisherman can bring the rod, the bait, the tackle, and the boat, and sit there and put the line in the water. Whether the fish bite is not up to him. The moment he starts beating himself up because the fish aren't biting, he's taken on a burden that was never his to carry.

This is not a call to care less. It's a call to locate responsibility correctly. Bob Smith — the friend's dad who taught Jeremy D&D while hammered on a summer afternoon — didn't set out to save a kid. He just taught his son's friend a game. The escape Jeremy found was real. Bob didn't manufacture it. He just showed up.

The War You Can't Win Against Yourself

Jeremy's coping strategy for the internal voice is to fight it. When it says you can't do better, he does better, and it goes quiet. He describes living a good life "out of spite" of the voice. This works — up to a point — but it carries a hidden cost.

His father's defining trait was the need for absolute victory: win an argument, then keep drilling until the other person is annihilated. Jeremy recognizes that pattern as toxic in his father. What's harder to see is that he uses the same pattern against himself. When the voice appears, he tries to crush it completely. But crushing a part of yourself is still losing something.

"An absolute victory against yourself is still a loss for you."

The alternative isn't surrender. It's something closer to acknowledgment. The voice is scared. It represents real doubt, real insecurity, things that deserve to be heard — not obeyed, but heard. When Jeremy (the scared kid from Arkansas) shows up to tell Arcadum (the confident professional) that he's afraid they won't be able to pull this off, the answer that actually helps isn't sit down and shut up. It's: I hear you. It might come crashing down. That's okay.

The Anger Behind the Curtain

There's a warded cave at the back of Jeremy's inner world, and what lives inside it is rage.

At 16, after years of instability and a near-fatal drunk-driving incident caused by his mother, Jeremy hit a breaking point over something small — she wouldn't give him a ride — and the anger that came out was frightening in its intensity and, worse, in how good it felt. That feeling of power scared him enough that he joined the military specifically to learn to contain it, and he has never let it out since.

The solution he found was to redirect anger inward. When something external makes him angry, he blames himself instead, because at least then he is in control and no one else gets hurt. It's a genuinely protective instinct. It's also the engine of his impostor syndrome and his crushing sense of responsibility, because if everything is always your fault, you are the dungeon master of every outcome, responsible for every roll.

Depression is sometimes anger directed against the self. The "burden of responsibility" Jeremy carries is partly this: forty players, their communities, their wellbeing — all of it absorbed as his fault if something goes wrong, because the alternative is feeling anger he doesn't trust himself to contain.

The path forward isn't to blow the ward off the cave and flood everything. It's to start getting familiar with what's in there — to notice what triggers the anger, to trust that the discipline and growth of the past decade means you're not 16 anymore, and that exploring the feeling won't automatically reproduce the behavior.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating impostor syndrome as a logical problem. You can understand every feature of the "class" and still feel exactly the same, because the voice doesn't come from a rational place. Arguing with it using evidence and achievements is farming XP — useful, but it doesn't close the portal.

  • Mistaking the voice for the enemy. Trying to silence it completely, or crushing it with spite-fueled achievement, is the same pattern as the abusive parent who wasn't satisfied with winning — he had to destroy. The voice is scared. It needs acknowledgment, not annihilation.

  • Confusing responsibility for actions with responsibility for outcomes. You control the preparation, the showing up, the quality of the attempt. You do not control whether it lands. Taking credit (or blame) for the dice roll is a fast path to burnout.

  • Turning progress into evidence of past failure. The moment you have an insight, the instinct is to say why didn't I see this earlier? That converts a success into a failure. The right response to learning something useful is to note that you learned it, not to grieve that you didn't learn it sooner.

  • Assuming meditation requires stillness. If sitting quietly and doing nothing sounds impossible, you're not incompatible with meditation — you've just encountered a narrow description of it. Movement-based breathwork achieves the same thing: a period of no-mind where impostor syndrome literally cannot exist, because it's made of thoughts and the thoughts are temporarily offline.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice what you're actually angry at. Before redirecting anger inward, pause and identify the real source. You don't have to act on it. Just name it accurately. I'm angry at this situation / this person / this outcome — not I'm angry at myself for letting this happen.

  2. Change how you talk to the voice. When the doubting voice shows up, don't fight it. Acknowledge it: "I hear you. You're scared. It might not work. That's okay." Then proceed anyway. This sounds small. It is not small.

  3. Locate the line between rolling the dice and controlling the outcome. In your work or creative life, draw a clear distinction between what is genuinely yours to control (preparation, effort, showing up) and what isn't (whether the audience responds, whether someone gets the escape they were looking for). Practice stopping at the line.

  4. Stop converting successes into failures. Each time you learn something or make progress, notice if your next thought is why didn't I do this sooner? When you catch that pattern, consciously replace it: It's okay that I didn't learn this earlier. I'm learning it now.

  5. Try the breath-movement practice. Sit at the edge of a chair, lean forward, let your head hang. Breathe in through the nose, raise your head and straighten (then arch) your back; breathe out and let everything curve back down. Five to ten slow rounds. The goal isn't relaxation — it's dropping into your body long enough that thinking stops. Notice what your mind does (or doesn't do) during it.

  6. Spend time in nature, alone. Not hiking with friends, not listening to a podcast. Just you and the outside world. This is a practical way to recalibrate an internal sense of self-worth that has become completely disconnected from external metrics.

  7. Seriously consider a therapist. Not because anything is catastrophically broken, but because the warded cave exists and has never really been opened. A good therapist can help you explore the anger safely — with the lights on and an exit nearby — in a way that a conversation like this, however useful, can't fully do.
