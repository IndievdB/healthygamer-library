A few important points:

  • Anger can be productive.   • Forced forgiveness can cause harm. When survivors are pressured to forgive — especially to preserve a family unit or social group — it often places the burden on the victim and can deepen feelings of low self-worth. "If I can't forgive, something must be wrong with me."
  • Forgiveness is optional. No one is entitled to your forgiveness. Whether to forgive is a decision that belongs entirely to the person who was harmed. Both paths — forgiving and not forgiving — can lead to healing if navigated thoughtfully.

Justice, on the other hand, appears consistently healing. Holocaust survivors described watching the Nuremberg trials as genuinely restorative. Having wrongdoing formally acknowledged and consequences applied — through legitimate institutions — can provide real closure.

The Distraction vs. Avoidance Distinction

These two things look similar from the outside — both might involve gaming, watching something, getting absorbed in an activity. The difference is internal:

  • AvoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → is unconscious disengagement. You drift into your phone or a game not because you chose to but because the discomfort pushed you there.
  • Distraction is intentional adaptation. You decide: "I'm going to take an hour away from this stressor and do something absorbing, then come back."

This is why gaming, for instance, is neither inherently healthy nor unhealthy as a coping mechanism. The question is whether you're using it as active, chosen adaptation or as unconscious escape from something you're not confronting.

Holding On to Hope

Holocaust survivors — people who lost families, homes, entire worlds — described emerging on the other side with something like gratitude and forward momentum:

"It was tough but it was paradise. Nobody was trying to kill you. You always had something to wear, something to eat. I mean, it's a haven."

The human brain, under threat, is wired to predict worst-case scenarios. That's not a malfunction — it's a survival mechanism. But the prediction is not a guarantee. History is full of moments where people were certain things couldn't improve and were wrong.

"People who hold on to hope are oftentimes not disappointed."

This isn't toxic positivity or a command to feel differently than you do. Feeling hopeless in a traumatic situation is completely normal and understandable. But the research — across Holocaust survivors, war refugees, first responders — consistently shows that human beings are far more resilient than any of us expect, including the people going through it.
