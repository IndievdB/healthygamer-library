Why Your Psychiatric Diagnosis Might Be Wrong (and Why That's Okay)

Psychiatric diagnosis has a fundamental limitation: there is no blood test, no scan, no objective marker. A clinician observes behavior, listens to what you and your family report, and checks boxes. That process is imperfect by design.

A wise framing: if someone has three psychiatric diagnoses, they're probably all wrong. The system tends to accumulate labels rather than revise them, which means patients end up with a stack of diagnoses that may not actually fit.

Child-on-Child Abuse: A Gap in Common Understanding

When children engage in sexual behavior with other children, the reflexive adult response is often "kids play doctor, it's normal." That framing misses something important.

An average nine or ten-year-old doesn't spontaneously invent sexual behavior. Children reenact what they've seen or experienced. Sexual behavior in young children is generally a red flag for prior abuse — the child acting out was likely exposed to it themselves. That doesn't eliminate the harm done to the other child; it just adds context.

The bigger wound is often not the original incident but the response of the people who were supposed to protect you. When a parent minimizes, dismisses, or sides with the person who caused harm, that betrayal can cut deeper than the original event.

Privilege, relative suffering, and comparisons to others' hardships don't cancel this out. You can acknowledge genuine advantages in your life and still have been genuinely harmed. One doesn't erase the other.

Advaita: Holding Two Truths Without Making One Win

The concept of advaita (Sanskrit: non-dual) offers a way out of a specific kind of internal conflict — the kind where you feel two contradictory things about the same person and try to resolve it by making one of them "win."

The common pattern: you're angry at someone who also loves you and has suffered themselves. You cycle between the anger and the guilt about the anger. Neither resolves. You exhaust yourself.

The conventional approach to conflict resolution assumes one side needs to prevail. Advaita says: both truths can coexist. The tool for making this work is deceptively simple — replace the word "but" with "and."

  • "He's been through a lot, but what he did was wrong" → the "but" cancels the first half
  • "He's been through a lot and what he did was wrong" → both get to be true simultaneously

This matters because guilt about anger is often the real source of suffering — not the anger itself. When you feel angry and then immediately feel guilty for feeling angry, you're doing to yourself exactly what was done to you: invalidating your own experience. Letting the anger exist without fighting it — while also letting the love exist — removes the war.

"When you are angry, be angry. When you are done, be done."

You don't need to choose between loving someone and being angry at them. You don't need to forgive in order to stop hurting. You just need to stop declaring one feeling illegal.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming a diagnosis is definitive. Psychiatric diagnoses are working hypotheses, not facts. Treat them as useful frameworks, not identities.
  • Treating mania as the goal state. The creative output of hypomania is real. The aftermath — depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, relationship damage, career disruption — is also real. Chasing the high by stopping medication tends to end badly, especially while the brain is still developing.
  • Using vague language when describing abuse. When you're imprecise, the listener fills in their own (usually less serious) interpretation. What they normalize isn't what you experienced.
  • Letting comparisons invalidate your experience. "Other people have it worse" is always technically true and always irrelevant to whether your suffering is real.
  • Confusing forgiveness with absence of anger. You can love someone, forgive them, and still feel hurt by what they did. Forcing the anger away doesn't make it leave — it just makes you feel guilty on top of hurt.
  • Fighting your own feelings. The guilt you feel for being angry at someone you love creates more suffering than the anger itself. The goal isn't to stop feeling angry; it's to stop punishing yourself for feeling it.
