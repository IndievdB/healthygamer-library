Growing Up Chronically Misunderstood

Paul's early life was shaped by a core experience: something was clearly wrong, but nobody could figure out what, and the response was almost always punishment rather than curiosity.

He was diagnosed with Asperger's only after years of wrong diagnoses, mismatched medications, and a revolving door of ineffective therapists. Before that diagnosis, his difficulty reading social cues, expressing emotions, and staying on the same wavelength as others read to the adults around him as misbehavior. The result:

  • Placed in increasingly isolated classroom settings, then an alternative school for kids on probation
  • Arrested at a school meeting for yelling, rather than de-escalated
  • Sent to a therapeutic camp for "troubled youth" at his parents' expense — a $6,000/month loan they took out voluntarily

The bullying during this period was severe. Being publicly humiliated in a locker room by the most popular kid in school, then branded a snitch when someone else reported it, then bullied harder — that kind of experience leaves a mark. What's notable is that Paul processed almost all of it as anger, which is one of the key insights of the talk.

"Whenever I would get angry or upset I just wouldn't know how to express myself, and my parents didn't know what to do — so they just kind of had to guess."

Anger as a Protective Emotion

One of the more clarifying ideas in the conversation is that anger functions as a shield against harder emotions. When shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, humiliation, or hurt are too painful to sit with, anger provides a target — something to be pissed at, so you don't have to feel hurt inside.

For Paul, whose childhood was full of genuine injustice he couldn't articulate, anger became the default output for everything. Shame, loneliness, fear, grief — all of it came out as lashing out. And because nobody looked past the anger to ask what was underneath it, he never learned to do that himself.

This pattern carried directly into streaming. The chat gave him something to be angry at. The audience's approval was a way of feeling good without having to be vulnerable. And when things went wrong, anger was still the first responder.

Memories Are Saved by a Younger Mind

A useful framework comes up when discussing how to revisit painful experiences with more nuance. The point: your memories were recorded by the version of you that experienced them.

A 12-year-old's memory of being put on Zoloft is going to be stored as anger and confusion — because that's all a 12-year-old could access at the time. The memory doesn't automatically update as you grow. It stays as the emotional resolution of the mind that laid it down.

But an adult can go back and re-examine those memories with their current capacity. A suggested technique: write out a memory in detail, then go sentence by sentence and ask yourself what else you might have been feeling — not just the dominant emotion, but the ones underneath. In Paul's case, that might mean recognizing that a humiliating locker room incident involved shame, injustice, and fear, not only rage.

"It's kind of like if I draw a picture of a dinosaur when I'm six. Twenty years later I'm a great artist — but the piece of paper with the dinosaur was still made by a six-year-old. My skill improving doesn't retroactively fix old pictures."

Discomfort With Being Cared For

There's a striking moment mid-conversation when Paul's face visibly shifts while talking about his parents taking out a loan to send him to a therapeutic camp — a decision that, by Paul's own admission, he wouldn't have made himself.

The emotion is hard for him to name. It's positive, but also uncomfortable. It involves revisiting painful memories while feeling something warm. The identification offered: this is sometimes what love — or feeling valued — actually feels like. It's not always clean warmth. Sometimes it's the uncomfortable realization that someone made a bad-looking decision for you, even when you didn't think you were worth it.

Paul also notes something important: he's far more comfortable receiving compliments from a chat full of strangers than from a single person in real life. The asymmetry makes sense — real-life care requires vulnerability in a way that anonymous approval doesn't.

A related problem: Paul tends to interpret pity, compassion, and empathy as a single signal — condescension — and respond with anger. When you can't distinguish between someone looking down at you and someone genuinely aching for you, you end up pushing away the people trying to help.

Giving Away Control to Avoid Being Alone

The most consequential decision Paul describes: on his first YouTube stream, after being banned from Twitch and terrified of losing everything, he announced to his communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → that they could control his life.

"I was so afraid of being alone again. I wanted to not lose anything. So I went to the absolute extreme to make sure viewers weren't going to leave me."

What followed was a slow-motion unraveling. The community voted on who he should date. They raged when he pursued someone they didn't approve of. He hid a seven-month relationship, asking his girlfriend to wear a hood in public. He issued crying on-stream apologies for choices that weren't wrong. He woke up to strangers in his apartment.

The mechanics here mirror an abusive relationship — and not just because of fear. Fear explains some of it, but the positive pull was equally powerful: the community genuinely liked him, gave him belonging he'd never had, and the whole arrangement started from a real place of mutual enjoyment. That mix of genuine warmth and toxic control is exactly what makes abusive dynamics so hard to leave.

The Apology Pattern That Backfires

One of the more subtle observations: Paul owns his mistakes clearly and specifically, but almost never says I'm sorry — and when he explains his mistakes, he almost always follows the admission with because [negative emotion].

"I cultivated this abusive relationship because I was afraid."

This construction is accurate. But it has an unintended effect: it places the agent of the action outside the self. It wasn't me — it was the fear. Listeners pick this up unconsciously, which is part of why Paul's apologies repeatedly failed to land with his community.

The more honest version, and the one that felt authentic in the conversation, is closer to: "I made these mistakes, and I'm not sure I regret them for myself — but I do regret the harm they caused people who didn't deserve to be caught up in it." That's not a better apology for optics. It's more accurate. And accuracy reads as genuine.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing validation with connection. A chat that laughs with you (or at you) is not the same as people who actually know and accept you. The difference matters, especially when you've been starved of the real thing.
  • Treating anger as the whole story. If every memory and every emotional response registers as anger, you're missing most of your own inner life. Anger is real, but it usually has something softer underneath it.
  • Assuming pity = condescension. This one actively damages relationships. Someone trying to console you may be offering empathy, not a power play. If you can't tell the difference, the default becomes aggression — and you end up alone anyway.
  • Teleporting to emotional intimacy. Jumping straight into deep emotional territory, whether in a relationship or on stream, skips the foundational steps. Trust and intimacy are built incrementally.
  • Mistaking an admission for an apology. Saying "I made a mistake" and "I'm sorry" are different speech acts. If you're not actually sorry, own that — it's more honest and will be received better than a technically-worded admission that lands as hollow.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Revisit one memory with your adult mind. Write a page-long account of a specific difficult memory from your past. Then go back through it sentence by sentence and ask: besides anger, what else might I have been feeling here? Look for shame, fear, grief, longing, confusion. You don't have to feel the emotion — just identify it as a possibility.

  2. Practice the pity/compassion distinction. The next time someone tries to console you and you feel the instinct to get angry or push them away, pause and ask: if a third person were doing this exact thing to someone else, would I read it as condescending? If you can't clearly say yes, assume it might be compassion instead.

  3. Signal before you shut down. When someone close to you tries to have an emotional conversation and you feel yourself closing off, don't just go silent or push them away. Use this: "I can tell you're trying to help, but talking about this makes the emotions harder, not easier. I want to be able to have this conversation — I'm just not able to right now." That single move is already opening up, not closing down.

  4. Name what you actually feel before saying sorry. When you need to take responsibility for something, try to identify the actual emotional truth first: Are you sorry? Grateful it happened? Relieved it's over? Regretful only about specific collateral damage? Then say that — whatever it actually is — rather than the expected script.

  5. Catalog the early experiences of being cared for. Think of one or two moments where someone took a real risk or made a real sacrifice for you — not because it made logical sense, but because they cared. Sit with the discomfort that comes with recognizing it. That discomfort is not a signal something is wrong; it's often what being valued feels like when you're not used to it.

  6. If you have a meaningful therapist from the past, consider reaching out. Not necessarily for ongoing treatment — just to acknowledge what they meant to you. The people who actually helped you deserve to know.
