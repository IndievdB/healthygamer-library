There are real neurological and hormonal mechanisms behind it — and real social skills that can bridge the gap.

If you've ever felt like you operate at a different emotional frequency than most people — craving depth while others seem to skim the surface — you're probably not imagining it. But the story is more complicated than "I'm deep and they're shallow." There are specific biological reasons some people feel emotions more frequently and intensely, and there are also specific skills that help translate that depth into actual connection.

The Neuroscience Behind Feeling "Too Deep"

ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by a disorder of attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition → is not just a focus disorder. It's a neurodevelopmental condition that shapes how emotional circuitry is wired — and that has direct consequences for how deeply someone feels.

Three mechanisms work together here:

1. Emotional reactivity In ADHD, the emotional circuitry activates more quickly, more intensely, and the activation lasts longer. Comorbidity rates for anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → sit around 25%, and rates for depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → are also elevated. This isn't coincidence — it's partly explained by synaptic gating, the way neurons activate and inhibit each other. In ADHD, pro-anxiety neurons tend to be overactive while calming neurons are underactive. The result is that people with ADHD simply spend more time in the emotional underground.

2. Internalizing symptoms Neurotypical people can have an equivalent capacity for deep feeling — but they tend to spend more time above ground. ADHD, especially in women, comes with more internalizing symptoms: ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition →, depression, inner hyperactivity. That means more time spent with depth, not just access to it. The difference isn't how far down the shaft goes. It's how often you're down there, and how fast you get there.

3. Impaired empathy through inattention Here's the part that feels counterintuitive. People with ADHD often describe a profound hunger for connection, yet feel disconnected. Part of why is that empathy requires sensory input — you need to actually pick up facial expressions, vocal tone, body language. If your attention is constantly being pulled inward or toward competing stimuli, you're literally missing the signals other people send. It's not that you don't care. It's that the data isn't getting through.

Why This Hits Women Harder

Women with ADHD are disproportionately the ones describing this feeling of being "too deep for the world around them." That's not a cultural quirk — there's a hormonal reason.

Estrogen sensitizes emotional circuitry. Higher estrogen levels increase the capacity for emotion and the sensitivity of the systems that process it. Estrogen also increases dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → receptor density, which matters enormously because dopamine is central to ADHD.

Here's what the developmental timeline looks like: - Boys have high dopamine receptor density early, which contributes to hyperactive ADHD presentations — the kind that gets noticed and diagnosed - As boys develop, that receptor density decreases significantly by adulthood - Girls show a later increase in dopamine receptor sensitivity tied to rising estrogen during puberty — which is one reason ADHD in girls often goes undiagnosed until adolescence or adulthood

This is also why ADHD symptoms in women can shift across the menstrual cycle. Medication doses that work well at one point in the cycle may feel different at another, because the hormonal environment is changing how the underlying neurology operates.

The estrogen piece also explains why even men can suddenly access greater emotional depth at certain life stages. When testosterone and estrogen levels shift — say, after becoming a parent — emotional sensitivity can open up in ways that feel genuinely surprising.

"Estrogen literally gives us emotional depth. The higher our estrogen levels are, the more capacity for emotion we have. It sensitizes our emotional circuitry."

The Narcissism Question

So is feeling deeper than everyone else just narcissism dressed up in neuroscience?

The honest answer is: it depends on what you do with the feeling.

The experience itself — feeling more intense emotion more frequently, struggling to find people who meet you in that space — is real and has a legitimate biological basis. Dismissing it as arrogance does a disservice to people who are genuinely navigating something hard.

But the leap from "I feel things deeply" to "everyone else is a sheep and I'm the only conscious person in the room" is where it tips over into something less useful. The XKCD comic referenced in the talk captures this: the solitary figure who looks at a crowd and concludes they're all automatons, unthinking and hollow, while only they are truly awake. That framing closes off connection rather than enabling it.

The crucial distinction is this: other people may not be shallow; they may just be operating above ground more of the time. They have access to depth. They can get there. They may simply not spend as much time there — which is different from being incapable of it.

"It just means they spend less time at deeper waters. Doesn't mean that they don't exist."

The Role of Technology in Making Everyone Shallower

There's a second, separate reason the world feels increasingly surface-level — one that has nothing to do with ADHD or hormones.

Technology pulls attention outward. Human beings are naturally somewhat internal — even reading a book requires the imagination to generate an internal experience. But screens, feeds, and notifications train attention to run along the surface of external stimulation.

Emotions get triggered — the sad three-legged cat, the surfing cat — but there's no time spent with those emotions. You dip, you feel a flicker, you move on. You're activating emotional responses without living inside them long enough for them to mean anything.

This is a population-wide phenomenon. So if you're someone who does go deep — through ADHD, through estrogen, through internalizing tendencies — the contrast with the average person around you may be growing. Not because you've gotten deeper, but because the average has gotten shallower.

Common Pitfalls

  • Conflating emotional depth with moral superiority. Feeling things intensely doesn't mean you understand them more clearly or that your perspective is more valid. Depth of feeling and quality of insight are related but not the same thing.

  • Using depth as a reason to stop trying to connect. The empathy deficit in ADHD is real, but it's driven largely by inattention — which is addressable. Writing people off as shallow is easier than the work of actually tuning in to them.

  • Assuming intensity = depth. Anxious, rapid emotional cycling can feel like depth because it's overwhelming. But being frequently activated isn't the same as having meaningful access to emotional experience. Depth involves spending time there, not just passing through quickly.

  • Diagnosing yourself with every ADHD symptom you read online. Feeling disconnected from others and craving deeper connection is a widely human experience. ADHD may explain and amplify it, but it doesn't automatically explain every instance of it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Track your emotional patterns across your cycle (if applicable). If you menstruate, notice whether feelings of disconnection or emotional overwhelm cluster at particular points. This isn't just anecdotal — there are hormonal reasons your ADHD symptoms may fluctuate, and awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → helps you work with them rather than against them.

  2. Practice deliberate sensory attention in conversations. When you're talking with someone, make a conscious effort to look at their face. Notice micro-expressions. Listen to tone, not just content. The empathy deficit in ADHD is partly an attention deficit — you can partially compensate by treating attention to others as a skill to practice.

  3. Give people time before concluding they're shallow. The depth you feel quickly takes neurotypical people longer to access. If someone seems surface-level in a first or second conversation, consider that they may just not be underground yet — not that they can't get there.

  4. Create conditions for internal experience. If technology is pulling your attention outward and making it harder to access depth, build in time that's genuinely internal: no screens, no input. Reading works if imagination is active. Sitting with a feeling rather than distracting from it. This isn't about suffering — it's about building the habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of going underground instead of just skimming.

  5. Notice when "I'm deep, they're shallow" is protecting you from something. Sometimes the framing serves a function — it makes the pain of disconnection more bearable by locating the problem outside yourself. Ask honestly: is this an explanation or a defense? If it's partly a defense, what would it mean to let go of it?
