Why Most Self-Improvement Content Doesn't Work

There's a gap most people never close: they consume content about getting better, but they don't actually get better. Books get read, podcasts get listened to, and then life continues exactly as before.

Jeremiah is an interesting case study because he closes that gap. He's read the stoics, Marcus Aurelius, Ryan Holiday, Dale Carnegie, Carol Dweck, James Clear — and when you talk to him, you can see those ideas operating in how he speaks, how he treats people, and how he structures his days. He's not just quoting them.

The question worth sitting with is: what is he doing differently?

The Three-Layer System for Actually Applying What You Read

Jeremiah's approach breaks down into three distinct layers that most people skip one or two of.

Layer 1: Selective extraction

He doesn't try to apply a whole book at once. He reads, identifies two or three things that actually resonate, and focuses on those. Nothing else.

"You don't want to read a whole book and then try to implement the whole book in a day or in a week. You want to take out the things that you can actually implement and set yourself up for success."

This mirrors what James Clear describes in Atomic Habits — starting smaller than feels necessary. If you want to start running, don't run two miles on day one. Put your shoes by the door and go for a walk. Go back so sore that you can't show up the next day and you've already failed at the only thing that matters: returning.

Layer 2: Intentional, focused practice

Once he has one or two concepts, he picks them as his active focus for a day or a week and deliberately looks for opportunities to practice them. The example he gives: reading The Seven Habits of Highly Effective People and landing on "seek first to understand, then to be understood." He didn't just note it. He watched himself in conversations, caught himself trying to relate instead of listen, and actively changed the behavior.

Layer 3: Saturation of attention

This is the part most people miss entirely. Jeremiah surrounds himself with the concepts he's working on. Lock screens that rotate through quotes. Sticky notes on the bathroom mirror. A whiteboard wall. A "success board" with favorite lines on it.

"Before I'm even out the door, I already got three or four hits of what I want to consume."

The mechanism here is the same one advertising uses: carpet-bomb your attention with something long enough and your mind begins to think in that direction automatically. He's just weaponizing it intentionally, for his own purposes. The goal is to move a concept from "a rule I remember" to "how I naturally operate" — so that when someone says something cutting, the response isn't wait, what was the third agreement again? It's just: that's about them, not me.

What Tiredness Actually Is

One of the more clinically interesting moments in the conversation happens when Dr. K pushes back on a common assumption: that feeling tired means you need to rest.

Tiredness has more than one cause, and only one of them is physical fatigue.

When your brain runs low on dopaminergic activity, the subjective feeling it produces is not "I want a reward" — it's exhaustion. This is why you can feel drained on the couch and then suddenly have energy the moment someone proposes something interesting. Your kids say they're too tired to clean up; you mention a movie; they're fine. Nothing physiological changed.

Tiredness as your brain discouraging action. This one is more dangerous. Your brain is constantly running calculations on whether an action is worth the energy. If it concludes the answer is no — because the outcome seems bad, unlikely, or insufficient — it will make you feel tired as a way of conserving resources. This is why people in a hopeless mental state can feel exhausted doing almost nothing. It's not their body. It's their brain's pessimistic model of the future suppressing action.

"If I don't believe that studying will get me an A, then I'll feel tired when I'm faced with studying."

The downstream implication is uncomfortable: if you consistently give in to tiredness — lie down, pick up your phone, find something easier — you train your brain to use tiredness as a lever. It learns that tiredness works. And you'll find yourself feeling increasingly exhausted while doing less and less.

The antidote Jeremiah stumbled into (and put on his lock screen): if you're tired, do it tired. Not as motivational fluff, but as a neurological training decision. Every time you act despite tiredness, you weaken tiredness as a signal that stops you.

Reframing Hardship Without Toxic Positivity

There's a distinction worth drawing carefully here because it's easy to collapse.

Toxic positivity is pretending a bad thing is good. Traffic is actually great! Everything happens for a reason! This is corrosive because it requires you to lie to yourself, and your nervous system knows.

What Jeremiah does is different, and Dr. K names it precisely: he adds context. He zooms out.

When Jeremiah is stuck in traffic, he doesn't tell himself traffic is fine. He acknowledges that traffic sucks. And then he zooms out to the frame around it: I have a car. I'm on my way to a job I love. I woke up this morning.

"Traffic's always going to suck. I don't want you to fake being happy. Yeah, it sucks, but it's okay that it sucks."

The "Obstacle Is the Way" Framing and Working Through Karma

There's a related idea that came up around Ryan Holiday's The Obstacle Is the Way and something Dr. K frames in karmic terms, which is worth unpacking regardless of whether you hold any spiritual beliefs.

Your past choices have purchased certain consequences. Not working out has purchased a body that feels heavy in the morning. Not sleeping has purchased cognitive fog. Not practicing social skills has purchased awkward interactions. The bill comes due, and there is no way to avoid paying it — only ways to delay it, which usually inflate the cost.

"You shouldn't run away from things that suck. You have to get to the light at the end of the tunnel. That means going through the tunnel."

Jeremiah's example: he broke his foot and had to borrow money from his mother. It genuinely sucked. He didn't pretend otherwise. But he also didn't treat it as a catastrophe to escape — he moved through it. A month and a half later, he booked his biggest commercial.

The important caveat: this framing can tip into something unhealthy if it becomes an excuse to manufacture suffering or to pathologize comfort. "Things are hard, so I must be growing" is not always true. But "things are hard, and avoiding them makes them harder" — that part holds.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to implement an entire book at once. You read something good, feel motivated, and attempt to overhaul your behavior overnight. You burn out within days and conclude self-improvement "doesn't work for you."

Using information as a substitute for action. "How do I know if I'm really tired or dopamineA neurotransmitter the brain uses primarily to reinforce behavior, drive motivation, and generate craving — not, as commonly assumed, simply to produce pleasure.Full definition →-tired?" is a question that feels useful but leads nowhere. What difference would the answer make? You still have to act. The question is a detour.

Confusing "zooming out" with toxic positivity. Genuine reframing acknowledges the bad thing is bad. If you skip that step and go straight to "everything is fine," you're lying to yourself and your nervous system will call it.

Going too hard after a long break. Whether it's the gym, a reading habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition →, or a morning routine — returning at full intensity after a long gap almost guarantees you won't return the day after. The goal of your first session back is to be able to show up the next day.

Over-identifying with outcomes to decide whether effort is worth it. If your brain concludes that working out won't solve your financial problems, it will make you feel tired when you try to work out. Recognize this pattern. Effort has value even when it doesn't solve everything.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Choose one thing from the last book or piece of content that genuinely stuck with you. Not a list — one thing. Decide that this week, that's the idea you're practicing. Watch for chances to apply it in real situations.

  2. Saturate your environment with whatever you're working on. Lock screen. Sticky note on the bathroom mirror. An index card on your desk. The goal is several exposures before you've left the house, so the idea becomes automatic rather than effortful to remember.

  3. When you feel tired, pause before defaulting. Ask not am I tired? but what am I about to do about that? If the answer is "reach for my phone" or "lie down," recognize you may be training your brain to use tiredness as a lever. Try doing one small thing anyway — even five minutes.

  4. Practice zooming out on something that annoyed you today. Name the annoying thing honestly. Don't minimize it. Then expand the frame: what's the context around it? What else is true at the same time? Notice whether this changes the weight of the thing without requiring you to pretend it's fine.

  5. When you're returning to a habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → you've dropped, start insultingly small. One set at the gym. One page of the book. Ten minutes of work. The session's only job is to make it easy to show up the next day.

  6. Audit one label you carry. "I'm a [job title]," "I'm a [relationship role]," "I'm a [identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → category]."
