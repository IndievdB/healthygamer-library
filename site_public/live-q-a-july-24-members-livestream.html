The Difference Between Observing Yourself and Obeying Yourself

Attachment Styles: Compassion Without Excuse

Understanding someone's attachment style — anxious, avoidant, disorganized — is not a tool for excusing their behavior. It's a tool for understanding it, and more practically, for knowing how to communicate with them effectively.

Attachment styles create inclinations and reactive patterns. They don't control actions. The distinction matters enormously:

  • Not fair to blame someone for having an anxious attachment style
  • Fair to hold them accountable for the actions that style produces, because impulses can be restrained

The practical framework for relating to a partner with attachment issues:

  1. Name what's happening without shaming: "When I don't text you back within an hour, I notice you become upset and pull away. I don't always have the ability to text back within an hour. I don't know how to make this work — what do you think we should do?"
  2. Give a few genuine opportunities to change. First time: benefit of the doubt. Second time: still possible. Third time: "I believe you want to change, but I'm not seeing a plan or investment in change."
  3. Distinguish between wanting to change and acting to change. "I'll just be different" is a belief. Therapy, couples counseling, specific behavioral commitments — those are actions.
  4. Adjust your language to their style — using the word "trust" with an anxiously attached person can trigger a cascade of exactly the behaviors you're trying to reduce.

Forgiveness and accountability are not opposites. You can forgive someone and still hold a boundary. The error is turning understanding into excuse — handing someone's ego an out so they never have to own the impact of their behavior.

"Don't use it as an excuse. Don't absolve them of responsibility because of their attachment style."

What to hold people responsible for is often not the moment of worst behavior, but the choices made before that — whether they were putting in effort to prevent it.

Attention Is the Only Mechanism Behind Attachment and Letting Go

Getting over a crush (or an ex) is not about time passing. It is about where your attention goes.

Here's the mechanism: some brief sensory input — a glance, a conversation — triggers mental engagement. The mind starts spending time on this person. Every additional cycle of thinking compounds the attachment. By the time you've been rejected, you may have built a substantial mental structure around someone you've spoken to twice.

"Attention is the fertilizer for thoughts. The more that we give our attention to a particular thought, the more the thought grows."

Moving on, by this model, is literally nothing more than attention migrating elsewhere. Someone who has moved on simply doesn't think about the person anymore. Someone who hasn't is feeding the attachment through:

  • Checking their social media
  • Rehashing memories internally
  • Comparing new people to this person

The same mechanism explains radicalization, conspiracy theory entrenchment, and delusional thinking — sustained attention to a thought makes it grow, regardless of what the thought is.

Practical application: - Reduce sensory input (block them, stop checking their profiles — not out of hostility but as hygiene) - Redirect internal attention — this is the one people miss. Blocking them while lying in bed thinking about them for three hours a night accomplishes nothing - Rebound relationships and new hobbies work not because they're distractions in a shallow sense, but because they physically occupy the attentional bandwidth that was feeding the old attachment - Video games help temporarily but don't build a sustainable replacement because when the game ends, the thoughts return to a vacuum

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your internal instruments for one decision you're stuck on. Write down separately: What is my Manas (emotional, reactive self) saying? What is my Ahamkara (ego, identity-protection) saying? What is my Buddhi (rational faculty) saying? Are any old samskaras — fears from past experience — active here? You don't have to follow the output. Just observe it and notice which voice has been doing most of the talking.

  2. When you hit recursion in meditation, treat it as the signal to shift — not a problem. If you find yourself observing the observer observing the observer, stop. Move deliberately to a simple attentional anchor: the tip of the nose, a mantra repeated silently, or the physical sensation of your heartbeat. Do this shift consciously rather than continuing to recurse.

  3. Build a graduated meditation practice to develop attentional strength. Start with Om chanting at audible volume. When comfortable, shift attention from the sound to the physical vibration. Then localize it — where in the body do you feel it? Then reduce the volume gradually. Eventually practice silent mental Om. Each step increases the "weight" your attention can carry, which is what makes it possible to stay present under stress.

  5. If you are trying to get over a crush or ex, audit both inputs. Treat both as variables to reduce, not through suppression, but by building activities that genuinely occupy your attention — ones that require real presence (sport, conversation, creative work, a pet, a new skill).

  6. In a relationship where attachment style is causing friction, try the three-step frame: Name your experience without blame ("when X happens, I experience Y"). Describe what you don't see a path through ("I don't know how to make this work while this dynamic continues"). Then ask a genuine question ("What do you think we should do?"). Avoid words that trigger the specific attachment pattern — learn what activates their defensive response and route around it without abandoning the substance of what needs to be said.

  7. When someone promises to change, ask what the plan is — not just the intention. "I'll do better" is not a plan. Ask: Are you willing to see a therapist? Can we agree on a specific behavior to practice this week? Is there something concrete I can do to help? Evaluate change by observable actions over the next few weeks, not by how sincerely the intention was stated.
