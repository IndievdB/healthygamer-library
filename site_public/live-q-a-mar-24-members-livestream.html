Values, Desires, Duty, and Shoulds — Four Different Things

Most people conflate these four, which is exactly why life feels like a tug-of-war. Here is how to pull them apart.

Values are internal principles — not specific outcomes. "I care about personal freedom" is a value. "I want to buy this specific car" is not. Values tend to be abstract, durable, and come from within. A quick diagnostic question: is this something you want, or something you care about? The two words exist for a reason. You might want peanuts on a flight, but you care about being loved. The scale and abstraction are completely different.

Dharma (Duty) is an obligation you have toward others. It is usually a loss for you in some material sense. Picking up the phone at 2 a.m. when a friend is in crisis, letting someone stay in your home when it costs you your personal space — these are dharmic acts. There can be internal satisfaction in doing your duty, and your values and duty can align, but they are still usefully separate. Dharma faces outward; values face inward.

Shoulds are the trickiest category because they feel internal but aren't. The diagnostic for a should: notice internal resistance. People living a life of shoulds often accumulate external accomplishment but carry little internal joy. They have a lot to show for themselves and feel hollow. By contrast, living in line with your Dharma tends to give you energy rather than drain it.

The most common trap is the should vs. want war — and it's a war nobody wins. You decide to eat the salad (should), feel deprived, crack and eat the burger (want), feel guilty, decide to eat the salad tomorrow. The cycle repeats indefinitely. Neither side produces lasting contentment because you're operating entirely within those two categories. Values and Dharma are a different axis altogether — one where you can make an external sacrifice and feel genuinely good, or follow your internal compass and feel genuinely good.

To find your actual values: ask yourself why you got into the things that now feel like obligation. If the answer is "to make my parents happy," you need a different reason — or it won't sustain you. The internal driver, once found, makes everything easier.

Raga and Dvesha — Noticing Attraction and Repulsion

Raga means attraction; dvesha means repulsion. These are not the same as vasanas (conditioned habits) — they are simply the mind's moment-to-moment pulls toward and away from things.

The practical value of this framework is observation. Your mind is going to be attracted to some things and repelled by others. That's just what minds do. Noticing that this is happening — without immediately acting on it — is most of the work.

Discipline is inversely related to the strength of your raga and dvesha. The more powerfully you are pulled toward or pushed away from something, the more willpower managing it requires. People we think of as "disciplined" tend to have a calmer relationship with these impulses — not the absence of them, but less automatic obedience to them.

There is often an evolutionary logic behind these pulls (we're repelled by the smell of infection for good reason), but evolutionary logic doesn't always serve your actual goals. A doctor needs to treat infected feet regardless of the dvesha that arises.

Faith, Tantra, and Why the Path Has No Instruction Manual

A member asked a sharp, honest question: you've told us all the things we can't do in Tantra — can't get a guru online, can't make up a mantra, can't work from an internet tutorial — so what exactly are we supposed to do?

The question itself is the point.

In the West, the dominant assumption is: if I understand all the steps and make a good plan, the outcome will follow. This assumption breaks spiritual practice specifically because it creates dependence on the logical mind. When you're given a clear roadmap and it doesn't work exactly as described, you either blame the method or blame yourself and quit. Even when it works at first, any obstacle breaks the chain because your motivation was always conditional on the plan holding.

"When you are able to commit to something without seeing any benefit, without understanding what's going on, without basically any kind of assurances — when you say I'm going to do this with 100% effort even though I don't know what happens — that's when you are truly in control."

That unconditional commitment is not just a prerequisite attitude — it is the practice. It is also why the tradition is deliberately opaque. An empty, expectation-free mind is the starting condition, not the destination.

This is also why predatory gurus exist and why they succeed. The same faith-based openness that serious practice requires makes people vulnerable to people who exploit devotion.

The actionable version of all this: start anyway, with whatever is available. Vajrayana Buddhism (Buddhist Tantra) is the most accessible entry point — temples exist, teachers are findable, the tradition is relatively open. Going to India, visiting ashrams, wandering in the Himalayas — these are real options, and cheaper than most people assume. On Ashrams, Gurus, and Doing It Alone

You do not need an ashram or a guru. All spiritual work is internal — no one else can do it for you, and your consciousness is sufficient equipment wherever you are. Simple practices like Om chanting, Vipassana, devotional practice (bhakti), or basic breath-awareness meditation can produce profound results if maintained consistently.

What ashrams and gurus do is accelerate the process, not enable it. An ashram provides structure and communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → (sangha) — it removes the logistical friction that your mind uses to avoid sitting down twice a day. A genuine guru makes the process dramatically easier. But neither is a requirement.

The honest reason to go to an ashram is that your mind won't let you maintain steady daily practice on your own. If that's true, the ashram solves a real problem. If you can maintain practice independently, you don't need it — though it would still help.

The Mandala and Practicing Non-Attachment

The mandala practice is about deliberately creating something you feel attached to and then releasing it. The value of the exercise depends entirely on your relationship to what you're giving up.

Throwing away a coloring book page you finished in 20 minutes is no sacrifice — you weren't attached to it. Discarding something you spent real time and care building, something that reflects labor you want to hold onto, is the actual practice. The mind has a strong drive to keep the fruits of its effort. Building that muscle of release — practicing it in small, concrete ways — is one path toward developing vairagyaThe Sanskrit term for detachment — specifically, detachment from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → (dispassion or non-attachment), which is a genuine tool rather than a philosophical abstraction.

Alternative: giving up something you genuinely enjoy for a sustained period (giving up ice cream for a year, for example) achieves a similar effect through a different route.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing "want" and "care about." These words exist because they describe different things. Using them interchangeably is how you end up building a life around desires while calling them values.
  • Treating "should" as neutral. Shoulds feel internal because they've been repeated long enough. They are almost always external in origin. The sign that you're living by shoulds is accomplishment without joy, plus constant internal resistance.
  • Thinking the should/want war is winnable. It isn't. There is no sustainable equilibrium between those two forces. The solution is to move to a different axis entirely.
  • Treating desire as the enemy. Suppression doesn't work — desire returns stronger. Control and mindful enjoyment are the actual alternatives.
  • Mistaking obliteration for enjoyment. Eating mindlessly while watching something is not enjoying food — it's maximizing dopamine delivery. Real enjoyment requires presence.
  • Expecting a roadmap for spiritual practice. If you need a guaranteed plan before committing, you're not ready — and the plan itself will undermine the practice by making your commitment conditional.
  • Assuming ashrams and gurus are required. They accelerate things enormously, but making them prerequisites is a way to avoid starting.
  • Interpreting setbacks on the spiritual path as failure. The teacher who says no, the year that seems to produce nothing, the practice that feels stuck — these are frequently more formative than easy progress.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the "want vs. care" diagnostic. Sit quietly and ask yourself two separate questions about something you're pursuing: Do I want this? and Do I care about this? Notice whether the felt sense of your answer differs. Write down what you actually care about, not what you think you should care about.

  2. Find your original reason. For any area of life where you feel burnt out or going through the motions — career, relationship, creative work — ask yourself why you started. Write the honest answer. If the answer is "to make someone else happy" or "because I thought I should," that's information you need.

  3. Practice the should audit. Pick one major "should" in your life. Trace it back: who first told you this? How old were you? How many times has it been reinforced? Notice whether it actually aligns with what you care about, or whether it's a vasana in disguise.

  4. Eat one meal mindfully this week. No screen, no podcast, no distraction. Chew thoroughly. Eat slowly. Notice what the food actually tastes like. Observe whether you eat less and feel more satisfied than usual.

  5. Do one thing from your values rather than your desires this week. Identify something you care about (not want) and do one concrete action in service of it. Notice the quality of the satisfaction afterward compared to satisfying a desire.

  6. Try the mandala exercise correctly. Spend at least 30–60 minutes creating something by hand — drawing, building, writing — that you care about the outcome of. Then deliberately release it: throw it away, give it to someone anonymously, or destroy it. Notice the resistance. That resistance is the thing you're training.

  7. Explore Vajrayana Buddhism if you're drawn toward tantric practice. Look for a local center or teacher. Treat showing up as the whole practice — not acquiring information, but putting yourself in proximity.

  8. Consider a short ashram stay as a concrete medium-term goal if consistent daily practice is genuinely difficult. Research costs — many ashrams in India charge little to nothing for sincere practitioners.

  9. Notice raga and dvesha in one interaction today. Pick any experience — a meal, a conversation, a task — and simply observe when your mind pulls toward something and when it pushes away. Don't act on it either way. Just notice that the pull exists.
