Summary

TL;DR — When someone hurts you and you love them, the mind's first instinct is to make excuses for them. That instinct feels protective, but it's actually what keeps you trapped.

Most people assume that staying stuck in a painful relationship is about weakness, denial, or low self-worth. The real mechanism is subtler and more ironic: the very thing you do to protect your sense of value — excusing the person who hurt you — is what prevents you from ever healing or moving on. Understanding that loop is the first step to breaking it.

The Psychological Trap: Why We Make Excuses for People Who Hurt Us

When someone mistreats you, your mind is immediately confronted with a brutal question: Why would they treat me that way? Two possible answers sit in front of you:

  1. Something is wrong with them.
  2. Something is wrong with me — I'm not worth being treated well.

The second answer is so painful that the mind almost automatically reaches for the first one. It starts constructing reasons: they had a difficult childhood, they're under stress, that's just how they are. In the short term, this feels like compassion. It might even look like compassion from the outside. But what it's actually doing is protecting your self-worth by offloading the weight of the hurt onto an explanation.

The problem is that once you hand someone an excuse, no one is doing anything wrong. And if no one is doing anything wrong, there's nothing to address, nothing to forgive, and no reason for anything to change. You're left with a wound that has no name and no cause — just a vague feeling that something is off.

"You're kind of stuck in this limbo where there's pain that you defend against, but you hold no one responsible, and therefore you can't forgive — because they didn't do anything wrong."

This is the trap in its purest form. Pain without accountability becomes impossible to process.

Why Love Makes the Trap Worse

The stronger you feel about someone, the harder this becomes. When someone you deeply love hurts you, the pain of accepting that they did something hurtful is itself almost unbearable. So the more you love someone, the more motivated you are to find an excuse for their behavior.

The love doesn't make you blind to the hurt — it makes you allergic to holding them responsible for it. And the more excuses you make:

  • The less you require them to change
  • The more the behavior repeats
  • The more hurt accumulates under the surface
  • The more your heart quietly closes off while your mouth keeps saying "it's fine"

Week after week, the pattern repeats. Another incident, another excuse, no forgiveness, no accountability, no real conversation. From the outside it might look like patience or generosity. From the inside, it's a slow build of resentment with nowhere to go.

Why Forgiveness Requires Blame First

Here's the counterintuitive core of this whole idea: you cannot forgive something you've pretended didn't happen.

Most people think of blame and forgiveness as opposites — that assigning blame is what keeps people stuck and that forgiveness is the way out. But that gets the sequence completely wrong.

Forgiveness is not the same as excusing. Excusing says they didn't really do anything wrong. Forgiveness says they did do something wrong, and I'm choosing to move through it anyway. One of those closes the loop. The other leaves it permanently open.

"If they didn't do anything wrong, then what is there to forgive?"

So the path forward, counterintuitively, starts with blame — with clearly naming what happened and who did it. Not to punish, not to ruin the relationship, but because you cannot process something you haven't accurately labeled. Once responsibility is assigned and accepted, then forgiveness becomes possible. And forgiveness — real forgiveness, not excuse-making — is what actually releases you.

The sequence is: 1. Assign responsibility — name what happened and who is accountable for it 2. Accept it — stop defending against the reality of it 3. Forgive — choose to move through it, not around it

This applies to how other people treat you, but it also applies to yourself.

The Flip Side: When You're Too Hard on Yourself

The same loop plays out in reverse for how many people treat themselves. If excusing others is the trap in relationships, over-blaming yourself is the parallel trap in your internal life.

Some people are very willing to hold themselves responsible — but they skip the forgiveness step entirely. They beat themselves up, commit to doing better, and beat themselves up again when they fall short. The blame lands, but it never resolves into anything useful.

The same three-step sequence applies here too. Yes, you did something wrong. Hold yourself accountable for that clearly. And then — crucially — forgive yourself. Not as a way of avoiding responsibility, but as the act that actually allows you to do better next time. You can't genuinely move forward while you're still mentally punishing yourself for the last thing you did.

"Yeah, you did screw up — but that's okay, because no one's perfect and you're going to do your best next time."

The goal is to be in your own corner. Willing to see clearly what you did, and also willing to give yourself another shot.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing excuse-making with compassion. Understanding why someone behaves a certain way — their upbringing, their stress, their struggles — is genuinely valuable context. But context doesn't erase impact. You can have empathy for why someone does something and still name the fact that it hurt you. Mistaking excuse-making for empathy is one of the most common ways this trap stays invisible.

Thinking blame means the relationship is over. Holding someone responsible doesn't require ending the relationship or hating them. You can love someone and still say, clearly and plainly, "What you did wasn't okay, and it hurt me." In fact, that's often the only thing that gives the relationship a real chance of getting better.

Waiting until you feel ready to forgive before you assign blame. It's tempting to think you'll naturally arrive at forgiveness once enough time has passed. But forgiveness isn't a feeling that shows up on its own — it's an act that follows the prior step of honest accountability. Skipping straight to "I'm over it" without ever naming what happened is just a slower version of the same avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition →.

Believing that feeling the hurt makes you weak. The instinct to protect yourself from the full weight of being hurt is understandable. But the hurt doesn't disappear when you protect yourself from it — it just goes somewhere you can't see or address it. Letting yourself actually feel it, sitting with the discomfort, is what makes it possible to move through it rather than around it indefinitely.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name what happened, plainly. The next time someone close to you does something that hurts, resist the urge to immediately explain it away. Before reaching for a reason, just describe the event to yourself in simple terms: What did they do? How did it make me feel? Get the facts of it clear in your mind before you do anything else.

  2. Separate understanding from excusing. You're allowed to understand someone's background and still hold them responsible. Practice saying (at least to yourself): "I understand why they might act this way — and what they did still hurt me." Both things can be true at once.

  3. Have the honest conversation. If someone you care about has been doing something that hurts, try naming it directly without softening it into nothing: "I care about you, and what happened wasn't okay. It hurt me." You don't have to be harsh — just honest.

  4. Practice the three-step sequence on yourself. Pick one recent thing you've been beating yourself up about. Clearly name what you did and accept that it happened (step one and two). Then actively give yourself permission to move forward — not by pretending it didn't happen, but by deciding that it doesn't define what you do next (step three).

  5. Notice the shell. When you catch yourself rationalizing someone's behavior or defending against a feeling, ask: What am I protecting myself from right now? What would I have to feel if I dropped this explanation? You don't have to immediately drop it — just becoming aware of it is enough to start.

  6. Sit with the discomfort. Deliberately let yourself feel the hurt, even briefly, instead of reaching for an explanation. Feelings don't expand indefinitely when you let them in — they move through. The ones you keep avoiding are the ones that stay.
