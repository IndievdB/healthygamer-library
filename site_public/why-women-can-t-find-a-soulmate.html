Summary

TL;DR — Finding the right person is not the hard part of relationships; building something real with them is. If your mental diet is warping your expectations and your attachment patterns are making you fall in love with fantasies, no real human can meet your standards.

This lesson works through a letter from a self-aware woman who is lonely, cycling through limerence and anxious attachment, and worried she's sliding toward a kind of "femcel" worldview. The core insight isn't about dating tactics — it's about what you bring to the table: the expectations you've built, the content you've consumed, and whether you're even capable of recognizing love when a real, flawed person offers it.

Finding "The One" Is the Wrong Goal

Most people frame their relationship problem as a search problem. Where are all the good men (or women)? As if locating the right person automatically produces the right relationship.

That framing is wrong.

Meeting someone is maybe five minutes of your life — a brief encounter, a match on an app, a conversation at a party. The relationship that follows takes years. Treating the search as the hard part and the relationship as the inevitable reward is like believing that if you just find the right seed, the tree will grow itself.

"Meeting your soulmate does not mean happily ever after. Happily ever after is what you build with the soulmate."

Soulmates may be real. But the work — the growth, the translation of love between two different people, the becoming — happens after you meet. Obsessing over the search while neglecting the skills required to actually sustain a relationship is why so many people feel perpetually stuck.

Your Mind Is What You Feed It

There's a useful analogy here: what you put into your body determines the health of your body. The same is true of your mind.

If your mental diet consists almost entirely of a narrow band of content — femcel forums, incel-adjacent communities, Jungian trope analyses of romcoms, relationship venting spaces — your mind takes on the shape of that diet. Not because any single piece of content is evil, but because variety matters and selection bias is severe.

The people writing bitter essays about romance archetypes are not a random sample of humanity. The people who dominate relationship advice spaces online are not representative of the people who are quietly in happy relationships. Those people are out living their lives. What you see on the internet is a skewed, algorithm-amplified slice — and the algorithm actively makes it worse over time, replacing more of your mental landscape with more of the same.

"Imagine a world where every time you go to a fast food restaurant, half the grocery stores in your area get replaced with fast food restaurants. What would your diet become over time?"

This isn't a "just go touch grass" dismissal. The mechanism matters: processed food tastes good because it's engineered to. Internet content tastes good because it's engineered to emotionally resonate. Incel and femcel spaces aren't popular because they're accurate — they're popular because they feel validating. They give you the calories of emotional recognition without the nutrition of actual insight or growth.

The question to ask yourself when you're consuming something online: What part of me is this feeding? And if I keep feeding it, will it grow or shrink?

The Limerence Trap

Limerence is an intense, almost obsessive attraction to another person — but what's really happening is that you've fallen in love with a fantasy, not a human being. It typically lasts one to seven years, and when reality eventually fails to match the fantasy, the limerence transfers to a new person, and the cycle repeats.

Where does it come from? The evidence points to a specific childhood pattern: an anxious attachment style (from parents who didn't reliably meet your emotional needs) combined with one crucial experience — a brief, intense encounter with someone who did give you exactly what you needed. A coach, a teacher, a parent's friend. Someone who wasn't your primary caregiver but who showed you, for a moment, that the kind of love you craved was real and possible.

That moment creates a template in the brain: there exists, somewhere out there, a person who can give me everything I need — and because the interaction was brief and distant, the fantasy stays pristine. You go looking for that person for the rest of your life.

The cruel irony of limerence is directional: the closer you get to the object of your limerence, the worse things feel. Reality can never compete with the template. So the limerence eventually detaches and relocates.

"You're looking for true love because you felt it once in your life. I would not blame you for that for a moment. But woe is the person on the receiving end of all of your hopes and dreams."

No normal, flawed, real human being can compete with that internal figure. Not because they're insufficient — but because the competition was never fair to begin with.

The Gap Between Perception and Reality

Anxious attachment also distorts how you read what's actually happening around you. When you're primed to expect abandonment, you interpret neutral behavior as rejection. When you've built a sophisticated internal model of what love should look like (assembled from romcoms, trope analyses, and years of emotionally resonant internet content), you may not recognize love when a real person tries to offer it.

Love doesn't arrive in one universal package. Some people express it by showing up. Some by acts of service. Some through words. If your template requires one specific delivery method and the person in front of you is offering it a different way, you'll feel unloved — even when you're not.

"I cannot send you love — I have to convey love in a package. And how you interpret that package matters."

This is why the work of a relationship is translation. Two people with different emotional languages, different histories, different ways of giving and receiving care — learning to understand what the other person is actually trying to do.

That work can't begin if one person is waiting for a fantasy to arrive.

Common Pitfalls

  • Conflating "finding the right person" with "having a good relationship." These are related but distinct. The second requires skills, effort, and time that have nothing to do with the search.

  • Taking fiction as data. Jungian analysis of romcom tropes, or any analysis of fictional romance, is analyzing fiction. There's value in understanding archetypes, but it's not a guide to real relationships. The selection bias in which stories get written and told skews heavily toward dramatic suffering.

  • Mistaking the algorithm for reality. If your feed is full of misogyny, failed relationships, and bitter content, that's not an accurate map of the dating world — it's an artifact of what you've clicked on. Most people in healthy relationships aren't posting about it.

  • Black-and-white attribution. "He left me because I was depressed" may be accurate. It may also be incomplete — the leaving may have been about a pattern of disengagement from treatment, not the depression itself. Both can be true simultaneously, and collapsing it to one explanation closes off the parts you might actually be able to do something about.

  • Setting telepathic standards. Expecting a partner to intuit exactly what love means to you, in exactly the form you need it, without ever communicating that clearly, is a setup for constant disappointment.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your mental diet. For one week, notice — without judgment — what content you're consuming and what emotion it leaves you with. Are you angrier? More hopeless? More anxious? More validated but also more isolated? The goal isn't to delete everything; it's to see what's actually going into the machine.

  2. Deliberately diversify. If your feed is dominated by relationship content, gender discourse, or any single emotional register, actively seek out unrelated spaces. Cooking communities, gardening forums, craft subreddits — places where people are focused on making things rather than processing grievance. Notice how your thinking shifts.

  3. Write out what love actually looks like in practice. Not in feelings or abstractions — in observable behaviors. If someone loves you, what specifically are they doing? How long does it reasonably take for someone to demonstrate that? This converts an impossible emotional standard into something a real person could actually be evaluated on.

  4. Trace your limerence template. If you notice a pattern of intense attraction that fades when you get closer, ask: who in my early life briefly gave me exactly what I needed? What did that feel like? How much of what I'm looking for in a partner is actually that person or that feeling?

  5. Before ending a relationship or writing someone off, ask: is this person trying to give me something I'm not receiving — and if so, is the gap about them or about translation? This isn't about tolerating mistreatment. It's about distinguishing "this person doesn't love me" from "this person loves me in a language I haven't learned to read yet."

  6. If you have an anxious attachment style, work on it before scaling up the search. Therapy, attachment-focused reading, self-reflection. Not because it's your fault that relationships haven't worked — but because the work you can actually do is on yourself, and that's also where the most leverage is.
