Summary

TL;DR — If no diagnosis has ever quite fit you, the problem probably isn't that you haven't found the right label yet. The problem is that you're being looked at through the wrong lens entirely.

Most people who spend years searching for a diagnosis that "clicks" aren't failing to find the right box — they're dealing with something that predates and underlies all those boxes. These are called transdiagnostic factors: psychological patterns that don't belong to any single disorder but quietly generate symptoms across many of them at once. Understanding this changes everything about how you approach getting better.

How Perfectionism Actually Works (and Hurts You)

Perfectionism isn't just having high standards. It's a specific cluster of four interacting patterns:

  1. Inflexible standards — The bar doesn't adjust based on circumstances. An A-minus registers as a failure, not a strong performance.
  2. Raising standards — The moment you succeed, the target moves. You go to the gym once and immediately decide you should go three times a week. This means the only available outcomes are disappointment or barely-adequate relief. You structurally eliminate the experience of exceeding your own expectations.
  3. Underestimating coping ability — Perfectionists tend to believe that if anything goes wrong, everything will fall apart. This belief isn't accurate, but it's self-reinforcing: because the stakes feel catastrophically high, every standard must be maintained perfectly, which increases stress and makes failure feel more devastating when it comes.
  4. Double standards — You'd tell a friend that an A-minus is great. You wouldn't extend that same grace to yourself.

The person who infuriates you — the one taking a gap year, not planning, "figuring it out as they go," somehow landing good opportunities anyway — isn't just lucky. They have flexible standards. They can tolerate uncertainty. They're not locked in place by the fear of imperfection. That ease isn't laziness; it's the absence of what's keeping you stuck.

The practical move here is not to abandon your standards, but to make them movable. If lowering or adjusting your standard feels genuinely threatening — if you resist it because you're afraid you'll stop achieving without it — that's precisely the signal that the standard is controlling you rather than serving you.

On the "raising standards" trap specifically: when you do something well, don't immediately convert the next step into a should. Keep the target where it was. If you worked out once this week, the goal for next week is once. If you feel motivated and go twice, you've exceeded your own expectations — which is an entirely different emotional experience than barely meeting them. That difference matters for both self-esteemSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → and sustained motivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition →.

How Rumination Hijacks Your Brain

RuminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → is often mistaken for thinking. It feels like you're working on a problem. You're not.

Here's what's actually happening. You take one unit of emotional damage — someone rejects you, something goes wrong at work, you make a mistake. A non-ruminator processes it and moves on. A ruminator starts cycling it, and each cycle amplifies the emotion. What started as mild rejection grows into I'm fundamentally unlovable, because the brain keeps re-experiencing and re-generating the emotional signal.

The second layer of damage is that ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → actively interferes with problem-solving. This is counterintuitive — it feels like sustained thinking should eventually produce a solution. But the style of thinking in ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → isn't analytical; it's abstract and generalizing. Specific situations ("I didn't date in high school because my parents were controlling") get transformed over repeated cycles into abstract self-judgments ("I am a loser"). And abstract conclusions are not actionable. You can't do five push-ups to address "I am a loser." So after hours of thinking, you've generated no solution — and you conclude the problem must be unsolvable.

"It is that the ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → is impairing problem-solving capability. And since it impairs problem-solving, you conclude it's unsolvable."

The third layer — and perhaps the most insidious — is reduced sensitivity to changes in context. Once you've concluded something is hopeless, your brain stops updating that assessment when circumstances actually improve. The video game analogy captures this perfectly: your team's odds shift from 10% to 20% after a good play, but the person who gave up at minute seven has been spiraling for eight minutes and cannot register that the situation has changed. Their model of reality is stuck. This is why people who ruminate often say "I did everything right — I went to therapy, hit the gym, changed my habits — and nothing got better." Their circumstances may genuinely have improved while their felt sense of the situation did not update.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your standards for flexibility. Pick one area where you have a recurring sense of failure. Ask: Is the standard itself fixed? What would it look like to shift it slightly, not abandon it? Notice the resistance you feel to doing this — that resistance is diagnostic.

  2. Stop raising the bar on success. The next time you hit a goal, deliberately keep the target where it was for one more cycle. Treat any extra effort as a bonus, not a new floor.

  3. Reconstruct your coping history. Write down three to five times in your life where something went wrong and you still managed to handle it — imperfectly, but adequately. Your brain will try to surface counterexamples first. Let it, then keep going until you find the times you adapted.

  4. Apply the double-standard test. When you catch yourself criticizing your own performance, ask: what would I say to a friend in exactly this situation? Then say that to yourself instead. It will feel wrong. Do it anyway.

  5. Separate emotional processing from problem-solving completely. When you're in emotional distress, give yourself explicit permission to not solve anything. Do something that helps you regulate — a walk, meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition →, a game, whatever works. Only once you're in a genuinely calmer state should you sit down and think concretely about what to do.

  6. Move from abstract to concrete when problem-solving. Catch abstract self-judgments ("I'm a failure," "I'll always be alone") and convert them into situational descriptions. What specifically happened? What context contributed to it? What is one concrete thing you could do — not to fix the whole problem, but to slightly improve your odds?

  7. Look at the context behind your core beliefsDeep convictions about yourself — statements like "I am unlovable" or "I will be alone forever" — that your brain forms by drawing conclusions from repeated experiences.Full definition →. If you carry a persistent negative belief about yourself, trace it back to the specific situations that built it. What was outside your control at the time? This isn't about making excuses — it's about updating a belief that was formed under specific, often unfavorable conditions and may no longer accurately describe your present circumstances.
