Summary

TL;DR — Your dharma doesn't stay found; it slips away and has to be reclaimed daily. Ego, expectation, and negative momentum are the forces that corrupt it, and understanding the mechanics behind each one gives you a way to fight back.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Ryan ("Simply"), the world-record holder for Super Mario 64 120-star speedrunning. Ryan dropped out of a master's program in computer science to chase a goal he'd been circling for eight years — the world record. He got it. And now he's lost. What unfolds is a surprisingly precise mapping of why meaning gets corrupted, how negative momentum builds, what jealousy is actually signaling, and how meditation can be a practical tool rather than a spiritual decoration.

Dharma Is Not a Destination — It's a Daily Practice

Dharma is roughly your duty, your calling, the thing you feel most aligned with. But there's a trap in how most people think about it: they treat it like something you find once and then have. Ryan's experience dismantles that.

He dropped out of school because something in his chest said this is what I have to do. For months he streamed, felt in tune, entered flow states, and was genuinely joyful. Then things got harder, his viewership dipped, and rent became a concern — and suddenly the clarity was gone.

The deeper point: dharma is not a prize you win. It's more like a signal you're always trying to tune back into. Even people who seem to have "found" their purpose struggle with this every single day. The practice is returning to it, not arriving at it.

"It's something that is like kind of an ongoing task. I struggle every day with finding my dharma."

The Two Forces That Corrupt Dharma

Ego

When Ryan is playing well and feeling aligned, he describes it in the third person — talking about the game, about what Mario can do, about the run. The second things go wrong, the language shifts to first person: viewers, his numbers, how he compares to other runners.

That shift is ego entering. Comparison is almost always its signature. You can study another runner's strategy without ego — looking at their approach as its own thing, useful information. Comparison is different: it's setting two things side by side in a way that produces a self-assessment. And that self-assessment, when ego is involved, is almost never accurate or useful.

The antidote to ego is practice — redirecting attention back to the task. When you're focused on the thing itself, there's no room for the comparisons that feed ego. Ryan describes this naturally: when he's playing well, he's just listening to the game's sounds, locked in. That's not an accident.

Expectation

Expectation is sneakier than ego because it disguises itself as reasonable planning.

Ryan describes a perfect example: you get through the hardest section of a run cleanly, and then you think, okay, this next part is easy. The moment you classify something as easy, you've created a trap. Failing something "easy" hits harder than failing something hard, and the emotional cost derails the next several attempts.

The same dynamic shows up in competitive games. Going 2-0 in a lane and then getting ganked produces more tilt than going 0-2 and getting ganked again — because when you're ahead, you expect to win. The expectation, not the actual game state, is what tilts you.

"Expectation is the enemy. You can literally train yourself to expect less."

This isn't resignation. It's precision. The world-record holder, when asked how much he thinks about the world record while playing, says: not at all. That sounds contradictory until you understand that the outcome and the task are separate things. You can have a goal without letting it colonize your attention while you work.

Karma as Momentum — Understanding How Small Thoughts Compound

Momentum is something most people observe but can't explain. The useful reframe is karma — cause and effect, seeds and fruit.

Every small action plants a seed. A bad night of sleep makes focus harder tomorrow, which makes performance worse, which makes you more likely to compare yourself to others, which makes you feel worse, which makes you reach for the nachos at 2 a.m., which makes sleep worse. It compounds.

The same is true in reverse. Doing your laundry plants a seed of "I can handle things." That makes it slightly easier to sit down and work on something that matters.

The critical insight is that the cycle starts with a subtle thought — so small you barely register it. You're not choosing to spiral. You're just letting a small possibility sit there, and then it grows. The way you interrupt it is by catching it before it has mass. Concentration practices — focusing your attention on a sound, a sensation, a breath — work precisely because a focused mind cannot simultaneously feed a growing negative thought. You're not suppressing it; you're starving it of attention.

"A tiny thought. You give in to that possibility, and then that thought breeds another thought, which breeds another thought, and then you find yourself two hours later covered in crumbs."

Positive karma follows the same logic. Choosing to go to sleep at a reasonable hour, or putting some vegetables on the nachos (or skipping the nachos), plants a seed you won't consciously notice. The yield comes three days later. The hard part is that your mind cannot make this tangible in the moment. You can't feel the difference between going to bed and staying up. That makes it extremely difficult to choose the better option — not because you're weak or lazy, but because your mind sees a concrete benefit (the food, the late-night scroll) against a vague, abstract one (feeling better eventually). Acting well anyway — not for reasons, but as a practice — is genuinely difficult and is, in fact, the practice.

Why You Look at Your Stats at the Worst Possible Time

Ryan describes finishing a stream, feeling mentally drained, and almost automatically opening his analytics. When his numbers are down, the comparison spiral begins.

This pattern is common and almost universal: we seek out potentially painful information at exactly the moment we're least equipped to process it. When you're out of mental energy, your mind can't catch subtle thoughts before they grow. You're more susceptible to cognitive biases — specifically, the tendency to notice confirming evidence and ignore contradicting evidence.

Looking at stats when you're exhausted also produces a skewed sample. You're comparing yourself only to streamers you can see — the ones still around and growing — not to the many who started at the same time and quietly stopped. The survivorship bias distorts your actual standing.

The fix is simple but requires deliberate structure: look at your data when your mind is rested and you have a specific question you're trying to answer. Separate analysis from emotional processing.

Jealousy Is Hunger, Not Malice

Jealousy isn't a character flaw. It's a signal that something you need hasn't been fed.

If you've just eaten a good meal and someone describes how delicious their food is, you can genuinely be happy for them. If you're hungry and broke, the same comment is painful. The jealousy isn't about them — it's about your own unfed need.

Ryan notices that his jealousy was worse after getting the world record than before, because during the grind he felt more fulfilled. The record created a void rather than filling one. That suggests the real hunger isn't about rankings or viewership — it's about having meaningful work to do, something that feeds the soul. For Ryan, that pointed toward coding and game-making, things he'd been circling but not starting.

Jealousy, viewed this way, is diagnostic. It tells you something in you is unsatisfied. The question worth sitting with is: what actually needs feeding here?

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking dharma is a one-time discovery. People spend enormous energy searching for their "purpose" as if it's something they'll find and then have permanently. It requires daily recommitment.
  • Confusing "just do it" with ease. Acting without expectation, acting for the sake of the action itself — these are hard. They involve doing things in spite of all the internal voices offering reasons not to. Acknowledging the difficulty is necessary; treating it as a reason to give up is not.
  • Looking at data when emotionally depleted. This virtually guarantees a distorted, negative reading that reinforces bad feelings and generates more negative karma.
  • Treating jealousy as a moral failing. It's information. Trying to suppress or deny it skips over what it's trying to tell you about an unmet need.
  • Believing you've "solved" a recurring pattern. Ryan thought he'd solved jealousy when he stopped comparing himself to other Mario runners. The pattern reappeared with streaming metrics. These tendencies don't get conquered once — they require ongoing attention.
  • Waiting to start until conditions are right. "Tomorrow" is always available and always a trap. Starting something you care about without having a clear reason is the actual practice.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Separate task-focus from outcome-focus while working. When you sit down to do your thing — stream, code, practice, create — notice when your mind drifts to metrics, comparisons, or results. Return your attention to the task. This is not a mystical instruction; it's a trainable habit.

  2. Do not check analytics, social stats, or messages right after finishing a demanding session. Schedule a specific, time-limited window earlier in the day — with a full mind, and a specific question you're trying to answer — for reviewing data. Treat it like a work meeting, not a mood check.

  3. Catch the first subtle thought. When you notice the beginning of a negative spiral — the small possibility of staying up, the passing glance at someone else's numbers — recognize it as the seed, not a neutral moment. You don't have to act dramatically; just notice it early enough to redirect attention.

  4. Practice a concentration technique. Ryan uses game sounds; you can use breath, a physical sensation, or any neutral anchor. When distracted thoughts arise, return to the anchor. Each return is the practice. Over time this builds the ability to catch subtle thoughts before they compound.

  5. Use the third-eye meditation sequence if you already have a foundation in concentration practices: First, follow the coolness of the breath from the nostrils up to the center of the forehead, imagining that clean air moving through and out. Then hover a fingertip near your forehead and focus on the sensation. Finally, with eyes closed, gently direct your gaze upward toward that same point — hold for five to ten seconds at a time, then release. Don't strain. If you feel pure focus or a tingling sensation, you're doing it correctly. (Do not jump to the third step without establishing the first two.)

  6. When jealousy appears, ask what's actually hungry. Don't try to eliminate it or shame yourself for having it. Ask: what need in me is unmet? Use it as a pointer toward what the next phase of meaningful work might look like.

  7. Start the project today, without a reason. If there's something you've been meaning to do — a game, a piece of music, a codebase — sit down and begin it without a goal attached. You won't feel any different afterward. That's fine. The seed is planted. Keep going.
