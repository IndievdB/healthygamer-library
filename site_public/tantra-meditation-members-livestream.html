The Rishi Model: Three Layers of Reality

The rishis — the ancient seers of the Himalayan tradition — essentially did what Western science did, but in the opposite direction. Science built instruments (telescopes, microscopes, X-rays) to perceive increasingly subtle aspects of the material world. The rishis built instruments out of consciousness itself — meditative practices — to perceive the non-material world with increasing resolution.

Through these practices, they mapped reality into three nested layers:

  • Matter (the physical plane; Sanskrit: anu, meaning atom)
  • Energy (ShaktiThe fundamental creative and generative energy of the universe — literally translated as "energy" or "strength" — that operates through and beyond conscious life.Full definition → or PranaYour basic unit of life force energy — the vital animating force that keeps you alive, known as qi in the Chinese system.Full definition → — kinetic, potential, chemical, biological energy)
  • Consciousness (Shiva or Brahman — the fundamental ground of existence)

The key insight is how these layers relate: Consciousness coalesces into Energy; Energy coalesces into Matter. Within a small amount of matter there is an enormous amount of energy (think nuclear fission). Within a small amount of energy there is an exponentially larger amount of Consciousness. This isn't quantum mysticism — it's the rishis' map of causation.

The practical implication: most humans operate exclusively at the level of matter. We try to change our circumstances by rearranging physical things. A nuclear bomb does immeasurably more damage than a pickaxe — not because someone tried harder, but because they accessed a deeper layer of causation. Tantra is the technology for accessing the deepest layer: Consciousness itself.

The Core Principle: Shiva and Shakti Must Both Be Present

Every failure to manifest something in your life — a finished book, a healthier body, a degree, a business — comes down to a deficit in one of two things:

  • Shiva (Consciousness, will, intention, direction)
  • Shakti (energy, effort, drive)

Serial failed entrepreneurs typically have plenty of Shakti — tremendous enthusiasm and effort — but their Shiva is unfocused. They launch, pivot, abandon. People with endless ideas who never act have Shiva without Shakti: vivid mental blueprints with no energy behind them.

"Anytime you look at your failure of manifestation you will discover one of two things: either there is a lack of Consciousness, will, or intent — or there is a lack of energy."

Any genuine tantric practice, simply by engaging both dimensions simultaneously, will begin to bind your will and your energy together. That binding is itself a side effect of the practice, before any specific technique even "works."

The Three Tools: Mantra, Yantra, and Yagna

The rishis didn't just discover these principles — they reverse-engineered them into repeatable tools. Think of the difference between inventing a cell phone and using one. They did the inventing; you just need to learn to use the device.

Mantra operates on the dimension of vibration. There are 12 bija (seed) mantras — each one corresponds to a fundamental quality of existence. Some examples:

  • Om — the vibratory oscillation of existence itself; the most basic quality of "isness"
  • The breath-sound (So-Hum) — the quality of duality (inhale/exhale, wave in/wave out, positive/negative)
  • Krim — the essence of action
  • Hum — explosive energy born of the Manipura chakra

Stringing these bija mantras together in precise sequences is like assembling a spell — you are invoking specific essences of the universe and combining them to create a particular manifestation. A Kali mantra (Krim Hum...) combines action, energy, love, and devotion to invoke that specific deity's attention.

Yantra operates on the dimension of form. Where a mantra is a vibrational arrangement, a yantra is a spatial/geometric one. Yagna refers to complex fire rituals involving specific offerings, invocations of deities, and ceremonial arrangements. This is beyond the scope of what most beginners need.

Kundalini, the Chakras, and the Sushumna Channel

Deities: Not Religion, Not Metaphor

Deities in the tantric framework are not objects of faith or belief — they are more like personified laws of physics. They are beings who exist at a level of Consciousness much closer to Brahman than we do, operating on timescales of cosmic cycles. They generally don't concern themselves with individual humans the way we'd like to imagine.

But you can get their attention — if you show genuine, sustained earnestness. From their perspective your lifespan is like an ant's, so a casual six-second interest doesn't register. Years of dedicated practice does.

A few deities relevant to practice:

  • Lakshmi / Devi / Tara — for material manifestation, wealthOne of the four goals of life in the Hindu framework, artha means wealth or material prosperity — understood specifically as a means, not an end.Full definition →, practical worldly outcomes
  • Kali — goddess of time and space; governs entropy, the big bang, the cycle of expansion and contraction. Her mantra string invokes action, energy, love, and devotion. She is loving and will respond to earnest practice — but on a timescale of 5–20 years.
  • Bhairava (a fierce aspect of Shiva) — invoked when you want to transcend material reality. He works by removing your attachments to this world, one by one. This is extremely powerful and extremely painful if you care about the things being removed. He doesn't ask permission; you asked.

"Bhairava is like: you said you want to see — I'll show you. I will take away everything that matters to you in this world until you have nothing left and the only direction you go is toward the Divine."

Don't invoke Bhairava casually. It's not a punishment; it's literally what you're asking for when you ask for transcendence.

A Practical Tantric Practice Structure

Here is how a beginning practice session can be assembled:

  1. Sattvic diet for the duration (ideally a 40-day anushthan — a committed run of practice)
  2. Spinal asanas — 10–30 minutes (or longer in early stages)
  3. Pranayama — nadi shodhana, kapalabhati, or bhramari to open sushumna
  4. Japa — mantra chanting with a rudraksha mala (108 beads; use real rudraksha, sourced through someone who knows) - Start with Om — one mala (108 repetitions) per session - Chant aloud at first; gradually reduce to a whisper; eventually internalize it entirely - Make the three parts of Om (A-U-M) even in duration — equal thirds - As the chanting quiets, shift mental focus from the sound to the rising energy in the body (from navel upward through the top of the head) - For those familiar with the "charging the laser beam" practice: as you chant Om, direct your intention to that sensation at the third eye / Brahma nadi; push into it gently
  5. Twice daily is significantly more effective than once for spiritual (not just physiological) progress
  6. Keep the practice secret — same space if possible, not shared with others

To work with a specific chakra, substitute the chakra's bija mantra. For muladhara: stare at the muladhara yantra (trataka), close eyes, hold the afterimage, and chant Lam 108 times.

Regarding a guru: you don't find one — they find you, through the network of sincere seekers. Show up consistently, build your practice earnestly, and remain open. The signal goes out. Someone will answer, potentially over a timeframe of years.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chanting mechanically without the right state of mind. A mantra chanted from ordinary waking consciousness is like typing commands into a text document rather than the actual system file. The chanting itself is not the magic — the state of consciousness from which it's done is.

  • Imprecision. Mantra requires exact pronunciation, tone, length, and mental focus. A small angular error in construction compounds over distance. This is why "just try it and see what happens" rarely works.

  • Skipping the vessel preparation. Most people want the technique without the diet, the spinal work, the celibacyBrahmacharya is the practice of dwelling in Brahman — the infinite or cosmic consciousness — which in practice means cultivating a state of non-lustfulness, not mere celibacy.Full definition →, and the substance elimination. This is equivalent to training like an Olympic Athlete but drinking every night. The practice won't compound.

  • Expecting results on a short timeline. The timescale is years to decades, not days. Science studies meditation over weeks. Tantric practice compounds over lifetimes.

  • Confusing psychedelics with practice. Psychedelics can open a window into higher states but do not build the capacity to get there voluntarily, sustain it, or direct it. You're a passenger, not a pilot. The spiritual technology built through dedicated tantric practice is, by this framing, incomparably more robust because it gives you control.

  • Invoking powerful deities without understanding what you're asking for. Bhairava in particular will deliver exactly what requesting transcendence logically requires — the removal of your material attachments — and that process is not comfortable.

  • Showing your mala or talking about your experiences casually. Your mala for practice is kept private. Spiritual experiences disclosed to the wrong person, or to anyone for ego gratification, will drain the Shakti you've accumulated.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start a 40-day anushthan. Pick a start date and commit to daily practice for 40 days. During this period: no psychoactive substances (including alcohol and caffeine), reduce or eliminate meat, and minimize sexual activity. This is the minimum "vessel preparation" run for any tantric practice to take hold.

  2. Learn and practice the spinal asanas. Go to a yoga studio or find a qualified teacher and learn: bhujangasana (cobra), parvatasana (mountain), marjariasana (cat-cow), utkatasana (chair), and suya namaskar (sun salutation). Do 10–20 minutes of these before each meditation session.

  3. Begin japa with Om. Obtain a rudraksha mala (108 beads; source it through someone knowledgeable rather than random internet orders). Each session: one mala of Om (108 repetitions), chanted aloud. Keep the three parts equal in length. Do this for at least one week before adding anything else.

  4. Graduate the Om practice in stages. After one week of audible chanting, begin reducing to a whisper. As the volume drops, move your attention from the sound to the rising physical sensation (navel → crown). Eventually internalize the chant completely. Note: this progression takes weeks or months, not days.

  5. Track sushumna windows. Several times a day, check which nostril is more open. When you notice the transition from one to the other, sit down and meditate or do mantra chanting immediately. Dawn and dusk are also naturally open windows — schedule at least one session there.

  6. Try the Brahma nadi extension. If you've done the "charging the laser beam" practice and felt the sensation, extend it: as you chant Om internally, put all your attention into that sensation and gently push your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → into it, as if finding a narrow channel extending outward from the third eye. Don't visualize or imagine — look for the actual sensation and follow it.

  7. Practice trataka on a yantra. Download or print a muladhara yantra. Stare at it without blinking for as long as comfortable. Close your eyes. Observe the after-image (antaraksha) and hold it. Simultaneously chant Lam internally. This is a complete beginner Kundalini session for the base chakra.

  8. Meditate twice daily. Even 15–20 minutes in the morning and again in the evening will compound significantly more than a single longer session. Think of the first session as paying your bills; the second is what gets deposited.

  9. Keep a private practice log. Don't post about your experiences publicly or discuss them casually. If something significant happens — unusual sensations, unexpected states of mind — hold it privately until you meet someone who clearly knows what you're talking about, at which point disclosing it to that person specifically is appropriate.

  10. Stay honest about where you actually are. Most people have not experienced samadhi even once. Most "meditation" happens far below the level where tantric technique produces strong results. The path is: prepare the body → stabilize the mind → attain dhyana → approach samadhi → practice mantra and yantra from there. Begin at the beginning and don't rush the stages.
