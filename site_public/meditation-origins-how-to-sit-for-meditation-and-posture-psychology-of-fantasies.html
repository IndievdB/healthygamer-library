Mantras offer a concrete example of this principle. The tradition holds that meditators began by listening — to the breath, to the environment — and noticed recurring fundamental sounds. The breath has two distinct sounds: one for inhalation, one for exhalation. Those sounds show up elsewhere in nature too. From careful listening, certain root sounds (bija mantras, literally "seed" sounds) were identified as building blocks. Combine them in different ways and you get different effects — like combining ingredients to make different dishes. Whether you take that cosmologically or simply as a useful framework for understanding why certain sounds affect the mind in certain ways, the underlying method is empirical: observe carefully, identify patterns, test what works.

The Physics of Sitting Up Straight

Most people struggle with meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → posture because they treat it as a discipline problem — just sit up straighter — when it's actually a physics problem.

The rule is simple: your knees need to be lower than your hips.

When your knees are higher than your hips (as they are when you sit flat on the floor cross-legged with no support), your center of gravity shifts forward and your body compensates by rounding the lower back into a slouch. You then have to use muscle effort to fight that tendency, which creates tension and fatigue — exactly the wrong conditions for meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →.

When your knees drop below your hips, your spine stacks naturally under the weight of your torso, and you can sit upright without muscular effort. That's why:

  • Standing is easy to do upright — your knees are well below your hips
  • Full lotus straightens the back — hips rise above knee level
  • Sitting on a pillow cross-legged achieves the same effect with less flexibility required

The practical fix, usable right now regardless of your flexibility:

  1. Sit at the edge of a chair (not back against the backrest)
  2. Stack two or three pillows under your seat — not under your legs, just under your hips
  3. Notice how your back responds

You can also test this on a staircase: sit on a higher step and let your feet rest on lower steps. Your hips will be above your knees, and your back will straighten on its own.

"Sitting up straight is not about posture, it's about physics. If the weight of your body is distributed through your spine into the ground, it'll be easy. You won't use any muscles."

The Trap of Chasing Meditation Experiences

One of the most common and most predictable problems in meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →: you have a striking experience early on, and then spend your next sessions trying to recreate it.

The moment you're looking for the experience, you're no longer focused on the practice itself. Your mind is split — part of it is doing the technique, part of it is scanning for results. That divided attention is exactly what prevents the concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → needed to produce the experience in the first place.

"If you chase an experience in meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → you won't be able to find that experience again."

The fix is mechanical: let go of the expectation and return full attention to the object of focus (a point on the forehead, the breath, a sound — whatever the technique requires). The experience was a byproduct of concentrated attention, not a thing you can aim at directly. Point your attention at the practice, not at the payoff.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating posture as willpower. If you're fighting to sit up straight, you've already lost. Fix the geometry — raise your hips, lower your knees — and your body will cooperate.
  • Chasing states. Having a powerful first experience and then meditating in order to feel that again is the fastest way to stop having powerful experiences. Focus on the practice, not the results.
  • Fantasizing as a substitute for action. Fantasy feels like it's related to ambition — you're imagining a better future, after all. But it consumes the exact energy that ambition runs on. Noticing this pattern in yourself is more useful than judging yourself for it.
  • Skipping techniques because they look strange. Bhramari mudra looks and sounds odd. If you didn't try it, it's worth asking yourself why — embarrassment, skepticism, fear of doing it wrong. Those reactions are information about how you approach things outside your comfort zone.
  • Treating mantras as something you can self-assign. Mantras in this tradition are given by a teacher based on a careful read of the student's mind and temperament. Picking one off a list doesn't carry the same weight.
