Summary

TL;DR — A single diagnosis rarely captures a whole person, and the same traumatic experience can produce wildly different psychological outcomes depending on someone's pre-existing personality and the unconscious conclusions they draw from it.

Using Eren Yeager, Joe Goldberg, Michael Scott, Jimmy McGill (Saul Goodman), and Levi Ackerman as case studies, this talk explores how trauma, personality temperament, and psychological needs interact to produce behavior. The goal isn't to reduce fictional characters to tidy labels — it's to use them as clear examples of real clinical concepts: moral injury, limerence, developmental deprivation, counterfactual thinking, and the difference between a clinical threshold and a personality quirk.

Eren Yeager and Moral Injury

Trauma typically teaches one lesson above all others: you have no agency. When Eren watches his mother die as a child, his brain draws a conclusion — the world is a machine, and you are just a passenger. The tragedy is that he carries that conclusion forward even after he becomes, arguably, the most powerful person alive.

There's a relatively new diagnostic concept called moral injury — distinct from classic PTSD — that describes the severe distress that results from being forced into situations where you have to do things that violate your own values. Soldiers who launch a drone strike on what turns out to be civilians experience this. Eren experiences it too, but he doesn't become impaired by it. That impairment is part of what the diagnosis formally requires. Instead, he becomes hyperfunctional. How?

By depersonalization and diffusion of responsibility. His framework is essentially: You didn't have a choice. I don't have a choice. No one is responsible. This is just the way the world works. This reasoning is psychologically elegant and deeply dangerous. If Reiner can't be blamed for what he did, then Eren can't be blamed either. The moral weight dissolves.

This is the same psychological architecture you see in abusive relationships — the classic "why do you make me do this to you?" It removes the self from the equation. And sitting with people who have fully internalized this worldview is, clinically, one of the most unsettling experiences possible, because there is nothing on the other side of their calculation that protects you. Empathy doesn't reach them, because empathy has been structurally dismantled.

The sociopathy dimension matters here too. Sociopathy exists on a spectrum, not as a binary. One of its features — reduced empathy — can be partly temperamental, even heritable. Pair a person with some degree of sociopathic temperament with a genuinely genocidal childhood experience, and you get someone who arrives at Eren's conclusions not just intellectually but emotionally. The conclusions feel true rather than chosen.

"Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate."

Eren never reckons with the fact that he's making enormous choices. He experiences those choices as fate. That is the real tragedy of unprocessed trauma — not that you're powerless, but that you believe you are, long after the circumstances that created that belief have changed.

Joe Goldberg and What Happens When Limerence Meets Sociopathy

Limerence is the state of being utterly consumed by a fantasized version of another person. It is not a character flaw — it's something that happens to you, often without warning. You meet someone once, and for years afterward your brain constructs an elaborate ideal of who they are. The real person is almost beside the point.

Key features of limerence: - The fantasy is more vivid and emotionally real than any actual interaction with the person - When you do enter a relationship with the person, reality dissolves the fantasy — and the limerence often transfers to someone new - Research suggests limerence cycles tend to last roughly 8–10 years before shifting to a new object

Limerence itself isn't pathological in the way it's often portrayed. What matters is the personality organization around it — how the person structures their behavior in response to it.

Joe Goldberg is what you get when limerence lands inside a sociopath. The limerence produces the obsession, the idealization, the desperate romantic logic. The sociopathy provides the rest: no genuine empathy, the ability to cage someone "for their own good," the cognitive (not emotional) understanding of how his actions affect others — which he uses to engineer emotional responses rather than to genuinely connect.

A critical distinction: Joe has cognitive empathy — he can model what someone is feeling and predict their behavior — but not affective empathy, the felt sense of what another person experiences. This is why his relationships eventually collapse at the same point: the other person looks at him and feels something hollow looking back. He sees a vision of them; they feel unseen.

His "moral compass" is entirely organized around his limerant object. He'll do anything for this person. That sounds like devotion. It isn't. Genuine love requires seeing another person as real and separate. Joe doesn't have the internal architecture for that.

When the limerence does transfer — as it inevitably does once the fantasy becomes domestic reality — he experiences it as a new great love, not as a pattern. He never identifies the machinery.

Michael Scott and Growing Up Without a Template

Michael Scott isn't best understood through social anxiety, low self-esteem, or attachment theory alone — though those things are present. His core issue is developmental deprivation: he grew up without adequate models for what real relationships look, feel, and function like.

He's not trying to connect with people. He's trying to perform connection according to an idea of what connection is supposed to look like. Inside jokes. Flat-screen TVs. Being "the cool boss." These are checkboxes on a list of what a life is supposed to contain, assembled by someone who never lived one organically.

This produces a painful irony: Dwight, who is deeply loyal and genuinely present, gets pushed away because he doesn't fit the image. Jan and Todd Packer, who treat Michael poorly, get pursued because they match some idea of what a cool life involves. He's chasing criteria instead of connection.

The research on friendship formation is instructive here: it takes 50 to 200 hours of contact to form a genuine friendship. Not emotionally intense interactions — just contact, proximity, accumulated time. Most people dramatically underestimate this, which is why "I joined a club and still don't have friends" is such a common experience. You might be at 16 hours. You're not even at the minimum.

Michael's professional self — the salesman — is a completely different person. Competence in one domain does not require emotional development in another. This is genuinely common: you can be profoundly stunted in relational skills while being brilliant at something else. Developmental stunting isn't global.

Jimmy McGill and the Gap Between Vision and Success

Jimmy (Saul Goodman) represents a specific kind of psychological trap: attaching your identity to a vision of success more than to success itself.

His setup is the classic golden child / black sheep dynamic. His brother is brilliant, respected, legitimate. Jimmy is scrappy, impulsive, and perpetually underestimated. He's not actually less capable — he's just wired differently, with an impaired ability to assess risk, a tendency to see only upside, and a reward processing system that doesn't adequately register mistakes.

Two mechanisms are worth understanding here:

  • Counterfactual thinking: The brain's capacity to rewind after an error and simulate an alternative — what you should have done. This is how we learn. People who struggle with addiction or compulsive risk-taking often have this system working poorly. They lose big and wake up the next morning ready to try again.
  • Reward prediction error: When you expect something and don't get it, your brain recalibrates. If that recalibration mechanism is weak, you keep expecting the same outcome from the same behavior.

The deeper issue is that Jimmy's psychological hunger — the need to be seen as legitimate, to have his brother's respect — cannot be solved by making partner at a law firm. So when that opportunity arrives (genuinely, on a silver platter), he sabotages it. Not out of stupidity. Because the real need isn't being addressed by the surface-level achievement.

Relational problems must have relational solutions. Emotional problems must have emotional solutions. There's no amount of intellectualization that will substitute for feeling loved.

This is the trap many people fall into with relationships, career milestones, body counts, status markers — pick your metric. You're trying to solve a right-brain problem with a left-brain solution. Getting the external thing doesn't touch the internal wound. And because it doesn't, you either self-sabotage before getting it, or get it and find yourself immediately disoriented by how little it changes.

Levi and the Importance of Clinical Thresholds

Levi is commonly diagnosed by fans as having OCD because he's obsessively clean. This is a useful case for understanding what a clinical threshold actually means.

A diagnosis isn't just a collection of traits. It requires functional impairment. Ask the right questions:

  • Is Levi clean? Yes.
  • Does being in a dirty situation impair his function? No — he fights Titans covered in blood all day without hesitation.
  • Does he have compulsions — rituals that relieve obsessive thoughts? It's not clearly demonstrated.

Contrast this with genuine OCD: a person so afraid of insects that they dig a literal moat around their bed and fill it with toxic substance. That is impairment. Levi being grossed out by blood on his sword hilt is not the same category of thing.

Everyone has some anxiety. Everyone has mood fluctuations. Neither makes them disordered. Levi likely has subclinical OCD tendencies — germaphobic inclinations, a strong preference for cleanliness — but tendencies are not disorders. The distinction matters both clinically and in how we talk about ourselves.

Common Pitfalls

  • Collapsing a person into a single diagnosis. Real people — and well-written characters — are the product of multiple interacting factors: temperament, trauma, learned conclusions, and circumstance. Joe Goldberg isn't just a sociopath and isn't just limerant. The combination is what produces the specific horror.
  • Confusing traits with disorders. Having obsessive tendencies, sociopathic features, or limerant experiences doesn't automatically mean you have OCD, antisocial personality disorder, or a pathological condition. Thresholds matter.
  • Assuming trauma produces one response. Not everyone who survives genocide develops PTSD. The same event produces different outcomes depending on personality, interpretation, and the unconscious conclusions drawn. This is hopeful — it means the conclusions are, in principle, revisable.
  • Trying to solve emotional needs with external achievements. Getting the job, the partner, the status marker doesn't resolve the underlying psychological hunger. Layering external solutions onto unmet internal needs usually produces self-sabotage.
  • Mistaking the fantasy for the person. Limerance is compelling precisely because the fantasy feels more real than the actual relationship. Recognizing that you're in love with an idea, not a person, is hard but necessary.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your conclusions about agency. Write down a few situations where you feel out of control or like things "just happen" to you. Ask: when did I first learn this? Am I still the same person in the same circumstances, or have I carried an old conclusion into a new situation?

  2. Separate your psychological needs from your surface goals. Pick one thing you're pursuing — a relationship, a job, a milestone. Ask honestly: what do I think getting this will feel like? Is that feeling something I could cultivate internally, without the external thing? Start there.

  3. Audit your contact hours. If you feel like you don't have close friends, count actual hours of contact — not emotionally significant moments, just time spent around people. Are you at 50 hours with anyone? If not, the problem may simply be volume, not chemistry.

  4. Check for functional impairment before applying a label to yourself. Do you have obsessive thoughts, anxiety, mood swings, or intense feelings? Before deciding you have a disorder, ask: does this impair my ability to function? Is it getting in the way of things I need or want to do? If not, you may have tendencies, not a diagnosis.

  5. Notice when you're in love with a vision. In any significant relationship — romantic or otherwise — periodically ask: do I know how this person actually feels right now? Have I made space for who they are, or am I mainly responding to who I need them to be?
