  3. Try the third-eye introductory practice. Hold your middle finger over your eyebrow center, close your eyes, and hold attention there for sixty seconds. Put your hand down and focus on whatever sensation remains. Use this as an anchor for internal attention rather than scanning outward.

  4. Separate the task from the goal. Instead of holding a destination in mind ("I want to become successful / get a girlfriend / get fit"), identify what the action looks like today. Ask: what is actually in front of me to do right now? Do that. The outcome is not yours to control; the action is.

  5. Do your highest-reward activity last, not first. If you play video games, watch pornography, or scroll social media in the morning, your brain's reward system is depleted for lower-stimulation tasks for the rest of the day. Reverse the order — do the hardest, least-stimulating necessary task first, and the rest becomes comparatively easier.

  6. Audit your comparisons. When you feel like things are unfair, notice what you are comparing yourself to. Ask: why that comparison specifically, and not a different one? The comparisons your ego selects are not neutral — they are chosen to support the emotional conclusion you've already arrived at.

  7. Before processing someone else's problem, ask one question. When someone you care about (a partner, a friend, a son) is struggling, try asking "help me understand what that's like for you" before offering a solution, correction, or counterpoint. Understanding what drove someone to where they are is usually the only intervention that actually works.
