Summary

TL;DR — Radicalization, echo chambers, and bad behavior online are less about broken minds and more about broken environments — and genuine curiosity, not debate, is what actually changes people.

This is a wide-ranging conversation covering online radicalization, school shootings, gambling sponsorships on streaming platforms, crypto collapses, conspiracy theories, and what it actually takes to understand — and maybe help — people who hold dangerous or fringe beliefs. The throughline is a persistent question: why do people end up in dark places, and what can we do about it?

How Echo Chambers Actually Work

The internet doesn't radicalize people all at once. It happens gradually, through a process sometimes called online drift. You watch one video, the algorithm surfaces more of the same, and over time your information diet narrows. You migrate from a general subreddit to a more extreme one, from a Discord server to a more radical Discord server, and each step feels normal because you've already taken the last one.

The key enabler is that you can always find someone who believes what you believe. That's not a bug of the internet — it's a feature. But it means every fringe idea, no matter how detached from reality, has a ready-made communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → to validate it. Once you're inside that community, you stop being tested by outside perspectives. The loop tightens.

"You can always find someone who believes in whatever garbage you believe in. There's always going to be groups for it."

What makes this especially hard to interrupt is that from inside the echo chamber, nothing looks like an echo chamber. The JFK revival believers waiting in Dallas weren't performing — they genuinely believed. And from their vantage point, the rest of the world was the one that was wrong.

What Actually Produces Extreme Behavior

The tempting explanation for school shooters, suicide bombers, or anyone who commits atrocities is that they must be mentally ill. The reality is more unsettling and more complex.

Mental illness — schizophrenia, psychosis, depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → — is often blamed because we can't otherwise explain behavior that feels so alien. But most people with schizophrenia are not dangerous. Psychosis typically brings disorganization, not coordinated violence. Attributing mass shootings to mental illness misrepresents both the shooters and the millions of people with psychiatric diagnoses who never harm anyone.

What may actually be happening is indoctrination — a process of conditioningThe brain's mechanism for repeating actions that produced pleasure or relief — when a behavior leads to a dopamine release (or suppresses negative emotion), the nucleus accumbens signals "do that again," building…Full definition → that isn't a neurological malfunction, but a learned set of beliefs reinforced by a social environment. This doesn't mean treatment wouldn't help; it very likely would. But it means the cause isn't necessarily biological in the way a heart attack is biological.

Two conditions seem especially predictive of someone becoming dangerous:

  • Loss of hope about the future. Once a person genuinely believes nothing they do can change their trajectory, extreme behavior becomes more thinkable. Some turn that inward (suicide); others turn it outward.
  • Group identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → overtaking individual identity. Most catastrophic human behavior in history has been motivated not by hatred alone but by people believing they're serving a higher cause. Suicide bombers think they're protecting something. School shooters often frame their acts as strikes against an unjust world. The group's logic overrides the individual's natural reluctance toward violence.

"Most of the worst behavior in human history is not due to negative emotions — it's actually due to positive emotions. They think they're doing something good."

Hatred itself usually comes from one of two places: conditioning (you're taught from childhood to hate a particular group) or hurt (we learn to hate what hurts us). Understanding which one is operating matters enormously for what kind of intervention might help.

The Case Against Debate

Political discussions about gun control, online arguments about conspiracy theories, streamers dunking on flat earthers — almost none of it changes any minds. Not because the people arguing are wrong, but because debate is structurally incapable of producing learning.

When you're in an argument, your mind is primed to convince, not to receive. Both parties are trying to corner the other into admitting defeat, which means both parties have to defend against attack. Nobody concedes under attack — they dig in.

"When we try to understand the person who disagrees with us instead of trying to convince them — that's when something actually happens."

There's also an ego problem. Forcing someone to admit they were wrong, especially publicly and especially on something they've doubled down on for years, requires them to also accept responsibility for harm caused by that wrong belief. Most people won't do that. The framing of political debates — you're either right or complicit — makes it structurally impossible for anyone to change course without humiliating themselves.

The better move is to actually be curious. Ask questions whose purpose is genuinely to learn, not to set up a gotcha. State opinions as opinions, not as loaded questions. Create conditions where the other person doesn't have to defend — and watch what happens.

Motivational Interviewing: Getting People to Their Own Conclusions

There's a clinical technique from addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → psychiatry called motivational interviewingA structured, evidence-based conversational technique used by a trained practitioner to resolve a person's internal conflict and increase their motivation to change.Full definition → that applies far beyond the therapist's office. The core insight: people who are in denial about a problem will not respond to being told they have a problem. They'll argue back, minimize, and deflect.

But if you ask open-ended, non-judgmental questions and just listen — really listen, as if you might learn something — something shifts. The person has to actually construct and articulate their own beliefs out loud. Inconsistencies surface. Contradictions become visible to them, not because you pointed them out, but because explaining something forces you to examine it.

A simplified version: 1. You said alcohol doesn't cause any problems. 2. You also said you hate being hungover. 3. What causes hangovers? 4. So what might you do about that?

They arrive at the conclusion themselves. That arrival sticks in a way that being told never does.

"You let them arrive at their own conclusions — because that's how people are going to change."

This is also why genuinely asking "help me understand why you believe what you believe" — without an agenda — tends to do more work than any argument. If someone's beliefs are internally inconsistent, patient questions will expose that. If their beliefs are actually coherent, you'll learn something.

The Crypto and Gambling Problem Worth Naming

A separate but concrete thread worth pulling: the online gambling platform Stake operates in a way that is almost certainly designed to exploit its users. It requires no age verification to deposit and create an account — but it does require verification to withdraw. Which means the platform knows exactly who is underage and simply refuses to pay them out rather than preventing them from gambling in the first place.

"They don't have verification to put money in, but they do have verification to take money out. They know what they're doing."

This is meaningfully different from a streamer who personally gambles with their own money (bad, arguably, but a personal choice). When a platform structures its systems this way and pays streamers to promote it to audiences that include minors, the harm is being manufactured deliberately.

The Luna crypto collapse follows a similar logic: a stablecoin built on algorithmic mechanisms that critics publicly demonstrated were exploitable, which then collapsed catastrophically. People who lost everything are now buying Luna 2.0. The sunk cost fallacy is part of it, but so is counterfactual thinkingThe brain's habit of mentally replaying past events and imagining alternative outcomes — the "what if" and "if only" loop.Full definition → — the mind replays what it would have done differently, concludes it now understands the game, and re-enters.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming extreme behavior means mental illness. It's a comforting explanation that misrepresents both the behavior and psychiatric conditions. Indoctrination and radicalization are not the same thing as a neurological malfunction, even if both can produce similarly frightening outcomes.
  • Thinking debate changes minds. It doesn't — not reliably, not at scale. Argument triggers defensiveness. Understanding is what changes people.
  • Insulting the intelligence of people with wrong beliefs. Flat earthers probably don't have below-average IQs. Most people who hold bad beliefs aren't stupid — they're ignorant of specific information, often because of the environment they grew up in. Treating them as stupid forecloses the only avenue that actually works.
  • Asking questions to get a particular answer. This is a loaded question, not genuine inquiry. If you already know what you think, state it as an opinion. Reserve questions for when you actually want to learn something.
  • Believing platforms are just ignorant of the harm they're causing. Some of them, like Stake, demonstrably know exactly what they're doing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your information diet. Look at your YouTube recommendations, Twitter feed, or subreddits. Notice whether the content is getting more extreme or narrow over time. Deliberately seek out one thoughtful perspective you'd normally skip.

  2. Practice asking questions to learn, not to win. In your next disagreement — with a friend, online, wherever — try asking one question whose honest purpose is to understand how the other person arrived at their view. Not to find the flaw. Just to understand. Notice how the conversation changes.

  3. When someone holds a belief you disagree with, ask them to explain it fully. Don't interrupt, don't counter. Let them build it from the ground up. Inconsistencies will surface on their own, and you might learn something you didn't expect.

  4. If you find yourself re-entering something that already burned you — a crypto investment, a relationship pattern, a habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → — write down specifically what you believe is different this time. Be honest. If the answer is mostly "I understand the game better now," that's the sunk cost fallacy talking.

  5. Look for the seed, not just the fruit. When you encounter someone doing or believing something that seems incomprehensible, ask yourself: what environment would have to exist for this to make sense to someone? What were they trying to solve or protect? This doesn't mean excusing the behavior — it means understanding it well enough to actually address it.

  6. If you work with or care about someone struggling (with addiction, with radicalized thinking, with hopelessness), try motivational interviewing basics: ask open-ended questions, reflect what you hear, and resist the urge to give advice until they've talked through their own contradictions. Solutions they generate themselves are the ones they'll actually act on.
