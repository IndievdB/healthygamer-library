Why We Are Getting Sicker: Systemic and Nutritional Roots

The mental health crisis didn't appear from nowhere. Several converging forces have degraded the raw material of human experience:

Soil and food quality. The roughly 18–20 inches of topsoil that produces nearly all human food has been severely weakened by 50–70 years of industrial farming and chemical contamination. The body is, at bottom, a sophisticated reorganization of soil — what that soil contains (or doesn't) shapes what the body and brain can do.

Chemical overload. Water treated with chlorine, air pollution, and processed food engineered for commerce rather than the body means the average person is absorbing more toxins and fewer micronutrients than any previous generation. The first system to fail under micronutrient deficiency is the psychological one — the "software" degrades before the "hardware."

Sociological anchors removed without replacement. Family structures, religious communities, and other institutions that provided emotional stability have been dismantled faster than anything coherent has been built to replace them. The result is a society with no reliable sources of emotional security.

Life arriving too fast. Experiences that once happened at 18 now arrive at 12; what happened at 30 now happens at 18. People are encountering complex emotional situations before they have the maturity or tools to process them.

None of this is destiny. But ignoring the physical substrate — treating mental health as a purely cognitive or neurological problem isolated from food, water, posture, and environment — misses most of the picture.

The Mind Is Not Just the Brain

A pervasive mistake in mainstream psychiatry is treating the mind as a product of brain chemistry alone. But every cell in the body carries memory. The DNA in a single cell processes more information per second than the brain can consciously handle. Your body remembers your ancestors' features, skin tone, and biological history in ways your conscious mind never could.

"What you call as mind is essentially a certain combination of memory and intelligence. Every cell in the body has more memory than your brain can ever think of processing."

This matters practically. When you take a psychiatric medication, you swallow it — it goes into your stomach, bloodstream, and everywhere. Pharmacology itself confirms that the mind is not local to the skull. The body is not a container for the mind; it is, in a very real sense, the mind's larger architecture.

This is why nutritional quality, physical posture, and the state of the nervous system aren't "lifestyle factors" that exist separately from mental health — they are mental health.

Compulsiveness Is the Root of All Psychological Suffering

The solution to compulsiveness is not more compulsiveness in the opposite direction. It is the same as the solution to darkness: you don't fight the dark, you turn on a light. You restore consciousness.

The Problem of Misidentification (Ahamkara)

The second root cause of mental suffering is ahamkara — often translated as "ego" in the West, but more precisely understood as the I-feeling, the habitual identification of "self" with accumulated things.

Your body is not you. It is matter you have gathered. The food you have eaten, the air you have breathed, the soil remade into flesh — all of it was borrowed from the planet and will return to it. Identifying your self with that accumulated matter is like a person drinking a glass of water and then insisting the vessel is them.

"You gathered this body and you start thinking this is me."

Similarly, your thoughts and emotions are not you. They are psychological activity — something happening in you, not something that is you. When the mind is healthy, thought is like hand movement: you initiate it when useful, it resolves when it is done. When you misidentify with the thought — when you believe the thought is you and is real — the thought cannot be stopped. It runs. It compounds. It creates suffering.

This misidentification is not abstract philosophy. It is clinically observable. In psychosis, thoughts are taken as 100% real. In anxiety, thoughts are taken as probably real. In a relatively healthy person, there is at least some distance between the thought and the person. The gradient of mental illness tracks almost perfectly with the degree of fusion between self and thought.

"You have misunderstood your psychological activity as existentially true. That's the problem."

Perspective and Scale as Medicine

One unusually direct way to loosen misidentification is to genuinely reckon with scale. This is not a motivational exercise — it is a perceptual correction.

The cosmos has no known edge or beginning. The solar system is a speck within it. Earth is a smaller speck. The city you are in is a speck within that. You, within that city, are a microscopic nothing — and yet, to that microscopic nothing, experience has been given. Individual, vivid, personal experience.

When you forget this, every thought feels enormous and urgent. The mind cannot quiet because it believes its drama is existentially significant. When you remember it — genuinely, not just intellectually — the thought becomes what it actually is: a small mental event in an unfathomably large universe. It loses the weight that made it compulsive.

"We are just a pop-up on this planet. We come for a brief amount of time and we'll pop out."

This is not nihilism. It is the prerequisite for freedom. Only when the thought stops feeling like ultimate reality can you choose which thoughts to follow and which to let dissolve.

Why Relationships Are Failing

Dating, marriage, and birth rates are in crisis in many parts of the world. The deeper reason is not economic or logistical. It is a collapse of engagement and the replacement of engagement with gratification.

Gratification is transactional: what can I extract from this? Engagement is involving: what am I putting into this? The two produce completely different internal experiences.

"You're thinking in terms of how can I give less and take more. With this you will never know joy in your life."

Falling in love — the phrase itself is instructive. You fall. Something of the constructed self has to come down in order for another person to actually enter. You don't rise, fly, or walk into real love. Some portion of the persona you've assembled has to make room. How to Regain Voluntary Control

Moving from compulsive back to conscious is not a single dramatic event. It is trained, incrementally, the same way any other skill is trained.

Step one: Recognize that thought output is not truth. The mind produces thoughts the way the stomach produces acid — automatically, constantly, whether or not the output is accurate or useful. Seeing a thought as a product of the mind rather than a fact about reality is the foundational move.

Step two: Start small — separate feeling from action in low-stakes situations. The mistake most people make is trying to apply this principle to enormous life problems first. Instead, notice in small moments when you don't feel like doing something minor — cleaning, taking out the trash, making the bed — and do it anyway. This is not about cleanliness. It is neuromuscular training for the frontal lobeThe brain region responsible for planning, executing tasks, controlling impulses, and regulating other parts of the brain — essentially the hardware of self-regulation and mature behavior.Full definition →: each small instance of action-despite-feeling weakens the automatic connection between emotion and behavior.

Step three: Shorten your time horizon under stress. Suffering expands when the mind extends it to "forever" or "never again." These are not facts. When caught in a painful mindset, deliberately focus on the next hour, not the next lifetime. Experience accumulates and gradually shifts the mindset from the inside, in ways that intellectual reframing alone cannot.

Step four: Change your daily experience to change your mindset. You cannot think your way out of a mindset that is continuously reinforced by your experience. The most powerful and automatic way to shift a mindset is to cultivate genuinely different day-to-day experiences. This sometimes means a phase of "checking out" of your current life pattern — which is not failure but a necessary precursor to plugging back in in a healthier configuration.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the 72 diagnoses as 72 different problems. They may share one root: compulsiveness. Naming the flavor precisely has some clinical utility, but it can distract from the underlying mechanism that all of them share.

  • Confusing pain with suffering. Pain is unavoidable and is not itself the crisis. Suffering is the psychological architecture you build around pain — the identifications, the forever-and-nevers, the I-am-statements that attach to a painful event. These are dismantlable; the original pain often is not.

  • Substituting dopamine for oxytocin. The two are not interchangeable. High-pleasure activities do not meet the need for genuine connection; they mask it while quietly eroding the capacity for it.

  • Trying to shift your mindset solely through your mindset. Cognitive reframingDeliberately shifting how you interpret an event to change its emotional impact — not by forcing positivity, but by questioning whether your automatic reading of the situation is actually accurate.Full definition → has limits if your daily experience keeps generating the same raw material. Experience is the most powerful mindset-shifter available — which means the environment and activities that fill your day matter more than most therapeutic frameworks acknowledge.

  • Treating "ego" as the translation of ahamkara. The Western concept of ego carries decades of psychoanalytic baggage and a specifically negative valence. Ahamkara is more neutrally the I-feeling — the habitual activity of identifying a self with accumulated attributes. Understanding it as identification rather than ego opens up more useful ways to work with it.

  • Waiting until you feel like doing the thing. Waiting for motivation before acting is the behavioral signature of a life run by compulsion. The feeling of motivation is often the result of beginning an action, not its prerequisite.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what you consume — physically. Look honestly at the quality of food you are eating. Is commerce deciding this, or is your body? Start reducing processed food and increasing whole-food micronutrient density, not as a diet but as a recognition that your psychological software runs on physical substrate.

  2. Practice thought-labeling. When a distressing thought arises, add the prefix: "My mind is producing the thought that..." This is not suppression — it is creating the perceptual gap between you and the thought product. Do this consistently for two weeks before judging its effect.

  3. Train the feeling-action gap on small things. Identify three daily tasks you consistently procrastinate or skip because you don't feel like doing them. Do them anyway, and notice the internal experience of doing something contrary to your emotional state. This is your primary training for distress tolerance.

  4. Do a perspective exercise. Sit quietly and genuinely walk through the scale of the cosmos down to your current location and your current worry. Not as a dismissal of the worry, but as a calibration of its actual size relative to existence. Notice what happens to the urgency of the thought.

  5. Distinguish what you actually need chemically. If you are reaching for high-dopamine activities (gaming, social media, pornography, food), pause and ask: Am I oxytocin-deficient? If the answer is yes, the dopamine activity will not help. Identify one action you can take toward actual human connection — even brief physical contact or a genuine conversation — and take it before reaching for the dopamine substitute.

  6. Shorten your suffering's time horizon. When caught in a painful thought loop, write down the thought and then write down what is literally true about the next hour only. Practice returning to the next hour whenever the mind extends to "forever" or "never again."

  7. Introduce variety into your daily experience deliberately. If your mindset is stuck, it is at least partly because your experience is stuck. Identify one structural change to your day — a new physical route, a different activity, a new social context — and maintain it for at least two weeks. The shift in mindset will follow the shift in experience, not the other way around.
