The Power of Walking, and What to Do After

Walking isn't just aimless escape. When you're overwhelmed inside four walls, the mind gets bombarded by too many thoughts at once. Walking gives you a way to process them. As you walk, blood glucose drops, breathing deepens, exhalation rate increases, and the body physically exhausts itself — which quiets the mind. The parasympathetic nervous systemThe branch of the autonomic nervous system responsible for rest and digest — the body's mode of recovery, digestion, and calm.Full definition → starts to engage. The hyperactive thought-loop dulls.

The key move is: once you're physically tired enough, sit down somewhere and let thoughts surface. Not to be bombarded again, but because in that state of physical exhaustion, one or two things will float up rather than twenty. That's what you work with.

Abstract Thinking Is the Enemy of Getting Unstuck

There is solid research on ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → — the pattern of thinking the same thoughts over and over without resolution. What this research shows is that people who ruminate tend to use an abstract, analytical processing style, and this is exactly what makes the ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition → useless.

Abstract processing means: - Forming general, decontextualized conclusions: I'm a loser. Women are all the same. I'll never find work. - Stripping away context so the conclusion feels total and permanent. - Focusing on why — Why am I like this? Why is the world this way? — rather than on how anything could actually change.

These meta-level conclusions feel deep and honest but they're cognitively paralysing. If you wake up on a Wednesday morning believing you are fundamentally a loser and the system is rigged, what do you do? Nothing. There is no action available.

Concrete processing is different. It asks: what was the specific context that produced this outcome? What were the variables? If those variables changed, what would change?

"If the reason I failed this class is because my bipolar disorder was way too active, that doesn't mean I'm going to fail forever. That means if I adjust this bipolar variable, I can pass."

The moment you add context, failure becomes contingent rather than permanent. You have something to work on.

One important warning: people with a heavily abstract processing style will often say "I can't think of any contextual variables" and then conclude that none exist. That's the bias at work. The difficulty generating concrete answers is a symptom of the pattern, not evidence that the situation is hopeless.

Narcissistic Conditioning: It's About Agency, Not Finding Better People

Growing up with narcissistic parents doesn't make you a narcissist, but it instills a narcissistic mode of relating — a framing of the world in which things happen to you and other people are the source of both your pain and your salvation.

Listen for the language pattern: I ended up in the wrong crowd. I got betrayed. I got exploited. I just want someone to trust. Notice that the speaker is consistently the object of the sentence. Things are being done to them. It was literally installed at age three when a parent said "you ruined my day" over a diaper, teaching a child that external people determine internal states.

The person in this voicemail had arrived at a logical but ultimately doomed solution: find people whose good outweighs their abusive tendencies. The problem is this is still the same frame. It still assumes that your wellbeing is determined by who you find. As long as that's the operating assumption, the cycle continues.

What actually needs to change is the question. Not "how do I find the right people?" but "what is it in me that gravitates toward the wrong people?"

Two connected mechanisms are at work: - Repetition compulsionThe unconscious drive to re-enter situations that mirror an old, unresolved wound — not out of self-destruction, but out of the deeply human impulse to fix what once went wrong.Full definition →: we unconsciously seek out people who recreate the relational dynamics we grew up with, because they feel familiar, even when familiar means harmful. - Love-bombing vulnerability: if you were starved of validation growing up, someone who floods you with praise feels like the answer to everything. That hunger causes you to ignore red flags. The worse the deprivation, the stronger the craving, the more flags you'll ignore.

The work is introspective: trace the chain of feelings and actions that led you to a bad place. In detail. What were you feeling? What did you do? What did they do that felt good? What were you chasing? Because you walked toward this — not voluntarily, not culpably, but step by step. And if you can see the steps, you can take different ones next time.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to fix your mind while ignoring your circumstances. Mental health work is not independent of material reality. Address the roof-over-your-head problem.
  • Mistaking "I can't think of a contextual explanation" for "there is no contextual explanation." The abstract thinker's brain genuinely struggles with concrete variables. Difficulty finding them is the symptom, not the answer.
  • Staying in the abstract when things are bad. Big-picture thinking about why the world is unfair feels like insight but produces no next action.
  • Thinking narcissistic conditioningThe brain's mechanism for making you repeat an action: when a behavior produces dopamine, the nucleus accumbens registers that reward and generates motivation to do the same thing again.Full definition → means you're a narcissist. It means you were taught a mode of relating. The key sign is noticing whether you're always the object — whether things keep happening to you.
  • Trying to find the right people instead of examining your own patterns. The frame of "I just need to find better humans" keeps the locus of change outside you.
  • Arguing logic with someone who is emotionally activated. Their logic is downstream of their ego, which is downstream of their pain. Logic will not reach them. The emotion has to move first.
  • Confusing ego-statements with facts. "I am a loser" is an ego-statement. "I failed this exam" is a fact. The ego-statement shuts down future action; the fact contains information about what to change.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Sort material from psychological problems. Make a list of what is causing your stress. Mark which items are genuine material threats (housing, income, safety). These have to be worked on in the physical world — no amount of reframing substitutes for a roof. Then separately list the psychological things to work on.

  2. Use networking instead of mass-applying. Go to one job fair, professional event, or conference in your field this month. Talk to people at booths. Ask directly if they're hiring. Trade contact information. One genuine human interaction is worth more than a hundred AI-filtered applications.
