The Two Traps: Fighting and Feeding

When an anxious thought shows up, the gut reaction is to push it away. That works exactly as well as trying not to think about a pink elephant — the thought comes back, usually stronger.

The other instinct is to do whatever makes the feeling stop. Avoid the scary situation. Check the texts. Seek the reassurance. This works beautifully in the short term, which is precisely the problem. Every time a behavior reduces anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →, the brain logs that behavior as useful and reinforces it. The relief you feel is positive reinforcement — the same mechanism that makes a dog keep coming back to whoever fed it last.

"Every time that we give in to anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → it actually grows a little bit stronger. And just like a dog it becomes hungrier and actually needs more food."

Over time, the behavior you used to calm yourself down becomes compulsive. You don't choose it anymore; you need it. And the threshold for needing it keeps dropping.

A Case Study in Feeding the Beast

Consider a woman who had been cheated on by a previous partner and later moved in with a kind, trustworthy new boyfriend. A small, understandable doubt crept in: what if he's doing something shady?

At first she suppressed it. Then one night she opened his phone, found nothing wrong, and felt immediate relief. That relief was the first feeding. A few days later the thought returned — and this time she didn't wait as long before checking. Over weeks it became part of her nightly routine, as automatic as brushing her teeth.

When her boyfriend eventually caught her, she fought the urge hard for months. But then she met one of his coworkers at a happy hour and noticed chemistry between them. The anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → came roaring back. She resumed checking — more carefully this time, cleverly avoiding messages marked as unread.

The outcome was worse than before. Even benign texts between him and his coworker started looking suspicious. The more she checked, the more paranoid she became; the more paranoid she became, the more she needed to check. Checking was supposed to produce certainty; instead it produced a hunger for more certainty.

"She was positively reinforcing the behavior that reduces anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → by giving into it."

There is also a subtler cost. Every time she checked the phone to know for sure, she was quietly eroding trust. Trust is not the state of having 100% certainty. It is the willingness to not have 100% certainty — to extend faith into the gap. Each check was practice in the opposite of trust.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the feeling before acting. The next time you feel an anxious urge — to check something, avoid something, ask for reassurance — pause and say (internally or aloud) exactly what you're feeling. "I'm anxious. I want to check." Just naming it, then waiting a few minutes, is a complete practice session.

  2. Trace your reassurance behaviors. Write down the things you do regularly to reduce anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →. Phone checks, avoidances, searches, asking people "am I okay?" Look at how the behavior has changed over time — has it increased in frequency? Do you need more of it than you used to? This tells you whether you've been feeding the beast.

  3. Sit with the feeling for a set amount of time. When the urge to act hits, set a short timer — even two or three minutes. You're not committing to never addressing the anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →. You're just not acting on it right now. Notice what happens to the intensity of the feeling while the timer runs.

  4. Bring a trusted person into the loop. If there's someone you trust, practice saying the anxious thought out loud to them without asking them to resolve it. "I'm feeling really paranoid right now and I want to check X" is different from "please tell me I have nothing to worry about." The first builds toleranceSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition →; the second is just asking someone else to feed the beast for you.

  5. Notice the hunger in small, low-stakes moments. Pay attention to minor versions of this — the pull to find out how a story ends, to check if someone texted back, to look up a trivial answer. Each one is a small, safe opportunity to practice noticing the hunger without immediately satisfying it. These small reps build the capacity for the harder situations.
