This conversation uses one person's lifelong struggle with catastrophic thinking — fear of flying, worst-case-scenario spiraling, compulsive reassurance-seeking — as a window into how anxiety actually works, where it comes from, and what to do about it.

Anxiety Is Not a Bug in Your Brain

The single most important reframe: your anxious mind is not broken. It is doing exactly what a well-functioning predictive brain is supposed to do — scan for danger and alert you before something bad happens.

Think about the evolutionary picture. Two ancient monkeys see tiger tracks in the dirt. One thinks probably nothing, the other thinks there might be a tiger behind that bush. The first monkey gets eaten. The second one's descendants are us. Anxiety is the survival mechanism that kept your lineage alive. Calling it irrational doesn't switch it off; it just means you're trying to fix something that isn't broken, using a framework that doesn't match reality.

"Anxiety is not an enemy. It's actually a really important member of your party."

The better you are at projecting catastrophe into the future, the worse your anxiety tends to be — and, paradoxically, the better you often perform professionally, because you notice risks and pitfalls others walk right past.

Where Anxiety Comes From

Anxiety generally has two sources, and they can combine:

1. ConditioningThe brain's mechanism for repeating actions that produced pleasure or relief — when a behavior leads to a dopamine release (or suppresses negative emotion), the nucleus accumbens signals "do that again," building…Full definition → (being taught to fear) Parents, and other early caregivers, are the primary way our minds learn. If a parent repeatedly communicated that the world is full of lethal consequences — don't ride without your helmet or you'll get a traumatic brain injury; it only takes one time with drugs — a child's brain doesn't file that away as safety advice. It internalizes a voice that scans every situation for the worst possible outcome. That voice is not you being irrational; it is you doing exactly what you were taught.

There's a striking research finding that illustrates this: in children diagnosed with anxiety disorders whose parents also have anxiety, treating the parent produces the same improvement in the child as treating the child directly. The source of the anxiety isn't just genetic — it's transmitted through the emotional environment.

2. A real catastrophe that happened When something genuinely terrible occurs — especially something with an astronomically low probability — your brain updates its model of the world. That one-in-a-million event is no longer theoretical; it happened. The turbulence on a flight that felt out of control, the strange package on the plane that felt threatening — these experiences give the anxious part of your mind evidence that its fears are justified. After that, the anxiety isn't irrational at all. It's a very rational response to lived experience.

"Your fears are real. They're not wrong."

Why "It's Irrational" Makes Things Worse

Almost everyone with anxiety knows, intellectually, that their fear is irrational. And almost everyone discovers that knowing this does absolutely nothing to help. Here's why:

Calling a fear irrational is a form of self-invalidation. You're telling one part of your mind that it doesn't know what it's talking about. That part doesn't shut up — it gets louder, because it's trying to protect you and you're dismissing it.

More practically: if labeling anxiety as irrational actually worked, people would stop having anxiety after figuring that out. But they don't. The data — your own lived experience — tells you it doesn't work. So it isn't a useful tool, even if it feels logical to reach for it.

The reframe that actually helps is to approach anxiety the way you'd approach any poorly understood system: assume it's working correctly, and try to understand what it's responding to.

The Reassurance Trap

When anxiety makes a demand, the natural instinct is to feed it. Check the flight data on your phone. Look up the weather. Ask a friend if you're a good person. Monitor the airspeed indicator for the entire flight.

In the moment, this works. Anxiety gets what it wants, it quiets down, relief arrives. But this is the same relationship as hunger and food — the relief is temporary, and the next time the same need comes back, often stronger. Reassurance doesn't address the source; it just silences the symptom and teaches the anxious part of you that asking loudly gets results.

This is why endlessly reassuring someone that they are great doesn't build their confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →. It feeds the moment, and the hunger comes back. A genuine solution has to go somewhere deeper.

What to Actually Do: Sit With It

The alternative to feeding anxiety or fighting it is a third path — simply being with it, without doing anything.

This is not distraction (putting on a happy song to take your mind off the flight), and it's not suppression (white-knuckling through the fear). It's noticing the anxious voice, recognizing where it comes from, and letting it be present without either obeying it or dismissing it.

In practice, this looks like: - Recognizing when the anxious voice activates (okay, here comes that part of my mind) - Asking honestly: does this part of me really need something right now, or can I get by without giving it anything? - If it genuinely needs something, give it something — without shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, and without pretending you didn't - Watching what happens after: does the anxiety settle? Does it escalate? Start to understand its pattern

The goal is familiarity, not elimination. You can't fix what you won't look at, and you can't look at something you keep judging and dismissing.

The chanting exercise at the end of the conversation is a small demonstration of this principle. The anxiety about giggling during something "serious" vanished almost immediately once it was acknowledged, named, and explicitly permitted. The same mechanism applies to bigger fears.

"If you look at it, it just goes away. That's the whole point."

On Privilege and the Right to Suffer

There's a quieter thread running through this conversation worth naming. The instinct to invalidate your own pain because you're "blessed" — I have a great life, I drive a Lambo (or don't), who am I to complain — is a version of the same self-dismissal that makes anxiety worse.

Suffering is not a competition with a finite supply. Acknowledging that your parents shaped your anxiety in some ways doesn't make them bad parents — it makes them human ones. You can hold two things at once: deep gratitude and honest acknowledgment that something was passed down that you'd rather not carry. Forcing yourself to choose between the two keeps you stuck.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating anxiety as an enemy to defeat. It's a part of you that's trying to help, just with outdated or miscalibrated information. Fighting it entrenches it.
  • Assuming knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → will calm anxiety down. Learning every statistic about airplane safety, becoming a pilot, memorizing turbulence data — anxiety hijacks all of it and uses it against you. Knowledge and reassurance are not the cure.
  • Confusing distraction with sitting with it. Playing music to take your mind off the feeling is different from reframing the feeling while staying present with it. One avoids; the other processes.
  • Waiting until you understand it perfectly before trying to change. Skepticism that anxiety can get better is completely rational when you've had it your whole life and nothing has worked. That skepticism itself is a symptom, not evidence that change is impossible.
  • Minimizing symptoms to doctors or anyone who can help. If you routinely reassure people that it's "not that bad" or "I can still function," you're blocking the information flow that would let someone actually help you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the voice, don't obey or dismiss it. Next time anxiety activates, try to identify whose "voice" it sounds like — a parent, a past experience, a learned belief. Don't argue with it. Just notice it.

  2. Ask one question before you reassure yourself. When you reach for your phone, or start checking, or seek confirmation from someone else, pause and ask: does this part of me genuinely need this right now, or can I sit here for a moment first? You don't have to deprive yourself — just create a small gap of awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

  3. Get a proper evaluation. If anxiety is crippling any part of your life, see a mental health professional (ideally one who does more than prescribe). Rule out physical contributors like thyroid imbalance through basic lab work. Ask about psychotherapy — it's as effective as medication, and both together outperform either alone.

  4. Try a brief meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → or chanting practice. The point isn't to eliminate the silliness or the discomfort. The point is to notice whatever arises — including anxiety, laughter, the urge to open your eyes — without acting on it or judging it. Three slow breath cycles is enough to start. Pay attention to where you feel sensation in your body, not the thoughts in your head.

  5. Practice holding two things at once. Find one area of your life where you've been forcing a false choice — either your parents were great or they contributed to your anxiety; either you're privileged or you're allowed to struggle. Write down both statements. Sit with the discomfort of them both being true simultaneously. This is the actual work.
