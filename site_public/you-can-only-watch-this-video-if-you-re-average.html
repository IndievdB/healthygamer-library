Summary

TL;DR — Most mental health advice targets people in crisis or people chasing peak performance. If you're somewhere in the middle — functional, not miserable, not exceptional — here's what actually applies to you.

The vast majority of people aren't climbing out of addiction or launching startups. They have jobs, relationships, routines, and a general sense of okayness. Almost nothing in psychology or self-help is aimed at them, which creates its own quiet problem. There are two distinct versions of that problem, and they pull in opposite directions.

The Two Types of "Average" Problems

The first type: you're internally drawn toward something — a passion, a risk, a version of yourself that feels alive — but a rational, self-protective voice keeps killing it before it gets started.

The second type: you're genuinely content with a moderate life, but the external world keeps telling you that contentment is a character flaw.

These look like opposite problems, but they share a root: a mismatch between what's happening inside you and what the world around you is saying you should feel.

Risk Aversion and Passion Aversion

If you've ever had an impulse — to learn an instrument, go skydiving, quit your safe major, start something — and then immediately talked yourself out of it with a list of reasons why it's stupid, you're doing this.

Risk aversion and passion aversion often show up together. They're usually baked in early. Parents narrow the menu ("you can do law, finance, or computer science — that's it"), and over time you internalize that same narrowing. The logical, cautious part of you gets so good at killing ideas that you stop noticing you're doing it.

The first move is just to notice. Pay attention to the moments when an impulse arises and something immediately dismisses it. You don't have to fight that critical voice or "overcome" it dramatically. Just observe it. Notice that there are things you've wanted to do that you've been quietly squashing, possibly for years.

The second move is to start leaning — gently — in the direction of the thing you keep dismissing. Not recklessly. Nobody is saying blow up your life. But if you've always wanted to learn to sing, sign up for lessons. If you've wanted to try something that feels embarrassing, try it. The goal isn't to become exceptional overnight; it's to stop actively suppressing that part of yourself.

"There's a rational part of them that is risk averse — it's telling them all the reasons why this is a bad idea."

When you read those action items and feel a small flicker of yeah, but that's ridiculous — that reaction is the thing to notice. That's the pattern in real time.

You're Allowed to Be Content

The second type of average person has a different problem. They're not secretly suppressing passions — they're actually fine. A decent job, a decent relationship, a decent life. And they feel vaguely guilty about it.

The world — social media, hustle culture, the people around them — keeps broadcasting that contentment with ordinary life is something between laziness and delusion. So they sit with this background anxiety: Should I be grinding? Will I regret this later? Am I wasting my potential?

Here's the thing: the fear of future regret, about a life that is currently going fine, is not real motivation. It's just discomfort. Trying to grind because you're afraid you might one day wish you had ground is one of the least effective ways to actually build a life. You're sacrificing present contentment to solve a hypothetical problem that may never arrive.

"You can live your entire life doing that — and I have worked with people who are 70 years old who lived their entire life avoiding future problems, and then the problem they have is they wasted their life solving problems that never arose."

Meanwhile, the people the world holds up as the goal — wealthy, beautiful, successful — a significant portion of them are deeply unhappy. External achievement does not reliably produce internal contentment. The people who are content and successful tend to be notable because they're the exception.

A Framework for What Actually Matters

Indian philosophy (specifically the Hindu framework of the four purusharthas) offers a useful map here — not as a religious prescription, but as a practical sequence:

  1. Dharma — fulfill basic duties: be a decent person, contribute, show up
  2. Artha — achieve basic security: financial stability, a roof, a foundation
  3. Kama — enjoy life: pleasure, relationships, good food, small adventures
  4. Moksha — seek deeper meaning: spiritual or existential questions about your purpose

What's useful about this framework is the sequence. You don't have to solve all four at once. It's actually normal and expected to spend your 20s and 30s working on the first three, and only start seriously wrestling with meaning and legacy later. A midlife crisis isn't a failure — it's what happens when you've handled the basics and your psyche starts asking the next question. Erik Erikson's developmental psychology maps onto this too: different life stages bring different psychological tasks, and it's normal for the deepest existential questions to emerge in later life.

Trying to solve your 60-year-old problems at 26 — preemptively grinding to avoid regret you haven't felt — is just a way to be miserable now while not actually preventing anything.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the voice of risk aversion for wisdom. The internal critic that dismisses your passions sounds reasonable. It uses logic. It cites practical concerns. That's what makes it hard to spot. Reasonable-sounding is not the same as correct.

Confusing external pressure with internal desire. If you're grinding because you genuinely want something, that's one thing. If you're grinding because you're afraid of what people will think of you for not grinding, those are completely different and they produce completely different results.

Thinking contentment is complacency. Being okay with where you are right now is not the same as giving up. It does not mean you won't grow or change. It means you're not torturing yourself in the present to solve imaginary future problems.

Waiting to feel content until you've "earned" it. There's no threshold of achievement that makes it officially acceptable to feel okay. People who keep moving that threshold tend to keep moving it.

Assuming exceptional is the only worthwhile destination. Not everyone needs to be exceptional. That is a real option. The majority of people who live meaningful, satisfying lives are not famous, wealthy, or optimized.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Inventory your squashed impulses. Sit down and write out things you've wanted to do — learn a skill, try an experience, pursue an interest — that you dismissed as silly, impractical, or embarrassing. Don't filter. Just list them. The point is to see how many there are and notice what they have in common.

  2. Catch the critic in real time. For the next week, pay attention to small moments when an impulse arises and something immediately says no, that's dumb. You don't have to act on every impulse. Just notice the pattern — how fast the dismissal comes, how automatic it is.

  3. Take one small step toward something you've been suppressing. Not a dramatic leap. Look up singing lessons. Find out how much skydiving actually costs. Sign up for a beginner class in something you've told yourself you're too old or too uncool for. The goal is to interrupt the habit of automatic suppression, not to become a professional.

  4. Audit your "I should be grinding" feeling. When you feel that background guilt about not doing more, ask yourself: Is this coming from something I actually want, or from what I think I'm supposed to want? Write down where that pressure is actually coming from — specific people, platforms, comparisons.

  5. Check the four basics. Are you doing reasonably okay on duty (showing up, being responsible), security (financial stability), pleasure (some enjoyment in your daily life), and meaning (a sense that your life matters to you)? If the first three are mostly covered and the fourth feels unresolved, that's normal — not a crisis. You don't have to solve all four right now.

  6. Give yourself explicit permission to be content today. This sounds too simple to be useful, but try it: write down, in a sentence or two, that it's okay to feel okay right now. That you don't have to earn it. That present contentment doesn't mean future failure. Then notice what your reaction to reading that sentence is — that reaction will tell you something.
