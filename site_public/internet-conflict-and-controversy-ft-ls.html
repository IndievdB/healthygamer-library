Why Internet Attacks Hit So Hard

When you're strawmanned online, two things happen simultaneously and they feel like one thing:

  1. An external event — someone misrepresents you, people watch the video, some of them form a wrong impression.
  2. An internal reaction — your sense of purpose, self-worth, and stability collapse.

The external event is actually smaller than it feels. In LS's case, the community largely defended him. People were pointing out the strawman on their own. He had significant evidence that his reputation was intact. And yet, for the first thirty-five minutes of the conversation, that didn't come up. What came up was the suffering — the paranoia that the video was doing invisible damage, the obsessive awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → that it had been up for ten hours before he saw it, the feeling that it was impossible to reach everyone who watched without reading the comments.

"I can't manage the people who don't read the comments and just watch the video. I don't know what that number is."

That unquantifiable, uncontrollable number is the real source of the pain. Not the video. The gap between what you can influence and what you cannot — and the fact that your brain keeps trying to close that gap anyway.

The Control-Suffering Loop

Your well-being is tied to your perception of other people's perception of you. Read that again, because it applies to almost everyone, not just people in public-facing careers.

When you internalize other people's opinions as the measure of your own okay-ness, you are permanently at the mercy of something you cannot directly touch. So you develop strategies. You monitor Twitter. You check Reddit. You respond quickly to attacks before they "fester." You correct people who are defending you "incorrectly." Every one of these behaviors is an attempt to manage what cannot be managed.

The loop works like this: - You make a judgment: people's opinion of me is currently wrong/insufficient/at risk - That judgment produces a desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → to control: I need to fix this - You can't fully control it, which produces suffering - The suffering confirms the original judgment: see, something really is wrong

Judgment and control are inseparable. You cannot want to control something without first judging it as not being where it should be. And you cannot stop trying to control something while you're still judging it. The only exit is learning to be with what is, rather than what should be.

"The only way you can tear yourself apart is to control that which you cannot control."

Your Thoughts Are Not Facts

The mind operates as if its opinions are truth. Most of the time this is adaptive — you assume the toilet will flush, you reach for the light switch without thinking. But this same mechanism, applied to social perception, becomes a trap.

Someone with social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → treats the thought everyone thinks I'm an idiot as if it were a verified fact, and suffers accordingly. LS treated the thought this video is destroying my credibility with people I can't reach as a fact, even as the visible evidence pointed in the opposite direction.

Your mind will tell you that someone defending you is doing it "blindly" or "inaccurately." It will tell you that 10 hours is a long time. It will tell you that you already know the unknowable number of people who watched without reading. None of these are facts. They are your mind's projections, and your mind is fallible.

This doesn't mean you're stupid. It means you're human. The problem isn't having these thoughts. The problem is treating them as unquestionable.

Why You Blame Yourself (And Why That's Actually Protective)

When bad things happen with no clear cause — years that feel stolen, a childhood that wasn't what it should have been, a career that took longer than it should have — the mind reaches for an explanation. Blaming yourself is painful, but it is less terrifying than the alternative: it just happened, randomly, and no one is responsible.

A world where random bad things happen to you for no reason is unlivable. You can't function if you believe a meteor might hit you every time you step outside. So the mind manufactures an agent — usually you — and assigns responsibility. The suffering is real, but it is, paradoxically, a form of self-protection.

Recognizing this doesn't erase the pain. But it changes its meaning. You are not weak because you suffer from anxiety and fear. You are not defective because you overreacted on stream. These are not character indictments. They are the ordinary results of being a mind trying to survive.

The Mask Problem and Self-Concept

There's a concept LS raises: we wear different faces for strangers, acquaintances, close friends, and ourselves. His complaint about himself wasn't about the public-facing layers — he was largely okay with those. It was the deeper ones: the private self that feels crippled by anxiety, that reacts with anger, that struggles to be what he wants to be.

But notice the contradiction: his criteria for evaluating a person is actions and choices over time. And his actions over time — the analysis, the teaching, the reflection, the willingness to examine himself publicly and try to improve — are things he's actually okay with. He likes what he does. He dislikes who he is. But if what you are is built from what you do, these aren't two separate things.

The "true self" that feels shameful is largely made of thoughts, fears, and impulses — not actions. Blaming yourself for having fears is like blaming yourself for being human. The relevant question is what you do with them.

Isness: A Meditation Practice for Control-Addicted Minds

Standard meditation techniques — focus on the breath, count to four, watch your thoughts — can make an analytically anxious mind worse. If that's you, stop doing them. There are other approaches.

The technique introduced here is contemplative rather than doing-based. The goal is to practice isness: sitting with what actually is, without attaching a judgment of good, bad, sufficient, or insufficient.

What the practice looks like:

  1. Sit in a posture — any posture
  2. Close your eyes and notice what you physically feel: heartbeat, tension, temperature, the urge to move
  3. If your body wants to move, let it move. Don't suppress it and don't force it
  4. Notice the difference between physical wanting-to-move and mental wanting-to-move (these are often different things)
  5. When a sensation resolves, notice that it resolved without you controlling it
  6. Sit with five different postures each day and repeat

The key insight from the live practice: when you stop forcing your leg to stay still, the urge to move resolves on its own. You don't have to manage it. The act of managing was creating the problem.

"What you're doing day to day is the trick. That's the problem."

Your mind going "a million places" cannot be where it actually is. Training it to return to present sensation — through repetition, the way you'd train a dribble — gradually makes that return automatic.

When a housemate told LS "the cable broke, go buy a new one," it worked instantly. That's isness applied externally by someone else. The practice is learning to do it for yourself.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking your suffering is about the external event. The video, the strawman, the 10 hours — these feel like the cause. They're the trigger. The actual cause is the attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → to controlling perception.

Mistaking reassurance for resolution. Being told "people defended you" doesn't fix the underlying loop. As long as your well-being depends on other people's opinions, every new reassurance just temporarily fills a bucket with a hole in it.

Believing that staying alert prevents damage. Monitoring constantly, responding quickly, correcting defenders — none of this actually protects your reputation as much as consistently doing good work over time. The community will defend you if you've earned it. You cannot manufacture that by staying vigilant.

Forcing meditation techniques that agitate you. If focusing on your breath worsens your anxiety, that technique isn't for you. Don't push through it. There are many approaches; find one that moves toward neutral, not toward distress.

Judging your inner self as separate from your actions. The shameful "fourth mask" is mostly made of thoughts and feelings, not deeds. If your honest actions over time are something you can stand behind, that is what you are.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice the five-posture meditation daily. Choose five different sitting positions. Spend a few minutes in each with eyes closed. Notice physical sensations without labeling them good or bad. If your body wants to shift, let it — then notice what changed. Do this every day for at least two weeks before evaluating whether it helps.

  2. When an online event upsets you, ask: what specifically can I not control here? Write it down. Name the uncontrollable thing precisely (e.g., "people who watched without reading comments"). Naming it doesn't fix the feeling, but it separates the external fact from the internal reaction.

  3. Delay your response to attacks. Not because ten hours is a long time, but because the urgency you feel is itself data — it tells you how tightly your self-worth is tied to the outcome. Practicing not responding immediately is practicing toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → of the uncontrollable.

  4. Notice when your mind treats a thought as a fact. When you catch yourself believing something like "they're blindly defending me" or "this is damaging my credibility," pause and ask: what's the actual evidence? Not to replace the thought with a better one, but to remind yourself the thought is a thought.

  5. When you find yourself spiraling about purpose or wasted time, return to isness. Use the physical anchor: close your eyes, find one sensation in your body, stay with it for sixty seconds. This is the cable-broke-go-buy-a-new-one move you can do for yourself.

  6. Stop suppressing and stop forcing. In conversation, in work, in your own mind — notice when you're clenching something still that wants to move. The goal isn't to force relaxation; it's to let the clench go and see what happens.
