Summary

TL;DR — Social anxiety isn't about being broken or weak; it's about a set of learned programs running in your mind that can be noticed, challenged, and gradually retrained.

This is a raw, honest conversation between Dr. K and Lou (Poopernoodle), a streamer who has lived with severe social anxiety — the kind that led to hiding in a dorm room for months, peeing in a sink to avoid flatmates, and using alcohol as the only way to feel socially "normal." What unfolds is less a how-to guide and more a live demonstration of how anxiety actually works from the inside: where it comes from, why it's so sticky, and what you can actually do about it.

How Social Anxiety Begins to Make Sense

Anxiety doesn't come from nowhere. In Lou's case, a pattern emerged over years: a quiet childhood that was actually fine, then a series of experiences — being excluded in a friend group, being mocked for her body, being assaulted — that slowly installed a core belief. The belief wasn't "bad things happen." It was something more personal: this person wants nothing to do with me.

That phrase is worth sitting with. A huge portion of social anxiety can be traced back to some version of it. And crucially, it says nothing about the other person. It says something about how you've come to perceive your own worth.

The shift happened somewhere between the girl who was content playing with toy cars by herself — who had no problem with her own company — and the adult who dreads being seen. That's not a personality trait. That's a wound with a traceable origin.

You're Not Misreading Faces — You're Overcalibrated

One thing that often gets mislabeled as paranoia is actually a form of heightened perception. Research shows that faces exist on a spectrum: a face can be 80% happy and 20% angry at the same time. Most people, when they see that blend, just read "happy." But people with social anxiety — or people who grew up needing to track whether a parent's good mood was genuine or about to flip — can detect that 20% dissatisfaction even when it's buried under 80% warmth.

"It's not that you're detecting false things. You're just detecting a small amount of dissatisfaction in spite of the large amount of approval."

The problem isn't the detection itself. The problem is that your brain zooms in on the 20% and ignores the rest. That's what needs retraining — not the sensitivity, which is actually a sophisticated skill.

The Weeks-Turn-Into-Months Trap

One of the most recognizable patterns in social anxiety is how avoidance compounds. Lou described it perfectly: freshers week was fine, then she stayed in her room for a week, then it felt too weird to come out, then two weeks had passed, then months. The logic is: I can't show up now because I missed so much.

The same trap plays out everywhere — skipping one class and then feeling unable to return, not sending a message for a few days and then the silence becoming too big to break, waiting 20 minutes to mention a stuck seatbelt because it's been too long now to bring it up.

"The only way you can show up the next day is to have been perfect the day before."

This is what speedrunning your life looks like. In a video game where you replay the same level, perfection makes sense — one mistake, reset, try again. But life doesn't replay. Every day is a new game, and playing for a flawless run means you stop playing at all.

The Rigged Game: How Self-Devaluation Gets Installed

Lou grew up being told she made herself a victim whenever something hurt her. That framing — you're just playing the victim — is a way of dodging accountability by making the person who got hurt responsible for the hurt. When you absorb that message, it quietly corrupts the internal voice that should say this isn't okay or I deserve better.

"Blaming the victim is the last refuge of [people who don't want to take responsibility]. It's a very easy way to dodge any kind of personal responsibility."

What happens next is subtle but devastating. When something unfair happens to you, your mind pre-empts sympathy — including your own — by calling you materialistic, oversensitive, weak, stupid. You devalue your own experience before anyone else can. Lou does this constantly: she calls herself materialistic for caring about a birthday that wasn't acknowledged, calls herself stupid for trusting someone who hurt her, labels herself pathetic for crying about being called by the wrong name.

Notice the structure of that game: Lou doesn't get to complain, and when something goes wrong, it's always Lou's fault. No one wins that game.

Why People Search for Certainty (Even Certain Bad News)

A counterintuitive feature of anxiety: given a choice between knowing for certain that something bad will happen versus not knowing whether something bad will happen, anxious minds often prefer the certainty.

"You'll take a certain bad thing over an uncertain bad thing."

If you knock yourself down first, you control the pain. You know what it feels like. You've survived it before. Hope is dangerous because hope means things could be better — and if you let yourself believe that and then fall, it hurts far more than just landing where you expected to be.

This is also why Lou would go online and search for nasty comments about herself on good days. Not out of masochism, but to manage uncertainty. To stay at the bottom where the terrain is familiar.

The Cost of Borrowed Confidence

Alcohol — and other substances people use to feel "normal" — works like a loan. In the moment you feel how other people seem to feel naturally: funny, relaxed, likeable, present. The problem is you pay it back with interest. The self-judgmental thoughts come back harder the next day, the relationships you tried to build often get damaged, and you reinforce the belief that sober you isn't worth being around.

"The hardest addiction to crack is when people tell me that when I use the substance, I feel the way other people look. I feel normal."

It's not easy to give up something that makes you feel normal. The ask is enormous. But it's worth recognizing that the confidence wasn't wrong — it was real. It just wasn't sustainable borrowed from alcohol. Which means there's something there that isn't alcohol-dependent. It just needs a different path to the surface.

Awareness Before Control

Here is the core mechanic for changing any of this. You cannot control something you cannot feel. Awareness precedes control, always. The moment Lou caught herself catastrophizing mid-sentence — about healing and being disbelieved and other victims being discredited — she literally forgot what she was anxious about. The thought train lost power the moment she got outside it and named it.

"If I could give you a pill that would make you literally forget your anxieties — that's what we just saw happen."

You don't eliminate the patterns by fighting them directly. You name them. You step back from them. You notice: oh, there's the devaluing-myself bot running again. The more you can spot it, the more control you have over it. It takes practice, and a therapist can help significantly with this — but the skill is learnable.

A Simple Exercise for Anxiety

Here's something concrete that came out of the session. Close your eyes. Sit with whatever comes up — the self-consciousness, the fear, the swelling sense of danger. Notice it in your body. Then open your eyes.

The room feels smaller. The fears shrink.

Close them again. They come back. Open them. They shrink again.

"Literally your fears can grow or shrink depending on what your eyelids are doing — and that's just not how the world works."

The point isn't the eyes. The point is that you're watching your fear expand and contract in real time based on your sense of vulnerability, not based on anything actually changing in the world. Your value as a person doesn't fluctuate in 90 seconds. The car isn't more likely to hit you because your eyes are closed. But the feeling changes radically — and once you can feel that, once you know how your mind inflates and deflates fear, you begin to get control over the mechanism.

This works particularly well for people who spend a lot of time in their heads. Years of internal experience isn't wasted — it's actually a head start on this kind of introspective practice.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing quietness with anxiety. Being introverted or content alone isn't the same thing as social anxiety. Something usually happens to turn one into the other. It's worth trying to identify when the shift occurred — because it wasn't always this way.

  • Trying to stop caring what others think. That's not the goal and it's not achievable by willpower. The goal is to gain distance from the caring — to notice it without being controlled by it.

  • Using mindfulness when your mind is already overwhelming you. Open observational meditation asks you to sit with your thoughts. For someone who finds their internal world frightening, that can make things worse. A more structured or directed practice — something that gives the mind a task rather than asking it to just watch — tends to work better.

  • Labeling awareness as weakness. Noticing that you catastrophized, or that you devalued yourself, or that you just blamed yourself for something unfair — that's not failure. That's the actual work. The noticing is the practice.

  • Believing the mind's confident-sounding answers. When Lou was asked how long she'd been catastrophizing, her mind immediately said "forever" — and it was wrong. Thoughts that arrive with certainty aren't more true than other thoughts. They're just louder.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Track the "this person wants nothing to do with me" thought. The next time you feel rejected, pulled away, or unwanted, notice the exact phrase your mind generates. Write it down. Ask: is this a fact, or is this a familiar program running?

  2. Try the eyes-open/eyes-closed exercise. Two to three times a week, for two or three minutes: sit somewhere you feel reasonably safe. Close your eyes. Let the fears and self-conscious thoughts come. Notice them in your body. Then open your eyes and watch them shrink. Do this repeatedly. The goal isn't relaxation — it's learning that your mind can expand and contract fear, which means you have more agency over it than you think.

  3. Practice catching self-devaluation mid-sentence. When you're explaining something that hurt you and you find yourself adding "but I'm probably just being dramatic" or "it sounds materialistic but" — stop. Notice that you just preemptively stabbed your own credibility. You don't have to fight the thought; just notice it arrived.

  4. Name the bots. There's the catastrophizing bot. There's the devaluing-yourself bot. There's the "this is going to go wrong if I don't control every variable" bot. Give them names if it helps. The moment you can say "oh, there's the devaluing-myself bot again," you've created distance between you and the thought. Distance is where change lives.

  5. If avoidance has been compounding, start with one tiny re-entry. The weeks-into-months trap only works if you keep waiting for the perfect moment to return. The longer you wait, the worse it feels. Pick the smallest possible version of the thing you've been avoiding — not the conversation, just standing near the kitchen. Not the class, just walking past the building. Break the seal.

  6. Consider therapy. This kind of reprogramming is possible on your own, but having someone on the outside — who can see the patterns you can't see because they're right in front of your face — dramatically speeds up the process. That's not a weakness. That's just how proximity and perspective work.
