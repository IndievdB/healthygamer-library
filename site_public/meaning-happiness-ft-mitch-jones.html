Summary

TL;DR — External success cannot fill an internal void, and the loneliness that comes from hiding your true self will follow you no matter how far you run from it.

This is a raw, unscripted conversation between a psychiatrist and a long-time Twitch streamer who walked away from peak career success because he was quietly falling apart. What emerges is a case study in the gap between what the world sees and what a person actually carries — and what it takes to begin closing that gap.

The Success Trap: Why Getting What You Wanted Feels Empty

It's tempting to assume that if you're not happy, you just haven't achieved enough yet. More viewers, more money, more recognition — surely that version of success will finally do it.

But there's a particular kind of devastation that comes from reaching those heights and discovering the emptiness is still there. After years of grinding toward streaming success, the moment it arrived was also the moment it became undeniable that success wasn't the problem to be solved.

"I thought if I had all the viewers and subs and all that I would be good. But I was worse than before."

The trap isn't failure — it's succeeding at the wrong thing. When the metrics go up and the feeling doesn't, you can no longer tell yourself "I just need a little more." The illusion collapses. That collapse, as brutal as it is, is also the beginning of something real.

Living a Double Life Costs You

When you perform a version of yourself professionally — a character, a persona, an optimized public face — it works. People respond to it. You get rewarded. And then, slowly, you lose track of where the performance ends and you begin.

The cost shows up in private. Long showers. Crying after streams end. Checking your heartbeat compulsively because anxiety has become your constant companion. A grinding sense that everyone around you knows Mitch Jones the streamer, and nobody knows you.

"I would go live and I'm all good when I'm live. But when I turn off the camera and it's just me alone, I'm not okay."

The persona becomes a prison not because it's entirely fake, but because it's selective. You show the parts that play well. You bury the parts that are complicated, sad, angry, or afraid. Over time, the buried parts don't disappear — they accumulate pressure.

Grief as a Wake-Up Call

Losing someone close has a way of destroying the comfortable lies we tell ourselves. When you lose a friend — especially one who knew the real you, not the character — you lose more than a person. You lose the relationship that made the double life tolerable.

"I was always living in the past hoping that the past would happen again. Once he passed away I realized the past will never happen again, and I need to stop chasing something that doesn't exist anymore."

Grief like this doesn't just make you sad. It reorganizes your priorities. The things you were tolerating become intolerable. The delay tactics you were using — I'll deal with this later, I just need one more successful year — stop working. The pain surfaces whether you invite it or not.

The question becomes: now that you're awake, what do you do with it?

The Limits of Going It Alone

There's real value in the work you can do by yourself — meditation, diet changes, unplugging from social media, getting out into nature, sitting with discomfort instead of running from it. These aren't small things. They can genuinely shift your baseline and help you shed layers of noise.

But there's a ceiling.

When the pain is rooted in something deep — family trauma, profound loss, years of loneliness — solo spiritual work can only go so far. You can open the window a crack and feel the fresh air. But at some point, you're still alone in the room with everything you've been avoiding.

"I was bleeding so hard that I didn't even want to continue."

Trying to process serious trauma alone, without support, means you're doing the equivalent of performing surgery on yourself. Some of it works. Some of it is just excruciating. And the parts that are most painful tend to be the parts you can never quite reach.

Fear Is the Thing That Keeps You Stuck

One of the most insidious patterns that emerges from early betrayal — by parents, by people who were supposed to be safe — is that you learn vulnerability gets weaponized. You open up, and it gets twisted. So you stop opening up.

That adaptation made sense at the time. It protected you. But it doesn't stay contained. The rule your nervous system learned ("sharing = danger") doesn't apply only to the people who hurt you. It bleeds into every relationship, every creative decision, every moment you think about saying something real.

"Most of the times I have made myself vulnerable, it has been twisted in some way. And I don't feel safe in that way."

You start policing your own authenticity. You don't share the deeper parts of yourself not because you don't want to, but because you've been punished for it before. The result is an ever-widening gap between who you are and who you let people see — and an ever-deepening loneliness that no amount of external success can touch.

What Healing Actually Looks Like

Healing isn't linear and it isn't pretty. It looks like opening a window you've kept shut for years and having the smell hit you all at once. It looks like losing composure in a conversation you thought you could handle. It looks like a 23-year-old standing in a room with cops, an autistic brother, and a mother with brain cancer, trying to manage a crisis his father handed him and walked away from.

"Surgery ain't pretty. Healing is more like blood magic — you pay the price to fuel that healing."

The goal of spiritual growth is not to stop feeling sad. Sadness is part of life. The goal is to find peace in the presence of sadness — not to eliminate it, but to stop being controlled by it. That's a different target, and it's actually reachable.

What moves healing forward is not willpower or isolation or more research. It's connection. It's letting one other person hold part of what you've been carrying alone. Even briefly. Even imperfectly. That moment of not being alone with it is not a small thing — it's the whole thing.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking success for happiness. Career success can coexist with profound internal suffering. Using external metrics as a proxy for internal wellbeing means you'll keep chasing the wrong target.

Spiritual bypassing. Meditation, diet, and unplugging are genuinely useful tools — but they can also become sophisticated avoidance. Cleansing your body doesn't clean out the cellar. At some point the emotional material has to be addressed directly.

Assuming forgiveness closes the wound. You can genuinely forgive someone and still have a hole left over. Forgiveness removes the active resentment; it doesn't automatically restore what was never there in the first place.

Generalizing old betrayals to new people. If the people who were supposed to be safe taught you that vulnerability gets punished, your nervous system will apply that lesson everywhere — including to people who would actually show up for you. Recognizing the pattern is the first step to interrupting it.

Going too deep too fast, alone. Opening the entire trauma archive in one sitting, without support, tends to produce flooding rather than healing. One small window at a time is not weakness — it's how the process actually works.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the gap. Take ten minutes to write about the difference between the version of yourself you present publicly and the version you experience privately. Where are they most different? What are you most afraid to show?

  2. Locate the root of your fear of vulnerability. Think back to a specific early moment when being open got you hurt. Ask yourself honestly: how much of your current guardedness is a response to that moment, not to the people around you now?

  3. Find one person to open a window with. You don't need to share everything. Share one thing that's true — something you'd normally keep to yourself. Notice what happens. The goal is not resolution; it's practice in not being alone with it.

  4. Pursue therapy with fresh expectations. If you've tried therapy before and it went nowhere, consider whether you were ready at the time. The spiritual and self-reflective work you've done since then may make you able to go somewhere you couldn't go before. Look specifically for a therapist who has experience with trauma.

  5. Let your creative work be real, not resolved. If you write, make music, or create anything — let it carry the actual pain, not a cleaned-up version of it. The most connecting art is not the art that has the answers; it's the art that names the experience honestly.

  6. Slow down the pace of healing. If you find yourself trying to process everything at once — all the grief, all the family pain, all the regret — that's a sign to pull back. Choose one thing. Sit with it. Return to the rest later.

  7. Don't walk it alone. Whatever the journey looks like for you — music, therapy, difficult conversations — build in at least one other person who knows what you're actually carrying. Not because you can't do it alone, but because you shouldn't have to.
