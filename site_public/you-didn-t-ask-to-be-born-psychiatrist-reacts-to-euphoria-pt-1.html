Birth Is Already a Trauma

Before you had any opinions about the world, the world was already doing things to you.

The psychoanalyst Otto Rank argued that the first major traumatic experience every human being goes through is simply being born. In the womb, every need is met automatically — warmth, nutrition, waste removal, sensory buffering — without you having to do anything. The amniotic fluid even dampens vibration and sound. It is, in the most literal sense, a perfectly managed environment.

Then birth happens. Suddenly you're cold, hungry, overstimulated, and completely unable to communicate what you need or even understand what you're feeling. You don't know what "hungry" is — you just know that something is wrong and you can't fix it.

The analyst Bion extended this further: in those first days and weeks, the infant brain has no framework for filtering raw experience. There's no language, no emotional vocabulary, no logic — just an unprocessed flood of sensation. Contentment arrives, then disappears. You have no control over when it comes back.

"You just feel hungry. You don't even know what hungry is. All you know is that there are all these signals working their way through your body that make you feel discontent."

This, these analysts argued, is where the basic template for anxiety gets stamped in. Anxiety is fundamentally worry about the future — what if things don't work out? — and that worry is first learned when you're an infant who cannot predict whether the next stretch of discomfort will end. The cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition → spike that accompanies birth is so significant it's actually physiologically necessary: it helps mature the lungs. Stress and survival are intertwined from literally the first breath.

How Consistency (or Its Absence) Shapes Your Whole Nervous System

What happens after birth matters enormously. Specifically, how consistent your caregivers are determines how safe the world feels to you on a very deep level.

Think about why toddlers are fearless around gravity. They'll jump off sofas, drop food off highchairs, fling themselves onto trampolines. That confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → doesn't come from intelligence or experience — it comes from the fact that gravity is perfectly consistent. It doesn't have moods. It's never drunk, never distracted, never randomly harsh.

Consistent caregiving works the same way. When a parent responds to a child's needs reliably, the child learns: the world is predictable, and I'm going to be okay. That's the foundation of secure attachment.

The second ingredient is emotional mirroringThe ability to literally feel what another person is feeling, rather than simply understanding it intellectually.Full definition →. Infants don't arrive knowing what their own emotions are. They look at the faces of adults to understand what they're feeling. When you fall down and a parent rushes over with concern — "Oh no, are you okay?" — you learn what hurt is supposed to feel like and that it warrants care. When you bring over a scribbled drawing beaming with pride and a parent matches that energy, you learn that your joy is real and shareable.

When these two things — consistency and mirroring — are present, children develop secure attachment: low anxiety, low avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →, willingness to engage with the world and with other people.

When they're absent or inconsistent, two other patterns tend to form:

  • Anxious attachment — high anxiety, but still reaching toward others. Often develops from unpredictable caregiving, where love and comfort came sometimes but not reliably.
  • Avoidant attachmentA pattern of relating to others in which a person keeps people at emotional arm's length — not because they don't crave connection, but because connection feels dangerous.Full definition → — fiercely independent, difficulty forming deep emotional bonds. This often develops when a child shares emotional needs and is repeatedly not met. The lesson learned is: if I'm vulnerable with people, they won't show up — so the safest thing is to stop needing people.

"The lesson I learn is: if I share my emotional needs with people and they don't meet them, I can't rely on people. So it doesn't necessarily make me anxious, but it keeps me separated from others."

Secure attachment is protective against substance use. The other three styles are not. This is the attachment foundation that the show Euphoria deliberately sets up through Rue's backstory.

The World You're Born Into

Rue is born three days after September 11, 2001. Her parents spend the first 48 hours of her life in a hospital room watching coverage of the attacks.

Children have no logic in those first days. What they do have is empathy — a raw sensitivity to the emotional state of the people around them. Children who grew up in chaotic or abusive homes often describe knowing within seconds of a parent walking through the door whether they were in danger that day. That attunement is extraordinarily precise because it had to be.

So what does it mean to be a newborn bonding with parents who are terrified and grieving? That question doesn't produce a clean one-to-one answer — plenty of people born around 9/11 have secure attachment. But it raises something worth sitting with: the emotional atmosphere during those first bonding hours is not neutral background noise.

Beyond the individual, there's a broader pattern worth naming. Young people today have grown up through an unusual concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → of large-scale crises:

  • 9/11 and the years of heightened fear and war that followed
  • The 2007–2008 global financial collapse
  • Growing up with ongoing, credible scientific concern about climate change
  • A global pandemic that shut down daily life for two years

Each of these, taken alone, would be significant. Together, they form something like a constant low-level hum of existential uncertainty that previous generations didn't experience in the same concentrated way — not because the past was easier, but because the information environment is different now.

The internet surfaces the most emotionally charged content first. That means the catastrophes, the outrages, the disasters — from everywhere in the world, constantly — are what float to the top. The cumulative psychological effect of absorbing that day after day is a kind of ambient dread that's hard to name but easy to feel.

"What is it like to be a 20-year-old kid existing in the world today?"

When the womb represented perfect contentment and the world outside offers repeated uncertainty and stress, it's not difficult to understand why some people — consciously or not — are looking for a way back to something that feels like that original state of relief.

Why Drugs Are Getting Harder to Resist

Substance use isn't worsening just because people are weaker. The supply side has changed dramatically.

  • Potency has increased: Decades of selective breeding have made marijuana dramatically stronger than it was 40 years ago, and that's before accounting for synthetic variants.
  • Variety has expanded: More types and variants mean people can find a substance that fits their specific neurochemistry almost perfectly. Different people have different dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → responses to different drugs — one person's brain lights up with alcohol, another's with cannabis. More options means a higher chance that someone finds their particular match.
  • Personalized addiction is selection pressure: When you try five variants of a substance and find the one that works precisely for you, your addictive potential with that substance increases significantly.

This combines with the psychological backdrop described above. People aren't just encountering random substances — they're encountering an expanding menu of highly optimized substances at a time when existential stress is high and attachment foundations may be shaky.

Common Pitfalls

Treating addiction as purely a brain/chemistry problem. Neurotransmitters and genetic vulnerability are real, but they're incomplete explanations. The attachment history, the birth environment, and the broader social context all contribute in ways that a purely pharmacological model misses.

Assuming a traumatic event = a damaged person. Being born during 9/11, or growing up in a difficult household, doesn't automatically produce insecure attachment or addiction. The relationship is probabilistic, not deterministic. Overgeneralizing causes real harm to people who lived through those events and are doing fine.

Dismissing avoidant attachment as "just being independent." Fierce independence that comes from learned distrust of emotional vulnerability is a coping mechanism, not a personality strength. Mistaking it for health means the underlying pattern never gets addressed.

Assuming the modern world is all bad or all good. Both framings miss the actual psychological challenge: life has measurably improved in many material ways and the information environment creates a psychological experience of ongoing catastrophe. Holding both is uncomfortable but more accurate.

Pathologizing normal variation in children. A kid counting objects at dinner is probably just a kid. The pressure to have an explanation for everything a child does — and the fear of missing a diagnosis — can turn ordinary behavior into a source of parental anxiety that then gets transmitted right back to the child.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your own attachment style. Reflect honestly on whether you tend toward security, anxiety, or avoidance in close relationships. Notice whether you pull away when things get emotionally intense, or whether uncertainty in a relationship sends you into a spiral. You don't need a formal test — just honest observation over a few weeks.

  2. Trace anxiety back to its earliest form. When you feel anxious about the future, try sitting with the question: what is the actual fear? Not the surface reason, but the deeper one — the fear that things won't be okay, that your needs won't be met. Recognizing the pattern doesn't fix it, but it changes your relationship to it.

  3. Audit your information diet. Notice how much of what you consume is emotionally charged catastrophe content. This isn't about denial — it's about recognizing that the brain experiences a news feed full of global disasters the same way it experiences being in danger. Deliberate limits aren't avoidance; they're hygiene.

  4. Practice emotional mirroring with someone in your life. When someone you care about is upset, instead of problem-solving, try reflecting back what they seem to be feeling before offering any response. "That sounds really scary" or "that makes sense that you're angry." Notice what happens.

  5. If you use substances, ask what they're replacing. Not to judge yourself, but to get curious. What state does the substance create? What does that state feel like it's solving for? BoredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition →, anxiety, emotional numbness, dread? The answer won't stop use on its own, but it points toward what actually needs attention.
