Western Science Has a Population Problem

Here's a core limitation that almost no one talks about: Western medicine and psychology are population-based, not individual-based.

When research says antidepressants are "30% effective," that's an average across tens of thousands of people — some of whom improved dramatically, some of whom got worse, and everything in between. That number tells you almost nothing about what will happen to you.

The 2,000-calorie daily recommendation? Not derived from physiology or metabolism. Researchers asked healthy Americans how many calories they ate and averaged the number. It's statistical fashion.

"What you really need to understand is amongst those 10,000 people there are actually 10,000 individual responses."

What Actually Creates Connection (And Why Dating Apps Miss It)

The information a dating app collects is not the information that predicts relationship success. This isn't a minor flaw — it's a structural one.

Four variables that research associates with attraction and connection:

  1. Shared emotional experience — People bond through emotion, not shared hobbies. A classic experiment: attraction was significantly higher between strangers who met on a rickety, fear-inducing bridge than on a solid one. Rehab facilities are notorious for romantic relationships forming at alarming rates, for the same reason.
  2. About 90 minutes of exposure — After this point, attraction and sexual interest tend to diminish rather than grow. Short first dates leave something unresolved and allow the relationship room to develop.
  3. Free of daily hassles — Anything that makes the interaction feel like a test, an interview, or a transaction undermines connection.

The deeper issue: dating apps position themselves as a filtering tool for a finished product. Real relationships don't work that way. Two people don't find a relationship — they build one. A successful long-term relationship requires that both people grow and change, that they bring intention to conflict resolution, and that they're genuinely committed to becoming what the other person deserves — not just being accepted as they currently are.
