Summary

TL;DR — Esports creates a perfect storm of psychological pressure: players who never learned how to learn, coaches who blame individuals instead of systems, and an industry that mistakes grinding for growth. This conversation between a psychiatrist and a professional esports coach/analyst (Jayne, working with Dallas Fuel and Team Canada) pulls apart why talented players flame out, what bipolar II actually looks like from the inside, and how stress physically collapses your capacity for nuanced thinking.

The Esports Industry Is Built on a Foundation Nobody Laid

The Overwatch League is modeled after major sports franchises, but it's missing almost everything that makes those franchises stable. There's no players' union, no institutional knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → of player development, no agreed-upon training methodology. Because the game publisher controls tournament rights, the structure of professional play is fundamentally different from physical sports — and nobody has really sorted out what that means for the people inside it.

What fills the vacuum? Grinding. Players who make it to the league have typically been playing eight to twelve hours a day since they were fifteen. That's not inherently wrong, but the problem is nobody taught them how to learn from those hours. They become, as Jayne puts it, "purely instinctual creatures" — capable of making brilliant plays they can't articulate or explain, built on thousands of accumulated hours rather than deliberate, reflective practice.

"Practice doesn't make perfect — perfect practice makes perfect."

In aviation, Jayne's background before esports, the ratio is roughly four hours of preparation for every one hour of performance. Esports reverses this: players play constantly but almost never step back to analyze, take notes, or understand why something worked.

Nobody Taught Them How to Learn

One of the most striking observations: some professional players don't know how to take notes. Ask them to write down things they noticed in game footage, and they can't do it — not because they're unintelligent, but because it's a skill nobody ever taught them.

Learning how to learn is a skill. Problem-solving is a skill. So is learning how to fail gracefully, how to concentrate, how to enter a flow state, and how to improve systematically. None of these are innate. The scientific method itself had to be invented — before that, people didn't have a framework for thinking about how to figure things out. Players arriving in professional esports at eighteen have spent years optimizing hours without ever optimizing process.

This has a compounding effect. Teams can't even agree on whether a loss was a strategy problem or an execution problem — and they rarely realize that it can be both. That's not a failure of intelligence; it's a failure of frameworks for thinking.

Stress Makes Your Brain Go Binary

There's a neurological reason why teams fall apart under pressure. This is a survival mechanism: when a predator is chasing you, you don't want to weigh subtle options. You want a binary answer — run or fight.

Adrenaline also causes your peripheral vision to physically collapse from 180 degrees down to about 30 degrees. The same thing happens cognitively. Under stress, you zero in. You stop seeing the full picture, and you assign blame to whoever is directly in front of you.

"You reverse-engineer the answer. You feel swamped, so you say 'that guy's not pulling his weight' — because you feel swamped."

This is why post-game debriefs go wrong. Someone raises the strategy. Someone else says it was execution. Both sides entrench. The stress of the loss is already in the room, cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition → is elevated, and the brain's capacity for "it could be both" has temporarily shut off. Understanding this mechanism doesn't fix it automatically, but it's the first step toward building environments — calmer, lower-cortisol, structured — where nuanced thinking can actually happen.

Aviation vs. Esports: Who Gets Blamed?

In aviation, when something goes wrong, the last thing you blame is the pilot. You blame the training, the procedures, the hardware, the workflow. Aircraft are designed to be triple-redundant and to compensate for human error by default. The system is built to protect against the pilot's worst impulses.

In esports, blame falls almost entirely on individuals. Organizations trade players instead of developing them. Coaches who happened to coach the team that won a round get credit, even if the team was essentially self-coaching. There's no systematic thinking about what made the win happen or what caused the loss.

The parallel to investment banking is striking: junior people are left out of important meetings, feel like tools rather than teammates, and under-deliver — not because they're bad, but because no one invested in making them feel like they belonged. The fix is embarrassingly simple: include people, express genuine appreciation, and give them real challenges. Two things people need to stay engaged:

  • To feel appreciated
  • To feel utilized — pushed, challenged, growing

When either of those is missing, the best people leave or go through the motions.

What Bipolar II Actually Looks Like

Bipolar disorder is widely misunderstood as rapid mood swings — someone who is laughing one minute and throwing things the next. That's not what it is. Bipolar involves sustained periods of mood that last days, weeks, or in some cases months. The shift between states is gradual. For Jayne, it took years to recognize the pattern in himself partly because he imagined bipolar as something you'd see mid-conversation.

Hypomania — the "elevated" phase of bipolar II — doesn't look like delusion or loss of control. From the outside, people may be envious of it. From the inside, it doesn't feel like illness — it feels like your best self.

The complication is that stress compresses the cycling. What was once a slow oscillation between elevated and depressed states can, under sustained pressure, accelerate into something much harder to manage. Jayne noticed this during the most intense periods of the Overwatch League season.

His therapist offered a counterintuitive intervention: stop fighting the upswing. Think of it like a pressure system with a valve — if you won't let the pressure out through the valve, it blows a gasket somewhere else. Allowing the energy to express itself (even imperfectly) dissolved the anxiety. The lesson generalized: many psychological symptoms are themselves the result of fighting against something your system is trying to do.

"Negative" Mental Patterns Are Usually Doing Something for You

This is worth sitting with. Anxiety keeps you from humiliation. Perfectionism keeps your standards high. Hypomania powers extraordinary output. Even the inadequacy that drives some people toward success — the constant sense of not being enough — is doing real work, even if it's also causing suffering.

This doesn't mean these patterns are good, or that you should cling to them. But the first step toward changing them is understanding what function they're serving. If you try to rip out a psychological pattern without understanding why it's there, you'll either fail or create a new problem elsewhere.

"The source of depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → is anger turned against the self."

Jayne's story is a clear example: he was hit by a car, injured, lost his degree and his military career — through no fault of his own — and ended up angry at himself for failing. That anger, having nowhere to go, transmuted into depression. Understanding that alchemy changes the treatment.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing mood reactivity with bipolar. If someone's mood swings in minutes, that's high mood reactivity — a real thing, but different from bipolar disorder, which requires sustained mood states lasting at least a week (or two weeks for depression). Calling rapid anger "bipolar" is wrong and obscures what's actually happening.

Thinking grinding equals improving. Hours spent are not the same as deliberate practice. If you can't articulate what you're working on or why, you're building instinct, not skill. Instinct plateaus. Deliberate practice doesn't.

Blaming the person instead of the system. When a player underperforms, the reflexive response is to trade them. But the same player in a different system — with a better coach, better team culture, more inclusion — might excel. The system shapes performance at least as much as the individual does.

Believing physical health is irrelevant to cognitive performance. They don't know how much better they could be. Trying to force outcomes instead of cultivating them. In games and in life, the instinct is to drive hard toward a goal. But some of the most effective approaches involve coaxing — understanding what the other side (or your own system) wants to do and working with it rather than against it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your practice vs. preparation ratio. For every hour you spend doing the thing (playing, working, performing), how much time do you spend reviewing, reflecting, and deliberately targeting weaknesses? If your ratio is way off, pick one session per week to do nothing but review.

  2. Learn one thing you're afraid of or annoyed by. In a game, play the heroes that kill you most. In work, study the domain you avoid. The fear or frustration is a signal that you don't understand something — and understanding it from the inside builds genuine empathy with opponents, clients, or teammates.

  3. Before a post-mortem or debrief, lower the cortisol. Take ten minutes of quiet before analyzing a loss. Walk around the block. The discussion will be more honest and nuanced when stress hormones aren't biasing everyone toward black-and-white blame.

  4. When you notice yourself suppressing an impulse or energy, ask what it wants. Not every impulse should be acted on — but if you're fighting yourself constantly, the fight itself may be creating symptoms. Identify what the pressure is, and find a legitimate outlet for it (exercise, creative work, structured rest).

  5. Think about your thinking. Once a week, take fifteen minutes to reflect not on what you did but on how you approached a problem. What assumptions did you make? What did you ignore? What would you do differently not in terms of action but in terms of process?

  6. Include your junior people. If you lead anyone — a team, a project, even informally — invite them into conversations they'd normally be excluded from. It costs nothing and often returns disproportionate effort and loyalty.

  7. Track your mood across weeks, not hours. If you suspect something cyclical is going on for you, keep a simple daily note (energy level, mood, sleep) for two months. Patterns that are invisible day-to-day become obvious across weeks. Bring that data to a therapist or psychiatrist rather than trying to interpret it alone.
