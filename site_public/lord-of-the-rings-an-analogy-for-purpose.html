Desires vs. Values: Where Does the Goal Actually Come From?

The key question to ask about any goal is: where did it originate?

According to yogic thinking, a desire originates in the indriyas — the sense organs, the things you take in from the outside world. You see something, you want it. That's the defining feature, not whether the thing itself is material or shallow. Consider:

  • You see a friend who lost 200 pounds and think, I should lose weight. That's a desire.
  • You walk past a park bench and see two people in love and think, I want that. That's also a desire — even though it's not about a car or a paycheck.
  • You watch someone eating a salad and think, I should eat better. Desire.

None of these are bad. Desires can point toward genuinely good outcomes. The problem isn't that they're wrong — it's that desires are not worth the suffering required to fulfill them. When the salad tastes like cardboard, when the diet hits week three, when building a business means grinding through months of uncertainty — desire doesn't hold. The math stops working. The cost outweighs the pull.

Values, by contrast, come from within. They don't require you to see or sense something external to activate them. Think about how differently you act when a friend needs something versus when you need it yourself. You'll cook nutritious food for a sick friend without a second thought. You won't do it for yourself. That's not a coincidence — helping the friend is connected to something that feels like a duty, a responsibility. That's closer to a value.

"Values drive you towards action in spite of suffering, and desires rarely do."

Why Your Goals Keep Failing

The 24-year-old who's unemployed, living at home, playing games all day — the goals in their head look like this:

  • Financial independence
  • Moving out
  • Getting healthy
  • Looking better
  • Feeling less stuck

Every single one of those is a desire. They all originate externally: from comparison, from what they've seen others have, from what society signals they should want. And because they're all desires, they all share the same weakness: when the work gets hard and painful, the pull isn't strong enough to keep going.

There's also a compounding problem specific to people stuck in this pattern. You'd rather do nothing than do something that might not work. Playing video games for a year feels safer than taking a risk that could fail — because at least doing nothing doesn't cost you anything emotionally. But this logic only holds if your goal is desire-based. If it's based in something that feels like duty, the calculus flips entirely.

The Main Quest vs. Side Quests

Here's where the RPG framing becomes genuinely useful rather than just decorative.

Losing weight is a side quest. Making money is a side quest. Getting healthy is a side quest.

The problem isn't that these things don't matter. They do. The problem is treating a side quest like it's the main quest. In an RPG, you grind a dungeon to get a piece of gear — but you're not grinding the dungeon for the dungeon. You need the gear to beat the boss. The gear has a purpose beyond itself.

When someone has a main quest — a Dharma, a duty or responsibility bigger than themselves — all those side quests slot into place. You lose weight because the main quest requires you to be functional. You build financial stability because the main quest demands it. The side quest isn't the point; it's the infrastructure.

"If you can find your Dharma, everything else becomes side quests."

Without the main quest, you're just doing side quests forever, wondering why none of them feel satisfying or why you can't stick with them. The answer is: side quests were never designed to carry the full weight of your motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →.

Dharma: Why It Has to Be Bigger Than You

A Dharma in this context means something like: a responsibility you can't afford to walk away from, one that exists outside your personal comfort or discomfort. It's not about what you want. It's about what you are obligated to do.

The reason this matters practically is simple: it changes your toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → for pain. When the thing you're working toward is genuinely bigger than you — when failing it means failing someone or something beyond yourself — you make different choices. You stay up the extra hours. You eat the thing you don't want to eat. You take the uncomfortable conversation. Not because it feels good, but because you don't have the option of walking away.

"The Dharma tells you that you can't afford to fail because it's bigger than you. It's a duty. It's a responsibility."

The Lord of the Rings makes this concrete. Frodo doesn't walk to Mordor because it's fun, or because he saw a vision board of what destroying the One Ring would do for his life. He walks because someone has to, and it turned out to be him. The journey is painful, exhausting, and nearly destroys him. He does it anyway — because it's the main quest. That's Dharma in narrative form.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking a noble-sounding desire for a value. Wanting to be healthy, wanting financial freedom, wanting love — these all sound like values, which is why it's easy to assume they'll function like values. They won't, unless they're connected to something larger than personal gain or comfort.

Trying to manufacture motivation without first finding the main quest. Productivity systems, habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → trackers, accountability partners — all useful tools, but they're gear. They don't replace having a reason to push through difficulty.

Confusing "I want this badly enough" with having a Dharma. Intensity of desire isn't the same thing as purpose. You can want something with genuine desperation and still quit when it gets hard, because the structure of desire doesn't hold under sustained suffering.

Thinking Dharma has to be grand or obvious. It doesn't have to be world-historical. Caring for someone who depends on you, showing up for a communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition →, carrying something forward that would otherwise disappear — these can function as a main quest. What matters is that it generates real obligation, not just aspiration.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your current goals by origin. For each goal you're actively pursuing, ask honestly: Where did this come from? Did I see it somewhere, or does it come from inside? This isn't about judging the goal — it's about knowing what kind of fuel it runs on.

  2. Look at where you already show up despite discomfort. Think of times you've done something hard, unglamorous, or costly for someone else without being asked twice. That pattern is pointing toward your values. Take note of it.

  3. Ask the main quest question. Write down: What is my main quest right now? If your answer is a side quest (lose weight, get a job, save money), keep asking why until you find something that feels like a responsibility rather than a reward.

  4. Test a goal against suffering. For any goal you're considering, ask: Would I keep doing this if it never felt good — if every step hurt — because it simply had to be done? If the honest answer is no, you're looking at a desire. That doesn't mean abandon the goal, but it means you need to find what larger purpose that goal serves.

  5. Find your fellowship. You don't have to carry the ring alone, but nobody can carry it for you. Identify people who can help you stay on the path — not people who'll set the destination for you, but people who'll keep your spirits up when the road is long.
