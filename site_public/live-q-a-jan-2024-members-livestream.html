The Three-Legged Stool: Vedanta, Yoga, and Ayurveda

Western medicine separates philosophy from practice from physical health. The ancient Indian system does not. It rests on three interconnected pillars:

  • Vedanta — philosophical understanding of the nature of reality (e.g., the non-dual view that "good" and "bad" are constructions of mind; without a perceiver, there is just weather, just a rock)
  • Yoga — the experiential and practical path to verify those philosophical claims directly. You don't just reason your way to non-duality; you practice until you perceive it
  • Ayurveda — the medical system, which assumes the worldview of the other two

Reflective Listening Without Becoming the Therapist Friend

The core difference between a friend and a therapist is reciprocityThe mutual exchange of care, effort, and goodwill between friends — not as an equal trade, but as each person contributing what they genuinely can.Full definition →. A therapist is structurally a one-way relationship. Friendship is not.

Reflective listening itself is not the problem. The problem is adopting the role of therapist — being entirely oriented toward the other person's emotional processing with no self-disclosure, no playfulness, no back-and-forth.

"Self-disclosure plus reflective listening makes things not feel like therapy."

If you only reflect, you are performing a function. If you also share your own perspective, commiserate, joke, offer opinions, or gently challenge — you are being a friend.

There is also a trap to watch for: people who grow up without a felt sense of intrinsic worth often learn that their value comes from what they provide. How Reflective Listening and Reassurance Actually Work

Reflective listening does two things:

  1. Decompresses emotional energy — each reflected statement lowers the emotional arousal slightly, allowing the analytical mind to come back online. You cannot think clearly in the middle of grief or rage; the emotion literally suppresses analytical reasoning
  2. Builds an accurate map of the other person's worldview — as they keep talking, you learn how they see the situation, not just what happened

This second function is critical for advice and reassurance. Generic advice fails because it is calibrated to the advice-giver's worldview, not the recipient's. Once you understand why this particular breakup is devastating — because this person believes in a single true love, not in the statistical abundance of compatible people — your response can actually land.

Reassurance requires two things: trust and demonstrated competence. Without both, reassurance is just noise. You earn the right to reassure someone by first understanding what they actually fear and why they fear it.

The sequence: decompress emotion with reflective listening → understand their worldview → then offer advice or reassurance, when their mind is in a receptive state.

Asking Open-Ended Questions

Six practical competencies:

  1. Eliminate yes/no questions. "Do you like it here?" → "What do you like about being here?"
  2. Favor "what" questions over "why" questions. "Why" sounds accusatory and creates defensiveness. "What" is curious and forward-looking
  3. Aim for opinions and worldview, not facts. "What did you have for lunch?" is closed. "What do you like about that place?" opens the floor
  4. Add specificity to reduce cognitive load. "What's life like?" is too open. "What's been the hardest part of the semester?" gives the mind a direction and makes the question easy to engage with
  5. Avoid rote questions. "How are you doing?" gets a reflexive "fine" because it demands no real thought. Ask about something specific and genuine
  6. Avoid value judgments. Don't assume that a job interview going well is good news, or that a parent's death is a loss. Ask what something was like before you decide how to frame it

A few reliable all-purpose phrases: "Tell me more," "What was that like?" "What's that like for you?" These are almost infinitely reusable without sounding scripted.
