Summary

TL;DR — A father and adult son want a closer relationship but don't know how to start. The conversation reveals that the missing ingredient isn't goodwill — it's the ability to stay in an emotion long enough for the other person to actually feel it.

This is a live family session with Zach, a young man who dropped out of a PhD program and moved back home, and his father Dave, a retired Air Force officer turned defense contractor. The family also includes Zach's younger brother Carson, who has autism. The session moves through three conversations — Zach alone, Dave alone, and then both together — and what emerges is a picture of two people who genuinely want connection but have developed very different relationships with their own emotional lives, which makes them hard to reach.

Two People Who Want the Same Thing But Speak Different Languages

Zach is a feeler and a talker. He's close with his mom, has developed real insight into the family's dynamics, and can describe the emotional texture of situations in vivid detail. Dave is an engineer and a retired military officer — someone who organizes experience around goals, outcomes, and problem-solving. When he feels discomfort, his instinct is to find the fix.

This isn't a personality flaw. It's a coherent system. Dave learned that feelings are useful insofar as they help you manage toward an outcome. When he's asked what value there is in understanding a negative emotion, his first answer is essentially: it helps you correct the problem. When pushed further — what if it doesn't lead to a goal at all? — he genuinely hasn't thought about it.

There's a useful distinction buried here: understanding your emotions so you can master them is very different from understanding your emotions because they matter in themselves. Dave has spent a lifetime doing the first. The second is largely foreign territory.

The Pattern That Keeps Repeating

Carson's autism has created a specific family dynamic. Carson lives by patterns — walking the dog at a particular time, following particular sequences. When those patterns are disrupted, he escalates. Dave's instinct under stress is to impose order and enforce rules. These two things are oil and water.

"The more conflict arises, the more your dad doubles down on control, and the more he doubles down on control, the more conflict arises."

Zach can see this cycle clearly from the outside. He can also see that it's not really about blame — Carson's meltdowns aren't personal attacks, and Dave's need for structure isn't malicious. But being able to see the cycle and being able to interrupt it are different skills.

What's striking about Zach's story is that he actually managed to interrupt his own version of this cycle. He used to treat Carson dismissively — not from cruelty but from exhaustion and the kind of impatience that comes from not knowing what else to do. After a few years away and some deliberate reflection, he started treating Carson as a person rather than a problem, and Carson now responds to Zach in ways he won't respond to anyone else in the family. Zach shrugs this off as not a big deal. It's a very big deal.

"Don't confuse being really good at something with the fact that it's easy."

Effectively communicating with an autistic adult is genuinely hard. The fact that it feels natural to Zach now doesn't mean it came for free — it means he's gotten good at it.

What Dave Actually Said (And What Got Lost)

The most important moment in the session is when Dave finally talks about lost time with Zach. In the one-on-one conversation, it's clearly emotional for him. He talks about the years from four to ten — the years when kids are forming their identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →, when everything is happy and uncomplicated — and how Carson's needs and his own demanding career meant he couldn't be as present for Zach as he wanted to be. He uses the word "hurts." You can see it on his face.

Then Zach comes back into the room, and Dave says essentially the same words — but the juice is gone. He talks about character development and lost time, but it comes out as a structural summary rather than a feeling. Zach hears the information but doesn't feel the weight of it.

This is a really common dynamic. When someone who isn't practiced at emotional expression tries to convey something important, the engineering brain — or whatever its equivalent is for a given person — takes over and translates raw feeling into manageable language. The content survives but the emotion gets stripped out. The other person hears words, not experience.

"You gave him the structure, but you left out the juice."

What Dave was actually trying to say: he mourns the time he wasn't able to give Zach. Not because anyone was at fault. Just because it's gone and he knows it mattered.

The Protection Reflex

Dave has a well-developed protective instinct around his inner life. He acknowledges he's been burned before when he opened up to people, so privacy became armor. There's a clear pattern: he can identify what other people feel, he can analyze situations, he can problem-solve. But when the camera turns inward, when someone asks him what he feels right now, there's a pause and then a kind of lateral movement toward abstraction.

This isn't unique to Dave. A lot of people — especially those who were raised in households or institutions where emotional expression was either absent or actively discouraged — develop a fluency with external situations and a relative silence about internal ones. The feelings are there. They just don't have reliable access to words.

The important thing here is that the absence of emotional vocabulary is not the same as the absence of emotion. Dave clearly feels things. When talking about what Carson has overcome, he smiles and sits up straighter. When talking about lost time with Zach, he becomes visibly quieter. These aren't subtle. He just doesn't have a practiced habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of naming what's happening and saying it out loud to another person.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking structure for coldness. Dave isn't cold. He's organized. The instinct to impose rules and maintain control is a coping mechanism for anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, not evidence of not caring. Zach knows this intellectually, but emotionally the experience of walking on eggshells — not knowing when the atmosphere will shift — reads as unpredictability and distance.

Mistaking insight for progress. Zach can describe the family dynamics with real clarity, but clarity isn't the same as resolution. Being able to name a cycle doesn't automatically mean you know how to break it.

Underestimating what you've actually done. Zach dismisses his relationship with Carson as easy, natural, not a big deal. This kind of self-deprecation keeps him from recognizing genuine growth — and also, quietly, fuels resentment toward his parents for not doing the thing he's convinced himself anyone could do.

Communicating the words without the feeling. Dave's instinct is to convert emotion into information before delivering it. The information lands. The feeling doesn't. The other person needs the feeling to know that what you're saying actually matters to you.

Waiting for the other person to ask. Dave made a point that around the campfire would have been a natural time for Zach to ask about his childhood. Zach didn't ask. Dave didn't offer. Neither of them is wrong, but the moment passed. Waiting for the conditions to be perfect is how decades go by.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the emotion before solving the problem. If you notice yourself immediately jumping to "here's what we need to do," pause and ask: what am I actually feeling right now? You don't have to act on it. Just identify it. This is the first step toward having access to it in conversations.

  2. Let someone else understand you. If you're the person who always tries to understand others, practice being understood. Share something about your own experience — not for the purpose of getting advice, but just to let another person know what it's like to be you. Notice what that feels like.

  3. Locate the inherited patterns. Dave's privacy, his structures, his difficulty with emotional vocabulary — these came from somewhere. Think about which of your patterns were modeled to you. Ask: is this still serving me, or am I just doing it because it's what I learned?

  4. When something is emotional, say so explicitly. If you're telling someone something important and you feel it in your chest, you can say "this is hard to say" or "this actually matters a lot to me." It's a small sentence that tells the other person to pay attention differently.

  5. Seek a facilitator for hard conversations. One of the real observations from this session is that the same words said by one person can land completely differently when a third party reflects them back. Family counseling, a mediator, or even just a mutual friend who can reframe things in real time can make the difference between talking past each other and actually being heard.

  6. Don't wait for the perfect moment. The campfire story is a lesson. There's almost never a moment that announces itself as the right time to ask your father about his childhood or tell your son you wish you'd been more present. Pick an ordinary moment and start anyway.
