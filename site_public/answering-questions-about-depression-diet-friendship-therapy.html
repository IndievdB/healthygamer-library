TL;DR — DepressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → isn't purely psychological, your body is deeply involved. And the people around someone who is struggling matter more than they realize.

This talk covers four practical topics: what friends can actually do for someone whose therapy and medication haven't worked, why depression gets triggered by seemingly unrelated events, how physical inflammation connects to mental state, and what the difference is between psychiatrists, psychologists, and therapists.

Your Body Is Not Separate From Your Depression

One of the most common and damaging assumptions people make is that "not feeling like doing anything" is a purely psychological failing — laziness, weakness, or bad thinking.

Think about the flu. When you have it, you feel like doing absolutely nothing. Nobody argues that's a character flaw. It's your body shutting down non-essential activity because it's fighting something.

So why assume it's different when you wake up depressed and can't move?

"Why do you assume that when you wake up in the morning and you don't feel like doing anything, why do you assume that is completely psychological? It's an incorrect assumption."

Your body can be in a pro-inflammatory state — driven by poor sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition →, disrupted circadian rhythm, chronic stress, or illness — and that state produces the exact same "I just can't" feeling as the flu. It's a complex physiological, neurological, and psychological response. The label "I don't feel like it" flattens all of that into something that sounds like a choice.

The field of psychoneuroimmunology (the study of the connections between the mind, brain, and immune system) has produced real findings here: - When you're grieving, your immune system is measurably suppressed and you're more vulnerable to viral illness - Depression blunts your body's response to vaccines - Disrupted circadian rhythm pushes the body toward a pro-inflammatory state, making mood and motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → worse

The important flip side: because the mind and body are connected in both directions, you can attack depression through physical means. You don't have to fight it only in the mental realm.

Fighting Inflammation When You Can't Do Anything

Here's the practical problem: the treatments that most reliably reduce inflammation — exercise, yoga — are often exactly what depression makes impossible. You know you should work out. You can't.

Yoga, for what it's worth, appears to be more effective than general exercise for reducing inflammation. But if you genuinely can't get off the floor, start somewhere lower-stakes.

Eat anti-inflammatory foods. Specifically: - Berries - Leafy greens — arugula, spinach (not iceberg lettuce) - Cruciferous vegetables — broccoli, cauliflower

There is also early research suggesting that anti-inflammatory medications may have a meaningful effect on depression symptoms, though that evidence is still developing and this isn't a recommendation to self-medicate.

The key mental shift is this: if you're in a pro-inflammatory physical state, your inability to act is partly a body problem. You can work on that body problem with food and sleep hygiene even when you can't manage anything else. That's a foothold.

Why Strangers' Deaths Can Make Your Depression Worse

Depression isn't only triggered by things directly related to you. Sometimes a celebrity death, or news of someone from your hometown dying, hits harder than it has any "right" to.

The mechanism is similar to how PTSDTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → works — certain external events tap into a pre-existing internal vulnerability. If you carry a background belief that the world is a hopeless or sad place, any news that confirms that belief can act as a trigger, pulling those feelings back up to the surface.

Think of the bathroom analogy: you can hold it in all the way across the building, but the moment you walk into the bathroom, the urgency becomes overwhelming. Your environment is triggering a response that was already there. The death of someone you didn't know isn't creating your depression from scratch — it's confirming something your mind already believes, and the feelings follow.

"There's something about the way that you view the world or that you view other people that's getting triggered by these random deaths."

The useful question isn't "why does this bother me when I didn't even know them?" It's "what does this say about how I generally see the world?" That belief about the world — not the individual death — is the thing worth examining.

What Friends Can Actually Do (And What They Can't)

If someone you care about has been through therapy and medication and hasn't found relief, it's tempting to either take on their healing as your project or to feel helpless. Neither is the right frame.

You are not a substitute for professional treatment. Don't put yourself in that bucket. Your role is not to replicate what a therapist or psychiatrist does — it's to do what only a friend can do.

"It's not the most common reason why someone who wants to kill themselves doesn't — it's because of the other people in their life. It's the people that matter to them, that they don't want to hurt, that keeps them struggling and wanting to live."

A therapist can provide clinical support. A therapist cannot tell someone "I love you" in the way a friend can, and mean it the same way. That sense of mattering to another person is something no prescription or treatment plan provides. That's yours to give.

Practically, you can: - Call them when they're having a hard day - Get them out of the house - Work out together or meditate together - Help them find a different therapist or psychiatrist if the current approach isn't working - Just be present and show that they matter

Help them find help — but don't try to be the help in a clinical sense. You'll burn yourself out and it won't work anyway.

The Difference Between Psychiatrists, Psychologists, and Therapists

These titles get used interchangeably and that causes real confusion when people are trying to find support.

Psychiatrist: A medical doctor who specializes in the mind. The training includes medical school, time in hospitals, and treating physical conditions — then a residency focused specifically on psychiatry. Because they're medical doctors, they can prescribe medications. They can also do therapy.

Psychologist: Someone trained specifically to study the mind, usually through a PhD — a research degree. The training is longer (typically five to seven years plus a postdoc) and involves significantly more scientific methodology and research. In most U.S. states, psychologists cannot prescribe medications. They can do therapy.

Therapist: A broader term. It refers to anyone practicing psychotherapy — which can include social workers (roughly two years of training), psychologists, or psychiatrists. The training level and background vary widely. The title itself doesn't tell you how much training someone has.

When the first therapist or psychiatrist doesn't help, it doesn't mean the whole category failed — it may just mean the specific person or approach wasn't the right fit.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming "I don't feel like it" is a moral problem. It's often physiological. Treating it as pure laziness prevents you from taking the physical steps that actually help.
  • Friends trying to be therapists. Wanting to help someone you love is natural. But taking on clinical responsibility for their mental health isn't sustainable and isn't your job. You'll exhaust yourself and create guilt when it doesn't work.
  • Dismissing physical interventions for depression. Diet changes, sleep, and movement feel too mundane to be "real" treatment. They're not a cure, but they address a genuine component of what makes depression worse.
  • Giving up on treatment because one provider didn't help. A psychiatrist or therapist who isn't effective for you isn't evidence that treatment doesn't work — therapist fit matters enormously, and switching is a legitimate option.
  • Pathologizing trigger responses. Feeling depressed after a stranger's death doesn't mean something is uniquely wrong with you. It's pointing at an underlying belief worth exploring, not a new problem on top of everything else.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your diet for anti-inflammatory foods. This week, add at least one serving of berries, leafy greens (spinach, arugula), or cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower) to your daily meals. This is an accessible physical lever when motivation is low.

  2. Protect your circadian rhythm. Pick a consistent wake time and stick to it for two weeks, even on weekends. Disrupted sleep is a direct route into a pro-inflammatory, low-motivation state.

  3. If you're supporting a friend: make one direct contact. Call, text, or show up. Say explicitly that you care about them. Don't frame it as fixing anything — just make them feel like they matter to a specific person.

  4. When you notice a trigger, ask the underlying question. Next time something that "shouldn't" affect you makes you feel worse, don't dismiss it. Write down: What does this confirm that I already believe about the world? That belief is the more productive thing to look at.

  5. Clarify who you're looking for before seeking help. If you're considering therapy, decide first what you need: a medication evaluation (psychiatrist), longer-term psychological assessment (psychologist), or talk therapy (any of the above, or a licensed counselor). Knowing the distinction helps you ask for the right thing.

  6. If treatment hasn't worked, treat it as a fit problem, not a dead end. Ask your current provider directly: "Is there a different approach or someone you'd recommend given that this hasn't been helping?" A good provider will help you find something better.
