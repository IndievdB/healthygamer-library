Summary

TL;DR — Most people would rather stay a hidden failure than risk being seen failing in public, and that preference quietly destroys them.

This talk unpacks one of the most self-defeating patterns in human psychology: the preference for invisible failure over visible struggle. Along the way it touches on identity, why help works so well, and why the "I am" framing is often more dangerous than the situation itself.

The Paradox of Getting Better

There is a brutal paradox buried in almost every attempt to improve: the very first step toward not failing is advertising that you are failing. That is how you access help, and help is, as the talk puts it, "op" — overpowered.

"If you want to stop being a failure, the first step is advertising it."

Think about what this actually means. There are people out there who have already figured out whatever you are struggling with. They would tell you how to do it if you asked. You could close months or years of painful self-teaching with a single honest conversation. But most people never have that conversation.

Why? Because it is one thing to be a failure privately. It is another thing entirely to let someone watch you be one.

Why We Play Solo

There is a familiar impulse — if you haven't played a game in a while and you're going to a LAN party, you practice first, alone, so nobody sees you get owned. That instinct makes complete sense. But when it runs your whole life, it becomes a trap.

Playing solo means no one has to see your failures. It also means no one can help you fix them.

"Most people would rather remain a hidden failure than advertise being a failure and no longer be one. We'll choose hidden failure over public success any day of the week."

That is not a small quirk. That is one of the most damaging default settings a person can run on. And online culture, which constantly surfaces curated success while burying struggle, makes it significantly worse.

The "I Am" Problem

Beyond the fear of being seen, there is a second layer that keeps people stuck: the language of identity.

When you fail at something repeatedly, the mind stops saying "I haven't learned this yet" and starts saying "I am this way." The shift from inexperience to identity is where things get genuinely dangerous.

  • "I am lazy" rather than "I haven't built the habit yet"
  • "I am an idiot" rather than "I don't understand this topic yet"
  • "I am an incel" rather than "I haven't figured out relationships yet"
  • "I am a failure" rather than "I've been failing at specific things"

Notice how "I haven't" keeps the door open. "I am" closes it. The be verb — am, are, is — fuses your performance with your person.

"We don't say 'I just haven't had a girlfriend yet.' We identify with the thing."

Once you identify with a condition, your subconscious starts treating that condition as load-bearing. Your mind is constantly running background calculations about what you can and cannot do. If the settled answer is "I am a failure," those calculations quietly rule out paths before you even consciously consider them.

What Changes Identity (And What to Be Careful About)

Research on meditation and psychedelic-assisted therapy points to the same mechanism: they seem to work largely by loosening the identification you have with a fixed condition. People in well-supported psychedelic experiences often report something like, "I realized I am not my failure — what I am is separate from those outcomes." Therapy and sustained meditation practice can produce the same shift.

The key move in all of these approaches is the same: divorcing yourself from the outcomes of your actions. Your actions are things you do. They are not what you are.

A word of caution on the more intense routes: both advanced meditation techniques and psychedelics carry real risk. Some people have developed lasting anxiety or PTSD from difficult psychedelic experiences with no clear path to resolution. Certain deep meditation practices — the ones described as more hallucinatory — are treated by experienced teachers as things that should only be attempted under careful guidance, not independently. The fire metaphor is apt: these tools can burn away accumulated difficulty, or they can just burn. Neither avenue is something to approach casually or alone.

The practical, lower-risk version of this work is simply: catch yourself using be verbs about your shortcomings and replace them with process language. "I am bad at this" becomes "I haven't put enough time into this yet."

The Internal Conflict That Blocks Therapy

There is a particular version of this problem that shows up for people who are trained in mental health themselves. A psychologist who has gotten little benefit from therapy faces a genuine internal conflict: the evidence says therapy works, but personal experience says it didn't. That conflict doesn't just create career doubt — it actively blocks further benefit from therapy, because you can't fully engage with a process you fundamentally don't trust.

The right move in that situation is not to abandon therapy or the profession. It is to bring that specific conflict into a therapeutic relationship and work through it directly. As long as the conflict remains unexamined, it will continue to sabotage the work. Naming it to a therapist — "I don't know if I believe this works, and I need to work through that" — is exactly the kind of honest advertising that opens the door.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking self-awareness for action. Knowing intellectually that you identify with failure does not automatically undo the identification. The insight has to be followed by consistently catching and correcting the language you use about yourself.

Thinking that staying hidden preserves dignity. It feels like it does. It actually just preserves the failure in amber while adding isolation on top.

Treating psychedelics as a reliable shortcut. The mechanism may be real, but the risk is also real. Bad experiences do not always produce useful learning — some produce lasting harm. This is not an area to self-prescribe.

Assuming that if therapy didn't work once, it never will. The conditions matter: the specific therapist, your readiness, whether you walked in with an unresolved internal conflict about the process itself. "Therapy didn't help me" is a data point, not a verdict.

Conflating inexperience with incompetence. You are bad at things you haven't done much. That is not a character flaw. It is arithmetic.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your be-verb framing. For one week, pay attention to how you describe your struggles to yourself and others. Every time you catch an "I am " attached to a shortcoming, write it down and rewrite it as "I haven't ___ yet" or "I've been struggling with ." The rewrite is not denial — it is accuracy.

  2. Advertise one failure deliberately. Pick something small you've been keeping hidden because you don't want to look bad, and ask someone for help with it. Notice what actually happens versus what you feared would happen.

  3. Audit where you're playing solo. List the areas of your life where you're grinding alone to avoid being seen. For each one, ask: is there someone — a mentor, a community, a professional — who has already solved this and could shorten the path?

  4. If therapy hasn't worked for you, bring that to a therapist. Don't frame it as "therapy doesn't work." Frame it as "I have this conflict I need help working through." Make the conflict itself the starting point.

  5. Try a basic meditation practice before anything advanced. If you want to work on identity and self-perception through contemplative practice, start with simple breath-focused sitting — 10 to 15 minutes a day — before exploring anything more intense. Build the foundation before touching the fire.
