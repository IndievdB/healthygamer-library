Summary

TL;DR — Mental illness, morality, and mental health are three separate things that our culture keeps muddling together. Untangling them is the first step toward understanding what it actually means to be psychologically well.

The question sounds philosophical but it has real practical weight: if spanking was universal for centuries, were all those children mentally unhealthy? If someone grows up in a culture that normalizes child marriage or slavery and never feels distressed by it, are they traumatized? Does committing something we now consider morally wrong make a person mentally ill?

These questions matter because how you answer them shapes whether you think mental health is a moving goalpost, a cultural construction, or something more stable. It turns out there are good answers — they just require separating three concepts that our everyday language constantly conflates.

Mental Illness Is More Consistent Across Cultures Than You Think

One of the most important pieces of evidence here comes from epidemiology. When researchers survey the prevalence of mental illness across wildly different cultures — societies with child marriage, arranged marriage, corporal punishment, very different moral frameworks — the rates of mental illness come out roughly comparable worldwide.

This is striking. It suggests that mental illness is not primarily a product of cultural norms. Anxiety disorders, major depressive disorder, and psychosis appear at broadly similar rates whether you're studying East Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, South America, or Western Europe.

What does vary is how mental illness expresses itself. In parts of South America, panic attacks have their own local name and framing. In East and South Asia, major depression often shows up as somatic complaints — fatigue, headaches, GI problems — rather than the sadness and guilt a Western clinician might look for. Culture shapes the language and symptoms of mental illness, but the underlying dysfunction seems to be a near-universal feature of being human.

The diagnostic line that psychiatry actually draws is not about cultural conformity — it's about functional impairment. Can you work? Maintain relationships? Manage daily life? A person in Japan and a person in the Netherlands with major depressive disorder may describe their experience very differently, but both are likely to have significant, prolonged difficulty functioning. That common thread is what mental illness actually refers to.

So the answer to "were all children who were spanked mentally ill?" is: not necessarily. The presence of a harmful practice in a culture doesn't, by itself, produce population-wide rates of clinical mental illness. Trauma and dysfunction are real, but mental illness as a clinical category is more specific than "exposed to something we now consider harmful."

Morality and Mental Illness Are Not the Same Thing

This conflation might be the most consequential mistake in how we talk about psychology today. Two places where it shows up:

Blaming violence on mental illness. When someone commits a mass shooting, the instinct is to say they must have been mentally ill. But this framing is both statistically misleading and unfair to people who actually have mental illness. The vast majority of people with depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, or any other diagnosis never harm anyone. Mental illness does not cause immoral behavior in any direct, reliable way. Treating it as though it does stigmatizes people who are already struggling and lets bad actors off the hook.

Treating immoral behavior as a symptom. The flip side is equally problematic. Historically, addiction was seen as a moral failing — a character weakness, a lack of willpower. Depression was treated as laziness or spiritual deficiency. Separating these things was hard-won progress. But we can over-correct: once people start attributing every moral lapse to a mental health condition, personal responsibility disappears.

"Once we start blaming our immorality on our mental illness, our immoral behaviors skyrocket — because we stop accepting responsibility, and once we forego personal responsibility for our behaviors, there's no internal check."

The cleaner model is this: mental illness and moral behavior are largely independent axes. You can be mentally ill and behave with integrity. You can be mentally healthy by clinical measures and behave cruelly. They intersect at the margins — antisocial personality disorder (sociopathy) is one genuine case where a neurological difference in empathic processing seems to contribute to a pattern of harmful behavior. People with this condition are measurably less likely to yawn empathically, which reflects a real difference in the brain's empathic circuitry. That's a real intersection. But it's the exception, not the rule.

The practical takeaway: stop using "mentally ill" as a synonym for "bad person," and stop using mental health diagnoses as a reason to exempt yourself from moral accountability.

Mental Health Is Not Just the Absence of Mental Illness

This is perhaps the most overlooked distinction. Treating mental illness — reducing symptoms, restoring function — is necessary but not sufficient. You can clear someone of a clinical diagnosis and they still have no particular experience of peace, meaning, or contentment. The absence of disorder is not the same as flourishing.

Spiritual traditions, particularly contemplative ones, have spent millennia investigating what flourishing actually looks like — and they arrived at something consistent across cultures and centuries. Yogic and Buddhist traditions, Stoic philosophy, and Viktor Frankl's work in the Nazi concentration camps all converge on the same insight:

"The path to enlightenment is not about what happens in the world — it's about how we react to it."

This is why this framework transcends cultural context. Whether you were spanked or not, whether your culture practiced arranged marriage or not, whether you grew up in poverty or comfort — what shapes your psychological wellbeing is not the external circumstances themselves but your relationship to them. Detachment from outcomes, separation of identity from ego, the ability to process emotions without being consumed by them — these are the levers.

This is also why mindfulness is the one intervention that shows measurable improvement across nearly every mental health condition — depression, anxiety, ADHD, schizophrenia. It's not that it treats each illness specifically. It's that the contemplative tradition it comes from addresses something more fundamental: the basic patterns of how the human mind generates suffering, regardless of what particular flavor that suffering takes.

The goal of mental health, in this fuller sense, is contentment — not the absence of negative emotion, not constant happiness, but a stable orientation toward peace that doesn't depend on things going well.

Common Pitfalls

Equating cultural normality with mental health. Something being widely practiced or accepted in a culture doesn't make it psychologically healthy or harmless. Cultural accommodation affects how mental illness manifests and what causes distress, but it doesn't redefine whether dysfunction is present.

Equating mental illness with moral failure. This was how psychiatry and society operated for a long time with addiction and depression. It caused enormous damage. Having a diagnosis does not make someone a bad person.

Using mental illness to explain away immoral behavior. The opposite error. Pointing to someone's psychological struggles as the cause of their harmful actions is usually an oversimplification, and it erodes the accountability that makes change possible — both for individuals and society.

Thinking that treating mental illness is the whole job. Getting rid of symptoms is valuable. But it doesn't automatically produce a meaningful, contented life. Those require additional work — the kind that spiritual and philosophical traditions have mapped out in detail.

Assuming that because something causes no visible distress, it causes no harm. People adapt. People normalize. Lack of obvious suffering doesn't prove wellbeing, especially when someone has never been given the framework to recognize a different possibility.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your language. For one week, notice when you (or others) describe bad behavior as "sick" or describe mental illness as a reason someone "can't help" acting a certain way. Practice substituting more precise language: "They're struggling with X" versus "X explains why they did Y."

  2. Separate the two questions. When you're evaluating yourself or someone else, ask them as distinct questions: Is there functional impairment here? (the mental illness question) and Am I acting in accordance with my values? (the moral question). Don't let one answer the other.

  3. Start a mindfulness practice — even a small one. Five to ten minutes of daily meditation has measurable effects across a wide range of mental health conditions. You don't need a particular tradition. The mechanism is the same: learning to observe thoughts and emotions without being hijacked by them.

  4. Ask the contentment question. Not "do I have symptoms?" but "what would a peaceful, contented life actually look like for me?" Write a short answer. Then notice where your current habits or patterns pull you away from that picture. This is the starting point for the mental health work, as distinct from the mental illness treatment.

  5. When working through a personal struggle, hold both paths. Address the clinical or functional problem (therapy, medication, structured intervention if needed) and engage the meaning-level question: what do you actually want your life to feel like, and how does this struggle connect to that? The best outcomes tend to come from walking both at once.
