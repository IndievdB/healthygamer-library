Summary

TL;DR — A history of being unwanted doesn't mean you are unwanted. But the evidence can pile up so convincingly that your mind does something logical and devastating: it steps back from your own life entirely.

This is a session with a young man called Doc — a university student studying to become a teacher, living in Germany, whose mother was murdered by his stepfather three years before the conversation. He opens by describing a strange flatness: no investment in his own life, happiness only available when he's helping others, and a sense of watching himself from the outside. What unfolds is an exploration of how a lifetime of being systematically excluded, discarded, and exploited builds a core belief that you are fundamentally unlovable — and how the mind protects itself from that belief by quietly checking out of its own existence.

What It Means to Live in the Third Person

Doc describes his daily experience in precise gaming terms: he went from carry to support, from main quests to side quests, from playing his own character to remote-controlling one. He doesn't feel suicidal. He just doesn't feel like his own life is worth being the protagonist of.

This has a clinical name: depersonalization. It's distinct from its close cousin, derealization (where the world feels unreal but you feel solid). In depersonalization, you feel unreal. The world keeps going; you're just operating a character inside it. It shows up as:

  • Emotional numbness to things that should matter (making 10k on crypto, feeling nothing)
  • Joy only arriving secondhand, through other people's reactions
  • A sense that years are passing without being experienced
  • Feeling like the NPC in everyone else's story, never the player character

This isn't a malfunction. It's a survival mechanism. When life has delivered enough pain, the mind does something compassionate and self-protective: it unplugs. The cost is that you stop feeling fully real to yourself.

How the Evidence for "I Am Unlovable" Accumulates

What makes Doc's situation different from simple low self-esteem is that his heart isn't distorting the evidence — the evidence genuinely pointed that way, for years, from multiple directions:

  • A father who conceived him as an anchor baby for immigration purposes, reappeared only when facing deportation, handed him cash, dragged him to a government office to vouch for him the same day they met for the first time, then went silent again
  • Neighbors told their children not to play with him — he initially assumed racism, which would have meant it wasn't his fault. It turned out to be rumors about his mother, which felt worse: maybe there is something wrong with him
  • A best friend who shaped him into someone more socially acceptable before actually befriending him — the implicit message being: as you naturally are, you're not worth it
  • Teachers who publicly humiliated him, one of whom failed him out of spite
  • A mother who sent him to live with his grandparents when he was around six, partly for financial reasons, and only took him back when threatened with losing child benefits
  • The one stable father figure — a stepfather who genuinely seemed to care about him, his sister, and his mother — who then murdered his mother in the garage and killed himself, having hugged Doc warmly the night before

Each of these alone would be significant. Together, they form something that functions less like a cognitive distortion and more like a reasonable conclusion: I am not worth loving. The evidence says so.

"I think it's very reasonable for you to believe that you are not worth liking, because honestly the evidence kind of points in that direction."

The important caveat: reasonable given the evidence doesn't mean true.

Why the Childhood Bullying Stings More Than the Murder

Doc admits something that confuses him: the years of social exclusion and teacher mockery feel more present in his mind than his mother's death. He expects people to find that strange, or wrong.

It isn't. A few reasons:

Repetition carves deeper grooves than shock. Being excluded, mocked, and told through a hundred small daily acts that you are fundamentally undesirable is continuous. It runs over the same neural territory again and again. A single catastrophic event — even a violent one — is a wound. Years of social rejection is a rewiring.

He was allowed to feel the bullying. With his mother's death, he immediately became the support pillar: for his sister, his grandmother, his family's stability. There was no space to process it as his loss. The childhood pain had no such container around it — it just landed.

Letting in the bigger loss requires letting yourself be real. If you've depersonalized, the murder of your mother sits in the same foggy distance as everything else. The bullying, which happened before the full dissociation set in, got in under the wire.

The Unlovability Core and What It Does to Relationships

There's a question mark sitting at the center of Doc's psychology: maybe I am lovable. It's fragile, but it's there. His behavior in romantic relationships makes complete sense once you see it:

  • He rarely initiates; people come to him
  • He agrees to relationships to avoid hurting someone, not from genuine romantic feeling
  • He describes it as "friendship plus" — present, caring, not infatuated
  • He has experienced one person who felt like a genuine match, and that relationship didn't survive the mismatch in sexual orientation
  • Since then, intimate closeness has become uncomfortable rather than appealing

The logic underneath this is protective, not broken. If you let yourself fall fully in love and that person confirms what you've feared your whole life — that you're fundamentally not worth it — that's the final nail. As long as you never fully commit, the question mark survives. You can still tell yourself: maybe I am lovable, I just haven't let someone close enough to find out.

"Right now there's still a question mark in your mind — maybe I am lovable. But if you love someone and they find you unlovable, it's going to be the final nail in the coffin."

This isn't cowardice. It's the last line of defense for a self that has been told it has no value from the very beginning.

The Guilt That Lives Between Grief and Anger

Doc describes his stepfather as both the best father figure he ever had and the person who destroyed his family. He's angrier that the man killed himself than that he killed Doc's mother — because the suicide took away the only target for the rage.

He also mentions that the stepfather had warned them, matter-of-factly, that they'd regret throwing him out. He visited Doc at work the night before, drunk, asked for a hug, got it. Left Doc out of the suicide note entirely — mentioned the sister, the mother, the brother, but not Doc.

These details create a specific kind of guilt that doesn't have a clean name: I was close to him. I gave him a hug. I feel something toward him. And he did this. The closeness doesn't cancel the horror. The horror doesn't cancel the closeness. Both are real, and they don't resolve into a single emotion.

The path forward here involves learning to hold contradictions without needing to collapse them — to feel grief and rage and love toward the same person without deciding which one is the "true" feeling.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking depersonalization for depression. Doc isn't sad about his life passing him by — and that's what scares him. Depression typically involves felt suffering. Depersonalization can look like calm, functionality, even social ease. Someone living in the third person may seem fine, and may genuinely not know why they feel so little investment in their own existence.

Confusing emotional flatness with being "over it." Doc returned to work within a week of his mother's death because structure helped. That was wise. But he also interpreted his ability to discuss the events calmly as evidence he'd processed them. Telling a traumatic story like it's an interesting anecdote isn't integration — it's a way of keeping it at a safe narrative distance.

Attributing the fear of closeness to personality or preference. "I'm just not a romantic person" or "I just don't get infatuated" can be real traits, but they can also be the mind's way of protecting the question mark. It's worth asking whether the unavailability is a fixed feature of who you are or a layer of protection that arrived after enough evidence that love leads to loss.

Performing stability for others. Doc is careful to discuss his experiences in a way that doesn't make people feel obligated to console him — in direct reaction to watching an uncle who used suffering to pull care from people. The instinct to not burden others is considerate. But it can also mean nobody ever gets to respond to you honestly, which keeps you permanently in the role of the person who is fine.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Find a therapist experienced with trauma, specifically complex/developmental trauma. PTSD from a single incident and PTSD from a childhood of accumulated rejection require somewhat different approaches. Look for someone who understands both. A post-crisis counselor assigned as a formality is not the same thing.

  2. Notice when you switch from living to narrating. Pay attention to the moments in conversation when you shift into storytelling mode — clear, calm, even entertaining. That shift is information. It doesn't mean stop talking; it means notice what's underneath the story that you haven't let yourself feel yet.

  3. Practice making things real in small doses. When something happens that should matter — good or bad — give yourself two minutes alone to actually ask: what do I feel about this? Not what you think about it, not what the logical read is. Just sit with the feeling, even if it's faint or strange.

  4. Resist the urge to immediately redirect your attention to someone else's needs. The next time you feel a flicker of something for your own life — excitement, grief, want — stay with it for a moment before turning it into someone else's side quest.

  5. Let people respond to you. Once, in a conversation you trust, share something real without editing out the parts that might make someone feel like they should say something. Let them say something. See what happens when you allow the interaction instead of managing it.

  6. Write down the evidence on both sides. On one side: all the evidence that you are unlovable (the list already exists in your head). On the other side: evidence that contradicts it. Your sister calling you her best friend and more of a father than a brother counts. Let the second list be as detailed as the first.
