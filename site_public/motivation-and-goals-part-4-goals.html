Summary

TL;DR — Most people fail at goals not because they lack willpower, but because they skip the foundational work: setting the right goal, breaking it into concrete steps, and learning from what actually works.

Goal-setting looks simple on the surface — pick a target, go get it. But the process most people use is almost entirely driven by gut instinct, social comparison, and wishful thinking. This talk builds a practical framework for setting goals that actually stick, drawing on neuroscience, behavioral economics, public health research, and clinical experience.

Why Goals Usually Fail Before You Even Start

Most people arrive at their goals through what Daniel Kahneman called Type 1 reasoning — fast, automatic, emotion-driven thinking. You wake up one day and decide you need to get in shape. You see someone else doing it. You feel bad about yourself. A goal is born. This feels logical from the inside, but it's essentially your gut making a snap decision and then constructing a rational story around it afterward.

The research on advertising illustrates this clearly: when a store places Oreos at eye level, shoppers pick them up — and then sincerely believe they bought them because they wanted them. The actual cause and the perceived cause are completely different things.

Type 2 reasoning — slow, deliberate, methodical thinking — is what actually produces better outcomes. It's cognitively uncomfortable. It feels boring. That's exactly why people avoid it. Training doctors to stop trusting their first instinct and use systematic differential diagnosis takes years of deliberate practice. The same principle applies to goal-setting. Your job as someone working on goals (or coaching others) is to drag the process from Type 1 into Type 2, even briefly.

The 25% Rule: Starting Where You Actually Are

The first concrete technique is the 25% rule. Instead of looking at where you want to end up and plotting a path there, find the halfway point between your current state and your goal — then find the halfway point of that. That smaller target is where you actually start.

This works because the brain has a circuit that measures the gap between current capability and desired outcome. When that gap is huge, motivation drops. Nobody wakes up motivated to become president of the United States because the gap between "person reading this" and "leader of the free world" is so large it feels unreal.

Shrinking the first target does several things simultaneously: - Brings the goal within range of positive reinforcement (you can actually succeed) - Adjusts expectations to something realistic - Forces a present-focused orientation - Models acceptance rather than self-criticism

The predictable objection from clients (or from yourself) is "that's not enough." The key reframe: enough has to do with where you want to go; a starting point has nothing to do with enough. Enough is an outcome orientation. Your baseline is not a failure state — it's just where you currently are. Establishing a clear baseline and asking "what's one step above this?" changes the entire cognitive framing.

Give Choices, Not Advice

A second evidence-based principle: choice beats advice.

When you tell someone what to do, they're receiving someone else's solution to a generic version of their problem. It doesn't engage them. It often feels invalidating. And it comes straight from the advice-giver's own Type 1 reasoning — a quick gut reaction dressed up as wisdom.

When you give someone a menu of options and ask them to choose, something different happens. They engage Type 2 reasoning. They think about their own situation, preferences, and constraints. The path they land on is theirs.

Concretely: instead of "you should go to the gym," ask "do you think a diet approach or an exercise approach fits better for you right now?" Then, "what are all the ways you could exercise?" Then, "which of those feels most realistic?" Even if both routes lead to the same answer, arriving there through choice produces meaningfully better outcomes. This is why the planning stage and the action stage are psychologically distinct — you can't shortcut one by jumping straight to the other.

Operationalizing: Acting as Someone's Frontal Lobe

The frontal lobe handles executive function — the ability to take an abstract goal and break it into concrete, sequenced steps. This capacity develops slowly (it's not fully mature until the mid-twenties) and is genuinely hard to deploy even in adults, especially under stress.

The two-year-old who can't "clean her room" but can "put your books on the shelf" isn't being difficult. She literally cannot process the abstract command into action steps yet. Adults face the same wall when they confront open-ended problems: get a job, get healthy, fix your life.

One useful pattern: gamers tend to be excellent at closed-ended problem solving — give them a discrete task with clear pieces and they execute beautifully. But open-ended problems (getting into college, finding a job, building a social life) have no obvious starting pieces, and that's where they stall.

Your job is to convert open-ended problems into closed-ended ones:

"This week you're not going to find a job. You're going to Google three resume templates, look at them, and pick one. Then you'll replace the name at the top with yours. Then you'll write down on paper everything you think belongs on a resume."

That's it. Three micro-tasks, each achievable in minutes. The abstract goal has been made concrete, and success becomes possible. Over time, as clients practice this process with your support, they internalize it — the frontal lobe trains itself, and what required coaching becomes automatic.

Making Things Conscious Before Making Things Better

There's a model of skill development that goes through four stages: 1. Unconscious incompetence — you don't know what you don't know 2. Conscious incompetence — you know you're bad at it 3. Conscious competence — you can do it, but it takes real effort 4. Unconscious competence — it becomes natural

Most people trying to change their behavior want to jump straight from stage 1 to stage 4. They want to skip the middle, where all the actual growth happens. The real job of goal-setting isn't moving you from "bad at working out" to "naturally athletic" — it's moving you from unconscious to conscious, because you can only fix what you can see.

"Change happens down here [at conscious incompetence], not up here [at unconscious competence]. As long as clients keep trying to go straight from the beginning to the end, they're doomed to failure."

This is why even a goal-setting session that produces a "bad" plan (like doing push-ups in a game queue) isn't a failure — it's getting someone to think about the problem deliberately. That shift is what produces results over time.

Troubleshooting: Three Goal Problems and What to Do About Them

1. No goal at all. Some people arrive directionless and hopeless. The work here is exploratory — not directing them anywhere, but mapping where they are and what they care about. Ask what makes it hard to choose a direction. Uncover the underlying hopelessness before trying to build anything.

2. Overly ambitious goals. These usually come from social comparison and the tyranny of "should" and "enough." Philosophically, you have to deconstruct the idea that "enough" is an inherent property of effort. Practically, you use the 25% rule and stay ready for the "it's not enough" resistance when it comes — because it will.

Also watch for goals that become wish lists. A single goal is manageable. The same goal with ten additions ("and I'll also eat better, and sleep earlier, and meditate, and call my parents…") overwhelms the frontal lobe's capacity. Scale back.

3. Misdirected goals. This is the trickiest case. A client who says "I need a bigger bucket to bail water from my sinking boat" is being logical — but they need to plug the holes. Misdirected goals come from cognitive blind spots, psychological defense mechanisms, and ego protection. They require challenging the client (once trust is established):

"I'm wondering if there might be a different way to think about how to fix this problem."

The other sign of a misdirected goal: solutions borrowed wholesale from what worked for someone else on the internet, without any consideration of whether the underlying problem is the same.

Learning from What Worked: Positive Deviance

When troubleshooting, most people focus entirely on failures. A more effective approach comes from positive deviance — a framework developed in public health.

When researchers were sent to address child malnutrition in rural Vietnam (with no food aid available), they asked a different question: half the children were malnourished, but why weren't the other half? What were the families who were succeeding doing differently? The answer turned out to be small behavioral habits — scooping protein-rich organisms from the rice fields, eating nutritious but socially stigmatized vegetables — that could be taught to everyone else.

Applied to personal goals: if a client did yoga for three days and then stopped, the question isn't only why did you fail days four through seven? It's equally what made days one through three work? Find the conditions of success and duplicate them. This shifts the lens from self-blame ("I'm a failure") to curiosity about circumstance ("what was different?"), which is both more accurate and more actionable.

The Dimensions of a Goal

Rather than treating a goal as a single thing to hit or miss, it helps to break it into distinct dimensions that can each be examined and adjusted:

  • Target — Is the goal itself the right one? Is it sized appropriately (25% rule)?
  • Actions — Have abstract intentions been operationalized into concrete steps?
  • Examples of success and failure — What have you learned from attempts so far?
  • Resistance — What emotions, identity issues, or unconscious patterns get in the way?
  • Physiologic factors — Sleep, diet, vitamin deficiencies, inflammatory states, circadian disorders all affect motivation in ways that have nothing to do with willpower
  • Psychological stressors — Stress consumes working memory and depletes the frontal lobe; a bad week at work isn't a character flaw, it's a RAM problem
  • Social influences — Who pulls you forward? Who holds you back? (Including people who think they're being supportive but aren't)
  • Environmental factors — Where is your phone? Where do you work? What does your physical environment nudge you toward?
  • Stakeholders — Especially for professional goals: who else has a stake in this outcome, and are they aligned with your goal or quietly working against it?

Common Pitfalls

  • Skipping straight to action. Jumping to "what do I do?" before examining intent, resistance, and baseline is the single most common mistake. It's why people end up feeling stuck despite "knowing what they need to do."
  • Treating a goal as a wish list. Adding more goals doesn't build momentum — it paralyzes the frontal lobe and guarantees failure across the board.
  • Focusing only on failures. Replaying losses without mining wins misses the most useful data you have.
  • Confusing the feeling of logic with actual reasoning. Type 1 thinking feels just as rational as Type 2. The fact that a goal "makes sense" doesn't mean you reasoned your way to it.
  • Anchoring on "enough." Measuring your starting point against the finish line destroys motivation before you even begin. The baseline is not a deficit — it's the starting line.
  • Adopting someone else's solution. What worked for a fitness influencer may have nothing to do with your actual problem. Borrowed solutions to a different problem don't fix your problem.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Establish your real baseline. Before setting any goal, write down exactly what you did in the last week related to that goal. Not what you intended — what you actually did. Call this your baseline, not a failure.

  2. Apply the 25% rule. Find the halfway point between your baseline and your goal. Then find the halfway point of that. Set your first target there. Expect to feel like it's "not enough" — notice that feeling and set it aside.

  3. Generate a menu before committing. Before deciding how to pursue your goal, list every possible approach you can think of. Aim for at least five options, including unconventional ones. Then choose one. The act of choosing (rather than being told) meaningfully improves follow-through.

  4. Operationalize one goal this week. Take a single goal and break it down until each step takes 30 minutes or less and requires no prior decisions to execute. If a step still feels abstract, break it down further.

  5. Run a positive deviance review. If you've attempted this goal before, write down every time you made progress — even small progress. What were the conditions? What time of day? Who were you with? What had happened earlier that day? Look for patterns in the successes, not just the failures.

  6. Audit your social and environmental influences. Write down the names of three people you spend the most time with and honestly assess: does spending time with each of them make your goal more likely or less likely? Then look at your physical environment — what does it automatically nudge you toward?

  7. When you fail, ask "what got in the way?" not "why did I fail?" The first question looks outward at circumstances you can change. The second invites identity-level self-criticism that leads nowhere useful.
