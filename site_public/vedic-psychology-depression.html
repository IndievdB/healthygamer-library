Vedic Psychology

In this video, I'll share an incredibly useful perspective on the mind developed by yogis in ancient India. In the West, our understanding of the mind was developed by external observation. In India, it was developed by internal observation. The internal understanding of the mind, or vedic psychology, is far more accessible to the average person because the tools you have to study your mind are the same as what yogis used. Understanding vedic psychology is a crucial first step to understanding yourself.

Summary

TL;DR — Your mind has five distinct parts, and understanding how they interact is the first step to actually changing your behavior. The Vedic model of the mind, developed by yogis through direct self-observation, offers a simpler and more personally applicable map of mental experience than the Western psychological tradition — and you can learn it in an hour.

Western psychology, starting with Freud, built its theories by listening to people's words and inferring what was happening inside. That's a weak signal: for every sentence you speak, there are thousands of thoughts behind it. Western science is also population-based — it tells you what the average mind does, not what your mind does. The Vedic model took the opposite approach. The yogis sat down, closed their eyes, and directly observed their own minds. Because you are the only one who can observe your own thoughts from the inside, this methodology gives a quality of data no external observer can match.

The result is a five-part model of the mind that is both precise and teachable.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking distraction for processing. When you feel bad and watch something funny, play a game, or eat ice cream, the emotion doesn't get digested — it goes dormant. Many people interpret feeling better as having "dealt with" something. They haven't.

Mistaking Ahamkara-driven reasoning for genuine logic. The thoughts your ego produces when it's protecting you feel completely rational. The tells: you're conveniently forgetting evidence that cuts against you, you're making comparisons between yourself and someone else, and the reasoning appeared right after you felt hurt or threatened.

Assuming emotional intensity means emotional maturity is impossible.
