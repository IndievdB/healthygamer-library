Summary

TL;DR — Tiredness is often an emotion, not just a physical state. Your brain uses it as an energy-conservation signal to stop you from investing in things it perceives as high-effort, low-success-probability — and understanding that signal is the first step to working with it.

Most attempts to fight chronic tiredness focus on the body: more sleep, better diet, supplements, cutting out gluten. Those things matter for physical fatigue, but they don't touch a different, equally real kind of tiredness — the kind that hits you instantly when you see your boss's name in your inbox on a Friday afternoon, or when your phone lights up with a call you don't want to take. That kind of tiredness isn't about calories. It's about perception.

Tiredness Is an Emotion — and Emotions Are Information

Emotions aren't purely psychological. They're physiological too — a lump in the throat, butterflies in the stomach, a tight chest. We already use the right language: I feel tired. It's a feeling. And like all emotions, it carries information.

Fear tells you something might hurt you. Anger signals that a boundary is being crossed. Tiredness signals something different:

"Tiredness is our brain's response to a perception of a high workload."

Just as anxiety keeps you from making certain mistakes, tiredness keeps you from wasting energy. It's not a malfunction. It's a feature — one that just happens to misfire constantly in modern life.

Why Your Brain Switches on the "Tired" Signal

The mechanism makes evolutionary sense. When your brain looks ahead at a task and estimates:

  • The workload is very high
  • The probability of success or visible progress is low

...it concludes: don't bother. There's no point climbing 25,000 feet of Everest if you're going to turn around before the summit. Your brain would rather conserve that energy entirely.

"Tiredness is an energy conservation strategy — a way to shape our behavior, a way that our brain is making calculations and then using emotions to keep us from making mistakes."

The problem is that this system evolved long before taxes, dissertations, or difficult family dynamics existed. It was designed for physical, immediate challenges where a massive effort with low odds of success really was a bad trade. It was not designed for:

  • A tax return that feels infinite but actually takes a few focused hours
  • A dissertation that spans a year but can be advanced one chapter at a time
  • A holiday visit that feels emotionally impossible but is actually finite

Your brain can't accurately calculate these modern workloads. It sees "pay taxes" and perceives an endless, uncertain slog — so it pulls the tired lever. It sees "write dissertation" and thinks there is no finish line — tired lever again.

The Emotional Math Behind Social Exhaustion

The same mechanism explains why certain people and situations drain you before you've done anything at all. When a person's name appears on your phone and you feel immediately exhausted, your brain is doing a rapid calculation:

  • This person needs something from me
  • I cannot fully provide it
  • The interaction will cost me energy
  • The problem won't be resolved — the calls will keep coming

The predicted outcome is a net loss. So your brain tries to stop you from engaging at all by making you feel tired right now, before the conversation even starts. You hit "ignore," do nothing, and still feel exhausted — which makes no sense if tiredness is purely physical, but makes complete sense if it's emotional.

"Tiredness is a signal that there's a part of you that feels like this is a waste of energy."

This is also why you might feel vaguely drained all the time without being able to point to anything specific. If you're surrounded by obligations your brain has quietly categorized as "high cost, low return," the tired signal runs in the background continuously.

How to Read the Signal Instead of Fighting It

Once you understand that tiredness is information, you can start interrogating it rather than just trying to push through it or medicate it away.

When you notice tiredness, ask two questions in sequence:

1. What is my body telling me not to do? Name the specific thing. Not "everything" — the actual task or interaction triggering it.

2. Why does this feel like a waste of energy? How would I actually feel afterward? This is where it gets interesting. Your mind will give you one of two answers:

  • "Actually, I'd feel pretty good if I got two hours of tax work done." — In that case, your brain's threat estimate is wrong. You can challenge it.
  • "I'd still feel terrible, because there's so much left to do." — This reveals that your brain is using the size of the total project to argue against starting at all. "You have 100 chapters to read. Don't bother with chapter one." That logic, taken seriously, means you never do anything.

Spotting that second pattern is significant. It means your brain is actively disincentivizing progress — not because progress is bad, but because it can't see the finish line and has decided the whole journey is pointless.

Why Chunking Tasks Actually Works

Breaking large tasks into small pieces isn't just a productivity trick. It directly addresses the brain's tiredness calculation.

If the task is "write a dissertation," the workload estimate is astronomical and success feels impossible. Tired lever: pulled.

If the task is "read one chapter today," the brain can evaluate: effort = 30 minutes, outcome = one chapter done. That's a trade it can endorse. The tiredness lifts — or at least loosens — because the brain no longer perceives a futile investment.

"If my target today is one chapter, I have completed my target. That's actually a pretty good trade — and suddenly we feel less tired."

This reframes chunking: it's not about tricking yourself into working. It's about giving your brain accurate information so it stops misfiring the exhaustion signal.

Common Pitfalls

Treating all tiredness as physical. If you're supplementing, adjusting your diet, and optimizing sleep but still feel chronically drained, the tiredness may be emotional in origin. Physical interventions won't fix an emotional signal.

Ignoring the signal entirely. Tiredness around a particular person or situation is real data. Sometimes the right response isn't to push through — it's to recognize that the relationship or situation genuinely is costing you more than you're getting back.

Using the size of the whole task to justify not starting. "I have 100 chapters left, so reading chapter one is pointless." This is the brain's logic when it's misfiring. Recognize it for what it is.

Assuming something is wrong with you. Feeling emotionally tired doesn't mean you're depressed, lazy, or broken. The mechanism is universal and adaptive. It just needs to be understood and recalibrated, not pathologized.

Avoiding without relief. Hitting "ignore" on a call, putting off the taxes, skipping the holiday — these don't end the tiredness if the underlying calculation hasn't changed. The calls keep coming. The return date keeps approaching. Avoidance delays the cost; it doesn't eliminate it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the moment tiredness appears. For the next week, notice when tiredness hits suddenly — especially in response to a thought, a notification, or a person. Write it down. "I felt tired when ___." This alone starts to reveal the pattern.

  2. Ask what your body is telling you not to do. Get specific. Not "everything" — the actual, named thing. "My body is telling me not to call my mother back." "My body is telling me not to open that document."

  3. Run the outcome question. Ask yourself honestly: If I actually did this thing for two hours today, how would I feel at the end of it? Don't answer immediately — sit with it. If the answer is "pretty good, actually," your brain's threat assessment is off and you can act on that.

  4. Catch the "don't even start" logic. If your mind says "there's so much left, starting now is pointless," write that thought down exactly. Then ask: Does doing one small piece today actually make my situation worse? Usually the answer is no — which exposes the faulty math.

  5. Shrink the task to today's unit only. Don't ask yourself to finish the project. Ask: what is one concrete, completable thing I can do today? One chapter. One form. One 20-minute walk. Frame it so "done" is actually achievable by tonight.

  6. Apply the same questions to draining relationships. When a person makes you feel tired before you've even spoken, ask: What outcome am I predicting from this interaction? If the honest answer is "I'll feel depleted and nothing will change," that's information worth taking seriously — either about what you need from that relationship, or about limits you need to set.
