You're Not Broken — You Were Hurt

Almost everyone in this conversation was exposed to pornography well before puberty, several following experiences of early sexual trauma. The word that kept coming up was damaged — not weak, not perverted, not lacking willpower. Damaged.

That distinction matters enormously. A disease happens to you. You don't contract a marijuana addiction by walking past a park. But for these men, the addiction had the character of something that afflicted them — an encounter at age 11 that felt like "tasting sugar for the first time," before they had any framework for what it was or what it would do.

"You guys are just normal people that had something bad happen to you, and then your brain is wired a certain way."

The brain of a ten-year-old exposed to high-stimulus pornography isn't choosing an addiction. It's encountering an overwhelming dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → signal with no context, no guidance, and no off-ramp. From that moment, the neural groove is cut deep.

What the Hunger Actually Is

The language everyone used to describe their experience was strikingly physical — not mental. Hungry. A wave. A coma. A tidal wave you push back with your hands. Nobody said "I find myself cognitively preoccupied." They said their lower spine lit up. Their whole body tensed. They trembled.

This is the key insight: the craving is not primarily a thought. It's a body state. And fighting it at the level of thought — telling yourself reasons not to do it, making arguments, bargaining — is like trying to hold back the ocean with your hands. You can do it for a while. You will eventually lose.

"You can't fight off hunger. Hunger needs to be satisfied. The question is — can you eat better food?"

The deeper hunger isn't actually for pornography. Pornography is the only food currently available to a part of you that is starving for something else: connection, warmth, the feeling of being whole and understood. When, during the call itself, everyone started feeling genuinely heard by strangers on the internet, the physical hunger softened — without anyone doing anything about pornography directly.

"The part of you that's hungry — what happens to it when you feel understood by others?" "It goes away. Right now it's completely gone."

That's the mechanism laid bare. The hunger is for wholeness. Porn is a counterfeit that quiets it for forty minutes and leaves you more depleted than before.

  2. The physical craving surges
  3. You fight it in your mind — and the smarter you are, the better your brain gets at manufacturing reasons to give in
  4. Eventually you relapse
  5. You feel shame and disgust
  6. That shame feeds the original emptiness, which increases the next craving

The mind prefers what it knows over what's good. After a decade of this pattern, there is actually a part of you that will resist getting better — not because you want to stay addicted, but because survival in a familiar hell feels safer than the unknown of a life without it.

As you genuinely start to heal, the hunger will fight back harder. It doesn't want to die. Expect this. It's not relapse evidence that you're hopeless — it's evidence that something is actually changing.

Why This Isn't About Controlling Your Mind

The solution most people try is mental: argue yourself out of it, set rules, track streaks, use apps. This gets framed as "willpower vs. addiction," and willpower loses, eventually, every time — because the craving lives in the body and you're defending the wall in your head.

The productive question shifts from "how do I stop doing this" to "how do I satisfy what's actually hungry?"

What actually weakens the craving: - Genuine human connection — being around people who care, feeling understood, not being alone with it - Physical practices that train the mind to stay present in the body (more on this below) - Reducing idleness — the craving surges in empty, unstructured time; it recedes when attention is genuinely demanded elsewhere - Treating the underlying wound — the shame, the early trauma, the belief (common in this group) that you somehow deserve the pain

The goal is not to destroy the hunger. It's to tame it by feeding what it's actually asking for.

Idleness accelerates the problem. So does isolation. These aren't minor contributing factors — they are the environment in which the craving thrives.

Practically, this means: - Structured time with other people is not a nice-to-have; it's treatment - Having even one person who knows what you're dealing with changes the internal landscape - CommunityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → with others who understand (like the people on this call) actively reduces the physical hunger in real time

The shame that surrounds this addiction keeps it in the dark. Things kept in the dark grow. The conversation itself — seven strangers admitting this to each other on the internet — was more therapeutic than years of private willpower attempts, not because any technique was deployed but because the darkness got some light.

Common Pitfalls

Treating it as a willpower problem. The craving is physical and deep. Trying to argue or discipline yourself out of it ignores where it actually lives.

Using sex as a substitute. Partnered sex and this addiction are not the same phenomenon. Replacing one with the other doesn't address what's actually hungry, and several people in this conversation found they still used pornography even in relationships.

Thinking you need to destroy it. The frame of "killing" or "eliminating" the addiction sets up a war you're likely to lose. The better frame is weakening it, taming it, and starving it of the conditions (isolation, idleness, shame-in-the-dark) it needs to stay strong.

Expecting the hardest moments to come early. As you actually start to heal, resistance increases. The craving gets louder right before it gets quieter. This is normal and not a sign you're failing.

Keeping it secret. Shame is the addiction's life support. Sharing — even with one person, even with strangers online — demonstrably reduces the physical grip of the craving.

Nofap as the whole plan. Abstinence tracking can be a useful tool, but on its own it leaves untouched the underlying hunger and the life structure (idleness, isolation) that feeds the cycle.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Find a yoga or Tai Chi class and go. This is the single most-recommended concrete step. Let the instructor know about any injuries. Don't compete with anyone in the room; the moment you compare yourself to others, your attention has left your body.

  2. Practice the arm-raise exercise daily. Hold your arms straight out or overhead. Hold them until your body is demanding your attention. Then let go. Do this until "letting go" is something you have felt, not just something you've read about.

  3. Learn urge surfing. Next time a craving arises, don't fight it in your mind. Step out of the mental argument and drop attention into the physical sensation in your body. Observe it without judging it as good or bad. Watch what happens to it over five to ten minutes.

  4. Practice mula bandha. At the end of a meditation session or yoga class, do 5–10 rounds of a three-second gentle contraction of the perineal muscle, followed by full release. Build gradually. Stop if anything hurts.

  5. Actively reduce idleness. Identify the specific times of day and environmental conditions (alone, at home, late at night, nothing demanding your attention) when cravings peak. Make a concrete plan for those windows — not just "stay busy" but a specific activity, ideally one that involves your body or other people.

  6. Find at least one person or community to be honest with. This does not require broadcasting it publicly. A discord group, a single trusted friend, a therapist, or an online community of people who get it. The craving weakens measurably in the presence of genuine understanding.

  7. Identify one thing you want to be different about your life in three years — something that requires consistent time and effort. Start doing one small thing toward it tomorrow. Not a wish list. One thing.

  8. If cravings feel unmanageable, see a professional. A doctor or mental health professional can evaluate whether anxiety, depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, or other factors are amplifying the cycle, and medication may be appropriate as one part of a broader plan. This is not failure; it's treating a physical condition with appropriate tools.

  9. If you believe you deserve the pain — if there's a voice that says this is yours to carry, that you're fundamentally damaged beyond repair — treat that belief as the actual target. It is the root that feeds the rest. A good therapist can help you trace where it started and begin to let it go.
