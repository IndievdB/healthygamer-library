Summary

TL;DR — The "Great Resignation" isn't a labor shortage, it's a wage and treatment shortage. Workers have finally gained the information and flexibility to act on what they've always known: many employers aren't worth the trade.

For decades, companies could get away with poor treatment, low pay, and dismissing employee concerns — not because those things were acceptable, but because workers lacked the tools to do anything about it. The internet and COVID-19 changed the underlying conditions. What looks like a sudden mass quitting is actually a long-overdue correction.

The Internet as an Equalizer

Two technologies reshaped power dynamics in human history before the internet: the printing press democratized information, and gunpowder democratized physical force. The internet is the third great equalizer — and its biggest effect on the labor market has been the removal of ignoranceAvidya is ignorance — specifically, a fundamental misunderstanding of what you truly are that sits at the root of all suffering.Full definition →.

Fifty years ago, if you lived in a small town with a bad job, you had a vague sense that better options existed elsewhere, but almost no ability to access them. Your comparison set was your immediate surroundings. Employers benefited enormously from that limitation. A worker who doesn't know what else is out there is much easier to underpay and overwork.

The same dynamic played out in restaurants. The reason food quality has broadly improved is that people can now instantly find out where good food exists. Bad restaurants lost customers they would have kept in an era of pure word-of-mouth. Bad employers are now facing the same reckoning.

What COVID Actually Revealed

COVID didn't create the problem — it removed the blindfold.

Companies had long sold workers on two unspoken assumptions: 1. You must physically be in the office to work. This meant accepting commutes of 30 minutes to two hours every single day as a fixed cost of employment. 2. Your job options are geographically limited. If you lived in a company town or a small city, the local employer held enormous leverage simply because switching jobs might mean uprooting your life.

Remote work demolished both assumptions simultaneously. Overnight, a worker in a small town in Alabama could apply for a position in a different state, a different industry, or even a different country. The same corporations that enthusiastically outsourced manufacturing to other countries because geography was no longer a constraint are now watching their employees apply the same logic in reverse.

COVID also gave people an extended, enforced look at what they actually need to be okay. The grinding promise — commute, work 9+ hours, grind for 40 years, retire somewhere warm at 65 — stopped sounding like a plan and started sounding like a bad deal. People discovered that an extra $5,000 or $10,000 a year is not worth an additional 15 hours a week of their lives.

Evolutionary Pressure (Or the Lack of It)

The clearest framework for understanding why companies developed such poor practices is evolutionary: behavior that faces no negative consequences will continue and expand.

For decades, there was no meaningful selection pressure against bad employer behavior. Workers couldn't easily leave. Unions helped, but they couldn't solve everything. Companies could treat people poorly, call anyone who complained lazy, and face no real consequences — so the behavior grew.

The game analogy is useful here. Research on play behavior shows that if one side loses more than 70% of the time, they stop playing. Workers have been losing for a very long time. Quitting en masse is just stopping the game.

"There is not a labor shortage. There is a wage shortage. The labor is there — it just doesn't want to work your crappy job."

Companies that adapted to what employees actually want — like giving people flexibility, decent food, the ability to set their own hours, genuine autonomy — found that loyalty followed, even at slightly lower salaries. The employers who are struggling aren't suffering from a bad job market. They're suffering from the consequences of choices they made over decades.

The Psychology of Invalidation

The deepest mistake many employers are making is psychological, not financial.

When a child acts out in school and gets told "you're a bad kid, you're lazy, you're the problem," the behavior gets worse, not better. The same dynamic plays out at scale. When companies respond to mass resignations by calling workers lazy, blaming government handouts, and outsourcing the problem to a PR firm, they're not fixing anything — they're accelerating it.

Somewhere along the way, many corporations substituted public relations for actual problem-solving. When a serious internal issue arose — a toxic culture, a discrimination investigation, unsustainable turnover — the response became: hire a PR firm, manage the perception, rebrand. Don't fix the underlying problem; fix how it looks.

This is the same as gaslighting in a relationship. You're not addressing the other person's legitimate concern; you're trying to convince the world (and them) that the concern isn't real. It's psychologically invalidating, and people who feel chronically invalidated eventually stop engaging entirely.

"Until you make your place a place where people actually want to work, you can whine about the government as much as you want — you're not fixing the problem."

The irony is that the companies best positioned to course-correct are the ones least likely to do so, because they've spent years attributing their problems to external causes. The mental model that protects their ego ("workers are lazy, the government is paying them to stay home") is exactly what prevents them from asking the questions that would actually help: What would make you want to stay? What can we do better as an employer?

What Good Employers Actually Do

Some employers have figured this out. The ones that tend to retain people share a few common traits:

  • They take care of basic quality-of-life needs (not just salary)
  • They give employees flexibility over when and how they work
  • They treat strong performance as a reason to invest more in someone, not to extract more from them
  • They actually listen when people describe what makes work difficult

When engineers love working somewhere even though they could make more money elsewhere, it's because the employer has done the math that most employers refuse to do: turnover is expensive, unhappy employees are unproductive, and treating people well has a measurable return.

The math isn't complicated. If a company's turnover dropped significantly, how many more deals could they close? How much institutional knowledgeVidya is the Sanskrit term for objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a book, verified by multiple people, and passed from one person to another without loss.Full definition → would they retain? How much would they save on recruiting and onboarding? The companies that ask these questions tend to get better. The ones that don't tend to appear on r/antiwork.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking this is purely about money. Salary matters, but people leaving well-paying jobs in investment banking or medicine for lower-paying alternatives tells you that compensation alone isn't the issue. Respect, autonomy, and whether the trade-off feels worth it are doing most of the work.

Assuming workers just "don't want to work." This framing is both inaccurate and counterproductive. It's the employer version of calling a struggling student lazy instead of asking what's actually going on.

Believing PR can substitute for culture change. Rebranding and managed messaging might work in the short term, but people talk to each other, reviews accumulate, and eventually the gap between the company's stated valuesValues are the things that, when you move toward them, make you feel genuinely fulfilled — not just pleased or validated, but deeply satisfied.Full definition → and the lived experience becomes visible.

Overcorrecting by blaming all employers. Some companies genuinely treat people well and are absent from these conversations. The presence of good employers makes the point stronger: it's possible to do this right, which means the ones doing it wrong are making a choice.

Ignoring that workers also have psychological biases. People do tend to overestimate how much they contribute and underestimate fair compensation — this is normal human psychology. Acknowledging it doesn't mean employers get a pass; it means the solution requires honest negotiation rather than workers being automatically right about every demand.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your own work trade-off honestly. Write down what you give to your job (hours, commute, stress, missed events) and what you actually get back (pay, flexibility, meaning, respect). Is the exchange genuinely fair to you, or have you been treating certain costs as fixed when they aren't?

  2. Map your options. If remote work expanded your geographic range, have you actually explored what that means for your career? Spend an hour researching roles in your field at companies you've never considered because they weren't local.

  3. Identify what you actually need vs. what you've been told you need. Make a short list of what genuinely matters to you in work (autonomy, schedule, mission, income, learning) versus what you've accepted as "just how it is" without questioning it.

  4. If you manage people, ask directly. Schedule one-on-ones with the specific question: What could we do differently that would make this a better place for you to work? Then listen without defending. The information you get is worth more than any PR strategy.

  5. If you're job-hunting, look for signals of how companies handle conflict. Search for the company name on review sites and notice not what problems come up, but how the company responded to them. A company that acknowledges problems and changes is categorically different from one that denies and deflects.

  6. Notice the validation gap at work. When something goes wrong in your workplace, observe whether leadership's response addresses the actual problem or manages perception. That one distinction tells you a lot about whether the culture is likely to improve.
