Why "Readiness" Is a Trap

Readiness feels like a reasonable prerequisite. Before you do the hard thing, you want to feel prepared, capable, and free of doubt. Sounds sensible. But look at what readiness actually means when you trace it:

"Readiness is this mythical thing that wipes away all of your anxieties. When you're ready — I'm confident, let's go — you don't want all those anxieties."

In other words, being ready means being certain. It means having no nagging doubts, no open wounds, no gaps in your qualifications. And that state never arrives — because you cannot know whether you can handle something until you have actually handled it. Waiting for readiness is logically absurd:

"How can you be ready for something before you do it?"

No one is ready to become a parent before their first child. No parent is ready for a second child just because they survived the first. These aren't failures of preparation — they're features of how new experience works. You cannot download competence in advance.

Readiness Is Just Procrastination in a Suit

Plain procrastination is "not today." Readiness-based procrastination is more insidious because it comes with a built-in justification: not today, but soon — once I've fixed X, healed Y, and achieved Z.

"Until you're ready, you have an endless supply of not-todays."

That supply never runs out. The person in the example has been interested in psychology since middle school, is now 25, and has a fresh list of conditions that must be met before they can start: fully overcome social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, finish processing past traumas, get stable enough to balance work and school. Each item is real and worth working on. But none of them are actually prerequisites — they're the vocabulary of an indefinite delay.

Notice the pattern in how the post reads: positive, positive, positive, positive — but competitive, but a lot, but struggling, but my own problems, but not ready. The passion is genuine and clearly documented. The buts are infinite.

The Seven-Year Cost

One of the sharpest points here is a simple arithmetic of time:

"You're 25 years old and you've been interested in psychology since middle school. Seven years from now you could have a doctorate, or you could be thinking about doing psychology."

This isn't meant to induce shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. It's an invitation to make the cost of waiting concrete. Every year spent waiting to be ready is a year that could have been spent doing the thing and learning from it — even failing at it. The person who applies and gets rejected learns something. The person who applies and gets accepted learns something. The person who never applies remains exactly where they were, only older.

Give yourself forgiveness for the years already spent. Then use the next seven differently.

Confidence Doesn't Come From Certainty

This is the counterintuitive core of the whole lesson. Most people assume the sequence goes: get ready → feel confident → act. But that sequence is backwards and, more importantly, self-defeating.

"Confidence doesn't come from certainty. Confidence can't exist when there's certainty."

If you use a cheat code in a game to guarantee you'll win, you don't walk away feeling confident in your skill. You walk away having won nothing real. Genuine confidence — the kind that actually carries you through hard things — is forged by acting under uncertainty and discovering that you didn't collapse. You survive not being ready, and that is what makes you believe you can do it again.

The sequence that actually works: act before you're ready → experience uncertainty and difficulty → survive it → develop real confidence. There is no shortcut that gets you to the confidence without the discomfort.

Passion vs. Purpose, and What to Do With Both

The original question frames this as "passion vs. purpose" — are they the same thing? The implicit answer here is that the distinction doesn't matter much if you're using either one as a reason to wait rather than a reason to move.

The person clearly has both: they think about psychology every day, they get excited when they feel like they've helped someone understand themselves, they've cared about it for over a decade. That is unusually strong signal. The concern about feeding one's own ego is actually a mark of self-awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → and integrity, not a disqualifier.

The issue isn't whether the passion is genuine or whether the purpose is worthy. It is. The issue is that passion and purpose, by themselves, do not generate action. They generate feeling. Acting on them — imperfectly, uncertainly, before you're ready — is the only way they become a life.

"If it's what you're interested in, if it's what you're passionate about, if it's what you've cared about since middle school — go do it."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking self-improvement for prerequisite work. Addressing your anxiety, processing traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, and getting more stable are genuinely good things to do. The mistake is framing them as requirements that must be fully completed before you can begin something else. Growth and action happen in parallel, not in sequence.

Confusing humility with paralysis. The person in the example shows admirable restraint — they don't give advice because they aren't trained yet. That's healthy and honest. But the correct response to "I'm not qualified" is to go get qualified, not to stay unqualified indefinitely out of caution.

Waiting for certainty to produce confidence. As covered above, this is backwards. If you keep waiting until you're sure it'll work out, you are actually waiting for the one condition under which confidence cannot form.

Using real obstacles as permanent excuses. Competitive programs, financial difficulty, a demanding job, genuine mental health challenges — these are real. They are also things that can be navigated during the process of pursuing a goal, not only before it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the condition you're waiting on. Write down, in one specific sentence, what you believe has to be true before you can start the thing you've been deferring. "I need to fully overcome my anxiety" or "I need to know I'll get in." Make it concrete and honest.

  2. Calculate the timeline cost of waiting. If your goal takes five to seven years to accomplish, and you start today versus two years from now, what does your life look like at each endpoint? Write both versions out.

  3. Separate prerequisites from parallel work. Review your list of things you think need to be fixed first. Ask of each one: does this actually have to come before I start, or can I work on it while I start? Most items belong in the second category.

  4. Take the smallest concrete next step this week. For the person in the example, that's researching accredited doctoral programs and noting application requirements. For you, it's whatever the equivalent is. Not a commitment — just information-gathering that moves you one inch closer.

  5. Notice the "but" pattern in your own thinking. When you catch yourself listing reasons you're not ready, write them down. Then read the full list back. Ask whether the positives in your situation genuinely outweigh the buts, and whether the buts are actual blockers or just discomfort wearing the costume of logic.

  6. Act once in the face of uncertainty this week — in any domain. Send the message, submit the application, sign up for the class. The goal isn't success; it's the experience of surviving the discomfort of not being ready. That experience, accumulated over time, is what confidence is actually made of.
