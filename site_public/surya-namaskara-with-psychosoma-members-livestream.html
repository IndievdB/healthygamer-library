Summary

TL;DR — This is a guided Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation) session led by a yoga and meditation teacher, moving from beginner modifications through the regular sequence to advanced variations, followed by a brief ego ("who am I") meditation.

The session is a full mind-body practice, not just a physical workout. It's structured around three tiers of Surya Namaskar — modified beginner, standard, and advanced — each done for three rounds, with breath and movement synchronized throughout. The physical practice closes with an ego meditation drawn from Dr. K's meditation guide, inviting you to drop the question "who am I?" into your awareness and observe what surfaces. Throughout, the emphasis is on building body intuition, staying present to sensation, and bringing the same quality of embodied awareness you cultivate on the mat into everything else you do.

Why Surya Namaskar Works as a Practice

The power of Surya Namaskar isn't just anatomical — it's the fact that you can fall into it. Because the sequence is fixed, once you've internalized the poses you stop spending mental energy on "what comes next" and can go deeper into what you're actually feeling at each moment. That quality of repetitive, patterned movement is what allows it to become meditative.

In the Ashtanga tradition, the traditional goal is 108 rounds in a single session. That number isn't the point for most people starting out, but it signals something important: the practice is designed to be cycled. Each round you notice something slightly different — more openness in the hips, a tighter morning hamstring, an emotion that surfaces without obvious cause. The sequence becomes a moving diagnostic of your inner state.

"You kind of fall into the flow of it rather than getting wrapped up on the technique."

This is also why Surya Namaskar works particularly well as a gateway to meditation. In the classical eight-limb framework of yoga, asana practice (physical postures) is explicitly meant to prepare the body and nervous system for pranayama (breath work) and eventually for meditation. If you find sitting meditation nearly impossible, the structured movement of Surya Namaskar is often a more accessible on-ramp.

The Three-Tier Structure: Beginner, Regular, Advanced

The session uses a layered approach so that practitioners at very different levels can follow along. Here's what distinguishes each tier:

Beginner modifications prioritize safety and accessibility: - Feet hip-width apart in forward fold rather than together - Knees bent in Downward Facing Dog - Knees-chest-chin (modified Chaturanga) instead of a full lowering - Sphinx pose (forearms on mat) instead of Cobra - Yoga blocks under the hands in Equestrian Pose (Ashwa Sanchalanasana) - The focus is on learning the shape and building abdominal and glute engagement, not depth

Regular (Surya Namaskar A) introduces the classical form: - Feet together, straight legs in forward fold - Full Plank → Ashtanga Namaskara (knees-chest-chin lowering) → Cobra - Straight knees in Downward Facing Dog - One breath per movement as the target once the shapes are familiar

Advanced modifications add depth and hip opening: - Urdhva Baddhangulasana (interlaced fingers, palms pressed upward) instead of standard Urdhva Hastasana - Low Lunge with the back knee lifted instead of dropped - Lizard Pose (front foot outside the same-side shoulder) and optional inner-thigh pushout for deeper hip flexor work - Three-Legged Dog and Three-Legged Plank - Upward Facing Dog (Urdhvanukha Svanasana) instead of Cobra — hips off the mat entirely - Fingertip weight-bearing for wrist and finger joint mobility (only if already comfortable with it)

The core technical advice that runs through all three tiers: the work in the prone poses is not passive. Thrusting the pelvis into the mat, engaging the abdominals and glutes, and using the tops of the feet as anchor points is what protects the lower back. If you feel pressure in your lower back during Cobra or Sphinx, the muscles aren't switched on.

Breath and Movement: How to Synchronize Them

The classical instruction is one breath per movement — inhale on expansive, opening movements; exhale on compressing or folding movements. In the session's faster third rounds this is demonstrated as a flowing rhythm rather than a held count.

If you want to stay in a pose longer than one breath cycle, you don't have to abandon the breath-movement link. A practical approach: continue breathing naturally while held, and then use the next natural exhale as the cue to move into the following pose. This preserves the rhythmic quality even when you're lingering somewhere.

The breath-movement synchronization isn't just mechanical. It serves the same purpose as the body check-in at the opening of the session — it keeps your attention anchored in the body rather than drifting to thought. Shallow, fast breathing during a pose is often a signal that you've pushed past your current edge.

The Warm-Up: Why It Matters

The session opens with a deliberate warm-up targeting the specific joints and muscle groups Surya Namaskar demands:

  1. Child's Pose with lateral stretches — opens the side body and begins grounding attention inward
  2. Neck rolls — prepares the cervical spine for the neck loading in Cobra and Downward Dog
  3. Shoulder rolls — mobilizes the shoulder girdle before the weight-bearing plank and dog positions
  4. Supine windshield wipers — opens the hips, which are heavily engaged throughout the sequence
  5. Bridges — activates the glutes and abdominals, the two muscle groups most responsible for protecting the lower back in the sequence

Skipping the warm-up and going straight into Surya Namaskar, especially first thing in the morning, increases injury risk. A light warm-up is the minimum; the one demonstrated here takes roughly ten minutes and targets exactly what the sequence needs.

Body Intuition Over Prescription

A recurring theme is that the "right" way to practice is the one that matches your body on that particular day. Blocks are not a sign of limitation — they allow better spinal alignment, better chest opening, and better shoulder positioning than forcing the hands flat to the floor when that's not accessible. Using them often means more correct form, not less.

A useful heuristic offered for whether to reach for blocks: if bringing your hands to the mat is causing your shoulders to scrunch in or your spine to round significantly, the blocks will open things up enough to let you breathe better and hold the shape. You feel it; trust that.

On the question of how many rounds to do: do as many as you can while still maintaining technique and presence. When you notice your form degenerating or your attention scattering, stop there. The traditional 108-round target is aspirational, not prescriptive — it's a direction, not a daily minimum.

The Ego Meditation: "Who Am I?"

The closing practice is drawn from Dr. K's meditation guide (listed there as ego or Aham meditation). It pairs naturally with asana because the physical work has already drawn your attention inward and loosened some of the cognitive chatter that makes meditation difficult.

The method is simple but not easy:

  1. Come to a comfortable seated position. Lengthen the spine, relax the shoulders, choose a physical anchor (breath at the belly, sensations at the hands or feet, contact with the seat).
  2. With the anchor in the background, mentally drop the question: "Who am I?"
  3. Don't construct an answer. Wait. See what surfaces.
  4. Answers may be verbal (labels, roles, narratives), visual (images, memories), or somatic (physical patterns, tension, feeling-tones associated with certain identities).
  5. After each answer surfaces, mentally acknowledge it — "thank you" — then ask again: "But who am I really?"
  6. When distraction or overwhelm arises, return to the physical anchor, then come back to the question.

The goal isn't resolution. You're not trying to arrive at a final answer. You're mapping the identities and self-concepts you carry — including ones you didn't know were there — and noticing your relationship to them. Some might surprise you. Some might bring up strong emotions. That reaction is informative.

"We're not trying to reach a resolution. We're coming to realize what sort of identities we hold that we might even be unaware we're holding."

This practice is especially useful if you've been doing somatic or movement work already (like the Surya Namaskar just completed), because the body is already in a more present, less defended state. What surfaces in the meditation will often have more texture and truth than what would surface sitting cold.

Common Pitfalls

Treating the practice as purely physical. The sequence is a vehicle for cultivating mind-body connection. Going through the poses without any attention to sensation, breath, or emotional content misses the most important element.

Skipping the warm-up. Joints and muscles — particularly hips, shoulders, and hamstrings — need preparation before the demands of a full sequence. Especially true in the morning.

Avoiding blocks out of ego. Using blocks when you need them usually produces better alignment than white-knuckling your way to the floor. There is no merit badge for not using props.

Passive prone poses. Cobra and Sphinx are not rest poses. If your glutes and abdominals aren't engaged and you're not thrusting the pelvis into the mat, the work shifts to the lumbar spine — which is where the lower back pain comes from.

Forcing flexibility timelines. Forward fold depth and hamstring flexibility will improve with consistent practice. Trying to sandwich your torso to your legs before that's available, with a rounded spine, strains the lumbar and doesn't build the flexibility any faster.

Conflating one yoga style's benefits with all yoga. Yin, Ashtanga, Hatha, and Vinyasa create quite different physiological and nervous system effects. Yin tends to engage the parasympathetic system and is particularly useful for people who struggle to wind down. Vigorous Vinyasa does the opposite. Matching the practice to the need matters.

Doing yoga post-workout and expecting to train hard after. Yoga tends to leave the muscles loose and elongated. If you need explosive, high-output training, yoga is generally better scheduled after that training rather than before it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the warm-up sequence before every Surya Namaskar practice. Five to ten minutes of Child's Pose, neck rolls, shoulder rolls, windshield wipers, and bridges. This is not optional padding — it directly protects the joints you're about to load.

  2. Start with three rounds of the beginner variation. Focus entirely on engaging the abdominals and glutes throughout the prone poses. If you feel anything in the lower back, add more engagement in those muscles before progressing.

  3. Run a three-layer body check-in before you start. Spend thirty seconds each on: physical sensations (where is tension, energy, fatigue?), mental activity (what thoughts are running?), and emotional content (what's present right now?). Set a simple intention — an attitude or quality you want to bring to the practice.

  4. Experiment with blocks in Equestrian Pose this week. Even if you don't feel like you need them, try one full round using blocks. Notice whether your spinal alignment, chest opening, and breathing change. Then decide whether to keep using them.

  5. Practice the one-breath-one-movement rhythm. Once you're comfortable with the shapes, try a full round where every transition is driven by a single inhale or exhale. Notice how this changes your mental state compared to holding poses longer.

  6. After your next practice, do ten minutes of the ego meditation. Sit comfortably, anchor to physical sensation, and drop the question "who am I?" Observe without engineering an answer. Write down anything that surfaces afterward — not to analyze it immediately, but to notice patterns over time.

  7. Cross-train intentionally. If you're doing Surya Namaskar regularly, add one other physical modality — gym work, running, swimming, a movement practice — to balance out flexibility work with strength and load-bearing. Pay attention to how the embodiment you cultivate in yoga transfers to those other activities.

  8. If you're new and struggling to maintain a routine: prioritize staying present over volume. Three rounds with full attention beats twenty rounds on autopilot. The Mind-Body connection is the practice; the physical sequence is the method.
