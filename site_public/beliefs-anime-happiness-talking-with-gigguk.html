Summary

TL;DR — Emotional numbness, especially in men, can make fictional media like anime feel more emotionally real than real life itself. Understanding why this happens — and what's underneath it — is the first step to living with more genuine feeling.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between a psychiatrist and Garnt (Gigguk), an anime YouTuber and co-host of the Trash Taste podcast. What starts as a question about why anime can hit harder than real life opens into a remarkably honest exploration of suppressed emotion, the origins of personal beliefs, what it means to belong somewhere, and a surprisingly vulnerable reckoning with anger. The insights are practical and broadly applicable well beyond Garnt's specific story.

Why Anime Can Feel More Real Than Real Life

Garnt's grandmother died when he was in his early twenties. At the funeral, he felt almost nothing — a dreamlike numbness. Months later, he watched Summer Wars, an anime featuring a grandmother figure in a very similar family dynamic. The grief he expected to feel at the funeral arrived all at once.

This isn't unusual, and there are a few reasons it happens.

First, cultural and emotional conditioningThe brain's mechanism for repeating actions that produced pleasure or relief — when a behavior leads to a dopamine release (or suppresses negative emotion), the nucleus accumbens signals "do that again," building…Full definition →. Many people — disproportionately men, but not exclusively — grow up in environments where expressing emotion is actively discouraged. Garnt grew up in a traditional Thai household where men were expected to be the emotionally strong, stoic pillar of the family. That kind of conditioning doesn't disappear; it just reroutes. The brain learns to suppress emotional responses before they surface.

Second, the suppression paradox. The bigger the imminent flood of emotion, the harder the brain works to shut it down. This is why something like a funeral — which carries an enormous emotional charge — can actually produce the most profound numbness. The suppression mechanism fires harder precisely when you need it to. All those unfelt feelings don't disappear; they're stored.

Third, what makes anime a safe space. Two things are true about anime as a medium: it is extremely high-octane emotionally (vivid color, anguished music, dramatic build-up), and it can be experienced completely alone. That combination matters. Most deep emotional experiences require other people to be present, but the very presence of others activates the social suppression instinct. Alone, with a fictional world turned up to full volume, the suppression mechanism can't hold. The floodgates open.

"I just felt more natural to bring these emotions up to the surface. In real life there might be a few barriers there."

There's also something deeper going on: human beings have processed emotion through narrative for thousands of years. Stories work because they map our internal world — archetypes, heroes' journeys, loss, belonging — onto something external we can safely observe. Anime doesn't create the emotion; it reveals what was already there.

The Problem With Chasing Acceptance

In his early twenties, Garnt went through a period of feeling genuinely lost. He became a Buddhist monk for about a month and a half — something culturally normal in Thailand, but which he approached with unusual intentionality. He wanted to strip away all the noise of societal pressure, relationship drama, and accumulated baggage, and figure out: what do I actually believe, versus what was installed in me by my culture and upbringing?

What he discovered was that a lot of his social energy was going toward being liked. He was performing different versions of himself for different people, hoping to earn their acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition →. The result was relationships that felt hollow — because the person being accepted wasn't really him.

The way he broke that pattern wasn't through willpower. He thought it through to its logical conclusion: even if the acceptance worked, even if people liked the version of him he was performing, he would never feel genuinely connected to those people. The lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → he was trying to cure would actually get worse. You can buy your space in a relationship today, but you'll have to pay for it again tomorrow with more performance, more inauthenticity.

"I accepted that in the short term, gaining acceptance from another person felt good. But I traced myself to the future and realized it would never fill that loneliness gap — it would make the loneliness even more expanded, because I had been faking myself to be someone else."

The practical upshot: real friendships form when you show up as yourself and let the chips fall. For a heavy introvert who had never really put himself out there, this meant going to events and talking to people and accepting that most interactions wouldn't click — and doing it anyway, repeatedly, over a long time. It wasn't fast or comfortable. But the friendships that formed from it are ones he'd show up for unconditionally.

Two Kinds of Self-Improvement

There's a version of the self-improvement drive that looks healthy but isn't: constantly needing to be better because you fundamentally don't accept who you are right now. No amount of progress satisfies it, because the feeling of inadequacy isn't coming from any actual deficit — it's coming from inside.

Then there's the version Garnt embodies: devotion to the process of growth rather than attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → to the outcome. Failure doesn't feel like a setback. The goal isn't to arrive somewhere; it's to keep moving. His sense of fulfillment comes from the act of working toward something, not from achieving it.

These look nearly identical from the outside, but they feel completely different from the inside — and they have completely different psychological consequences.

"I don't want to be a better person because it's like a constant goal. I feel like if I lose that drive, that's a slope to fall back down the hill."

The distinction matters because many people pursuing self-improvement are actually running from self-rejection, not toward growth. The way to tell the difference: does failure devastate you, or does it just redirect you?

The Gap Between Expectation and Experience

In Buddhist philosophy, dukkha (suffering) literally translates to "empty space" — a gap. Any unhappiness, if you trace it back, contains a gap between what you expected or wanted and what actually is. Fulfillment, by contrast, is the absence of that gap — not perfection, but a sense that things are full enough.

Garnt's instinct about happiness maps onto this: he describes it as his "soul feeling full." And his ongoing motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → to grow isn't really about fixing something broken — it's about closing the gaps as they appear over time.

The important corollary: happiness isn't a destination you reach and stay at. New gaps appear as you grow. The question isn't whether you'll ever stop encountering them; it's whether your relationship to the gap-filling process is one of meaning or of exhausting necessity.

The Anger Blind Spot

Late in the conversation, Garnt raises something he's been wondering about: he almost never gets angry. Is that healthy? Should he feel more anger?

The answer that emerges is layered.

Anger is an emotion that protects territory. It signals that a boundary has been crossed and coordinates the response: remove the threat, hold the line. People who can't access anger can sometimes become doormats — not because they're weak, but because the early warning system has been disabled.

Suppressed anger doesn't disappear; it migrates. It shows up as passive aggression, as overly logical arguments that are actually emotionally driven, as disconnection. When you turn down the volume on one emotion, you tend to turn down the volume on everything.

Anger can become guilt. Many people who suppress anger feel guilty the moment they start to feel it — "I shouldn't be angry, I should be better than this" — which pushes it down further. The logical case against anger then piles on top of the guilt, and the emotion becomes almost completely inaccessible.

Garnt's example: his mother was wronged by someone, and she responded not with anger but by forgiving and moving on. Logically, this looks like grace. But watching a saint absorb harm without consequence is its own kind of maddening — and you're not allowed to be angry on behalf of a saint. The anger gets trapped.

"Frustration is anger — diet anger."

The goal isn't to rage more. It's to notice when frustration, guilt, or disconnection might actually be the surface expression of something underneath that deserves acknowledgment.

On Finding Home

Garnt grew up between cultures — Thai parents, raised in England, now living in Japan — and has never quite felt fully at home anywhere. Too British for Thailand, too Thai for Brighton, accepted but not belonging in Japan.

The reframe worth sitting with: home is probably not a place you find, it's something you build and grow into. The closest thing he currently has to "home" is the smell of his mother's cooking when he walks in the door — which is really about people and connection, not geography.

For people in similar situations — expats, third-culture kids, anyone who feels like a misfit no matter where they are — the search for home often involves looking in the wrong places. You're not going to find a location that perfectly accepts you. What you're more likely to do is wear a place in, gradually, the way shoes become yours after you've broken them in.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking emotional numbness for calm. Feeling nothing at a funeral and feeling peaceful during meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → look similar on the surface but have very different causes. One is suppression, one is regulation.
  • Treating therapy as only for people with "real problems." The most useful thing Garnt took from this conversation was insight he describes as something that "should have been obvious" — but it wasn't, and it took two hours of honest dialogue to surface it. Self-reflection has a ceiling you can't see from inside.
  • Assuming self-improvement motivation is always healthy. The outward behavior looks the same whether you're growing or fleeing. The internal experience — and the long-term trajectory — are very different.
  • Pathologizing the absence of anger. Not getting angry is often framed as mature or spiritually advanced. But if it's chronic and total, it's worth asking whether you've been conditioned to disable a necessary signal.
  • Looking for home in geography. Home tends to be built around people, routines, and belonging — not a country's culture or climate. Searching for the right city often keeps people from building the right life.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice what media hits you hardest — and ask why. The next time a story (anime, film, book) produces a strong emotional reaction, don't just feel it and move on. Sit with it afterward and ask: what in me does this reflect? What might it be unlocking that I haven't accessed directly?

  2. Try the "trace the future" exercise. When you're tempted to seek acceptance or take the socially safe path, mentally fast-forward. What does the relationship look like in two years if you keep performing? In five? This isn't pessimism — it's using your imagination to get honest about long-term costs of short-term comfort.

  3. Distinguish your beliefs from your conditioning. Ask yourself about a core value you hold: "Do I believe this because I thought it through, or because it was installed in me?" You don't have to reject the belief. You just want to own it consciously rather than carry it as inherited weight.

  4. Track the words you use for mild negative emotions. If "frustrated," "disappointed," or "logical disagreement" come up often, that may be worth pausing on. Frustration is often the accessible surface of anger. What's underneath it?

  5. Be a little less positive on purpose. When you catch yourself making lemonade — converting a difficult experience into a growth moment immediately — try not to. Let something just be bad for a bit. This is especially useful if you notice you almost always find the silver lining.

  6. Ask someone close to you a direct question. If you suspect you might have a blind spot around a particular emotion (anger, grief, fear), ask your partner or a trusted friend: "Do I ever seem [angry / sad / afraid] in ways I don't seem to notice?" Their answer is data.

  7. Consider what "home" actually means to you, concretely. Write down the two or three moments in your life when you've felt most at home. What were the common elements — people, smells, routines, feelings of acceptance? That list is more useful than a list of places to consider moving.
