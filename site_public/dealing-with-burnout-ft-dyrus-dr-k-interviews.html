Summary

TL;DR — Burnout often isn't caused by the external things you blame it on. The feelings follow you because they come from inside, not from whatever game, job, or lifestyle you're currently in.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between a psychiatrist and Dyrus, a former League of Legends pro and longtime streamer. What starts as a discussion about gaming burnout quickly reveals something deeper: a pattern of suppressed feelings, a lifelong sensitivity to not being heard, and a habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of looking for relief in external places — new games, breaks, hobbies, relationships — that never quite works. The core insight is that the burnout is carried within the person, and no amount of external change will dissolve something that lives on the inside.

What Burnout Actually Feels Like (vs. What It Actually Is)

When asked about burnout, the instinct is to point at the obvious culprits: grinding 12+ hours a day for years, a game meta that felt unfair, developers who didn't listen to pro player feedback, the pressure to keep streaming for income and fans.

These explanations feel completely logical. But there's a problem with them: Dyrus took a three-month break from streaming. It helped a little. Not a lot — a little.

That's a scientific result. If the burnout were really caused by streaming, removing streaming should have removed most of the burnout. It didn't. That gap between "I stopped doing the thing" and "I still feel the same way" is the clue. The burnout wasn't living in the stream schedule. It was living somewhere else.

"I don't know how to take a break. I don't know what to do to feel relaxed."

That sentence points inward. Not knowing how to relax isn't a scheduling problem. It's an internal one.

The Cord Tangle: When Life Doesn't Add Up

There's a specific kind of confusion that burnout produces: you've done all the things that are supposed to help, and they haven't. You've moved somewhere new, found a good relationship, identified toxic friendships, picked up a fun new game — the boxes are checked — and yet something still feels off. Tangled.

"It's like if behind your PC there's a bunch of cords and it gets all over the place. That's how I feel about my different phases of life. The cords are just everywhere and I don't know how to untangle them."

When someone is looking for answers in the external world — new hobby, new game, new relationship, three-month break — they're essentially fishing in a bathtub. The fish aren't there. Every time the dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → from one thing (a new game, a show, a platform) fades, you rotate to the next thing and hope it holds. It doesn't, because the thing you're trying to escape is portable. It goes with you.

Where the Burnout Actually Comes From

Three recurring themes surface, and they're not about gaming:

  1. Feeling forced into things — Playing a game you don't enjoy, for fans who expect it, for money you need. Being stuck in a meta you didn't choose. But also: being a kid who got put in anger management for fighting back against bullies.

  2. Not being listened to — Attending developer summits where pro player feedback was ignored. Answering the same chat question ten thousand times. Telling friends something important and having it dismissed. And earlier: trying to explain to a father or a teacher that there was another side to the story, and being shut down.

  3. Feeling left all alone — The top lane metaphor is almost too perfect. Top laners in League are literally isolated, given fewer resources, and expected to survive on their own. But the pattern runs deeper than a game role.

When you ask someone "have you ever felt like you weren't being listened to?" and they answer all the time — that's not an external problem. That's a wound. It's everywhere because it's in you, not in your circumstances.

The Childhood Wiring

The roots of the emotional suppression trace back clearly once you follow the thread.

A kid who was expressive, liked making people laugh, wanted connection. Got bullied. Fought back. Was sent to anger management — a class explicitly designed to teach you to not show what you feel. The bullies provoked, he reacted, and the system's response was to train him to suppress.

"You were literally put in a class that taught you how to take what's on the inside and not show it to people on the outside."

That's not a personality trait. That's a lesson that got taught. Kids do what they're taught. Mean kids aren't broken — they usually learned that behavior from somewhere. And kids who go quiet and monotone usually learned that too.

The monotone, the self-deprecation ("I'm not good at telling stories," "I'm not a good explainer"), the tendency to downplay ("maybe a tear in my eye," "it helped a little") — these aren't personality quirks. They're the behavioral residue of being repeatedly taught that expressing things leads to punishment or being ignored.

Why External Solutions Don't Fix Internal Problems

The rotation from game to anime to manga to Netflix to new game isn't a hobby problem. It's dopamine toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → management. Each source of stimulation delivers diminishing returns, so you cycle through them, waiting for your baseline to reset so the first one feels good again.

This isn't a willpower failure. It's just how a nervous system responds when it's been heavily stimulated for years and when the uncomfortable internal feelings have never been processed. The external stimulation functions as avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → — not relaxation.

True relaxation would require knowing how to be with yourself without distraction. That's not a skill you develop automatically. For someone who was taught early that inner feelings lead to trouble, sitting with those feelings is genuinely difficult. The outside world has to be doing something or the discomfort surfaces.

The Vulnerability You Carry

Everyone has a weak spot — a place where hits land harder than they should. For Dyrus, and for a lot of people, it's not being listened to. It's feeling forced. It's being left alone.

This doesn't mean the external world never actually produces those things. People genuinely don't listen sometimes. Games genuinely do have bad patches. But the intensity of the reaction is disproportionate when it's hitting an old wound. The dagger isn't epic gear. The wound is just already there.

"It's not about the weapon. It's about you."

That's not victim-blaming — it's the opposite of helplessness. If the wound is yours, you have power over it. If it were purely external, you'd have none.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking burnout for overwork. The number of hours is a factor, but if the feeling persists after a real break, the hours were never the core issue.

  • Thinking a new hobby will fix it. A new hobby teaches you to be distracted from the feeling. It doesn't resolve the feeling. You'll burn through the hobby too.

  • Looking for answers in the external world when the problem is internal. Changing your job, game, city, or relationship won't move something that lives inside you.

  • Downplaying your own signals. "It helped a little," "maybe a tear," "I kind of felt something" — the habit of minimizing your own experience makes it nearly impossible to accurately diagnose what's wrong. The small words aren't modesty; they're a trained response.

  • Confusing understanding something consciously with actually knowing it. You can have the insight intellectually and still not feel it or act from it. The dots might connect but not feel connected. That gap is normal and worth naming.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the scientific test on your own burnout. Ask yourself: if this feeling were caused by X (my job, my game, my relationship), then removing X should significantly reduce the feeling. Have you removed X, or something close to it? Did it work? Use that result as data, not self-criticism.

  2. Look for the three signals. Notice when you feel (a) forced into something you don't want to do, (b) like no one is listening, or (c) alone and unsupported even when people are around. Don't try to fix them immediately — just notice them and tag them: this is the pattern again.

  3. When you notice those signals, ask inward before outward. Is this situation actually producing the feeling, or is the feeling already here and the situation is just triggering it? Both can be true at once — the key is learning to distinguish them.

  4. Trace the wound back. Think about the earliest moments you can remember feeling forced, unheard, or abandoned. Not to blame anyone, but to understand where the sensitivity comes from. This is work best done with a therapist, but you can start by writing it out.

  5. Try bellows breath (kapalbhati) as a meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → on-ramp. If standard meditation feels impossible because your mind won't settle, use an active technique first: 20 forceful exhalations (emphasis on the exhale, letting the inhale happen naturally), then sit in the stillness that follows. The physical exertion exhausts the mind enough to let you experience a few seconds of quiet. Start with 3–5 minutes, 3–5 days a week.

  6. Consider a dopamine fast or cleanse. If you cycle constantly between games, social media, streaming, anime, and YouTube without ever feeling genuinely rested, your reward system may need recalibration. Deliberately cutting stimulation — even briefly — can help you notice what you're actually feeling underneath all the noise.

  7. See a therapist, specifically to talk about the internal stuff. Not just coping strategies. The actual history: what you were taught, where the suppression came from, what it felt like not to be heard. The goal isn't just symptom management — it's understanding where the vulnerability lives and beginning to heal it.
