Summary

TL;DR — Sobriety alone doesn't end addiction. To actually move on, you have to give up the emotional investment in the thing — not just the behavior — and be willing to pass through a period of emptiness to get there.

Quitting a substance, a game, a relationship, or any source of intense joy often leaves people in a strange in-between state: sober but joyless. Life is technically better, but nothing feels worth doing. This isn't a willpower problem or a neurochemistry problem (though neurochemistry plays a role). It's a psychological problem that Freud identified over a century ago in a paper called Mourning and Melancholia — and understanding it is the key to actually moving on.

The Real Problem: Your Emotional Investment Hasn't Gone Anywhere

Freud used the term libidinal energy to describe the energy of emotional investment — the feeling of being genuinely into something. When you're deeply into a game, a drug, a person, or a former version of yourself, you're not just enjoying a thing. You are emotionally attached to it. It's where your mind goes. It's what makes you feel alive.

The critical insight is this: when you quit the behavior, the libidinal attachment doesn't automatically follow. The emotional investment is still there, still pointed at the old object. You're sober, but you're still in relationship with the substance. You're single, but you're still emotionally married to your ex. You haven't played in months, but you're still a gamer at heart, scanning every new release hoping it scratches the itch.

This is why people in AA introduce themselves as alcoholics even decades after their last drink. It isn't purely a statement about genetics or brain chemistry. It's a statement about an attachment that was never fully released.

One Addiction Is Actually a Thousand Attachments

Here's where it gets more complicated — and more honest. What you think you're attached to (the drug, the game, the person) is actually a constellation of smaller attachments.

Alcohol, for instance, isn't just alcohol. It's: - Barbecues with friends - Happy hours after work - A glass of wine on a vacation through wine country - Going to Vegas with your crew - A cold beer at a football game - The gift bottle of Bordeaux a friend brings back from France

Gaming isn't just gaming. It's the feeling of getting lost in a world, the satisfaction of leveling up, the specific tension of a clutch comeback, the aesthetic of a cutscene, the social normalcy of being just like everyone else.

When you give up the thing, you are actually giving it up in all these forms, one by one. Freud described this process as decathexis — the gradual withdrawal of libidinal energy from every individual memory and experience attached to the object. He said it explicitly: the process "is carried out bit by bit, at great expense of time and cathetic energy."

This is why grief doesn't arrive as one clean wave. You don't mourn a person once; you mourn them at their birthday, at Christmas, when a song comes on, when something good happens and they're the first person you want to call. Each moment is a separate, small severance.

Addiction recovery works the same way. It is not one act of quitting. It is a thousand tiny severings.

The Gray Zone You Have to Walk Through

Once you begin withdrawing that investment — once you stop looking for the thing or a replacement for it — you enter what might be called the gray zone. Life feels flat. Nothing is exciting. The new game is fine, but it's not that. The new partner is kind, but they're not your soulmate. The award at work means nothing compared to going all-in on a 4% chance and pulling it off.

That comparative feeling — "nothing measures up" — is itself the evidence that you haven't fully let go. You are still holding the old object as the standard. The libidinal energy is still over there, and everything new only gets measured against it.

The gray zone is not a sign that something is wrong with you. It is not proof that life will never feel good again. It is a natural, expected phase of transition between an old attachment and a new one.

"You can't fall in love with somebody else until you've truly given up on your ex."

The same logic applies to anything you're deeply attached to. As long as that investment is still pointed at the old thing, there's no emotional space for something new to grow.

Why People Get Stuck: The "Forever" Trap

The most important psychological trap people fall into in the gray zone is this: they conclude that gray means forever.

When you're in the middle of that flatness, your brain looks at it and says: This is what the rest of my life will feel like. And because that feels unbearable, you do the only thing that makes the emptiness go away — you go back to the old thing, or you keep hunting for a replacement that can fit the same sized hole.

"Once you're here, this feels like forever. And since this feels like forever and is intolerable, we end up going back."

The terrible irony is that the very act of avoiding the emptiness is what keeps you stuck in it. The avoidance prevents the mourning. You never get through it because you keep retreating from the edge of it.

The people who make it through are the ones who — at some point — accept the emptiness as a temporary state of life rather than a permanent sentence. They say: yes, this is gray. So be it. And then the libidinal energy, which has a natural tendency to reach out and form new attachments, eventually finds something new to invest in.

The decathexis (withdrawal from the old) has to happen before re-cathexis (investment in something new) can begin. You cannot skip the empty middle.

The "Soulmate" Illusion

One way libidinal attachment disguises itself is through the concept of the soulmate — the person (or thing) that feels uniquely, cosmically irreplaceable.

The concept of a soulmate isn't evidence that a particular person is objectively irreplaceable. It is the experience of having your total emotional investment concentrated in one place. The soulmate feels like a soulmate because all of your libidinal energy is pointed at them. The investment creates the feeling of irreplaceability — not the other way around.

This matters because it means that once the investment is genuinely withdrawn and allowed to resettle, it's possible to feel that depth of connection again with someone else. The people who've been through a devastating breakup and eventually fell in love again know this. There was a period where they were certain they would never love again. Then they did.

Common Pitfalls

Treating sobriety as the finish line. Stopping the behavior is necessary but not sufficient. You can be completely sober and still be emotionally attached to the substance. The attachment is what needs addressing.

Looking for a replacement. The hunt for "what scratches the same itch" is itself a sign that you haven't let go. You're trying to import the old investment into a new container. It never fits perfectly, which leads to frustration and eventually relapse.

Mistaking the gray zone for permanent damage. The flatness after withdrawal — whether from drugs, a relationship, or a former identity — feels conclusive. It isn't. It's a passage, not a destination.

Treating addiction as purely neurobiological. Brain chemistry matters, and the dopamine system does recalibrate over weeks to months. But for many people, neurochemistry alone doesn't explain why the pull remains years into sobriety. The psychological attachment is its own layer that needs its own work.

Assuming giving up is a single event. "I decided I was done" is a starting point, not the whole process. The true giving-up happens in hundreds of small moments — every memory, every associated experience, every context where the old thing used to live.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your constellation of attachments. Pick the thing you're trying to move on from. List every specific experience, context, memory, and feeling associated with it — not just the core behavior, but everything satellite to it. This is what you're actually mourning. Seeing the full list makes the process more honest and less mysterious.

  2. Notice when you're comparison-shopping. When a new game, relationship, hobby, or accomplishment feels "not as good," pause. Ask yourself: compared to what, exactly? That comparison is a live wire connecting you to the old attachment. Naming it doesn't cut the wire, but it keeps you from acting on it unconsciously.

  3. Practice sitting in the gray. Set a small window of time — even 10–15 minutes — where you deliberately do nothing to fill the emptiness. No substitute hunting, no distraction, no productive reframing. Just notice the flatness. Do this regularly. You're practicing tolerance for the state that the mourning process requires.

  4. Mourn in slices, not all at once. When a specific context comes up (the holiday, the song, the part of town, the kind of game), let yourself fully feel the loss of that specific thing rather than numbing it or rushing past it. Each of these moments, fully felt, is a small piece of decathexis completed.

  5. Separate psychological giving-up from behavioral stopping. Ask yourself honestly: have you stopped the behavior, or have you also stopped wanting it? If you've stopped the behavior but still secretly hope for a substitute that measures up, that's useful information. The psychological surrender — "I am truly done with this" — is a separate act from behavioral sobriety, and it may need to happen consciously and deliberately, possibly many times.

  6. Hold the gray zone as temporary but real. When the emptiness feels permanent, remind yourself of the mechanism: libidinal energy has a natural tendency to re-attach. The gray is not a life sentence. It's a gap between old attachments and new ones. You have to cross it rather than retreat from it.
