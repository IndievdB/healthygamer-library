Summary

TL;DR — A fulfilling career isn't something your job gives you; it's something you build through clear vision, internal work, and strategic external effort.

Most career frustration comes from a category error: expecting one thing (a job) to deliver something it was never designed to provide (growth, fulfillment, meaning, validation). Once you untangle what a job actually is from what you actually need, the path forward becomes far less overwhelming — even when the job market is brutal, the workplace is toxic, and progress feels impossibly slow.

This talk works through three interconnected themes: how to find mentorship, how to separate growth from advancement, and how to do the internal work that makes external effort sustainable.

What a Job Actually Is (and Isn't)

A job is a transaction: you perform actions, you receive money. That's it.

The modern promise — "find your passion and you'll never work a day in your life" — turned that simple transaction into a vessel for meeting almost every human need. We now expect our jobs to provide: - A sense of purpose and meaning - Validation and recognition - Opportunities to grow and advance - Financial security and emotional fulfillment

Some employers offer some of these things as incentives to keep you around. That's a perk, not an obligation. The moment you treat perks as entitlements, you've handed the keys to your emotional life over to your employer.

Separate what a job is from what it isn't, and take personal ownership of the things that fall outside the transaction. This single reframe dissolves a significant portion of workplace misery — not by excusing bad employers, but by stopping you from depending on them for things they were never built to provide.

How to Actually Find a Mentor

Most people approach mentorship backwards. They lead with enthusiasm — I'm really passionate about psychology, I'd love to learn from you — and wonder why no one responds.

Put yourself in the mentor's shoes. If you have limited time and five people asking for mentorship, who do you choose?

"There's a huge difference between going to someone and saying, 'I want to start a business, will you help me?' and saying, 'Hey, I started a business. This is how far I've come. I don't know what comes next — will you help me?'"

The most attractive mentees are people already on a trajectory. They've done something. They have work behind them — a draft novel, a side project, a certification, a prototype, a portfolio. The mentor's job is to take that existing momentum and accelerate it, not to generate momentum from scratch.

Willingness, open-mindedness, and enthusiasm are table-stakes. They don't differentiate you. What differentiates you is demonstrated effort.

Practical implications: - If you want to write, write a full draft before approaching a writing mentor - If you want to build an app, build a rough version before approaching a developer - If you want to work in mental health, get a certification or degree before asking a clinician for guidance - If you want to work in a specific company or industry, research their job postings, reach out to people on LinkedIn who have those roles, and show up to relevant conferences — then approach someone for help navigating the next step

The mentors worth having are usually a few tiers above where you are right now, not light-years ahead. A research assistant finding a research fellow as a mentor. A junior developer finding a senior one. Start local.

Growth vs. Advancement: Two Different Things

These two words get conflated constantly, and that conflation causes enormous frustration.

Growth = you leveling up your skills, resilience, and capabilities. It's internal.

Advancement = your title, compensation, and status changing within an organization. It's external and depends on what your employer needs.

They often travel together, which is why the brain assumes they're the same. But they're not, and treating them as identical leads to a specific trap: you acquire new skills, expect to be promoted, get passed over, and feel betrayed.

The key insight is empathy for your employer — and empathy here means perspective-taking, not emotional warmth.

"If I'm a psychiatrist in a clinic, what's a good job? Some people are saying good reviews, competent help, patient outcomes. Those are all wrong. What matters is throughput — the number of patients I see per hour."

An employer pays you to do a specific thing. Extra effort only translates to advancement if it improves what they actually measure. Organizing birthday parties for morale while someone else improves sales by 6% and reduces costs by 4% — those people get promoted, not you. This isn't necessarily fair. It is, however, predictable once you stop looking through your own eyes.

To advance: understand what your employer valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →, deliver it at a high level, document the impact in their language (revenue, efficiency, cost savings), and then make a direct case. A high performer who says "I'm one of your top three producers, and if you promote me I'll implement a system that raises productivity across the clinic" has a much stronger position than someone who feels their hard work should speak for itself.

To grow independently: pick the skills you want to develop, develop them regardless of whether your current employer rewards them, and then find the employer or environment where those skills are valued. Staying in a "level 1 zone" while you've leveled up to level 5 is a signal to move — not a personal failure.

Reverse Engineering Your Path

For anyone who doesn't know what career they want yet, or who has a dream that feels impossibly distant: start at the end and work backward.

Pick a specific goal, even an audacious one. Then ask: what is the step immediately before that? What would I need to have done to qualify for that step? Keep pulling the thread backward until you reach something you can do this week.

If you want to work at a specific company, look at their job postings and identify exactly what qualifications they list. That is your roadmap. Find people who already have those roles on LinkedIn and ask how they got there. Attend relevant conferences. Build the portfolio or credential the job listing requires.

This process has worked in practice. A client who wanted to work at a specific game company spent five years reverse-engineering that path and got there. A patient who developed coaching skills in her spare time while working an Excel-spreadsheet job eventually found a VC firm that valued both skill sets — and has been steadily promoted since.

The Internal Work: What Makes External Effort Sustainable

All the strategic advice above fails if your internal state is governed by external circumstances. The experience of feeling overwhelmed, burnt out, anxious, and demoralized is real — and it won't go away by finding a better job. It follows you.

If your job search feels crushing because you have too many applications to send, and you believe the overwhelm is caused by the number of applications, you've outsourced your mental state to a number that will always be large. The job gets done when you detach that link.

A few practical framings that help:

Treat the job search like a job. Six hours a day. Specific tasks: polish the resume, reach out to 30 people on LinkedIn, apply to 40 positions. No expectation of results that day. Just the work.

Use the NECI effect. When the boring necessary steps feel unbearable, frame them as the bridge to what you actually want. Not "I hate doing my resume" — but "if I want that dream role, this is the necessary step." It's not a motivational trick; there's actual data supporting it as a feedback and framing mechanism.

Build in a hard stop and recovery. When working through a difficult season, separate your work desk from your rest — literally if possible. Work hard during the work time, then fully stop. Work-life balance during a grind period isn't a luxury; it's what prevents the accumulation of fatigue that becomes burnout.

Curate your information diet aggressively. The internet is not a neutral information source. Algorithms serve content that produces emotional activation, not content that's accurate or balanced. A ragebait feed about corporate malfeasance, or a forum full of people either bragging or despairing, shapes your expectations, your attitude in interviews, and your ability to produce your best resume. Vision, Energy, and Karma

Underneath the practical career mechanics is a framework worth taking seriously even if you strip out the spiritual language.

To manifest anything in the world, you need two things: vision (Shiva — intention, direction, a living sense of where you're going) and energy (ShaktiThe fundamental creative and generative energy of the universe — literally translated as "energy" or "strength" — that operates through and beyond conscious life.Full definition → — effort, sustained action).

Most struggling careers are missing one or the other. Either someone is grinding without any clear sense of where they're going, or someone has a vivid dream but hasn't done the sustained work to move toward it. Neither alone is enough.

Vision is not the same as a goal. A goal is specific and can fail. A vision is broader — it implies a direction and a span of possibilities, so when one specific path closes, you can still move toward it another way. The vision behind Healthy Gamer wasn't "build a company with X million users." It was: someone needs to help the people everyone else is leaving behind.

There's often a backlog of negative karma — past avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →, accumulated setbacks, structural disadvantages — that has to be worked through before positive outcomes start appearing. That period of sustained effort with no visible results isn't wasted; it's clearing the debt. Recognizing this makes it possible to keep going through long stretches of rejection without collapsing into despair.

"Each failure is one debt that is being paid, one debt that has been wiped away. And if you keep going, things will start working."

This pattern shows up again and again in practice. People who work hard through a long stretch of failure, and keep going, eventually find that something breaks open. It's not random.

Common Pitfalls

  • Expecting fulfillment from the job itself. Fulfillment is internal. Outsourcing it to your boss's approval or your company's recognition means your emotional wellbeing rises and falls with things you can't control.

  • Conflating growth with advancement. Acquiring skills does not automatically produce a promotion. Make the explicit case, tied to what your employer actually values.

  • Approaching mentors without work behind you. Enthusiasm and interest are table-stakes. Mentors choose mentees who are already moving.

  • Applying the same short-term fix logic to career building. Solving one immediate problem (get a certification, get any job) without a larger vision means you'll always be one crisis away from the next problem.

  • Black-and-white reading of this advice. Saying "find your fulfillment internally" doesn't mean "never expect recognition from your employer." Saying "have empathy for your employer" doesn't mean "employers are wonderful and all their behavior is justified." The nuances matter.

  • Letting the internet radicalize your expectations. Rage-inducing content about work and capitalism produces an emotional state that makes it harder to perform well in interviews, negotiate effectively, or produce good work. Vigilance about bad corporate practices is useful; continuous outrage is not.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down what you actually expect from your job — include the obvious (salary, benefits) and the hidden (validation, a sense of purpose, growth opportunities). Then separate the list into "reasonable to expect from an employer" and "I need to find this somewhere else."

  2. Identify one task you've been avoiding in your job search or career development — resume, LinkedIn outreach, a difficult conversation about a raise. Apply the NECI framing: write one sentence that connects this task directly to something you actually want. Then do the task.

  3. Reverse engineer your dream role. Start with something ambitious. Pull the thread back: what is the step immediately before that? What credentials or experience does it require? Find three actual job postings for that role and list every qualification. That is your roadmap.

  4. Research a specific target company or role using LinkedIn. Find two or three people who currently hold the job you want and message them a short, specific question about how they got there. Do not ask them to mentor you — just ask one concrete question.

  5. Build something before asking for mentorship. If there's a mentor you want, identify what you need to have done (not just planned or considered) before you approach them. Set a deadline for reaching that milestone first.

  6. Audit your information diet for one week. Track which online communities, subreddits, or feeds you're consuming around work and career. Ask honestly: is this making me more capable and clear-headed, or more activated and despairing? Reduce or cut the sources that leave you feeling worse without making you more effective.

  7. Write a one-paragraph vision statement — not a goal, but a direction. What kind of impact do you want to have? What kind of work do you want to be doing? Keep it broad enough that multiple specific paths could lead there. Return to it when the day-to-day grind feels pointless.

  8. The two-desk rule (or equivalent). During an intensive job search or career-building phase, create a physical or temporal boundary between work time and recovery time. Define your daily work hours, do the work, then fully stop. Protect the recovery.
