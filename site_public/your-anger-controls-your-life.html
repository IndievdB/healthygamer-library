Summary

TL;DR — The desire for triumph is just as much a trap as anger itself, and you can't fully release one without releasing the other.

Most conversations about emotional regulation focus on letting go of negative emotions — anger, resentment, bitterness. What gets overlooked is the flip side: the hunger for triumph. The craving to put someone in their place, to win the argument, to finally be proven right — that desire controls your behavior just as much as the anger does. This talk walks through a guided meditation designed to release both, and explains why you can't have one without the other.

Anger and Triumph Are Two Sides of the Same Coin

When you feel wronged, two things happen simultaneously. First, the negative emotion — anger, resentment, a sense of injustice. Second, and less obviously, a fantasy starts building: the moment you get even. You imagine the perfect comeback. You picture putting them in their place. You fantasize about them finally seeing how wrong they were.

That fantasy is triumph. And it feels good — which is exactly why it's dangerous.

"Resentment fuels the triumph, and chasing the triumph fuels the resentment."

They are not separate emotions. They are a loop. The resentment generates the desire for victory, and the desire for victory keeps the resentment alive. You can't feed one without feeding the other. This is why people who are intensely angry are also, underneath it, intensely ego-driven — they need to win to make the injury feel resolved.

The classic video game example illustrates it cleanly: you tell someone "1v1 me mid" because you're frustrated and want to assert dominance. But if you're losing the game, you might be losing because they're actually better than you. In chasing the moment of triumph, you expose yourself to an even more humiliating outcome. The anger pushed you into a bad decision; the desire for triumph made it worse.

What This Meditation Actually Does

The meditation has two phases: evocation and transcendence.

In the evocation phase, you deliberately call up the thing that makes you angry — a real resentment, a situation where you felt wronged. You let the feelings surface. Then, critically, you also build the triumphant fantasy: what would victory look like? What would justice feel like? You let that come up too.

The purpose of doing both is to see them together. When you visualize both the injury and the imagined victory side by side, the connection between them becomes undeniable — they are one knot of feeling, not two separate problems.

In the transcendence phase, you release them. The meditation offers three anchors depending on your cognitive style:

  • Breath-focused: Breathe into the feeling located in the belly. On each exhale, release one emotion at a time — first working through the resentment, then through the triumph.
  • Insight-focused (third eye): Rather than breathing out the emotion, you observe it. You look for the ego inside both the anger and the triumph, and recognize that the concept of deserving — the thing powering both feelings — originates entirely from ego. The insight itself becomes the release.
  • Mantra-based: Use a mantra (om, or whichever resonates) to seal the meditation and anchor the state of equanimity you've arrived at.

These aren't competing techniques — they're options suited to different minds. Choose whichever pulls you into stillness most naturally.

The Role of Ego and Deserving

One of the clearest insights surfaced in the insight-focused part of the meditation: the engine running both anger and triumph is the concept of deserving.

Resentment says: I deserved better than what happened. Triumph says: They deserve to be put in their place, and I deserve the victory. Both are ego. Both require a story in which you are the rightful protagonist and the universe owes you a particular outcome.

"The anger and resentment that you feel is born of entitlement — of what you deserve. And the concept of deserving comes from the ego."

When you shift attention away from that ego-driven narrative — stepping back into what the meditation calls the "observing self," the broader awareness that watches experience without needing to win — the feelings lose their grip almost immediately. From that vantage point, the need for justice starts to look less like a moral imperative and less like something worth sacrificing your peace over.

"This whole process has already deprived you of peace. Engaging in it further will only deprive you of more."

What to Do If You're Not Ready to Let Go

This is important: don't force it.

If you sit with this meditation and find that you genuinely don't want to release the resentment or the fantasy of triumph — that's valid. Holding on to it might be what you need right now. Like a young child with a security blanket: the blanket doesn't do anything practical, but yanking it away forcibly isn't better than the attachment. We let things go when we're ready.

If you're not ready, the more useful move is the insight phase: don't try to release it yet — just understand it. Can you at least see that the resentment and the triumph are linked? Can you see the ego in both? That awareness, even without letting go, is meaningful progress.

The time to return to this practice is when you notice that the strategy of holding on — the controlled, guarded approach built around maintaining that edge — stops working as well as it used to. That moment will likely come. When it does, the practice will be there.

Finding the Right Meditation for Your Mind

No two minds work the same way, which means no single meditation technique works for everyone. The failure mode in most meditation instruction is that teachers are trained deeply in one tradition and assume it applies universally. When students struggle, they're told to try harder, be more consistent, trust the process. Rarely is the possibility raised that the technique itself might be a bad fit.

Different minds need different anchors:

  • Some people focus naturally when given something concrete to repeat (mantra)
  • Some people need sensory precision — describing the exact sound of breath, as if trying to solve a puzzle — to stay present
  • Some people enter deep states through visualization
  • Some through breath awareness alone

The practical approach is to experiment — try many techniques until you find one that produces stillness relatively easily. Then practice that one consistently, potentially rotating in others over time. The goal is finding your cognitive fit, not winning a badge in someone else's tradition.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating triumph as a reward, not a trap. The fantasy of victory feels like the solution to anger. It isn't. It's what keeps the anger on life support.
  • Skipping the triumph visualization. It's tempting to just work on releasing the negative feeling and ignore the fantasy of winning. But if you only release the resentment without examining the triumph, the loop remains intact.
  • Forcing the release before you're ready. Trying to let go before genuine willingness creates a performance of acceptance rather than the real thing. Acknowledgment that you're not ready is more honest and more useful.
  • Assuming one meditation tradition is "correct." If a technique hasn't clicked after genuine experimentation, that's information about fit — not evidence of your failure.
  • Confusing the ego-driven "observing" mind with genuine observation. The insight phase requires actually stepping back from the narrative, not just narrating it more calmly. True observation means you stop needing the story to resolve a particular way.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your resentment-triumph pair. Pick one real situation where you feel wronged. Write down both: (a) what happened that made you angry, and (b) what your fantasy of "winning" or "justice" in that situation looks like. Just naming both, side by side, begins to reveal the loop.

  2. Try the meditation as described. Sit quietly, evoke the resentment, build the triumph fantasy, then work through the release using whichever anchor suits you — breath, insight, or mantra. Don't push for a dramatic release; just observe what comes up.

  3. If you can't let go, use the insight phase instead. Rather than trying to release the feeling, just look at it. Ask: where is the ego here? What specifically do I believe I deserve? Stay with those questions without forcing an answer.

  4. Experiment with at least three different meditation anchors over the next few weeks — breath, visualization, and mantra — and note which one makes stillness feel most accessible. Commit to the one that works and practice it consistently.

  5. Check in on your "control strategy." If you've built your emotional stability around staying guarded, holding onto righteous anger, or maintaining the upper hand — ask yourself honestly: is it still working? What is it costing you in peace? You don't have to change anything yet. Just look clearly.
