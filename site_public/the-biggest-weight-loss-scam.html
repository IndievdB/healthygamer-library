Summary

TL;DR — Crash diets are not just ineffective; they can actively make you fatter over time. Understanding the science of set point theory is the key to why, and it points toward what actually works.

The diet and wellness industry is worth billions of dollars, employs armies of credentialed professionals, and has been growing for decades. Obesity rates have also been rising for decades. That's not a coincidence — it's a clue. If the dominant approach to weight loss actually worked, the industry selling it would shrink, not grow. The fact that it keeps expanding is strong evidence that its methods fail repeatedly and predictably, leaving customers to come back for more.

The core problem is that most commercial weight-loss programs rely on rapid, drastic calorie restriction — crash diets — without understanding (or caring about) the biological backlash they trigger. Once you understand that backlash, the whole cycle makes a different kind of sense.

Why Your Body Fights Back: Set Point Theory

Your body maintains an internal "target weight" through a process called set point theory. Think of it like a thermostat for body mass: your physiology is constantly trying to return you to a particular weight, pushing back when you go too far above or below it.

This explains something that calorie math alone can't: why two people eating roughly the same diet end up at very different weights. It's not purely about how much food goes in — each body has its own internal calculator quietly managing how energy is stored and spent.

"Each of us have this internal calculator that tells us how much we should weigh."

When you go on a crash diet and lose weight rapidly, your body interprets this as an emergency — specifically, as starvation. It responds by doing two things:

  1. Slowing your basal metabolic rate — you burn fewer calories doing the same activities
  2. Increasing calorie-hoarding efficiency — your body becomes better at holding onto fat

Research published in the New England Journal of Medicine supports this: significant weight loss causes measurable metabolic compensation. Your body essentially "nerfs" your ability to lose weight.

The Part That Makes It a Scam

Here's what makes this cycle genuinely dangerous rather than just frustrating: research in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition suggests that the metabolic changes caused by crash dieting can persist even after you regain the weight. Your body learns from the starvation event and recalibrates upward.

That means:

  • You crash diet and lose 15 pounds
  • Your metabolism slows and starts hoarding calories
  • You return to your normal eating habits (the same ones that previously kept your weight stable)
  • You gain the weight back — and potentially end up heavier than when you started
  • Your new set point is now higher than your old one

Do another crash diet, and the cycle repeats with your set point ratcheting upward again.

"The crash diet that you went on is actually changing your metabolism to gain weight afterwards."

The cruel irony is what happens psychologically at this point. When the weight comes back, almost no one blames the diet — they blame themselves. I lacked discipline. I should have stuck with it. This self-blame sends people right back to the diet industry to buy the next book, try the next program, and trigger another round of metabolic damage. The industry profits precisely because its products fail in a way that feels like the customer's fault.

What Actually Works: Sustained Action Over Drastic Measures

The logic of set point theory points clearly in one direction: don't trigger the body's starvation alarm. Small, consistent, sustainable changes fly under the radar. Drastic ones set off a biological counterattack.

The goal is not to go to war with your physiology — it's to work with it through gradual adjustments your body doesn't interpret as a crisis.

A few concrete examples of this kind of intervention:

Flaxseeds and leptin sensitivity Two key hunger hormones regulate appetite: leptin (the "I'm full" signal) and ghrelin (the "I'm hungry" signal). Many overweight people are leptin resistant — the fullness signal is there, but their body has stopped responding to it effectively. Flaxseeds have been shown to increase leptin sensitivity. Adding even a teaspoon to a meal can meaningfully increase how satisfied that meal makes you feel, without any dramatic dietary overhaul.

Sunlight and vitamin D There's a plausible evolutionary link between reduced sun exposure and weight gain. In northern climates, less sunlight historically meant approaching winter — a time when food would become scarce. The body's response: store more calories. Research in the journal Medical Hypotheses explores the connection between vitamin D deficiency and obesity. Simply getting more regular sun exposure may nudge your body's energy-regulation mechanisms in a favorable direction.

These aren't magic bullets. They're examples of the broader principle: small, biologically sensible adjustments that don't cause your body to panic.

Common Pitfalls

Blaming yourself instead of the method. The most common and damaging mistake. When weight returns after a crash diet, the instinct is to internalize failure — to see it as a character flaw rather than a predictable physiological response. This framing keeps people trapped in the cycle and keeps them paying for the next diet program.

Assuming a diet "worked" because you lost weight. Short-term weight loss is easy to produce. The relevant question is what happens to your metabolism and set point in the months that follow. A diet that produces rapid loss followed by full regain (plus metabolic damage) has not worked — it has made your situation worse.

Expecting to return to old eating habits at a new lower weight. Set point theory shows this doesn't work. If your body has recalibrated to defend a higher weight, eating at your old "maintenance" level will now feel like eating at a surplus. Sustainable weight change requires sustainable behavioral change — not a temporary sprint followed by a return to baseline.

Trusting industry growth as a signal of effectiveness. A growing diet industry is not evidence that diets work. Given how this business model functions — selling products that fail in ways customers blame on themselves — growth is actually more consistent with the opposite conclusion.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Stop any current crash diet or rapid restriction protocol. If you've been severely cutting calories, don't continue. The short-term loss is likely to trigger the metabolic compensation described above, raising your set point over time.

  2. Identify one small, daily dietary change you can sustain indefinitely. The key word is indefinitely. If you can't imagine doing it for a year, it's not the right change. Smaller is better here.

  3. Add flaxseeds to one meal per day. Start with a teaspoon. Sprinkle on oatmeal, yogurt, a smoothie, or a salad. This is a low-effort, research-backed way to improve leptin sensitivity and naturally reduce how much you eat at that meal.

  4. Get deliberate daily sun exposure. Aim for at least 15–20 minutes of direct sunlight each day, particularly if you live in a northern climate or spend most of your time indoors. This is free, takes almost no effort, and may support vitamin D levels linked to healthier weight regulation.

  5. Reframe past weight-loss "failures." Write down one or two previous diet attempts that ended in regain. Consider what metabolic and set-point mechanisms, rather than personal discipline, may have driven that outcome. This isn't about letting yourself off the hook — it's about diagnosing the actual problem so you can address it correctly.

  6. Track consistency, not scale weight, as your primary metric. Because set point changes happen slowly and the scale fluctuates day to day, measuring whether you maintained your small sustainable habits each week is a more useful and less demoralizing indicator of progress.
