Summary

TL;DR — When you can't change your circumstances or other people's behavior, the only real leverage you have is understanding your own patterns and slowly expanding the range of what feels emotionally safe to you.

This is a conversation between Dr. K and streamer Sweet Anita about a problem that resists every obvious solution: Anita has spent her life unable to maintain close friendships because people — men and women alike — repeatedly develop romantic or sexual feelings for her, ignore her clearly stated disinterest, and eventually either push boundaries or disappear when disappointed. She has tried changing herself, changing others, being warm, being cold, being explicit, being gentle. Nothing has worked. The conversation works through why that is, what patterns are actually driving it, and what — if anything — can be done when the source of your problem is mostly outside your control.

When You've Tried Everything and Nothing Works

There's a specific kind of frustration that comes from being a thoughtful, creative problem-solver who has genuinely exhausted the obvious options. Anita wore a trench coat as a teenager to remove the perceived cause of unwanted attention. She tried being blunt. She tried being warm. She tried rehabilitating people. She tried keeping people at careful emotional distance. None of it changed the outcome.

This matters because the instinct when something isn't working is to try harder or try differently. But when you've already varied the key variables — your appearance, your warmth, your directness, your willingness to fix people — and the result stays the same, you're being given important information: those variables are not actually driving the outcome.

This is a useful principle beyond Anita's specific situation. If you've changed X, Y, and Z and the result doesn't change, X, Y, and Z are probably not the cause. Something else is.

Repetition Compulsion: What It Actually Means

It's worth being precise about what this does and doesn't mean.

It does not mean: "you are causing this, it's your fault."

It does mean: if a painful pattern repeats consistently in your life, there's likely some structure to it — something about how you select situations, how you respond early in relationships, or what feels emotionally normal to you — that's worth examining carefully, not to assign blame, but because patterns contain information that can be used.

The trap Anita identifies is one many people fall into: the situations that feel familiar, even when they're harmful, tend to persist precisely because they feel familiar. You're not consciously choosing them. But familiarity has a gravitational pull that operates below conscious awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

One concrete hypothesis worth testing: Anita mentions she's not good at keeping up with friendships, and that the people who stay most present in her life tend to be the ones who push hardest for attention. This creates a selection bias — the people who are merely genuine friends drift away naturally, and the ones who remain are disproportionately the ones with ulterior motives. The pattern then looks like "everyone eventually wants more than friendship," when the truer picture might be "the structure of my social life filters for that outcome."

The Space Between Extremes

One of the clearest practical insights: Anita's friendship strategy has collapsed into two poles.

  • Too much distance: keep people at arm's length, see them rarely, preserve the friendship by never letting it deepen — safe, but lonely and not what she actually wants.
  • Too much closeness: emotional intimacy that, given how people are conditioned, tends to tip into romantic feelings.

"Do you want to spend more time with them?" "No, because then awful things might happen."

The goal — and the difficult work — is finding a middle path. Not abandoning emotional authenticity, but paying close attention to the early stages of friendships to notice what's happening before the familiar pattern locks in.
