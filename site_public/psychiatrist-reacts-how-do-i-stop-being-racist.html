Summary

TL;DR — Unwanted racist thoughts that feel alien to you are a very different phenomenon from ordinary racism, and when those thoughts are derailing your life, that's the clearest possible signal to seek a mental health evaluation.

An 18-year-old describes a sudden onset of racist and racially hierarchical thoughts that he finds deeply disturbing — he knows they're wrong, he's been actively trying to undo them, yet the thoughts keep intensifying. The situation has gotten bad enough that he cancelled college, stopped leaving the house, and fears he might "snap." Understanding why this pattern looks so different from ordinary racism — and knowing when to get professional help — is what this piece is about.

Ego-Syntonic vs. Ego-Dystonic Thoughts

To make sense of what's going on here, you need one foundational concept: the difference between ego-syntonic and ego-dystonic thoughts.

  • Ego-syntonic thoughts fit who you are. They feel correct, natural, even welcome. Someone who is genuinely and comfortably racist has racist thoughts that are ego-syntonic — they share those views openly, defend them, and experience no internal conflict about them.
  • Ego-dystonic thoughts clash with your sense of self. They feel foreign, wrong, or disturbing. You don't want them there. You might be ashamed of them or actively working to get rid of them.

Neither category is automatically good or bad — you can have ego-syntonic thoughts that are healthy, and ego-dystonic thoughts that are harmful. The category just tells you about the relationship between the person and the thought.

The person in this scenario is not a comfortable racist. He is horrified by the thoughts showing up in his mind. He's spending hours reading and watching content to argue himself out of them. He recognizes intellectually that they're wrong. That's the hallmark of ego-dystonic intrusive thought — the thought is there, it feels wrong, and the more you fight it, the worse it seems to get.

"I've spent months binge reading and binge watching YouTube channels about how race isn't real, and I know it's wrong to be racist logically speaking, but I can't make myself fully believe it."

Why Trying Harder to Fix It Makes It Worse

There's a cruel irony in how ego-dystonic thoughts work. The more effort you pour into suppressing or "desensitizing" yourself from the thing that disturbs you, the more mental energy you're directing toward it — and the more entrenched the discomfort becomes.

This person describes exactly that loop:

"The more I try to desensitize myself to non-Asian people, the more uncomfortable I get."

This isn't a failure of willpower or moral character. It's a feature of how intrusive thoughts operate. Forcing yourself to be exposed to a feared stimulus while you're in a heightened state of distress can reinforce the distress rather than dissolve it. The brain is learning the wrong lesson from the repeated exposure.

This pattern — sudden onset, rapid escalation, ego-dystonic quality, compulsive attempts to neutralize the thought, and behavioral avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → — points toward something that needs a clinical eye, not just more self-directed YouTube watching.

When Impaired Function Is the Signal

A critical reframe: mental health evaluations are not just for people who are "really sick" or who have a confirmed neurochemical diagnosis. The more useful question is much simpler — is something inside your mind preventing you from living the life you actually want?

That framing shifts the whole conversation. It removes the stigma of needing a "label" to justify getting help, and it puts function — your ability to actually live — at the center.

In this case, the person: - Cancelled college plans - Stopped leaving the house - Is afraid he might become violent - Has essentially put his life on hold

That's impairment of functionThe clinical threshold used to distinguish a disorder from a difference: is someone's ability to carry out the basic demands of daily life meaningfully compromised?Full definition →, and that alone is sufficient reason to see a mental health professional — regardless of whether any formal diagnosis applies.

"It's not an issue of like whether you actually have a neurochemical imbalance or not. My point is that if your life is so severely impacted by this stuff, whether you have a real diagnosis or don't have a real diagnosis — that's irrelevant. The main thing is that you want to live a particular life, and something is going on inside your mind that is preventing you from living this life."

Think of a mental health professional not as someone who certifies whether you're broken, but as a resource — a tool in your toolbox — for building the life you're trying to build. If you keep "zoning in" to the challenges of your life and wiping every time, that's the signal. You don't need to be at rock bottom. You need to be stuck.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking ego-dystonic thoughts for character flaws. The presence of a disturbing thought — racist, violent, shameful, whatever — does not mean that thought represents who you are. Ego-dystonic thoughts feel alien precisely because they are alien to your valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →. Treating them as evidence of your true self and then spiraling into shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → makes the problem worse.

Trying to logic your way out of intrusive thoughts. This person spent months reading and watching content to intellectually refute the thoughts. That's not how intrusive cognition works. Understanding why a thought is wrong doesn't dissolve the compulsion. In fact, engaging with the thought — even to argue against it — feeds it attention and energy.

Waiting for a "real" diagnosis before seeking help. A lot of people don't reach out for mental health support because they feel their situation isn't severe enough, or because they don't want to walk away without a diagnosis, or because they're afraid of what a diagnosis might mean. None of those are the right threshold. The threshold is: is this getting in the way of the life you want?

Assuming sudden-onset personality or ideological shifts are purely social. When something shifts dramatically and rapidly in how a person thinks — especially when it's accompanied by fear, compulsion, and functional impairment — that warrants clinical attention, not just introspection or internet rabbit holes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ask the function question honestly. Sit down and ask yourself: is what I'm experiencing preventing me from living the life I want? Not "am I a bad person" or "is this serious enough" — just whether it's in your way. If the answer is yes, that's your cue.

  2. Seek a mental health evaluation. If your thoughts, whatever their content, are impairing your ability to work, study, leave the house, maintain relationships, or pursue goals, book an appointment with a psychiatrist or therapist. This doesn't require a crisis. Impaired function is sufficient.

  3. Stop trying to argue the thoughts away. If you're dealing with ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts, intellectual counter-programming (reading articles, watching videos to "disprove" the thought) tends to maintain the loop rather than end it. A clinician can help you find approaches — like certain forms of CBTA structured, results-focused form of psychotherapy that works by breaking a problem — say, procrastination or social anxiety — down into its three components: thoughts, emotions, and behaviors, then targeting each one…Full definition → or ERP — that actually work with the mechanics of intrusive thought.

  4. Notice whether you're fighting the thought or accepting its presence. There's a difference between agreeing with a thought and simply observing that it exists without acting on it. If you find yourself in a war with your own mind, that war itself may be part of what's keeping the thought alive.

  5. If you're worried about harming yourself or others, say so explicitly to a professional. Fear of losing control or committing violence is something a clinician needs to hear clearly. Don't soften it. That information matters for how they help you.
