Your Mind Is Built for Survival, Not Happiness

The mind didn't evolve to feel good. It evolved to keep you alive. That's not a pessimistic statement — it's a clarifying one, because once you accept it, a lot of things that seem broken about your inner life start to make sense.

Every negative emotion has a job:

  • Fear protects you from physical danger
  • Anger helps you defend your boundaries and prevent others from taking advantage of you
  • Anxiety is your predictive capacity — it lets you plan for bad futures before they happen (think storing food for winter)
  • Shame regulates social behavior and keeps you embedded in your communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition →

There is no meaningful variation in the human population around whether people experience these emotions. Unlike eye color or hair color — traits where neither option is clearly better — negative emotions are universal. That universality is the proof they serve a function. Evolution doesn't conserve something this costly unless it pays off.

The implication: suffering is not your enemy. It's information.

Why Mental Illness Feels Worse as Life Gets Better

Here's a paradox worth sitting with: as societies get better at meeting basic needs, people become more mentally unsatisfied, not less. This isn't a flaw — it's what you'd expect given Maslow's hierarchy. When survival is guaranteed, the mind moves up to harder questions: meaning, purpose, identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →.

If your grandmother was living under colonial-era rationing — where you couldn't buy flour even if you had money — self-actualization was irrelevant. The mind was fully occupied with real, immediate problems. Now that many basic needs are met, the mind turns inward, and finds it doesn't know what to do there.

This explains something important: depression and existential dissatisfaction tend to get worse the more comfortable your external life becomes. That's not ingratitude. That's the natural progression of a mind that has run out of survival problems to solve and hasn't yet found something deeper to move toward.

The Hedonistic Treadmill and Why Kings Got Enlightened

There's a striking pattern in the spiritual traditions of the Indian subcontinent: the figures who became enlightened — the Buddhas, the Ramas, the Krishnas — were almost never from the priestly class (Brahmanas). They were Kshatriyas: nobles, warriors, kings. The people who had everything.

Why? Because the Brahmanas, despite their vows of poverty, still had a seed of desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → in the mind. They wanted things they didn't have. That wanting kept them anchored to a story: "if I had this, I'd be happy."

The nobles had already climbed that ladder. They had money, respect, power, relationships. And then they woke up one morning and still felt empty. With nowhere left to go on the material treadmill, the only remaining direction was inward.

"If my mind is unhappy, it tells me: if I had a girlfriend I'd be happy, if I had more money I'd be happy, if I had a PS5 I'd be happy. And so what happened with the nobles is they got all those things, climbed the hedonistic treadmill for a while, reached the top, and were like — wait, I'm still not happy. Now what?"

This is why depression and dissatisfaction, when understood correctly, can be an advantage. They are the signal that the external world has failed to deliver what it promised. That failure, painful as it is, points somewhere real.

The Real Meaning of Renunciation

A common misreading of spiritual poverty goes like this: spiritual people don't want material things, so if I stop wanting material things, I'll become spiritual. This gets it exactly backwards.

A monk who doesn't wear a fancy watch isn't made strong by avoiding the watch. The detachmentThe Sanskrit term for detachment — specifically, detachment from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → comes first — the simple living is a consequence of that internal shift, not the cause of it. When someone forcibly deprives themselves of material comfort in order to feel spiritual, what's usually driving it is ego: "I'm above that. I'm better than people who want those things."

"The desire to not want material things should come naturally as a consequence of your spiritual practice. It's the detachment that allows me to live a simple life — not forcibly living a simple life that will create the spirituality."

True renunciation requires having something to give up. That's why good teachers told a 21-year-old with nothing to his name to go back, get a doctorate, build a life — and then consider giving it up. Escaping a bad life isn't renunciation. Choosing to walk away from a good one is.

Common Pitfalls

Treating negative emotions as enemies. The instinct to eliminate anxiety, fear, or depression as quickly as possible makes sense, but it misses what those emotions are doing. Suppressing them doesn't resolve the signal — it just delays it.

Believing the next thing will finally do it. The hedonistic treadmill is very convincing. The mind is extremely good at generating plausible reasons why the next achievement, relationship, or purchase will be the one that actually works. The pattern only becomes visible after enough cycles.

Performing spirituality instead of practicing it. Imitating the behaviors of people who have achieved internal change — fasting, simple living, meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → apps — can be valuable as an introduction, but mistaking the behavior for the thing itself is a trap. The external change is downstream of the internal one.

Using spirituality to escape. Wanting to renounce the world before you've built anything in it is often escapism, not enlightenment. It's a way of framing running away as spiritual progress.

Expecting meditation apps to go deep. Apps are designed to keep the monkey mind engaged through novelty — new techniques, new visualizations. That's useful for starting out. But real, lasting meditation tends to come from one practice, done consistently, over years, ideally with real instruction.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the function of your worst emotion. The next time you feel anxious, ashamed, or depressed, pause and ask: what is this emotion trying to protect me from or point me toward? Write down a one-sentence answer. This isn't toxic positivity — it's treating your mind as a signal generator rather than an enemy.

  2. Notice the "if only" pattern. For one week, catch every thought of the form "I'd be happy if ___." Write them down. At the end of the week, look at the list. Notice whether any of those things have already been true at some point, and whether they delivered what they promised.

  3. Audit your spiritual imitation. If you have any practices you've adopted because "this is what people who have it together do" — a certain diet, a fasting schedule, minimalism, journaling — ask yourself honestly whether the behavior is producing an internal shift, or whether you're performing the output without doing the underlying work.

  4. Pick one meditation technique and stay with it. Rather than rotating through an app's library of techniques, choose a single simple practice — breath awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, a body scan, mantra repetition — and commit to it daily for 30 days. Notice what changes when the novelty pressure is removed.

  5. Take your suffering seriously as information. If you are materially comfortable and still deeply unhappy, resist the urge to feel guilty about it or push it down. That discomfort is historically what has driven people toward genuinely meaningful change. Treat it as a compass, not a character flaw.
