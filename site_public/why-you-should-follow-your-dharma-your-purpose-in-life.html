Summary

TL;DR — Your Dharma isn't a fixed destination you find by thinking harder; it's something you uncover by creating, grieving what didn't work out, and then asking the right questions.

Most people trying to find their purpose get stuck in their heads. They analyze, they plan, they wait for clarity to strike before they act. That approach almost never works — and the reason why reveals something important about how the mind itself is structured.

Two Minds, One Imbalance

You have two distinct modes of thinking: a creative mind (the author, the artist — the part that generates something from nothing) and an analytical mind (the editor — the part that evaluates, critiques, and responds).

For a lot of people — especially gamers, but really anyone who spends more time consuming and commenting than making — the analytical mind has been trained obsessively while the creative mind has atrophied. Video games are a perfect example: they require constant analysis and reaction to external stimuli, but they require you to create absolutely nothing. You respond to what the game gives you. There's no internal generation.

The result is a person who has lots of thoughts but produces nothing from them. You can think but you can't produce.

This isn't a character flaw. It's a muscle imbalance — like working one arm at the gym for years and never touching the other. The fix isn't more analysis. The fix is to exercise the weak muscle.

"Your analytical mind is way overblown, and your creative mind is like — it's weak. It's like people who skip leg day."

In yogic terms, this maps onto the pairing of Shiva (consciousness/intention) and Shakti (energy/creative force). To manifest anything in the world, you need both. Most people struggling to find their purpose have plenty of Shiva — clear-eyed analysis, self-awareness, good intentions — but almost no Shakti. They can see the problem clearly. They just can't do anything about it.

The Writing Exercise That Actually Locates Your Dharma

Here's the first concrete step: take a piece of paper and write one page about what you think is wrong with the world.

That's it. No format requirements, no length minimum beyond a page, no rules about what counts.

The reason this works is that when you sit down and actually write — rather than just think — your creative mind has to show up. The things that rise to the surface when you're forced to produce something reveal what actually matters to you. The topics you reach for, the problems that feel most urgent, the injustices that make you want to fill the page — those aren't random. They're signal.

The analytical mind will immediately try to sabotage this. It will ask whether you should use single spacing or double spacing. It will tell you the first sentence doesn't sound right. It will remind you that whatever you write probably won't be very good. This is the editor trying to shut down the artist before the artist can do anything.

Your job is to ignore all of that and just produce something. It doesn't need to be good. The goal isn't quality — it's output. You're building the creative muscle, not winning a prize.

"Write the shittiest book in the history of humanity, and somehow that's gonna make you feel good. It doesn't have to succeed. It doesn't have to make you rich."

After you've written the page, then you turn on the analytical mind. Read what came out. Ask yourself: what does this tell me about what I actually care about? What patterns show up? What problems kept pulling my pen forward? That's where your Dharma is hiding.

Grieving Shattered Dreams Before Moving Forward

For anyone whose original dream has already been ruled out — you wanted to be a professional player and realized you're not good enough, or you wanted a particular career that isn't going to happen — there's a step that comes before any new direction can take hold: grief.

This isn't a metaphor. It's a psychological necessity.

When you let yourself want something deeply and then lose it, that loss is real. A shattered career dream is a kind of miscarriage — there was a whole imagined life, a trajectory of hope and meaning, and now it's gone. The appropriate response is to grieve it honestly.

What does that look like? First, acknowledge that you're entitled to feel bad. Don't minimize it. Don't rush past it. Say clearly to yourself: I really wanted this. It's gone. That's genuinely sad.

Second — and this is equally important — recognize that grief has limits. Losing this particular dream does not mean your life is over or that no meaningful path exists. It means this specific path is closed.

The reason grieving matters so much is that unfelt loss doesn't go away. It sinks into the subconscious and weighs you down invisibly. You end up going through motions in a new direction without ever fully committing, because some part of you is still frozen at the moment you didn't let yourself feel.

"Sometimes we don't let ourselves want what we cannot have, and then that want lives in our subconscious and weighs us down."

Finding the New Direction Inside the Old One

Once you've genuinely grieved the lost path, something useful opens up. Often, what you loved about that path still exists — just not in the form you originally imagined.

Take someone who wanted to become a professional esports player and isn't good enough. The esports industry isn't just players. It's coaches, managers, psychologists, PR professionals, financial people, content creators, event organizers. Most of the people working in esports don't play the game at a professional level.

The question to ask isn't "how do I accept that esports is over for me?" It's: what's missing in esports? Write that page. Let the creative mind answer it. Then look at your actual skills and ask what you could realistically do about that gap.

This reframe applies everywhere. The roguelike analogy is useful here: you wanted a fireball spell and built your whole character around being a fire mage. Six levels in, there's no fireball spell. You can either rage at the dungeon, or you can adapt your build to what's actually available — and sometimes, on level seven, you find the spell anyway. But you only get there by continuing to play.

Common Pitfalls

  • Staying in analytical mode and calling it self-knowledge. Thinking hard about your purpose is not the same as finding it. More reflection without output just deepens the rut.

  • Letting the editor kill the artist before anything gets made. Questions about formatting, quality, and whether the output will be good are your analytical mind's way of avoiding the discomfort of creating. They feel practical. They're not.

  • Skipping grief and going straight to "moving on." If you haven't genuinely acknowledged the loss, your subconscious will drag you back to it. Forced optimism isn't the same as being okay.

  • Assuming Dharma has to look like what you originally imagined. The underlying thing you care about — the problem you want to solve, the world you want to exist — is usually broader than any one career path. The form can change; the substance often stays consistent.

  • Waiting for purpose to arrive before acting. Purpose is more often discovered through action than through contemplation. You write the page to find out what you care about, not after you already know.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write one page about what you think is wrong with the world. Don't plan it first. Sit down with a blank page and write. Turn the analytical mind off. Let whatever comes out, come out. Don't edit while you write.

  2. After writing, read it with your analytical mind. Ask: What topics kept pulling me forward? What problems felt most urgent to write about? What does this tell me about what actually matters to me?

  3. If you have a shattered dream, grieve it explicitly. Write down (or say out loud, or say to someone you trust): I really wanted this. It's gone. That's genuinely sad. Then write a second line: This loss is real, and my life is not over.

  4. Write a second page about what's missing in the field you care about. Not what's wrong with your life — what's missing from the domain itself. What problems does nobody seem to be solving? What would you want to exist that doesn't yet?

  5. Make something bad on purpose. Commit to producing one piece of creative output — a page, a short video, a design sketch, anything — that you have zero expectation will be good. The goal is just to finish it. Notice how it feels when you've actually made something, even something terrible.

  6. Audit which mind you're exercising most. For one week, notice how many hours you spend responding/analyzing (games, social media, commentary, critique) versus generating (writing, building, making). Then deliberately add one generating activity per day, however small.
