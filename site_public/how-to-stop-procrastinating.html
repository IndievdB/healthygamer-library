"Try" Is the Problem

There's a subtle but important distinction between trying, doing, and failing — and most people get it backwards.

Trying feels like action but isn't. It's a holding pattern. It keeps you in a state of perpetual almost-starting, which is exactly where procrastination lives. Failing, on the other hand, is real. It happened. You can look at it, learn from it, and move forward.

"Do or fail — I'll take either of those. Failing is fantastic. Just do it and screw up. Try is the future killer."

This reframes the whole goal. You don't need to succeed. You just need to exit the "try" state. Success and failure are both fine outcomes. Neither of them is procrastination.

There's also a widely believed myth that confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → comes from success. It doesn't. ConfidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → comes from failure — specifically from surviving it. When you fall down and get back up, you learn that falling isn't fatal. Once you know that, you stop being so afraid of the next step. Success on its own doesn't teach you that you can handle failure. Only failing teaches you that.

There Is No "First Step"

A common way people frame procrastination is: "the hardest part is taking the first step." This framing is well-meaning but it actually makes things worse.

Calling something "the first step" is already future-oriented thinking. It implies a sequence, a staircase, a destination — all of which exist in the future, not the present. And the future isn't where you are.

"There is no such thing as a first step. There is only a step — the step that you can take. That's not a philosophy. That is an actual reality."

Right now, in this moment, there is only one action available to you: the one you can take now. Not the first of many. Not the beginning of a plan. Just the thing in front of you.

This is especially relevant for people stuck in "one day" thinking: one day I'll get fit, one day I'll move out, one day I'll start that project. But you cannot exercise "one day." You can only exercise now. The future version of you who finally does the thing — that version doesn't exist yet. Only the present version does. The present version is the only one who can act.

The Future-Thinking Mind Has a Volume Problem

Future thinking isn't inherently bad. Planning, scheduling, setting goals — these are all legitimate and necessary uses of the future-oriented mind. You can't organize a life without it.

The problem is control. For most people who procrastinate, the future-thinking mind isn't a tool they pick up and put down. It runs the show. It decides when things will happen ("later"), it generates elaborate plans that never get executed, and it constantly defers action to a time that never arrives.

"The future thinking mind is one function of the mind that needs to be under your control. You should decide when it gets to run the show — and when it doesn't."

Procrastination, by this definition, is a future-thinking mind that's been given too much authority. This reframe matters because it stops you from treating procrastination as a character flaw. You're not lazy. You're not broken. You have a mind with a normal function that's currently overweighted. That's fixable.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until tomorrow (or next week) to start. This is exactly the mechanism of procrastination. Any version of "I'll begin soon" is the future-thinking mind taking control. The only workable start time is now.

Confusing planning with doing. Thinking through how you're going to do something feels productive. It isn't the same as doing it. Planning has a place, but when the future-thinking mind is running unchecked, planning becomes a substitute for action rather than a preparation for it.

Believing confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → is a prerequisite. A lot of people wait to feel ready or capable before they start. But confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → doesn't arrive before action — it's built by surviving the failures that come from acting. Waiting to feel confident before you try is just procrastination with better branding.

Framing everything as a long journey. When you think of a task as "step one of many," it psychologically distances you from the action. The mind drifts forward into the full weight of what the journey will require. Keeping your attention on just the one thing in front of you right now removes that weight.

Treating "try" as equivalent to "do." Saying you'll try gives you an out. It keeps the door open to not following through. Either do the thing or don't — but deciding to "try" is a liminal state that doesn't resolve in either direction, and it tends to resolve into nothing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Replace "try" with a binary. The next time you're about to say "I'll try to do X," stop and make a real decision: either commit to doing it, or consciously decide not to. Remove the middle ground. Both outcomes are more useful than trying.

  2. Do one thing right now — today, not tomorrow. Whatever you've been putting off, identify the smallest concrete action and do it before this day ends. Not because it's "step one" of a plan, but because it's the only step that exists.

  3. When you fail, debrief it. After a genuine attempt that didn't go how you wanted, spend a few minutes asking: what actually happened? Was it motivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition →? Discipline? Unclear expectations? Fear? The failure itself is information — treat it that way instead of treating it as a verdict on your worth.

  4. Notice when the future-thinking mind is speaking. Start catching the moments when your internal monologue is scheduling action to "later" or "someday." You don't have to eliminate future thinking — just notice when it's doing something useful (planning a schedule) versus when it's deferring action (telling you to start Monday).

  5. Practice collapsing the question to the present. When you feel stuck, ask yourself: "What is the one thing I can actually do right now?" Not the first step of a plan. Not the most important thing. Just what's possible in this moment. Then do that.

  6. Stop waiting to want it badly enough.
