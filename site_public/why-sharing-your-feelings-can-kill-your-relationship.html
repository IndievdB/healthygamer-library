Summary

TL;DR — Whether sharing your feelings helps or hurts a relationship depends on several factors: your partner's own emotional style, whether either of you has mental illness, and whether the relationship is oriented toward getting closer or simply staying stable.

The internet is split on emotional disclosure in relationships. One camp says sharing your feelings is healthy and builds intimacy. Another says it gets men punished and makes partners lose attraction. Both camps are drawing on real experiences — they're just describing different situations. The research is more nuanced than either side admits, and understanding the actual data helps you figure out when to share, when to hold back, and how to tell the difference.

Correlation Isn't a How-To Guide

Studies do show that self-disclosure in relationships is positively associated with self-esteemSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →, relationship satisfaction, love, and commitment. But "associated with" is not the same as "causes." A high-quality relationship with mutual trust is also the kind of relationship where people feel safe sharing — so the causality could run either direction. The cluster of good things tends to appear together. That doesn't mean dumping your emotional state onto a partner will produce those good things.

A second important finding from the same research: the more a woman perceived that her partner was disclosing, the less likely the couple was to break up. Notice the word "perceived." Her belief that her partner is being open matters — which is not the same as saying maximum emotional output is always best.

Bell Curves: Why "Men Don't Share" Is a Bad Generalization

Even when there is a measurable average difference between men and women on some trait — say, willingness to emotionally disclose — the variation within each group is almost always larger than the variation between groups. Some men share constantly. Some women rarely do. The gap between the most and least disclosing men is bigger than the average gap between men and women. This is why broad gender rules ("men should never show emotions," "women always want emotional openness") are statistically weak. The person in front of you is more likely to match someone from the opposite end of their own group's bell curve than to match the group average.

Two Relationship Goals That Require Different Strategies

Not every relationship is built around the goal of getting closer.   • Promotion-focused: The goal is to grow closer, build more intimacy, and keep moving toward each other.
  • Prevention-focused: The goal is to not lose what you have — to maintain stability and avoid drifting apart.

For prevention-focused individuals, emotional suppression was associated with greater marital satisfaction — but only when both partners were similarly suppressive. The match matters as much as the behavior itself.

So the first question to ask yourself is: what is this relationship actually trying to do? An equilibrium relationship isn't bad. Two people settling their lives, coexisting with warmth, waiting to see where things go — that's real and common. But the strategy for emotional disclosure in that relationship is different from one where both people are actively trying to deepen intimacy.

The Volatility Finding (and Why "The Relationship Improved" Is Hard to Measure)

One daily-diary study tracked couples over a week, asking both partners each day about their sense of love, commitment, satisfaction, and closeness. It also tracked each person's emotional labor — how much effort they put into supporting their partner emotionally. The key finding:

  • When a man did more emotional work, the woman's daily experience of love, satisfaction, and closeness became more stable (less volatile).
  • When a woman did more emotional work, the man's daily experience of love and commitment became more volatile — it fluctuated more, not less.

This is a strange and uncomfortable result. The intuitive expectation would be that a partner working harder emotionally makes the other person feel more secure. For women, that's what happened. For men, the opposite appeared — the more she did, the more his feelings swung around.

This matters for the original tweet ("I stopped sharing my feelings and the relationship improved"). The honest question is: improved by whose measure, and what were they actually measuring? A man might feel calmer when he's no longer sharing difficult emotions — that's real. But his partner might be quietly doing more emotional management work to compensate, and his perception of relationship quality might be fluctuating in ways he's not even tracking consciously. Social media shares experiences; it doesn't give you the controlled study with both partners reporting every day.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating one study or one experience as a universal rule. The people saying "sharing feelings destroys attraction" have had real experiences. So have the people saying the opposite. Both are describing specific conditions — not universal laws.
  • Assuming your perception of relationship quality is the full picture. Your partner has their own internal experience that you may not be measuring at all. "The relationship improved" based on your own mood is incomplete data.
  • Making your partner your therapist. If you have diagnosed or significant mental health struggles, leaning entirely on a romantic partner for emotional processing is a different ask than ordinary emotional sharing. It tends to produce burnout over time, regardless of how much they love you.
  • Ignoring the match. Emotional disclosure works best when it's somewhat reciprocal. If your partner is generally non-disclosing and prevention-focused, opening up frequently and heavily is likely to feel destabilizing to them — not connecting.
  • Thinking equilibrium relationships are broken. A stable, low-drama relationship that isn't racing toward deeper intimacy isn't automatically unhealthy. The question is whether both people are okay with that and whether it matches what they actually want.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your relationship's current orientation. Ask yourself honestly: are you and your partner actively working toward more closeness, or are you in a stable equilibrium you both seem to prefer? There's no wrong answer — but knowing this helps you calibrate how much emotional disclosure is appropriate right now.

  2. Observe your partner's emotional disclosure level. Over the next week, notice how often and how openly your partner shares their own negative emotions or vulnerabilities with you. Their behavior is a meaningful signal of how much disclosure they're comfortable receiving.

  3. If you're dealing with mental illness or significant chronic stress, identify at least one non-partner outlet. This might be a therapist, a trusted friend, a support group, or a coach. The goal isn't to hide your struggles from your partner entirely — it's to make sure they are not your only outlet.

  4. Before sharing something difficult, briefly check your goal. Are you sharing because it will genuinely help the relationship move toward something? Or are you offloading to relieve your own discomfort? Both happen — but being honest with yourself about the difference lets you communicate more deliberately.

  5. If you've recently changed your behavior (like the person in the tweet), ask your partner directly. "When I was sharing more of what was going on with me, how did that feel for you? How does it feel now?" Their answer is data you're probably missing. It might surprise you.
