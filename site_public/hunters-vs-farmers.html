Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your cognitive type honestly. Over the next week, notice when you feel genuinely absorbed and energized by work versus when you feel like you're dragging yourself through quicksand. Don't judge it — just log it. Look for patterns around task type, time of day, and environmental conditions.

  2. Audit your current environment against your type. List the three biggest demands your daily routine places on your attention. For each one, ask: does this require farmer-style sustained focus or hunter-style dynamic switching? Then ask: which do I actually do well? If there's a mismatch, that's your friction point.

  3. Experiment with task structure. If you suspect you lean hunter, try breaking long work blocks into shorter, varied segments with genuine switching between different types of tasks. If you lean farmer, try protecting a consistent block of time each day for your most important work and reducing mid-task interruptions.

  4. If you experience periods of deep absorption, think back on the last two or three times it happened. What were the conditions — topic, time of day, environment, emotional state? This won't give you an on/off switch, but it will help you create conditions where it's more likely to occur on useful tasks.

  5. Find or build external structure where your internal structure is weak. If your attention fluctuates and you know it, don't rely on willpower to compensate. Use external anchors: co-working with someone, public commitments, timers, or environments (like a library) that impose structure from outside.

  6. If you're in a role or environment that persistently doesn't fit, take the mismatch seriously. Before concluding you're simply not capable, ask whether the environment itself is the problem. That doesn't always mean quitting your job — but it might mean negotiating for a different kind of role, a different physical workspace, or a different distribution of responsibilities.
