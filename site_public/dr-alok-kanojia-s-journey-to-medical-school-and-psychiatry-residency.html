Summary

TL;DR — A directionless kid who was failing out of college ended up becoming a psychiatrist through a winding path of video games, a trip to India, yoga, neuroscience research, and a deliberately chosen difficult goal.

This is a personal origin story — how one person went from nearly dropping out of college with under a 2.0 GPA to completing medical school and psychiatry residency. The throughline isn't a clean plan. It's a series of honest realizations, a core curiosity about how the mind works, and one deliberately irrational-sounding commitment: do something hard because it's hard.

Starting a Year Behind Everyone Else

The story starts early, and it starts with a misread situation. After attending a small school with a high teacher-to-student ratio, an early academic acceleration meant entering second grade as a five-year-old among six-year-olds. That one-year gap doesn't sound like much, but developmentally it's enormous — especially in sports, where a five-year-old simply cannot keep up with six-year-olds physically.

The conclusion drawn at the time: I'm bad at sports. I'm just not athletic.

The actual explanation: age difference.

This is worth sitting with, because it's a pattern that plays out for a lot of people. You perform poorly at something for a structural reason you can't see, you draw a conclusion about your identity from that performance, and then that conclusion sticks around long after the structural reason has disappeared. The early turn toward video games came out of this — in games, the age gap didn't matter. You could be just as good as everyone else.

From there: more gaming, a directionless high school experience, a lot of ego, and then failing out of college with under a 2.0 GPA.

The Trip to India and What Yoga Actually Is

At 21, with nowhere to be and no clear future, a trip to India changed the trajectory. What got discovered there wasn't yoga in the sense most Westerners know it — not the asanas, not the stretching in a studio. What got discovered was yoga in its original form: a systematic framework for understanding how a human being works.

"I found a system to understand myself in a methodical way — I can sit down and study who I am and how I work as a person, and then I can get better at being me."

That framing is surprisingly powerful. We treat mathematics, neuroscience, economics, and psychology as legitimate fields where systematic study produces real mastery. Traditional yoga, in its original form, applies that same logic to the self. There are principles that explain why you feel compelled to keep cleaning after you've already cleaned, why you can't make yourself start a long paper, why certain urges arise, why shame shows up when it does. These aren't random. There's a system underneath them.

Finding that system — and finding that it produced real answers — was genuinely exciting. But there was a catch: a skeptic by nature, the goal wasn't just to accept the framework on faith. The question became: what is this doing to my brain?

From Meditation to Neuroscience Research

The experiences that came from meditation practice were strange and compelling enough to demand a scientific account. The response was to start doing EEG research on meditators — measuring electrical activity in the brain during meditation states — and then move into neuroscience research more broadly.

This is a good example of how genuine curiosity works. It doesn't stay neatly inside one discipline. The question "what is happening in my brain right now?" is both a spiritual question and a neuroscientific one, and following it seriously meant engaging both.

This dual interest — in the inner experiential side of things and in the biological mechanisms underneath — became a core reason for choosing medicine over psychology later on.

Why Medical School? An Honest Answer

Here's the part that doesn't show up in most professional origin stories: the decision to pursue medical school was partly arbitrary.

A teacher in India said something that landed hard:

"Unless you can succeed in the material world, you're not going to succeed in the spiritual — because anything you do in the material world is going to be way easier than what you do in the spiritual world. The real world is for noobs. The spiritual world is for experts."

The logic that followed from this was: set a hard goal. Something that seems genuinely impossible given where you're starting from. If you can achieve that, you'll have the discipline and inner development for deeper work.

At the time, with a 2.5 GPA, becoming a doctor seemed impossible. So: become a doctor.

That's not the whole story — there was also a genuine intellectual reason. Ayurvedic medicine doesn't separate the body into isolated organ systems the way Western medicine does. A doctor trained in that tradition is a whole-person practitioner. And the idea that you can understand the mind without understanding the body felt like a fundamental gap — one that choosing psychology over psychiatry would leave unfilled.

"I just didn't believe that you can understand the mind without understanding the body."

Emerging research on the brain-gut connection, and on how physical health shapes mental states, supports this instinct. Choosing psychiatry (the medical route) over psychology wasn't a slight against psychologists — it was a personal decision to keep access to the full toolkit.

Ego, Oncology, and the Late Pivot to Psychiatry

Even after getting into medical school, the stated goal wasn't psychiatry. Initially it was oncology — partly because of admiration for a father who was an oncologist, partly because of a lingering belief that psychiatrists aren't "real" doctors. There was a pull toward something that felt like it was visibly saving lives: curing cancer, treating heart attacks, becoming a trauma surgeon.

The oncology vision was actually kind of beautiful: use herbs, teach yoga and meditation, help patients find a connection to their spirituality before death. A holistic cancer doctor. That's not a cynical choice — it came from real values.

But in the fourth year of medical school, the obvious thing finally became obvious: the mind was always what was most interesting. It had always been the center of gravity. The pivot to psychiatry came from finally letting go of the ego that wanted to be seen doing something dramatic, and acknowledging what had been true the whole time.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing a bad outcome for a fixed trait. The early athletics story is a clean example: performing poorly because of a structural disadvantage, then concluding you're just bad at something. A lot of people carry these conclusions into adulthood without ever revisiting whether the original situation actually warranted them.

Waiting for a clear, logical reason to pursue something hard. The decision to go to medical school was partly arbitrary and self-described as such. If you wait until your reasons are airtight and your odds look reasonable, you may never start. The commitment came before the clarity.

Thinking curiosity needs to stay in its lane. The assumption that spiritual practice and neuroscience are in separate categories almost prevented the deeper integration that made the work meaningful. Following genuine curiosity tends to cross disciplinary lines — that's often a feature, not a problem.

Letting prestige anxiety override honest self-knowledge. Wanting to be an oncologist because it seemed like "real" medicine delayed acknowledging what was actually interesting. The ego argument (surgeons save lives visibly; psychiatrists don't) is seductive and worth watching for in yourself.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Revisit one story you tell about yourself as a fixed limitation. Pick one — "I'm bad at sports," "I'm not a math person," "I'm just not disciplined." Ask whether the original data actually supports the conclusion, or whether there was a structural factor (age, environment, circumstance) that you didn't account for at the time.

  2. Find the underlying system in something you're struggling with. Rather than treating your procrastination, your compulsive habits, or your avoidance as character flaws, ask: what principle is operating here? Yoga, psychology, and neuroscience all suggest there are consistent mechanisms underneath these patterns. Start treating yourself as a subject worth systematic study.

  3. Set one goal that seems impossible given your current starting point. Not recklessly — but with the explicit intention of doing something difficult because it's difficult, as a form of inner development. Notice whether the goal clarifies what you actually care about as you pursue it.

  4. If you're drawn to mental health work, ask what kind of completeness you want. Do you want to understand the body as well as the mind? What do you believe about the connection between physical and mental health? Let your actual beliefs — not prestige hierarchies — guide how you orient your learning.

  5. When you notice ego pulling your goals toward what looks impressive, pause and name it. Ask what you'd be drawn to if no one was watching and there was no status attached to either option. That question doesn't always override other considerations, but it tends to surface honest information.
