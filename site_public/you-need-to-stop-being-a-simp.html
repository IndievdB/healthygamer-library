How Beetles and Beer Bottles Explain Your Love Life

In 2011, two researchers won the Ig Nobel Prize for discovering that Australian jewel beetles were trying to mate with brown glass beer bottles. The beetles weren't broken — their mating instinct was being hijacked. Their brains were wired to look for certain features: brown, dimpled, large. The beer bottle was extremely brown, extremely dimpled, and extremely large — a more intense version of every signal they were designed to respond to.

This is the concept of a supernormal stimulus: an artificial trigger that hits the brain's response system harder than anything found in nature, causing an exaggerated — and misdirected — response.

Human sexuality follows the same logic. Normal attraction responds to real cues. But when those cues get amplified artificially — more exposure, more intensity, more variety — the brain's response escalates beyond what any real relationship could produce. This is why pornography has evolved steadily toward the more extreme: each escalation activates more of the brain and creates a stronger pull.

Why Streaming and OnlyFans Are Different From Ordinary Porn

Older forms of pornography activated one layer — physical arousal. What platforms like OnlyFans and live streaming have done is stack multiple layers of normal relationship cues on top of that, each one a supernormal stimulus in its own right:

  • Daily casual interaction — seeing someone every day, hearing them talk about their life, is something your brain associates with a close relationship
  • Special attention — being noticed and acknowledged by someone with hundreds of viewers triggers the feeling of being uniquely valued
  • Emotional vulnerability and intimacy — watching someone cry, sharing in a private-feeling moment, registers in the brain as deep closeness
  • ReciprocityThe mutual exchange of care, effort, and goodwill between friends — not as an equal trade, but as each person contributing what they genuinely can.Full definition → — giving money or gifts and receiving personalized attention back mirrors the transactional give-and-take of a real relationship
  • Secrecy — sharing something you'd be embarrassed to tell a real partner, without fear of judgment, mimics the private intimacy of genuine closeness

Stack enough of these boxes together and the brain genuinely cannot tell the difference between this and a real relationship. That's not stupidity. That's neuroscience.

Simping Is Addiction, Not Love — Here's the Difference

This distinction matters more than almost anything else in this topic.

When you reduce contact with someone you're genuinely in love with, the love remains. It doesn't demand constant feeding. You can want good things for them, feel warmth toward them, and let them live their life — because real love is oriented toward the other person.

When you reduce contact with someone you're addicted to, something different happens: withdrawal. You get restless. You find excuses to check their social media. You feel a hunger that pulls you back. You start resenting them for not giving you what you need. The "sacrifice" you make for this person doesn't feel like genuine care — it's a mechanism to keep the supply of stimulus flowing, which is why it always leaves you feeling empty and taken advantage of.

The tell is in the frustration. If you're angry that this person won't give you what you deserve after everything you've done, that's not love — that's the picture of addiction. Real love doesn't accumulate resentment like that.

"Love is reciprocal. Love prioritizes the other person. But in simping, that sense of sacrifice actually becomes a way to fulfill your own emotional needs."

There's also a subtler trap: telling yourself "their happiness matters more than mine, so I'll keep suffering and supporting them." That sounds selfless. But if you can't actually walk away and feel okay, if you need to stay connected to feel good about yourself as a "tragic lover," that's the addiction talking — not love.

Why Simp-Haters Are Usually Former Simps

Almost everyone who aggressively hates on simps started as one. The journey into red-pill thinking typically begins with real pain: getting cheated on, having years of emotional investment thrown back in your face, paying someone's rent for months only to watch them date someone else.

That kind of wound causes a person to look inward and ask how they got there. And the answer they find — the capacity to fall in love, to be vulnerable, to care deeply — feels like the enemy. So they do what humans do with painful things: they start to hate it.

"The things that I hate within myself are the things I will hate the most in other people."

This is a real psychological principle called reaction formationA defense mechanism in which the mind takes an unacceptable internal quality — a feeling, impulse, or trait — and converts it into its opposite, expressing it outwardly as virtue or righteous condemnation.Full definition →. If I hate a part of myself, I can externalize that hatred — project it outward onto other people who display that same quality — and feel temporarily better. I don't have to sit with "I hate myself for being weak." Instead I can point at other simps and feel contempt. It's less painful, but it solves nothing.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking hatred of women is justified rather than self-protective. The reason simp-to-misogynist pipelines exist is that hating women is a (maladaptive) way of breaking an addiction to women. Understanding this doesn't mean the women involved did nothing wrong — some genuinely did. But hating an entire group is something you maintain for your psychological protection, not because it's accurate.

Confusing the "no contact" advice with a complete solution. Going no contact works, but only if you understand why — every interaction feeds the supernormal stimulus loop. Without that understanding, people tend to cave because the withdrawal is uncomfortable and they misinterpret it as proof they're truly in love.

Mistaking self-sacrifice for love. The feeling of being a devoted, suffering person who gives everything is emotionally rewarding in its own right. That reward is the addiction sustaining itself. Real love doesn't require suffering to feel meaningful.

Thinking this only applies to parasocial relationships. The same supernormal-stimulus mechanism operates in in-person dynamics — the coworker who only talks to you when she needs something, the friend who shares emotional intimacy but never reciprocates romantically. The platform doesn't matter; the stimulus stack does.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your stimulus stack. For the person you're fixated on, go through the list: physical attraction, daily casual contact, special attention, emotional vulnerability, reciprocity, secrecy. Count how many boxes are being checked. The higher the count, the more likely this is addiction rather than love.

  2. Run the withdrawal test. Deliberately reduce contact for a few days — stop the intimate conversations, don't check their social media, don't watch their streams. Pay close attention to what you feel. Craving, restlessness, and anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → point to addiction. Sadness and genuine concern for their wellbeing point toward real feeling.

  3. Practice going no contact — and understand why it works. Every interaction replenishes the stimulus. Reducing all forms of contact (in person, social media, streaming, texting) isn't punishing yourself or the other person; it's cutting off the supply that's driving the addiction. Expect withdrawal. Ride it out.

  4. If you're a simp-hater, trace the origin. Ask yourself honestly: was there a specific experience — a betrayal, a rejection, a financial or emotional loss — that started this? If so, consider that your hostility toward simps (and women) may be protecting you from revisiting your own vulnerability, rather than reflecting reality.

  5. Separate the capacity for love from the addiction. The part of you that wants connection, intimacy, and partnership is not the problem. The problem is that capacity getting hijacked by supernormal stimuli. You can protect yourself from exploitation without killing off your ability to love — and a healthy, reciprocal relationship is precisely where that capacity belongs.
