Safety Comes First, Always

Before any other work can happen, you need to be physically safe. For some people, that means immediate steps: calling emergency services, going to a hospital, or accessing resources to leave an abusive relationship. Therapy can't do its job while you're still in danger of harm — from others or from yourself.

If you're in a situation where your immediate wellbeing is at risk, that is the first thing to address. Everything else comes after.

Why Self-Harm Behaviors Make Sense (Even If They're Harmful)

One of the most important things to understand about coping behaviors like cutting is why they work — at least in the short term.

When someone is emotionally overwhelmed, physical pain cuts through everything. It's so intense and immediate that it clears the mind of whatever was spiraling. People who cut tend to do it superficially — maximizing pain while minimizing actual tissue damage. That's not random. It's a crude but effective emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → technique.

"They are inducing the most amount of pain while minimizing the damage that they're actually doing to their body."

Understanding this matters because it reframes the goal of treatment: you're not trying to eliminate the function that self-harm serves. You're trying to replace it with something that accomplishes the same thing — interrupting the emotional spiral — without the damage. DBT (more on that below) is built around exactly this.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying one therapy and concluding therapy doesn't work. Talk therapy not working doesn't mean nothing will work. EMDR, DBT, exposure therapy, somatic approaches — these are all distinct and may land very differently.
  • Using psychedelics to treat an acute crisis. The evidence (and clinical observation) suggests that the worse your baseline state going in, the higher the risk of a negative outcome. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Assess your safety first. Honestly evaluate whether your current living situation, relationships, or internal state put you at risk. If yes, prioritize changing that before anything else — contact a crisis line, a shelter, or emergency services if needed.

  2. Try a grounding technique the next time you're emotionally overwhelmed. Hold an ice cube in your hand. Focus entirely on the sensation as it intensifies. When it becomes too uncomfortable, let go. Notice how much less mental bandwidth is available for spiraling thoughts while you're doing it.
