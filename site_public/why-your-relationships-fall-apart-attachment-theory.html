Summary

TL;DR — Most relationship problems in men aren't personality flaws; they're the predictable outcome of how emotional needs were handled in childhood, and they can change.

Attachment theory — the science of how early caregiving shapes the way we relate to other people — explains a lot of what goes wrong in adult relationships: the mixed signals, the moodiness that can't be explained, the pushing away of people you actually want to be close to. The good news is that attachment style isn't fixed. The people you choose to spend time with as an adult have more influence over it than your childhood ever did.

The Strange Situation and What It Reveals

Back in the 1970s, a researcher named John Bowlby designed an experiment called the Strange Situation: a mother and young child in a room, the mother leaves, a stranger enters, then the mother returns. What fascinated researchers was how differently children responded when mom came back.

Four patterns emerged:

  • Secure attachment — the child cried when mom left, but was quickly soothed when she returned.
  • Anxious ambivalent attachment — the child cried when mom left and was visibly conflicted when she returned: wanting comfort but also pulling away, sending mixed signals.
  • Anxious avoidant attachment — the child appeared calm and independent when mom left, didn't go to her for comfort when she returned, but physiological measures (heart rate) showed the child was internally distressed the whole time.
  • Disorganized/insecure attachment — the child was inconsolable both when mom left and when she returned.

The two patterns worth understanding in depth are the anxious ambivalent and the anxious avoidant, because they show up constantly in adult relationships.

How These Patterns Form: Emotional Mirroring

To understand why attachment styles develop, you need to understand emotional mirroring. Young children don't arrive knowing what they feel. They learn it from the adults around them.

The classic example: a toddler trips and falls. Before crying, the toddler looks around. If the adults panic — "Oh my god, are you okay?!" — the toddler cries. If the adults laugh, the toddler gets up and moves on. The adults' emotional response is literally how the child learns what the experience means.

When that mirroring is inconsistent — sometimes warm and present, sometimes cold and dismissive — the child grows up genuinely confused about what they're feeling and why. When that mirroring is absent or rebuffed — the child reaches out and is essentially told to shut down — the child learns to stop reaching out at all.

This is further compounded by how boys are socialized. "Man up." "Boys don't cry." "Stop being so sensitive." These aren't just cultural clichés — they actively interrupt the emotional mirroring process. A boy who is punished or mocked for expressing emotion stops communicating about what he feels, which means he also stops learning what he feels. Over time, the ability to identify his own internal emotional state becomes genuinely stunted. This is called alexithymia — difficulty identifying and describing emotions — and it's far more common in men.

Anxious Ambivalent Attachment: The Mixed Signals Pattern

This style develops from inconsistent caregiving. Sometimes the caregiver is warm and responsive; other times they're cold or unavailable. The child never knows what to expect, so they develop a contradictory internal strategy: reach out for connection, but also protect yourself from the hurt of not getting it.

In adults, this looks like:

  • Feeling angry, frustrated, or upset — but also feeling stupid for feeling that way
  • Being unable to articulate what's actually wrong, even when something clearly is
  • Saying "I'm fine" or "nothing's wrong" when clearly something is
  • Moodiness that lingers for days without explanation
  • Getting ticked off by things that seem small or trivial to others

The underlying dynamic is a war between two drives: I want to connect and connecting means risking being hurt again. The result is ambivalence — perpetually sitting on the fence. Interestingly, this same on-the-fence quality is linked to low intrinsic motivation in life more broadly. The same logic applies: I want to go for that job, but what if I get rejected? The protective withdrawal from emotional risk bleeds into other domains.

Anxious Avoidant Attachment: The Emotional Robot Pattern

This style develops when a child reaches out emotionally and receives no response at all — not inconsistency, just a wall. The clearest example: a seven-year-old goes to his dad upset about being bullied, and dad says "man up, push back." The message received isn't just "your feelings don't matter right now" — it's "expressing feelings gets you nothing, so stop."

The child's solution is adaptive but costly: build your own wall first. Become fiercely independent. Don't need anyone.

In adults, this looks like:

  • Feeling emotionally numb or cold — like a robot
  • Maintaining a "safe zone" in relationships: not too close, not too far
  • Pushing people away when they get intimate, but not wanting them to leave entirely
  • Appearing totally fine on the outside while being genuinely distressed internally

That last point was confirmed directly by the Strange Situation research. Children who appeared completely unbothered by their mother leaving actually had significantly elevated heart rates. The calm was a facade. The distress was physiological and real.

The avoidant person isn't afraid of commitment in some abstract way — they're afraid of trusting, because trusting led to being shut down. So they send tiny, almost imperceptible signals to a partner — can I trust you? do you see me? — and when those signals aren't noticed (because they're too subtle), the rejection confirms: don't bother.

"The more you get rejected, the more independent you become, the more you stay in that safe zone."

Your Attachment Style Can Change

Early research suggested attachment style was set in childhood. More recent work tells a more hopeful story. Yes, your starting point is shaped by how you were raised — but in adulthood, what matters more are selection effects: the people you actively choose to spend time with.

"Foundations are not fake... but the further you go into the game, the more of a choice you have about how you play."

Your parents gave you your starting gear. The people you surround yourself with now determine how your character develops from here.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking someone needs to change their behavior to fix this. They don't. Your partner doesn't have to cancel plans or rearrange their life. The healing mechanism is emotional mirroring, not behavioral correction. What needs to change is whether you acknowledge and share the need — not whether the need gets perfectly met.

Waiting until you have your feelings fully figured out before communicating them. This is the trap. People hold back because they think "what's my partner supposed to do with half-baked confusion?" But sharing the confusion is the point. The two of you figuring it out together is what emotional mirroring actually looks like.

Judging yourself for having the emotional need in the first place. The self-judgment — I shouldn't feel this way, this is stupid, I need to grow up — is itself a symptom, not a rational assessment. Calling your need stupid is how you avoid acknowledging it. And acknowledging it is the whole job.

Assuming this is permanent or biological. Neither anxious ambivalent nor anxious avoidant attachment is a fixed personality trait. They're learned patterns, and they can be unlearned through consistent new experiences of emotional safety.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your pattern. Do you feel internal turmoil and mixed signals (anxious ambivalent) or emotional numbness and fierce independence (anxious avoidant)? Naming it clearly is the first step.

  2. Practice the half-baked share. The next time something feels off — you're moody, irritable, or vaguely hurt — don't wait until you have a polished explanation. Say exactly where you are: "Something feels wrong and I can't tell what it is. I'm upset and I feel stupid for being upset." That's it. That's enough.

  3. Acknowledge your emotional need without requiring it to be rational. Before bringing it to someone else, spend a moment just letting the need exist. You felt lonely last weekend. You wanted to have brunch with your partner. That's a real need. You don't have to justify it or argue for it — just acknowledge it to yourself first.

  4. Audit who you spend time with. Are the people around you capable of sitting with your emotional messiness without dismissing it? You don't need a therapist for a best friend — but you do need people who won't respond to vulnerability with "man up." The company you keep is literally reshaping your attachment style right now.

  5. If you're the partner of someone like this: when they say "I'm fine" and clearly aren't, try: "I want to help but I don't know how. If something occurs to you, please let me know." You're not required to fix anything. You just need to stay in the room and not make them feel stupid for having the need.

  6. Replace correction with mirroring. When your partner shares something emotionally confused or seemingly irrational, resist the urge to solve it. Instead, reflect it back: "It sounds like you felt really lonely that weekend — that makes sense. Can we figure out how to make next weekend feel different?" The acknowledgment is the medicine.
