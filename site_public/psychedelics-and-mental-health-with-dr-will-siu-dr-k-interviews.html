Summary

TL;DR — Psychedelics work not by chemically fixing broken brains, but by creating the conditions for emotional catharsis that ordinary life and conventional psychiatry rarely provide. Understanding why they work matters far more than simply taking them.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between two psychiatrists — one who came up through traditional academic medicine and found his way to psychedelic-assisted therapy through personal crisis, and one whose practice centers on meditation, Eastern psychology, and emotional processing. Together they trace a coherent theory of how mental suffering forms, why conventional treatments fall short, and how psychedelics — used carefully — can accelerate the healing that the human system is already capable of doing on its own.

How Mental Illness Actually Forms

Most psychiatric training frames conditions like depression, anxiety, and phobia as brain diseases to be managed with medication. A more useful frame: they are the body's adaptive responses to unprocessed emotional experience.

When something painful happens — a parent's cancer diagnosis, an embarrassing failure, a moment of terror — the healthy response is full physical expression of the emotion. Think of a bird that flies into a window: it falls, shakes, chirps, and goes back to hunting worms. It doesn't spend the next two years worrying about glass.

Human culture interrupts that process. Sadness, fear, and shame get labeled as weakness, especially for men. So the emotion never fully moves through. It gets stored in the body, and the psyche tries to put it somewhere. Depending on genetics and life history, that "somewhere" might look like a phobia, an eating disorder, addiction, or PTSD.

"The psyche, the unconscious, tries to put that somewhere. And that somewhere, depending on your genetics and your upbringing, sometimes looks like alcoholism, sometimes an eating disorder, sometimes a phobia."

This reframe has a practical implication: the category of diagnosis matters less than the underlying dynamic. A flying phobia and generalized anxiety and compulsive overeating may all be the same unprocessed grief wearing different clothes.

Why Conventional Psychiatry Often Misses

Look at the language of psychiatric medications: antidepressants, antianxiety. The prefix is the tell. The entire framework is suppressive — damp down what you're feeling so it doesn't interfere with functioning.

"Antidepressants, anti-anxiety — what do those say? Anti-sadness, anti-fear. If you're feeling something, numb it down."

This isn't just a philosophical critique. It has a practical consequence: if emotions are signals pointing you back toward what actually matters to you, suppressing them means you stop receiving the signal. The GPS keeps sending recalculation alerts; you keep turning off the volume.

Psychotherapy can fall into the same trap. Years of talk therapy that carefully avoids the emotional body can produce intellectual insight without the felt-sense release that actually changes things.

What Psychedelics Actually Do

Psychedelics are evocative rather than suppressive. They don't create new brain chemistry — they unlock what's already there, the way any other experience (meditation, breathwork, even a meaningful conversation) can. The distinction is speed and depth of access.

The proposed mechanism isn't serotonin correction. It's cathartic emotional processing — specifically, the convergence of three things at once:

  1. Emotional memory — the felt-body experience of something that happened
  2. Narrative memory — the cognitive story connected to that experience
  3. An empathic setting — the presence of someone (or something) that communicates I understand what this cost you

When all three align, the stored material can finally move. This is why you see people describe releasing something they'd been carrying for twenty years after a single session.

"When those three things happen, that's when tears come, when the body can shake, when the fear leaves the body."

And it's why set, setting, and dose matter so much — they're not safety protocols bolted onto a chemical reaction. They're the actual mechanism.

The Spectrum of Psychedelics

Not all psychedelics do the same thing, and understanding the differences matters:

  • MDMA — Often described as a "heart opener." Builds trust, reduces fear response, and stays on the plane of ordinary reality (this life, these relationships, this body). Most studied for PTSD. A relatively approachable entry point.
  • Psilocybin / LSD — In the middle of the spectrum. Can produce transpersonal experiences at higher doses, but at moderate doses they can ride what's called the liminal space — the productive edge where you have one foot in ordinary self-awareness and one foot in something larger. LSD's 10–12 hour duration makes it logistically impractical for clinical settings.
  • Ketamine — The only currently legal option outside clinical trials in the US. Works via dissociation. Dose matters enormously; high doses used in anesthesia are not therapeutic. The power is in careful dosing that keeps a person in that liminal space, not under it.
  • Ayahuasca — A plant-based brew from Amazonian traditions. Highly transpersonal; people commonly report experiences of past lives, death-and-rebirth, or contact with entities. Profound but not necessarily the right starting point, especially for someone from a conservative religious background or with no prior therapeutic foundation.
  • DMT / 5-MeO-DMT — The most intense. The smoked forms can produce a complete dissolution of ordinary consciousness within seconds. Extraordinary, but the very intensity makes therapeutic integration difficult. Going fully "out" and coming back doesn't necessarily give you anything to work with in daily life.

"I am actually much more of an advocate for lower doses. People pull toward psychedelics because of the stories — I took ayahuasca and saw this panther that turned into my grandmother. And I'm like, okay, but how does that help you on a daily basis?"

The Three Levels of Healing

Healing isn't only cognitive. There are three domains that need to come into alignment, each progressively harder to work with:

  • Mind — Understanding what's happening, naming patterns, identifying true values versus inherited ones. Essential but insufficient on its own.
  • Voice — Actually expressing what you feel and want. You can understand intellectually that you're in an abusive relationship for years before you can say "I'm leaving." The move from insight to spoken self-expression is its own distinct step.
  • Body — Living physically in alignment with what you actually value. Are you spending your time doing things you care about? Are you moving through the world in a way that reflects who you are? This is the hardest level because it's no longer internal — it's out in the actual world with real consequences.

True Values vs. Perceived Values

A lot of suffering comes from chasing what culture, family, religion, or capitalism has told you to want — degrees, prestige, productivity — while suppressing the quieter signal of what you actually want. These perceived values can become so ingrained they feel like your own.

The heart, in this framing, is like a GPS installed at birth. It doesn't malfunction. The mind, voice, and body can drift from it, but the signal keeps broadcasting. What we call painful emotions — shame, fear, sadness — are that GPS saying recalculate. They're not signs that something is wrong with you. They're signs that you've drifted off course.

Common Pitfalls

Psychedelics as a magic bullet. The emails keep coming from people who did an ayahuasca retreat and feel significantly worse. A powerful experience without preparation, integration, and ongoing support is not healing — it's just a powerful experience.

Confusing the experience with the work. The transpersonal stories are compelling. But the measure of a psychedelic experience isn't how dramatic it was. It's whether it changes how you live on Tuesday afternoon.

Taking without adequate support. This applies both to set/setting (who is with you, where, what's the environment) and to aftercare. People can and do develop lasting anxiety and PTSD from difficult, unsupported trips.

Assuming the healer has done their own work. There are a lot of wounded healers — people who entered mental health fields because they're struggling and haven't actually worked through their own material. A healer can only take you as far as they've gone themselves. Credentials are not a guarantee.

Suppression framed as treatment. If you go on medication specifically to stop feeling what you're feeling, and the thing you were feeling was a signal worth following, you've just turned off the GPS. This doesn't mean medication is never appropriate — but the goal shouldn't be numbness.

The narrative "I don't know what I want." This often functions as emotional protection. The more painful (and more honest) position is: I know what I want, and I'm not doing it yet. From that position, change becomes possible. From "I don't know," it doesn't.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the emotion, not just the thought. When anxiety or shame comes up, try to locate it in your body before trying to analyze it. Where do you feel it physically? This is the first step in building a connection to the signal rather than immediately trying to suppress it.

  2. Distinguish your true values from your perceived values. Write down three to five things you believe you "should" want or be doing. Then write down three to five things that have actually brought you joy or a sense of rightness, even briefly. Notice the gap.

  3. Apply the catharsis equation to your own life. Think of something you've been carrying — a past hurt, a fear, a shame. Ask yourself: have you ever felt it fully in your body and told the story and had someone who actually understood? If not, that's not a personal failing — that's the equation being incomplete.

  4. Use your shame as a compass, not a verdict. The next time you feel shame, instead of suppressing or spiraling, ask: what is this pointing me toward? Make one small behavioral adjustment in that direction. That's healthy shame doing its job.

  5. If you're considering psychedelics, honestly assess set, setting, and dose. Set: Where are you emotionally right now? Are you in crisis, or are you in a stable enough place to do difficult work? Setting: Who will be with you? Can you trust them? Is the environment safe and quiet? Dose: Start small. The liminal space — where you have access to both ordinary awareness and something deeper — is more therapeutically useful than being fully overwhelmed.

  6. Look for the integration work, not just the experience. If you've had a meaningful psychedelic experience (or any peak experience — meditation, therapy, a meaningful conversation) and haven't done the work of translating it into daily life, that's where the actual healing happens. What did it show you? What would have to change for your life to reflect that?

  7. Find or build an empathic container. This doesn't have to be a licensed therapist. It can be a small, trusted group of friends willing to sit with difficult emotions without immediately trying to fix or minimize them. The catharsis equation requires that third element — someone who genuinely gets it.
