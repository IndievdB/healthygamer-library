Three States of Mind

Think of your mind as existing on a spectrum with three broad zones:

  • Tranquil mind — thoughts are slow, relatively focused, and emotionally quiet. This is the baseline you're trying to return to.
  • Active mind — thoughts speed up, hop between topics, and carry some emotional charge. A normal, engaged state.
  • Anxious mind — thoughts race, jump around a central worry, and carry heavy emotion. Consequences feel visceral and immediate.

The direction of travel matters. Energy gets added to the mind through worry, ruminationRumination is the mind's tendency to replay negative thoughts or painful events over and over — not to solve them, but in a way that makes them worse.Full definition →, and — crucially — fighting against the anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → itself. Every additional mental effort pushes you further from tranquility, not closer.

The Two Arrows

This pattern was recognized thousands of years ago in Buddhist thought through a concept called the two arrows.

You can't always control whether that arrow hits you.
  • The second arrow is what your own mind does in response. You shoot it at yourself by fighting, resisting, or beating yourself up for being in the state you're in.

The first arrow hurts. The second arrow is optional, and it's the one that does most of the lasting damage.

This same pattern shows up across mental health struggles: - Getting depressed, and then beating yourself up for being depressed again - Having an anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → spike, and then berating yourself for not being able to control it - Relapsing after sobriety, and then spiraling into shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → — which, in turn, drives the relapse further

The second arrow doesn't come from weakness. It comes from a very natural, very human impulse to fix the problem by fighting harder. The insight here is that the fighting is the problem.

The Way Out: Channel Your Inner Coach

If adding tension makes things worse, the move is to deliberately reduce it — and you do that through the way you talk to yourself.

The key shift is from adversarial to compassionate self-coaching. Think about what actually works in a sports locker room at halftime, when the team is losing. The coaches who get results don't berate their players. They say something closer to: "I know things aren't going our way right now, but I believe in you. We've made mistakes, but the game isn't over."

That's the tone you need to bring to your own anxious mind.

Mistaking self-compassionTreating yourself with the same basic understanding you'd extend to another person who made a mistake — instead of turning anger inward and assigning blame.Full definition → for giving up. Trying to force tranquility. Telling yourself to "just calm down" or "stop being anxious" is still fighting — it still adds tension. The goal isn't to command your mind into a different state; it's to stop escalating it.

Expecting one round of self-coaching to work instantly. This is a skill, not a switch. The first time you try to talk to yourself like a coach, it may feel awkward or unconvincing. That's normal. The practice is in returning to it consistently.
