Summary

TL;DR — A panic attack is a symptom, not a diagnosis, and the thing that truly ruins your life isn't the panic attack itself — it's how you adapt to it.

Panic attacks are widely misunderstood, partly because they feel so physical. People assume their problem is mental, get confused when they don't feel particularly anxious, and end up either avoiding treatment or getting the wrong kind. Understanding what a panic attack actually is — and what to do immediately after one — matters a lot for getting your life back.

Panic Attacks Are Physical, Not What Most People Expect

The primary experience of a panic attack is physiological. Your heart pounds, you can't breathe, you feel nauseous, you shake, you sweat. And then, because every alarm in your body is going off at once, your brain draws the most reasonable conclusion it can: I'm dying.

That sense of impending doom isn't just dramatic — it's a core feature of the experience. If you had a panic attack and someone asked whether you genuinely felt like the world was ending or you were about to die, the answer would be yes. That's what distinguishes a true panic attack from general anxiety.

One working theory for why this happens involves a suffocation reflex — a hard-wired survival mechanism that kicks in when you're choking or drowning. The hypothesis is that in panic disorder, this reflex activates inappropriately. You're not actually suffocating, but your brain fires off the same cascade anyway: the amygdala (your fear center) activates, adrenaline floods your system, your CO₂ levels shift, your breathing becomes disrupted, and the whole thing snowballs. The "I'm dying" thought isn't a delusion — it's what your body is screaming at your brain.

This also explains why people say it doesn't feel mental — because in an important sense, it isn't. The origin may be a misfiring survival reflex, not a deep psychological fear.

A Panic Attack Is a Symptom, Not a Diagnosis

Think of it like a fever. Fever can come from strep throat, pneumonia, COVID, cancer — many different causes. Similarly, a panic attack can come from panic disorder, but it can also come from:

  • Cardiac arrhythmias (irregular electrical activity in the heart)
  • Hyperthyroidism (an overactive thyroid mimics many panic symptoms)
  • Other medical conditions

This is why going straight to a therapist after a first panic attack isn't always the right call. A therapist can't order labs or read an EKG. If there's a fixable medical cause — and there sometimes is — treating that cause can make the panic attacks disappear entirely. No therapy, no medication, just fixing what was actually broken.

The Real Problem: What You Do After the Panic Attack

The diagnostic criteria for panic disorder include not just the attacks themselves but either a persistent fear of future panic attacks or significant changes in behavior to avoid them. That second part is what actually disables people.

What really impairs our function is not the original issue — it's the way that we adapt to the issue.

Agoraphobia — rarely leaving home out of fear of having an attack in public — is the extreme version. But subtler adaptations are far more common. People quietly restructure their entire lives:

  • Only going to restaurants with three or more bathroom stalls (so there's always a stall free if they need to hide during an attack)
  • Planning every outing around escape routes
  • Avoiding movies, concerts, workplaces, or anywhere they can't easily leave

The panic attack happened once or twice. The behavioral reorganization lasts for years. That's the actual disability.

This pattern shows up in other areas of life too — someone has a bad experience in a relationship, then generalizes it into rules about an entire gender or ethnicity. The original event was bad enough. The maladaptation makes it permanently limiting.

What to Do: A Practical Sequence

During a first panic attack:
Call emergency services or go to the ER. This isn't overreacting — it's appropriate. You cannot be expected to distinguish a panic attack from a cardiac arrhythmia while it's happening. Emergency services exist for situations where you don't know yet whether something is going to kill you.

After the panic attack passes:
See a medical doctor, not a therapist first. Get your thyroid checked. Get a cardiac evaluation if warranted. Rule out medical causes before assuming it's psychological.

For ongoing panic disorder:
Psychotherapy and SSRIs/SNRIs (selective serotonin or serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors) are comparably effective — the research suggests roughly similar effect sizes for both. Benzodiazepines like Xanax or Klonopin can help manage an individual attack but are habit-forming and not a good long-term solution. They're often still prescribed too readily.

Therapy is especially powerful for the adaptation problem. Once a medical cause has been ruled out, a therapist can help you notice and gradually undo the maladaptations — the contingency plans, the avoidances, the fear of the next attack. That's where psychotherapy does some of its best work.

Therapy can also interrupt the interpretation cycle — the moment when physical signals get read as I'm dying. Learning to recognize "this is my suffocation reflex misfiring, not a real emergency" doesn't cure the reflex, but it can break the feedback loop that makes the experience worse.

What About Breathing Techniques?

Breathing is often the first thing people reach for, and the mental image of panic attacks usually involves someone breathing into a paper bag. The reality is more complicated.

The research on breath training for panic attacks shows mixed results — not clearly helpful, not clearly useless, just inconsistent. Part of the problem is that "breath training" in studies lumps wildly different techniques together. Something like bellows breath or breath of fire (from yogic traditions) works by intentionally hyperventilating to drop CO₂ levels, which then triggers a physiological slowdown in breathing rate — which can be deeply calming. That's mechanically very different from slow deep breathing, which doesn't always help and sometimes makes nausea worse.

The lack of technical specificity in the research means you can't confidently say "breathing helps" or "breathing doesn't help" — it depends heavily on how it's done. Mindfulness and meditation do show some benefit for panic disorder, but neither should replace medical evaluation or evidence-based treatment.

A long exhalation is generally what correlates with relaxation — when you focus on breathing out, you slow the exhale down, and that's likely what's actually calming you.

Common Pitfalls

  • Going straight to therapy without a medical eval. A good therapist will refer you to a doctor anyway, but don't assume that because it feels mental it is mental. Rule out physical causes first.
  • Treating benzodiazepines as the solution. They work in the moment but create dependence and don't address the underlying disorder. SSRIs are first-line.
  • Confusing adaptation with recovery. Building elaborate avoidance plans — always knowing where the exits are, limiting yourself to "safe" places — feels like coping but is actually the disorder spreading. Reducing the panic attack's grip on your life, not accommodating it, is the goal.
  • Dismissing physical symptoms as "just anxiety." They are real physical symptoms. Validating that doesn't mean catastrophizing — it means understanding what's actually happening so you can address it correctly.
  • Giving up on therapy because the panic attacks themselves haven't stopped. Therapy works best on the behavioral and cognitive layer — the adaptations, the interpretation cycle — not necessarily on the reflex itself.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you're having a panic attack right now and have never had one before: Call emergency services or go to an emergency room. Don't try to manage it alone at home.

  2. After any panic attack, schedule a medical appointment. Ask your doctor to check for cardiac arrhythmias, thyroid function, and other physical causes. Bring a description of exactly what happened — when it started, how long it lasted, what the physical symptoms were.

  3. Map your adaptations. Write down all the ways you've changed your behavior since your first panic attack. What places do you avoid? What contingency plans have you built? This list is what therapy needs to work through — and seeing it written out is often revealing.

  4. If medical causes have been ruled out, seek both therapy and a psychiatric evaluation. You don't have to choose one over the other. They're comparably effective and can be done simultaneously.

  5. Experiment with breath techniques — carefully. If you want to try breathing exercises, look into techniques that involve a longer exhale than inhale, or structured practices like cardiac coherence breathing. Notice whether a given technique calms you or makes symptoms worse. If it worsens nausea or anxiety, it's not the right technique for you.

  6. If you already have agoraphobia or significant behavioral avoidance: Bring the adaptation map from step 3 to a therapist. Exposure-based therapy (gradually re-engaging with avoided situations) is specifically designed for this and is where professional support really earns its place.
