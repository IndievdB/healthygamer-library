Summary

TL;DR — When nothing seems to work, stop analyzing your failures and start studying your successes. The solution to your problem already exists in your own life — you just haven't looked at it yet.

Most people who feel stuck in life share a particular habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition →: they focus relentlessly on what's going wrong. They catalog their failures, hunt for new solutions, and try the next supplement, technique, or expert recommendation. The problem isn't effort — it's direction. The Vietnam Story That Changes the Framework

Two physicians were sent to rural Vietnam to fix a severe malnourishment problem — over 50% of children in the region weren't getting adequate nutrition. When they arrived, the promised food aid had fallen through. No money, no supplies, just a van and a translator.

Instead of giving up, they did something counterintuitive. They drove around and noticed something strange: if everyone in the region was equally poor, equally dependent on rice farming, with equal access to food — why wasn't malnourishment affecting 100% of children? Half the kids were fine. Why?

They stopped studying the problem and started studying the exception.

What they found was that the parents of healthy children had two small behavioral differences. While working in the rice paddies, they'd collect tiny shrimp and clams from the water using their aprons. They also ate certain weeds and leafy greens that others considered "poor people's food" and discarded. These two habits — available to everyone, costing nothing — were the entire difference.

The researchers didn't bring in food aid. They simply documented what was already working and helped other families duplicate it. They fixed the malnourishment problem without spending a single dollar on food.

"When there's a problem, you don't actually have to go looking for a solution. A solution already exists, and all you have to do is duplicate it."

Why This Applies to Your Personal Problems

Here's the key insight: you almost certainly already have your own version of the healthy Vietnamese families. There are days when you studied well. Days when you exercised. Maybe it's one day in fifteen. Maybe less. That doesn't matter.

The mistake is spending all your attention on the fourteen bad days — asking "why do I keep failing?" — while ignoring the one good day where something actually worked.

Positive devianceThe practice of solving problems by studying and duplicating your own past successes rather than analyzing your failures or importing someone else's solutions.Full definition → flips the question entirely. Instead of asking what's wrong with me, you ask what was right that one time?

This is more reliable than any external solution because it's already calibrated to you. No expert, no study, no hack can account for your specific genetics, history, physiology, and circumstances. But your own successes already have.

How to Actually Apply Positive Deviance

This is a practical, iterative process. Here's how to work through it:

Step 1: Identify a genuine success, however rare. Pick a specific instance when the problem you're struggling with was not a problem. Don't look for a perfect day — just one real example. A morning you exercised. A day you focused. A conversation where you felt confident. If you think you've never had one, look harder — you almost certainly have.

Step 2: Do a qualitative analysis of the 24–48 hours before that success. The goal isn't to immediately find the answer. It's to generate hypotheses about what conditions allowed you to succeed.

Step 3: Shift your target. This is where the real insight lands. Suppose you want to exercise in the mornings but keep failing. Positive devianceThe practice of solving problems by studying and duplicating your own past successes rather than analyzing your failures or importing someone else's solutions.Full definition → might reveal that you exercise on days when you didn't stay up late on Discord. The target isn't "exercise tomorrow" — the target is "don't open Discord after 8pm tonight." That's a much more tractable problem, and it's the actual upstream cause.

"It shifts our target."

Step 4: Test, iterate, pay attention going forward. Try to recreate the conditions. See if your success rate improves even slightly. If it does, ask again: what worked this time? What can you refine? If it doesn't work, go back and look for what you might have missed. This is an iterative process, not a one-time fix.

Step 5: Actively observe your successes as they happen. Going forward, when you have a good day, don't just feel relieved and move on. Pay attention. Take mental notes. Ask yourself what conditions are present right now. Build a running understanding of what your personal "healthy family in the rice paddy" looks like.

Common Pitfalls

Believing you have no successes to study. Almost no one is failing 100% of the time. The pull toward believing you're a "unique case" who has tried everything is strong — but it usually means you've been looking outward for solutions while ignoring the internal evidence you already have.

Continuing to focus on the failure. The instinct when something goes wrong is to analyze the failure. That's natural but counterproductive here. If you slept badly last night, don't spend energy figuring out why you slept badly. Figure out what was different on the night you slept well.

Expecting one insight to solve everything permanently. Positive devianceThe practice of solving problems by studying and duplicating your own past successes rather than analyzing your failures or importing someone else's solutions.Full definition → is iterative. You find one upstream factor, adjust, improve somewhat, then look again. It's not a single revelation — it's a practice of paying attention over time.

Looking for big dramatic differences. The Vietnamese families weren't doing anything extraordinary. They were doing two small, cheap things their neighbors weren't. Your own positive devianceThe practice of solving problems by studying and duplicating your own past successes rather than analyzing your failures or importing someone else's solutions.Full definition → is probably similarly quiet and unglamorous. Don't dismiss factors because they seem too small or too obvious.

Treating external solutions as inherently more valid than internal ones. There's a tendency to trust a new app, supplement, or technique more than the evidence of your own life. External solutions are averages. Your successes are specific to you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name one success. Right now, identify a single specific instance in the past month when a problem you're currently struggling with was not a problem. Write it down — day, context, and anything you remember about how it went.

  2. Reconstruct the 24 hours before that success. Write down everything you can remember: sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition →, food, social interactions, mental state, what you did the evening before, stress levels. Don't try to find the answer yet — just document everything you can.

  3. Generate three hypotheses. Based on what you wrote down, list three things that might have contributed to that success. Frame them as conditions, not character traits. (Not "I was disciplined" — instead, "I hadn't been on my phone before bed.")

  4. Identify the upstream target. For the hypothesis that seems most plausible, find the specific behavior or condition you could try to recreate. Write it as a concrete, observable action ("be off Discord by 9pm" rather than "use my phone less").

  5. Try to replicate it once. Don't aim for a habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → yet. Just try to create those conditions one more time and observe whether your success rate goes up even a little.

  6. Start logging successes going forward. Keep a simple running note — physical or digital — where you record good days and what surrounded them. Over time this builds a personal map of what actually works for you, not what works for the average person in a study.
