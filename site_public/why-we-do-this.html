What Coaching Is (and Isn't)

The person in this story is clear: coaching is not therapy. That distinction matters because people either expect too much from coaching or dismiss it entirely.

What coaching actually provided for this person was a safe, unbiased space to vent and dig deeper — a place to start understanding what internal patterns were keeping them stuck. The questions a coach asks aren't clinical diagnoses. They're prompts to help you see yourself more clearly: What is it within me that perpetuates this cycle?

That self-understanding is how you figure out how to break out of something. You can't interrupt a pattern you can't see.

Meanwhile, therapy handles things coaching genuinely can't. Some struggles — deeper traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, medication management, clinical mental health conditions — require a licensed professional. One of the actual goals of coaching is to help people get into therapy when they need it, because stigma and uncertainty ("What if they're not a good fit? I have so much to say") create real barriers that coaching can lower.

The two aren't in competition. They serve different purposes, and in some cases you need both.

The Work Behind the Story

It would be easy to read this person's outcome — sober, employed, social, back in school — and think: I'll sign up and that'll happen to me.

But there's something the story leaves out: all the work they put in.

"If you come into coaching, it's not going to fix you. It's going to help you fix yourself."

If you show up to sessions expecting to be coached into sobriety and success while changing nothing else — while still not willing to give up weed, not willing to move, not willing to do anything differently — it doesn't work. A coach can help you. A coach cannot do this for you.

What coaching can genuinely help with is something most people who are stuck actually need: knowing how to start. When your life is tangled up — habits, substances, isolation, no job, no direction — it's hard to know which thread to pull first. Coaching helps you untangle it. The untangling still requires your hands.

Realistic Timelines

Progress isn't instant, but it also isn't as far away as it feels.

  • 2–3 months: You start to see some real changes. Early outcome data backs this up.
  • 6–18 months: You really start to get going. Things begin to feel meaningfully different.
  • 2+ years: Putting a life together takes this long. That's not a failure — that's just how it works.

This person's two-year arc is realistic, not exceptional in its speed. What's exceptional is how much they actually changed — and that came from genuine commitment.

"Your mileage may vary. This person really put their life together. That's exactly why we're here."

It's worth holding both things at once: this kind of transformation is possible, and it's in the minority because most people don't put in this level of work. That's not a judgment — it's just honest.

The Goal Is to Not Need It Anymore

One of the clearest things worth understanding about the coaching model: the primary goal is for people to eventually not need coaching.

This isn't about building dependency on a program or a coach. It's about equipping you with a skill set and a level of self-understanding that you carry with you after it ends. The endpoint is you being able to handle your own life — not you needing check-ins forever.

That reframe matters. When you engage with coaching (or therapy, or any serious personal growth work), you're not outsourcing your development. You're borrowing scaffolding while you build something that stands on its own.

The Cost Question

There's a practical note worth addressing directly: money is a real barrier for some people, but not for as many as claim it.

Therapy is expensive. That's real. Coaching is designed to be more accessible precisely because cost keeps people from getting help. But the person in this story points out something honest — they were paying for weed every month. If you're spending money on something that's making things worse, the money isn't actually the obstacle.

For people who genuinely can't afford help, that's a real problem worth solving separately. But for people who are spending on habits and telling themselves they can't afford to improve — that's worth being honest about.

Common Pitfalls

Expecting coaching or therapy to do the work for you. No program, coach, or clinician can want recovery on your behalf. Showing up is necessary but not sufficient.

Treating coaching and therapy as interchangeable or competing. They're complementary tools for different things. Using one doesn't mean you don't need the other.

Giving up before the timeline plays out. If you expect dramatic change in two weeks and don't get it, it's easy to conclude "this doesn't work." Two to three months is when early changes typically show up. Two years is what a real turnaround looks like.

Reading success stories as guarantees. This person's outcome is genuinely impressive — and described explicitly as being in the minority. It's evidence of what's possible, not a promise of what's automatic.

Conflating venting with the full process. The person in this story described their sessions as "a place to vent" — but the coaching was doing more than that. The questions asked during those sessions were driving real self-examination. If you only vent and never sit with what comes up, you're using the space but not the process.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Be honest about your actual barriers. Write down what's stopping you from getting help — money, fear of a bad fit, not knowing where to start. Then ask: which of these are real obstacles and which are avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →? The answer changes what to do next.

  2. If you're considering coaching, enter with a specific question or pattern. "What holds me back?" is a better starting point than "fix me." Identify one recurring cycle in your life you don't understand and bring that as your starting point.

  3. If coaching hasn't led to therapy yet but you suspect you need it, name that. The fact that you're not sure if you need a therapist, or you're nervous about the cost or fit, is itself worth raising with a coach. That's part of what the process is for.

  4. Track early progress in small units. Don't wait for your life to be "put together" to decide whether things are working. Look for concrete, small changes at two to three months — a habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → slightly more consistent, one relationship slightly less strained, one pattern you can now see even if you haven't broken it yet.

  5. Set an honest commitment horizon. Before starting, decide that you'll give the process at least six months of real effort. Going in with a two-week test-and-quit window almost guarantees you won't see meaningful results — not because the process doesn't work, but because you've set a timeline too short for the work to show up.
