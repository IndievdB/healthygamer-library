Summary

TL;DR — You're probably not spoiled, but your expectations about what work is supposed to feel like may be unrealistic. The question isn't "passion vs. stability" — it's how to stop thinking in binaries and start crafting a career deliberately.

Feeling stuck between a safe job you don't love and a passion that feels financially risky is one of the most common career dilemmas of your 20s. Before making a drastic move, it's worth examining three things: whether your current frustrations are temporary rather than structural, where your idea of what work should feel like actually came from, and whether "engineering vs. fitness" is even the right question to be asking.

Temporary Factors Are Driving More Decisions Than You Realize

When you feel the urge to blow up your career, the first question to ask isn't what should I do with my life — it's why is this question so urgent right now?

Look at what's actually making the current situation painful: a mentor who isn't helpful, a steep learning curve, anxiety about not keeping up, feeling like you don't belong. These are real problems, but they're also largely situational. A different company, a different team, a better mentor — any of those things could change the picture entirely.

This matters because making a permanent decision to fix a temporary problem is a very expensive mistake. If those same frustrations show up again in the next role, in a different industry, that's useful signal. If they don't, you've just learned that the problem was the context, not the career.

"Try to fix all of the things that are wrong in your job first before you switch careers."

The pattern of leaving after three months, then seven months, then another seven months — combined with earlier examples like quitting piano after third grade, dropping Japanese after losing interest, and stopping pursuit of someone who was actually receptive — suggests something worth sitting with honestly. It's not that any individual exit was wrong. It's that the exits keep happening at roughly the same point: when the initial novelty is gone and the actual work begins.

The Myth of the Career You're "Supposed To" Love

Society has built a very specific fantasy about what a fulfilling career looks like: you wake up energized, you grind because you love it, passion drowns out everything else. That image comes from movies, social media, and hustle culture — not from how most people actually experience work.

Consider what lawyer movies look like versus what being a lawyer actually is. The movie is 50% dramatic courtroom scenes. The reality is 99% reading documents. Medical dramas show ticking-time-bomb surgeries. Most of medicine is typing notes and calling pharmacies. The same gap exists in almost every field.

"Even I can say this — I really enjoy what I do, and I don't think you guys see this, but I don't enjoy what I do most days. Most of my day is taken up with stuff I find absolutely exhausting and unenjoyable."

That's not a complaint — it's a description of reality even in a genuinely fulfilling career. The stuff that feels tedious or draining doesn't mean you're in the wrong place. It's often exactly the work that generates real value.

The physics you loved in high school felt exciting because it was conceptual and exploratory. The "technical boring stuff" that killed that excitement is how you actually build things that matter in the world. The boring math is what gets a spacecraft off the ground. The pharmacology flashcards are what keeps patients safe. The unglamorous parts of a job aren't a sign that you've made the wrong choice — they're usually the actual substance of the work.

What Work Is Actually For

Stripping away the mythology, work is fundamentally about generating value. Two specialists each doing their one thing produce more than two generalists each doing both things — the blacksmith and the farmer are more productive in combination than either would be alone. That's the basic engine underneath every career.

This reframe matters because enjoyment and value generation are not the same thing. You can generate enormous value doing work that isn't fun every day. And you can have fun doing something that generates very little value. Expecting the two to perfectly overlap, all the time, is the expectation that gets people into trouble.

"Spoiled is more about expectations than anything else. You have this idea that it's supposed to be this way. Stop looking for that unicorn — or maybe a better way to put it is: the unicorn is not found, it's crafted."

The "Either/Or" Trap

The biggest reframe available here is rejecting the premise that you have to choose between engineering and fitness. That binary is a cognitive shortcut, not a real constraint.

Think about it from a hiring perspective. A fitness startup needs engineers. They could hire a technically competent engineer who doesn't care about their product at all, or they could hire someone who is a solid engineer and a genuine fitness enthusiast who actually cares what they're building. The second person is more valuable precisely because of the combination — not despite it.

Your highest leverage in the job market isn't going to come from becoming a personal trainer who left engineering, or from staying in engineering and abandoning fitness. It's likely to come from being the engineer who genuinely understands and cares about the fitness industry. That intersection is rare. Rare intersections are where the most interesting opportunities live.

The same logic applies to job applications. If a fitness company is hiring for a type of engineering that isn't quite your specialty, apply anyway. Write a cover letter that's honest: I know I'm not exactly what you're looking for technically, but I'm a strong engineer and I genuinely care about this space. Startups especially are often looking for core technical competence and real enthusiasm — not a perfect credential match.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking situational frustration for career mismatch. Hating your job because your mentor is unhelpful and you're behind the learning curve is not the same as hating engineering. Don't make a permanent exit based on fixable circumstances.

Treating privilege as a reason to dismiss your own frustration. Suffering isn't a competition. Having a stable job doesn't mean difficulty at that job is invalid or that you're spoiled for feeling it. The question isn't whether your problems are "bad enough" — it's whether they're addressable.

Romanticizing the passion path. The fitness industry has its own version of the boring stuff, the administrative overhead, the tedious parts. The grass isn't greener — it's just a different color of difficult.

Quitting too early to get a real read. Three months and seven months are long enough to know a place isn't for you, but they're not always long enough to distinguish "this company is wrong for me" from "this is just what the learning curve feels like."

Binary thinking. Engineering or fitness. Stable or passionate. The assumption that these are mutually exclusive is where a lot of the distress lives, and it's probably not accurate.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your current situation for temporary factors. Write down every specific thing that's making your current job hard. For each item, ask: is this about this company and team, or would it follow me anywhere? Things like "bad mentorship" and "steep learning curve" are potentially fixable. Things like "I fundamentally don't find this work meaningful" are different.

  2. Do the daily log exercise. At the end of each workday for two weeks, write down two things: what you liked about today, and what you didn't. Don't think about big-picture career direction — just what the actual day felt like. After two weeks, look at the patterns.

  3. Identify what you should have asked in your last interview. Given what you now know about what made each job painful, what questions could you have asked that would have surfaced those problems earlier? Write those questions down. Use them in your next interview.

  4. Research the intersection. Spend a few hours looking for companies in the fitness, nutrition, or health space that employ engineers. Look at job boards, LinkedIn, startup directories. See what kinds of engineering problems exist in that world. You may find the overlap is more accessible than you assumed.

  5. Apply to one stretch opportunity. Find one role at a fitness or health-tech company where you're not a perfect match on paper. Write a genuine cover letter explaining the combination you bring. Send it. The downside is a rejection you were already expecting.

  6. Commit to a minimum time horizon before the next decision. If you've told yourself you'll stay for a year, hold to that — but use that time actively. Fix what's fixable (find better mentors, look for more engaging projects, build the skills you're missing) rather than waiting passively for the year to end.
