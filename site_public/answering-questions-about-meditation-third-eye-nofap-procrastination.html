What Meditation Is Actually Training

Most people approach meditation hoping for a mystical experience — warmth, vibration, a sense of oneness — and then conclude they're failing because nothing dramatic happened. This gets it backwards.

The core purpose of meditation is to control your attention. That's it. When you sit to meditate and your mind wanders, and then you notice it has wandered and bring it back — that act of returning is the workout. Every single return is one repetition, like a mental push-up. People who think they're "bad at meditating" because their mind wanders constantly are often the ones doing the most reps.

There are two things you're genuinely aiming for:

  1. Sustained concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → — keeping attention fixed on one object (breath, forehead, sound) for a prolonged period
  2. No-mind states — moments where thoughts cease, time seems to dissolve, and you're aware but not thinking. You're not asleep, but the mental chatter stops

The spiritual and sensory experiences — warmth, pressure at the forehead, a sense of oneness — are real, and they become more accessible the longer you practice. But they're byproducts, not the goal. Chasing them as the goal makes them unreliable and sets you up for disappointment.

The Third Eye: Experience Before Explanation

The concept of the third eye didn't come from someone inventing a cool metaphor. It came from meditators who, through years of practice, started noticing a persistent sensation — a warmth, a pressure, a tingling — in the space between the eyebrows, and then tried to describe what they were experiencing to others. The label followed the experience.

A simple way to begin exploring this: sit up straight, hover your middle finger about a centimeter from the center of your forehead — close but not touching. Close your eyes. Focus entirely on whatever sensation arises in that spot. Then lower your hand and keep your attention there.

Some people will feel something immediately. Some won't feel anything yet. Neither outcome means you're doing it right or wrong. Different techniques resonate differently with different people, which is exactly why so many meditation traditions exist.

If you feel something and want to understand it more, the answer isn't to read about it — it's to do the practice every day and let your understanding develop through direct experience. The gap between knowing nothing about a sensation and having just felt it is already enormous. That gap keeps widening with practice.

"Before you did the practice you had no idea what this was. Now you don't even know what you understand, but it's like night and day."

Brahmacharya and NoFap: The Real Question Underneath

Brahmacharya is a Sanskrit concept usually translated as celibacy, but the translation loses most of the meaning. Literally, it means to dwell in Brahma — to keep the mind resting in infinite consciousness. As it relates to sexuality, it's better understood as non-lustfulness: maintaining a mental state free from compulsive craving, not necessarily abstaining from sex entirely.

The spiritual logic behind energy retention practices in many traditions is that the generative force involved in procreation is a form of energy — and redirecting that energy inward is thought to build spiritual strength and focus.

The most useful frame for thinking about NoFap, regardless of what you believe about retention, is this: it gives you a controlled arena in which to study how your own mind works.

  • What triggers it?
  • Is it the stimulus that creates the desire, or is the stimulus just a vehicle for something already present?
  • What happens after you satisfy it vs. after you don't?

The practice of resisting an impulse you're used to following on autopilot is really practice in noticing how much of your behavior is driven by forces you never consciously chose. Procrastination Is a Feature, Not a Bug

Here's the reframe that most productivity advice misses: procrastination is not a failure of willpower — it's your brain being efficient.

If you can pass a test by cramming for one weekend instead of studying consistently for three weeks, your brain has correctly identified the path of least effort to an acceptable outcome. That's rational. Fighting it with willpower and guilt is fighting your brain's own logic.

The solution isn't to stop procrastinating. It's to engineer your environment so that procrastination produces the behavior you want.

Procrastination means you work right before a deadline. So:

  • If you have one deadline per month, you work once a month
  • If you have a deadline every three days, you work every three days
  • If you have something due every day, you work every day

The fix is to take on more, not less. More classes, more projects, more commitments — staggered so there's always something due imminently. The procrastinating brain will kick in automatically. You don't have to manufacture discipline; you just have to manufacture deadlines.

"Every day is the last day before something. Make it so that every day something is due the next day."

This won't make your life calmer. It will make it more productive. Those are different things, and conflating them is what keeps people stuck. If overwhelm, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, or depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → are causing you to not show up at all — that's a separate issue that procrastination management won't fix. But if you're simply deferring consistently because the deadline feels far away, more deadlines closer together is the actual solution.

How to Make Expectations Impossible

Disappointment requires expectation. You cannot be disappointed by something you never anticipated. The usual advice — "just lower your expectations" or "expect nothing" — is useless because you can't directly command your mind to stop anticipating outcomes.

What you can do is shift attention away from outcomes entirely, which makes expectations structurally impossible.

Expectations are about the future. Action only exists in the present.

You cannot study for an hour. That's a future-oriented goal. You can open your notebook right now. You can read one page. You can walk to the library. Each of those is a present-moment action.

"The strategy is not to let go of expectations. The strategy is to make it impossible to have an expectation — and the way you do that is to live in the present, to focus on action instead of outcome."

This is the same principle as meditation, restated in practical terms. In meditation, you don't try to clear your mind — you give your mind something present to focus on, and clarity emerges. In life, you don't try to stop expecting outcomes — you give yourself present-moment actions to focus on, and the expectation dissolves because there's nowhere for it to land.

You can't lose 50 pounds. You can go to the gym today. You can't get an A in a class. You can read for twenty minutes right now. You can't build a relationship. You can have one honest conversation today.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking a wandering mind means you're meditating wrong. It means you're doing the most important part of the practice — noticing and returning. The return is the rep.
  • Chasing spiritual experiences as the goal of meditation. They're real and worth having, but treating them as the metric makes you dependent on something unreliable and distracts from the actual training.
  • Trying to solve procrastination by doing less. Lighter course loads, fewer commitments, and more "space" usually make procrastination worse, because there's less urgency at any given moment.
  • Trying to drop expectations through willpower or positive thinking. You can't tell your mind what not to think. You can redirect it toward present-moment action, which structurally removes the future-orientation that expectations require.
  • Dismissing practices that seem paradoxical before trying them. "That's impossible" is often just unfamiliarity. The catch-the-moment-of-sleep exercise sounds impossible; the third eye exercise sounds made up. Both produce real experiences in people who actually try them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try the third-eye focus practice today. Sit up straight, hover your middle finger a centimeter from the center of your forehead without touching, close your eyes, and notice any sensation. Then lower your hand and keep your attention on that spot for 3–5 minutes. Do this daily and let understanding grow through repetition rather than research.

  2. Reframe what "doing it right" in meditation means. Every time your mind wanders and you bring it back, count that as one successful rep. Your session's quality isn't measured by stillness — it's measured by how many times you noticed and returned.

  3. If you procrastinate chronically, add 50% more to your plate. Take one more class, one more project, one more commitment — staggered so deadlines fall every few days. Track whether your output per day increases over two weeks.

  4. Audit one goal you've been stuck on by converting it to a present-moment action. Take the outcome ("get fit," "finish my project," "improve my relationship") and reduce it to the single smallest action you can physically do right now. Do that one thing. Don't plan the whole arc — just take that action.

  5. If you're curious about NoFap or brahmacharya, treat it as a self-study experiment. The question isn't "does it work?" The question is: where does the impulse come from, what feeds it, and what happens when you redirect it? Keep a short journal of what you notice.

  6. Try the catch-the-moment-of-sleep practice. Lie down in a comfortable position as if going to sleep. Stay as aware as possible. Try to identify the exact moment of transition from wakefulness to sleep. Don't judge whether you "succeed" — the practice of trying trains attentiveness regardless of outcome.
