Your Individual Nature Is the Starting Point

The single most important premise here is that each person is unique. That sounds obvious, but it has a real consequence: the most successful meditation practices are tailored to the person doing the meditating, not borrowed wholesale from whatever happened to become popular in Western wellness culture.

This means before asking "how do I meditate?", it's worth asking "what kind of person am I, and what am I actually trying to get out of this?" Those two questions will shape everything — which technique you try, how you measure progress, and whether the practice sticks at all.

Mindfulness Is a Tiny Slice of a Much Larger Tradition

Mindfulness — the practice of observing present-moment experience without judgment — is probably the word most people associate with meditation. And it's genuinely useful. But it represents a tiny fraction of what the broader meditation tradition has to offer.

Meditation as a practice has a long history, branched across many lineages and cultures. Mindfulness, as it's commonly taught today, is one particular branch of that tree. Treating it as the whole tree means missing out on a wide range of techniques that address different problems and produce different kinds of insight.

Understanding where mindfulness came from — and how it fits into the larger picture — helps you use it more intelligently and opens the door to practices that may serve you far better depending on your situation.

The Deeper Question: What Actually Makes Up a Person?

One of the core questions the broader meditation tradition takes seriously is: what is the nature of a human being? Not in an abstract philosophical sense, but in a practically useful one.

A lot of the mental patterns that cause suffering — thoughts like I am a failure, I am unlovable, that person is better than me — stem from a mistaken or incomplete understanding of what you actually are. When you don't have a clear sense of your own nature, you become vulnerable to treating these thoughts as permanent truths rather than passing mental events.

As you start to genuinely explore and understand what makes up a person, something loosens. The things that hold you back lose some of their grip. This isn't about positive thinking or reframing — it's about a clearer seeing of reality that naturally undermines those thought patterns at their root.

A Map of Different Meditation Practices

Rather than prescribing one method, it helps to understand the landscape of techniques and what each one is built to do:

  • Inquisitive practices — approaches that involve actively investigating your own mind and the nature of experience, rather than simply calming or focusing it.
  • Focusing practices — techniques that train sustained, directed attention, building concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → as a capacity.
  • Open awareness meditation — sitting with a broad, non-directive awareness and observing thoughts as they arise, without latching onto them.
  • Mantra-based practices — using the repetition of a sound or phrase as an anchor, which produces a very different mental effect than silent observation.

These aren't just stylistic variations. They genuinely produce different benefits and are suited to different kinds of people. Someone who is highly analytical might find an inquisitive practice more engaging and sustainable than a pure breath-watching exercise. Someone dealing with a scattered, overactive mind might benefit most from a mantra. The point is that the match matters.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming mindfulness is meditation. Mindfulness is a valuable tool, but walking away with only that one technique means you've seen the menu and only ordered one item — and it may not even be the item that's best for you.

Treating meditation as a universal prescription. "Just meditate" is advice that often doesn't stick precisely because it ignores the individual. If a particular technique isn't working for you, the problem may not be your discipline or your mind — it may simply be that you're using the wrong tool.

Trying to think your way out of self-limiting beliefs without practice. Understanding intellectually that thoughts like I'm a failure aren't permanent truths is a start, but it doesn't automatically dissolve those patterns. The actual practice — sustained, embodied, repeated — is what produces the experiential shift.

Approaching meditation as a performance. Especially for people who are achievement-oriented, there's a temptation to evaluate every session as a success or failure. That framing tends to undermine the practice, because most of what meditation does happens beneath the level of what any single session feels like.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify what you're actually trying to address. Before picking a technique, get specific: Are you trying to manage anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →? Build focus? Understand yourself better? Work through negative self-beliefs? Your answer should influence which practice you try first.

  2. Sample more than one technique. Try a simple breath-focusing practice for a week, then try an open-awareness practice (letting thoughts arise and pass without engaging them) for a week. Notice what each one feels like and what changes, if anything.

  3. Try a mantra-based practice as a contrast. Even five to ten minutes of silently repeating a simple phrase or sound gives you a direct comparison point against observation-based practices. Pay attention to which mode your mind takes to more naturally.

  4. Sit with the question: what am I? This doesn't need to be formal. Spend a few minutes simply asking yourself — not to answer immediately, but to hold the question — what it is that's actually experiencing your thoughts right now. Notice whether the question itself shifts anything in how the next few thoughts feel.

  5. Read or learn about where mindfulness came from. Tracing mindfulness back to its origins in broader meditative traditions gives context that makes the practice itself richer and helps you understand what it was originally designed to do.
