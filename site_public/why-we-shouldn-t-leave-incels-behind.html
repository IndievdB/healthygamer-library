Summary

TL;DR — Writing off incels as evolutionary dead ends feels satisfying, but it's both factually wrong about how humans work and socially counterproductive. People who feel completely abandoned by society don't become better citizens — they become angrier ones.

A comedian made a viral joke arguing that men who can't attract women should be left behind by natural selection — and the crowd loved it. The argument is clever and funny. But the logic underneath it has a serious flaw, and that flaw has real consequences for real people who are already struggling.

The "Natural Selection" Argument and Why It Breaks Down

The joke goes: if your whole identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → is built around blaming women for your lack of sex, nature is telling you your genes shouldn't continue. Darwin said adapt or die.

There's something true in it. But the logic applied consistently leads somewhere most people don't actually want to go.

Consider other groups where nature or bad choices have similarly "selected" against someone:

  • A 21-year-old gets drunk at a party, jumps off a third-floor balcony, and becomes quadriplegic. That's natural selection acting on a choice he made. We still treat him.
  • People who share needles and contract HIV made a choice. We don't let them die — in fact, we've mostly stopped moralizing about it.
  • Domestic violence victims sometimes stay in relationships that endanger their lives. That involves choices too. We still build shelters for them.

The question isn't whether someone made choices that contributed to their situation. Almost everyone in any kind of crisis did. The question is whether this particular group is the one group in society that doesn't deserve help. And if you draw that line somewhere, you have to be able to say exactly where it is.

"When does someone cross the line to no longer deserving help and we should let them die? If you're 18 years old and a virgin, do you deserve help at that point? What if you're 20 years old?"

There's no clean answer to that, because there isn't a clean line. The "leave them behind" logic only works if you never press it.

The Developmental Reality of What's Actually Going On

There's a parallel in how society has historically thought about criminals and addicts. Both groups used to be treated as simply bad people making bad choices — morally deficient, not worth helping. Then research caught up, and what we found was a consistent pattern of developmental traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, genetic predispositions, and environmental risk factors that made certain paths much more likely for certain people.

The same pattern is starting to emerge with incels. Clinically, many of them show features that look like a modern version of body dysmorphia — hyperfocused on specific physical traits (jawline, height) as explanations for why they're unlovable. Many have histories of traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →. Many struggle with pornography or other addictive behaviors. The developmental picture is usually not "guy who had every advantage and chose to be resentful." It's usually more complicated than that.

When a large group of people all behaves in a similar problematic way, that's a systems issue, not just a collection of individual moral failures. And systems issues require systemic solutions — not a culture-wide cheer for leaving that group behind.

What Abandonment Actually Produces

Read enough of these manifestos — the ones written before mass shootings — and a pattern emerges. These aren't primarily political documents. They're documents about not being seen. About reaching out, in whatever clumsy or broken way someone who doesn't have good social skills reaches out, and being rejected or ignored by everyone.

"These are people who were looking for one person, one person to show them kindness."

When someone has been completely written off by society, there's no particular reason for them to feel invested in society's wellbeing. That's not an excuse for violence — people who commit violence should face full legal consequences. But it is a predictable human response to total ostracism. The current cultural reaction to incels, where even other men are told not to help them, isn't an intervention. It's an accelerant.

The One Place Darwin's Analogy Actually Fails

The "adapt or die" framing works for finches. A finch on the Galápagos whose beak doesn't fit the available food cannot decide to change its beak. It dies, its genes don't continue, the population gradually shifts. That's the whole mechanism.

Humans are not finches. Humans have cognition, self-reflection, access to information, and the ability to deliberately change how they relate to the world. "Adapt or die" for a human being means something completely different — it means there are actual things you can do. The adaptation is available to you. The question is whether you know what those things are and whether you're willing to do them.

That distinction matters enormously, because it means the situation isn't hopeless even if society isn't going to help.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking validation for progress. Incel forums are genuinely validating. They tell you it's not your fault, that the world is unfair to you, that you're not alone. Some of that may even be true. But they almost universally also tell you there's nothing you can do — that you should blame external forces and wait. Feeling understood and actually improving your situation are two completely different things. Spaces that provide the first while blocking the second are comfortable traps.

Confusing "it's not entirely your fault" with "you have no agency." Both things can be true simultaneously: the circumstances that made this hard for you aren't entirely your fault, and you are the only person who can actually change your life. Incel spaces tend to collapse these into one — if it's not your fault, then nothing you do matters. That's a path to staying stuck indefinitely.

Applying "natural selection" logic as if humans are passive organisms. The comedian's framing is clever but misleading. Natural selection is what happens to organisms that can't respond to their environment. Human beings can respond, reflect, and change. Treating "adapt or die" as a death sentence rather than a call to action misunderstands what adaptation means for a species with cognition.

Thinking the problem is purely external. The post-COVID social world changed dramatically, and some people adapted and some didn't. The ones who didn't often have specific patterns in how their minds work — around ego, desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition →, and relating to others — that are getting in the way. Focusing entirely on what the world owes you keeps your attention away from the one thing you can actually influence.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your content diet for one week. Track how you feel before and after spending time on incel forums, certain subreddits, or other spaces organized around shared grievance. Not whether they feel validating in the moment — but what your emotional state is an hour later, and whether your actual situation has changed. Be honest with the results.

  2. Separate "fault" from "agency" in your own thinking. Write down the circumstances in your life that you genuinely didn't choose or cause. Then, separately, write down the things you could actually do differently starting this week. Notice that both lists can exist at the same time.

  3. Identify one person — just one — to be honest with about where you're at. The pattern in these stories is people looking for a single human being to extend some basic kindness. You can be that person for yourself first, and then look for whether there's someone in your life you've been hiding this from who might actually respond well.

  4. Study how your own mind works, specifically around desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → and ego. The shift that matters isn't learning pickup techniques — it's understanding why your wants feel so urgent, where your self-concept is fragile, and what's actually driving the emotional state underneath the behavior. This is internal work, not social strategy.

  5. If you're in a position to help someone who seems to be heading in this direction, don't leave them behind. Not by endorsing the worst of what they say — but by staying in contact, pushing back with warmth rather than mockery, and being the one person who doesn't write them off. The data on what precedes radicalization consistently points to isolation. Reducing isolation reduces risk.
