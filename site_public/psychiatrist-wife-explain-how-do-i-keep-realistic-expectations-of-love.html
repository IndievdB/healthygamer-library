Summary

TL;DR — You don't need a roadmap from your past to build a great relationship. You and your partner get to write the rules yourselves.

Most people approach relationships as though they need to find the "realistic" template first — the right model, the right benchmark, the right set of expectations inherited from somewhere. But your relationship is genuinely new. No one who has ever lived has been exactly you, exactly your partner, in exactly your circumstances. That means looking outward for a standard to match is the wrong move from the start. The real work is figuring out what you want, telling your partner, and building it together — even when it looks nothing like what anyone else has done.

Your Relationship Has Never Existed Before

This sounds like a platitude, but it has a real practical implication: external examples of love — whether from your parents, your friends, movies, or culture — can only take you so far. They were built by different people in different circumstances. Borrowing their blueprint wholesale doesn't make sense, because none of those people were you.

This is actually good news if you grew up without healthy examples. You're not behind. You're not missing a crucial piece of software. You're in the same position as everyone else: starting something that has never been done before, with someone who has never existed before. The absence of a model doesn't disqualify you from building something real. It just means you have to be intentional about it.

"You have never existed before. Your partner has never existed before. Y'all's relationship has never existed before."

The only thing that matters is your own ability to create the thing you want. That's what's actually realistic — not some external standard, but what the two specific people in this relationship are capable of making happen together.

The Honeymoon Period Is Supposed to End

Struggling with the post-honeymoon period isn't a personal failing — it's basically universal. The early phase of a relationship runs on hormones. Everything feels electric. Then real life shows up: finances, where to live, how to handle rent, whether to have kids, what to do about the internet bill. These things are boring. They're also unavoidably part of a shared life.

The drop feels jarring because the honeymoon phase sets an emotional baseline that was never sustainable. When that phase ends, it can feel like something broke. Nothing broke. The relationship is just becoming real — which is actually what you want, even if it's less thrilling.

The question isn't how to get the honeymoon feeling back. It's how to build something that feels worth showing up for once the initial rush has settled.

Stop Asking If Your Expectations Are Realistic

The framing of "realistic expectations" is subtly backwards. When you ask whether your expectations are realistic, you're handing authority over your relationship to some outside judge — convention, statistics, what other couples do. That judge doesn't know you.

A better question: What do I actually want from this relationship, and can I communicate that to my partner? Then flip it: What does my partner want, and can I actually offer that?

"Set your own expectations. Determine your own standards of love. Don't rely on whether it's realistic or not — f*** that."

Some of the best parts of a relationship come from holding "unrealistic" expectations and finding a way to meet them anyway. Deciding something matters to you and then doing the work to make it happen — that's not delusional. That's what commitment looks like in practice.

Communication Is the Actual Foundation

Two people can want the same thing and still fail to build it, because they're each operating from a map the other person has never seen. A relationship is a multiplayer game. If you're not communicating, you're just playing your own solo version inside your head, assuming the other person is doing the same.

The conversation worth having isn't abstract ("do you value love?") — it's specific:

  • What do I need from you?
  • What do you need from me?
  • Which of those things are you genuinely able to give?
  • Which ones are you asking me to give even though they're hard for me?

That last one matters. Some things your partner needs will be difficult for you. Doing them anyway — not resentfully, but because you've actually talked about it and agreed it's worth it — is what makes a relationship durable past the honeymoon period.

You Write the Rules

This is the part people underestimate. Couples get to decide, together, what their relationship looks like. The rules don't have to resemble anyone else's. If Friday nights mean something specific to you two, they mean that. If a tradition seems strange from the outside but it works for you, it works.

"The cool thing about a relationship is that ultimately you and your partner get to write the rules together — and you guys write whatever rules you want."

This applies to expectations too. You're not bound by what's considered normal, what your parents modeled, or what popular culture suggests a partnership should look like. The only thing that actually matters is whether both people are genuinely on board.

The flip side of this freedom is responsibility. If you can write any rules you want, you can't really blame external templates when things go wrong. You're the author. Which means you also get to revise the story when something isn't working.

Common Pitfalls

Treating "realistic" as an objective standard. There's no universal measurement for what a relationship should look like. When you ask whether your expectations are realistic, you're often just asking whether they're conventional — which is a very different question.

Waiting to see a healthy example before trying. You can want something you've never witnessed. Not having a model doesn't mean you're incapable of building something good. It means you have to be more deliberate about it.

Staying in your own head. A lot of relationship problems aren't really compatibility problems — they're communication failures. Assuming your partner knows what you want, or that wanting something different means you're incompatible, skips the step of actually talking.

Expecting the honeymoon phase to be the standard. If you measure your relationship against its most hormonally-charged early period, everything that comes after will feel like a loss. The post-honeymoon phase isn't the decline — it's where the actual relationship lives.

Treating difficult moments as proof the relationship is wrong. Doing hard things for your partner — things that take real effort — is part of the deal. Difficulty is not a red flag. Resentment over unspoken expectations is.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Have the explicit expectations conversation. Sit down with your partner and each answer: What do I need from this relationship that I'm not saying out loud? Then share your answers. This isn't a one-time event — revisit it when something feels off.

  2. Write down what you actually want your relationship to look like. Not what you think is achievable. Not what other couples do. What would genuinely make you happy? This is your starting draft for the rules you'll write together.

  3. Name one "unrealistic" expectation and take a small step toward it. Pick something you've told yourself you couldn't ask for, and bring it to your partner. See what happens when you stop self-editing before the conversation even starts.

  4. Build a ritual that belongs only to your relationship. Something specific, maybe even strange to outsiders, that is yours. This is a small, concrete way to practice being the author of your relationship rather than borrowing someone else's template.

  5. When the post-honeymoon friction hits, name it out loud. Rather than interpreting low-stakes irritations (money, logistics, routines) as signs of a deeper problem, try saying: "This is the boring-and-real part of being together, and I'm still in." Naming it takes away some of its power.
