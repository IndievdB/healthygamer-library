Summary

TL;DR — Getting a loved one into mental health treatment is one of the most common and genuinely hard challenges families face. There's a real skillset for doing it well, and knowing that skillset changes outcomes.

Most people who try to convince a friend, partner, or family member to see a therapist go about it the wrong way — not out of malice, but because no one teaches this. The conversation usually happens at the worst possible time, in the wrong setting, with language that triggers defensiveness instead of openness. There's also a harder truth lurking underneath: sometimes what looks like caring is actually enabling, and sometimes moving a person toward help requires something closer to coercion than encouragement. Naming these things honestly is the first step to doing them well.

Why This Is So Hard in the First Place

Most medical problems don't come with a persuasion problem attached. You don't need a strategy to convince someone to see an orthopedic surgeon about a broken foot. Mental health is different for several overlapping reasons.

Stigma shapes how people see psychiatrists and therapists before they ever meet one. A lot of what the general public "knows" about psychiatrists comes from movies — Hannibal Lecter is the enduring example — which makes seeking help feel more threatening than it needs to be.

Lack of insight is also a clinical feature of many serious conditions. Someone in the grip of mania, psychosis, or severe depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → may genuinely not perceive their own suffering accurately, or may not connect their behavior to the distress they're causing.

And for less severe problems — the excessive gaming, the emotional unavailability, the drinking that's gotten out of hand — there's a different obstacle: these problems are real and impairing, but they don't look dramatic enough to justify the word "disorder." People can always find a reason it's just stress, just a phase, just how they are.

What Enabling Actually Looks Like

Enabling started as a term in addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → treatment and has since spread to describe any pattern where the person closest to someone with a problem inadvertently helps the problem continue. It almost always begins with love.

"Enabling is really a subset of love. It begins with the good intentions, it begins with trying to do your best, it begins with love, with caring, with compassion. However, as the effort goes on, it becomes increasingly problematic how you are deploying your caring."

A typical scenario: parents of a young adult who is struggling — sleeping all day, not working, possibly hallucinating — who simultaneously ask the person to get help and pay for their apartment, give them a car, cover their phone, finish their homework. The person has every reason to stay comfortable and no real consequence for refusing treatment.

The key structural insight is that privileges and responsibilities are supposed to be coupled. When you provide all the benefits of family membership while accepting zero corresponding responsibility from the other person, you have removed the most natural pressure toward change. And this happens across the spectrum — from serious psychiatric illness down to the adult child who's been gaming for months while a parent covers the rent.

If you find yourself doing more and more to compensate for someone else's dysfunction — covering for them, making excuses, shielding them from consequences — it's worth asking honestly: is this helping them, or is it helping me feel less afraid?

Setting Up the Conversation: The Basics

Before thinking about what to say, consider when, where, and how to say it. These variables matter more than most people realize, and most people ignore them entirely.

Don'ts: - Don't have this conversation in the middle of an argument, or right after one (your amygdalaThe brain's negative emotional center — the region that generates fear, anxiety, and survival responses.Full definition → is still firing even when you think you've calmed down) - Don't do it late at night - Don't do it when either of you has been drinking - Don't do it at family gatherings, holidays, or emotionally loaded occasions - Don't do it over text, email, or social media if you can possibly avoid it — these formats strip out body language, tone, and the possibility of real-time adjustment

Dos: - Signal it in advance: "There's something important I want to talk with you about — not bad news, but something that really matters to me. Can we sit down tomorrow evening?" This creates a mental set so the person isn't ambushed, and it gives both of you time to arrive at the conversation more intentionally. - Choose a neutral location. Not the bedroom (too many associations), not a childhood home loaded with family history, not a space where either person has felt attacked before. A kitchen table works. For conversations where you're genuinely uncertain how the other person will react, a semi-public place like a park bench or quiet cafe table can actually reduce the risk of someone losing control. - Where possible, meet in person. So much of human communication is nonverbal. The physical presence of another person — proximity, facial expression, the possibility of touch — changes the conversation in ways that a phone call or Zoom simply cannot replicate.

What to Actually Say

Once you're in the conversation, the sequence matters.

Lead with the relationship, then lead with the feelings that invite empathy. Start by making clear that the relationship is the priority — you're raising this because you care, not because you want to be right. Then share what you're feeling. Crucially, certain emotions invite empathy and others trigger defensiveness:

"Worry, concern, fear, scared — these are the things that evoke empathy more than defensiveness."

Saying "I'm worried about you" lands differently than "I'm angry at you." Both may be true. But anger tends to make people defensive; fear tends to make people lean in.

Focus on specific behaviors, not diagnoses. You're not there to tell someone they have OCDObsessive-Compulsive Disorder is a psychiatric condition defined by intrusive, unwanted, and persistent thoughts (obsessions) that are neutralized by mental or physical acts (compulsions).Full definition →, an alcohol use disorder, or a gaming addiction. You're there to describe what you've observed:

  • "I've noticed you haven't gone to work two weeks out of this month."
  • "I miss you — you're spending so much time on games that I feel like I don't see you anymore."
  • "Our kid is missing time with you."

Naming specific behaviors rather than diagnostic labels does two things: it keeps you in a lane you actually have expertise in (you saw what happened), and it avoids the defensiveness that comes with being told what you are rather than what you've done.

Point to consequences, not causes. When someone deflects by explaining why their behavior makes sense ("I'm stressed," "it's because of my PTSDTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →," "work is impossible right now"), don't get drawn into arguing about the cause. Gently return to the consequences:

"Whatever the cause — the DIY approach doesn't seem to be working. There's still suffering happening. Let's see if we can get some help beyond what either of us knows how to give."

The "we" framing is particularly useful in close relationships. We need more help than we know how to give positions you as a partner, not an adversary.

Be prepared for resistance — and don't get hooked. The person may push back, get irritated, deflect, or turn it back on you. Prepare yourself for this in advance, so it doesn't knock you off balance. When it happens, you can recognize it and return to the basics: I love you, here are the behaviors I'm worried about, and that's what I'm here to talk about.

Persuasion Tools That Actually Work

Beyond the basic approach, a few specific techniques have shown up repeatedly in practice:

Asking for it as a gift. Framing therapy as what you most want for a birthday, anniversary, or holiday. It's unusual enough to land differently and carries genuine emotional weight without being a demand.

Sharing your own experience. If you're in therapy yourself, talking honestly about your own reluctance, what helped you get started, what you've gotten out of it, and what you were afraid of — this is some of the most persuasive material available. Personal testimony from someone close to you cuts through abstraction.

Using a trusted third party. Primary care physicians write nine out of ten psychiatric prescriptions in the US — they're often already in a relationship with the person you're trying to help. Leaving a brief message with someone's doctor before an appointment ("I'm worried about my mom — can you check in about her emotional state?") can change a routine visit. Clergy, coaches, and other respected figures can sometimes reach people in ways that family members can't, because the dynamic is different.

Intermediate steps. Not everyone will agree to see a therapist on the first ask. Support groups — especially illness-specific ones — are often more acceptable to people who are resistant to formal treatment. And people in support groups frequently share their own experience of therapy, which can make a referral feel less scary. Think of these as bridges.

Persistence. This is not a one-conversation project. Most successful outcomes involve repetition over time.

"What people think success looks like is a straight arrow. What success really looks like is an incredible, knotted path that eventually has an arrowhead at the end."

When Persuasion Isn't Enough: Therapeutic Coercion

Here's the part most books won't say plainly: sometimes persuasion isn't enough, and what's required is closer to coercion. The term is uncomfortable, and it carries real ethical weight — which is exactly why it's worth using honestly rather than dressing it up as something softer.

The ethical framework for coercive action in this context is specific: it's only justified when the goal is to ultimately restore the other person's autonomy. Serious mental illness diminishes a person's capacity for self-determination. A coercive action aimed at getting that person into treatment is aimed at giving them back their capacity — not permanently overriding it.

In practical family terms, coercion usually means closing doors and opening others: withdrawing privileges that are being provided without corresponding responsibility. Adult children do not have a right to parental financial support. Every benefit a family provides to a non-minor is optional and negotiable. That negotiability can be used:

"You can't have internet access, you can't have a car, you can't have an independent apartment until you have taken the responsibility for this — and that responsibility includes getting yourself evaluated."

The hardest version of this, in romantic relationships, is the ultimatum: by this date, or we're done. This requires being honest with yourself about whether you mean it. If it's a bluff, it may still prompt movement — but if it isn't followed through, it teaches the other person that your limits aren't real.

One clarifying move worth trying before an ultimatum: remove every obstacle you can remove. Offer to find a therapist, make the appointment, and go to the first session. If the person still doesn't show up when all they have to do is walk in the door, that's information. You've removed every excuse and the only remaining variable is willingness.

Common Pitfalls

Having the conversation during or right after an argument. This is probably the single most common mistake. It feels urgent in the moment — you're already talking about the problem — but neither person can really hear the other when emotions are that activated.

Jumping to diagnosis. Saying "you're an alcoholic" or "you have OCD" or "this is literally the definition of addiction" almost never helps. You don't have the expertise, and the label triggers defensiveness before you've gotten to the conversation you actually need to have.

Enabling while asking for change. Simultaneously telling someone they need help and ensuring they have no consequences for not getting it is one of the most common and most counterproductive patterns. If you're providing all the material conditions for someone to stay comfortable and unchanged, you've already answered the question of whether they need to change.

Trying once, concluding it doesn't work. One conversation proves very little. This is a project.

Calling coercive behavior something else. Doing something controlling or limiting to someone and labeling it love — without acknowledgment of what it actually is — is genuinely confusing and damaging. Name what you're doing honestly, at least to yourself. If you're enforcing a consequence, call it that.

Forgetting that you might be part of the problem. The person asking for help sometimes needs to look carefully at their own role in the dynamic. The helpers who come seeking advice are not always simply innocent bystanders. Sometimes the most important work is the helper's own.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what you're providing. Make a list of the material support, privileges, and accommodations you're currently extending to the person you're worried about. For each one, ask honestly: is this coupled with any corresponding responsibility? This is how you identify whether enabling is happening.

  2. Choose the right time and place before anything else. Before you decide what to say, decide when and where. Pick a calm moment, at least a day out. Signal it in advance: "There's something important I'd like to talk about — nothing terrible, but something that matters to me. Can we set aside some time this week?"

  3. Write down two or three specific behaviors you want to raise. Not a diagnosis. Not a character judgment. Concrete, observable things you've seen or experienced. Practice saying them aloud so they come out as observations, not accusations.

  4. Identify the emotions you want to lead with. Worry, concern, fear, and sadness invite empathy. Anger and resentment tend to produce defensiveness. You may feel all of these — decide which ones to share first.

  5. Find your intermediate step. If a therapist feels like too big an ask right now, what's a smaller bridge? A support group, a primary care visit, a trusted coach, a clergy person? Start there.

  6. Remove every logistical obstacle. If you're going to ask someone to go to therapy, offer to find the therapist, make the appointment, and go to the first session with them. If they won't go even then, you have clarity.

  7. If you're in treatment yourself, share it. Talk about your own reluctance, your experience, what surprised you. This is often more persuasive than any argument.

  8. Plan for resistance before you walk in. Imagine the conversation going sideways. What will you do when they push back, get angry, or deflect? Decide in advance that you'll return to: I love you, and here are the specific things I'm worried about.

  9. Treat this as a project, not a single conversation. If the first conversation doesn't land, that's expected. Build in the assumption that you will return to this, calmly, more than once.

  10. Consider your own support. If you're in this position — trying to help someone who isn't helping themselves — you may need your own space to process it. The burden of being the person holding the relationship together while someone you love struggles is real. Getting your own support isn't a distraction from helping them; it's what makes sustained effort possible.
