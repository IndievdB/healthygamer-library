Masculinity Is a Fiction That Still Hurts You

The dictionary definition of masculinity is essentially "society's impression of traits associated with men." That's it. Society isn't a person with a consistent opinion — it's an abstraction, and it shifts. Some cultures consider physical bulk to be the mark of a man; others prize obesity as the sign of masculine power. The biology of testosterone is real, but masculinity is the interpretation layered on top of biology, not the biology itself.

What this means practically: there is no stable target to hit. And yet men spend their lives chasing one.

The labels "toxic masculinity" and "healthy masculinity" make this dynamic worse. In practice, those terms usually just mean:

  • Toxic masculinity = something you did that I disapprove of
  • Healthy masculinity = something you did that I approve of

The standard belongs entirely to whoever is judging. A man can be told he should express his emotions, and then be condemned for expressing anger. He can be told to be vulnerable, and then be told to "man up" about his shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. There's no winning because the rules are someone else's and they shift constantly. Trying to optimize for those rules is a trap.

The Barrier: How the Outside Gets Inside

Here's the mechanism that makes any of this matter. Society's expectations float around as an abstract cloud. They have zero effect on you until they cross a barrier and become internal — arriving as thoughts, emotions, or identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →.

That crossing is the whole problem. And the crossing happens through internalization: someone believes something about you, you absorb that belief, and it starts living inside you as a pattern. You wake up in the morning, look in the mirror, and a thought appears — I'm not man enough — without anyone saying a word. It has been fully installed.

Once installed, these patterns feel automatic and uncontrollable. That's not a character flaw; it's how the mind works. The grooves get deeper with every repetition.

"The source of healthy masculinity is not in the societal expectation. It is the way that we receive, metabolize, interpret, and internalize external expectations that ultimately determines whether we have healthy or unhealthy masculinity."

This reframes the whole project. You can't control what society expects. You can work on what happens inside you when that expectation arrives. That's where the leverage is.

Vasanas: How Mental Habits Form and Lock In

Not a scar exactly, but a groove — a well-worn channel that the mind flows into automatically.

Think of pouring hot water over ice. The first pour creates a shallow channel. Each subsequent pour deepens it. Eventually water flows that way effortlessly, without any conscious direction. That is how a vasana forms.

Every time you were told "boys don't cry," every time anger was the only emotion that stopped the bullying, every time you saw what a "real man" is supposed to be — each of those was a pour of hot water. The channel got deeper. Now, in the presence of any trigger, the mind rushes down that channel automatically.

This is why masculinity as you currently experience it is a vasana — a habitual collection of thoughts and emotional responses, not a real thing you chose. Your immediate answer to "what is a man?" isn't the product of reflection. It's the output of a groove worn in over years.

The good news: vasanas are not permanent. They can be dissolved.

Insecurity-Based vs. Tranquility-Based Masculinity

Here's a practical diagnostic. When you make a list of traits you associate with being a man, pay attention to how each one feels.

  • If thinking about a trait produces shame — if it makes you feel deficient, exposed, or lesser — that belief is rooted in insecuritySelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →. The trait has become a mirror for your inadequacy.
  • If you can think about the same trait neutrally — I don't have this yet, but I could develop it — that belief can be approached from a place of tranquility.

Insecurity-based goals are dangerous because the insecurity doesn't actually care about the goal. It will find a new thing to be deficient in as soon as you make progress. The incel who believes masculinity is all the things he lacks will, even if he acquires them, find new things he lacks. The insecurity is the driver, not the destination.

"The things that you choose to believe are masculine can be born of your insecurity. Masculinity becomes all of the things that I am not."

Tranquility-based goals look like this: This is something I want for my life. I'm not there yet. No one is perfect. I'm going to move toward it. Same goal, completely different energy. One leads to self-punishment at the gym; the other leads to making friends there.

The exercise: sit down with a piece of paper. List what you believe are masculine traits. Notice which ones produce shame. Those are the ones rooted in insecurity, and they need a different kind of attention — not more striving, but investigation.

Choice Is the Core of Healthy Masculinity

The concept that holds all of this together is choice. Not what you do, but whether you chose it.

Consider two scenarios: you get fired, or you quit. Either way you don't have the job. But one tears you apart and one fills you with a sense of agency. The difference is entirely internal — it lives in whether you chose.

The same logic applies to sacrifice. Sacrifice chosen freely — for your kids, your partner, people you care about — can be meaningful and energizing. Sacrifice extracted from you through guilt, manipulation, or the threat of disappointment is a completely different thing wearing the same costume.

"Choice is in here. For someone else forcing you, backing you into a corner, guilt-tripping you into sacrifice — that is no longer your choice."

This is how manipulation works on men specifically: external pressure creates internal shame, and then the only way the man knows to relieve the shame is to take action in the outside world — give money, make sacrifices, comply. The shame is the lever. The action is the payout for whoever is pulling it. Shrinking the permeability of the barrier — developing an internal sense of self that doesn't automatically absorb others' disappointment — is how you stop being manipulable.

Why Living Up to Others' Expectations Makes Things Worse

Trying to meet other people's expectations seems safe. It's not.

First, their expectations of you aren't actually about you. They're based on their own experiences, their own vasanas, their own history. Your mother's impression of you was formed when you were 15 and hasn't fully updated since. A stranger's judgment of why you're doing something is a projectionProjection is when something inside you — a feeling, impulse, or trait you can't consciously accept — gets attributed to someone else instead.Full definition → of their own assumptions. None of it is a reliable read on who you actually are.

Second, and more insidiously: the more you meet someone's expectations, the more they expect. Behavioral science is clear on this. If you always lend the money, eventually not lending it feels like betrayal — even though you were never obligated. You've trained them to expect it, and now you're trapped.

Some of the most successful men — high earners, visibly accomplished — carry enormous suicidal ideation because they became the one person their entire extended family depends on financially. They can't stop. They can't fail. They're not living their life; they're servicing everyone else's expectations of it.

Living by your own internal compass does not mean ignoring the world. Bills still get paid. Difficult situations still get navigated. But there is a profound difference between accepting a constraint and being crushed by it. The first is a choice. The second is a loss of self.

Dissolving Vasanas: Two Practical Methods

Selfless action (KarmaThe fundamental principle that every action has a cause and every cause produces an effect — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition → Yoga)

The most powerful tool for weakening a vasana is genuinely selfless action — doing something good for another person without expecting anything in return. Not performatively charitable, not strategically kind. Just help someone, let it go, and don't track the ledger.

This works mechanically. The system that makes you miserable runs on back-and-forth exchange: they disapprove, you feel shame, you act to relieve shame, they approve, the cycle repeats. Selfless action breaks the loop. You send something out — and you genuinely don't care what comes back. That breaks the permeability. It dissolves ego. It exercises pure choice.

Important caveat: this is not the same as being a "nice guy." A nice guy performs selflessness to get something — affection, approval, sex, gratitude. That's not selfless; that's commerce dressed up as kindness. Genuinely selfless action asks nothing. If you help someone and then monitor whether the vasana dissolved, it wasn't selfless.

Also important: selfless action toward strangers or acquaintances, or small acts with no expectation of acknowledgment, works better initially than trying to be selfless in your most entangled relationships.

Discrimination (Viveka)

The second tool is learning to tell things apart — specifically, to observe the contents of your own mind and ask: where did this come from?

  • Is this thought mine, or is it something I absorbed from someone else?
  • Is this a habitual pattern or a fresh response?
  • Is this belief something I chose, or something I was handed?
  • Do I actually want this, or do I want to want it?

That last question is particularly powerful. The difference between genuinely wanting something and being conditioned to want it is real and detectable if you sit with it honestly.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking rejection of external standards for freedom. Some people hear "other people's expectations don't matter" and their ego grabs it as license: now I can tell everyone to go screw themselves. That's not freedom — that's a different vasana. The goal isn't to be defiant; it's to be centered.

Treating the wrong thing as the source of the problem. A lot of men try to fix internal feelings through external actions — cosmetic surgery, extreme income goals, status signaling. These occasionally help at the margins but they don't touch the underlying groove. The shame comes back wearing a new costume.

Nice-guy-ism dressed as selflessness. Performing goodness in order to be seen as authentic, or to be rewarded for your sacrifice, isn't selfless action. If you're thinking about how selfless you are while you're doing it, you're still in ego.

Expecting vasanas to dissolve quickly. They were formed over years of repetition. Discrimination and selfless action work, but they work the way water reshapes rock — gradually, through consistency, not through a single dramatic insight.

Conflating "tranquil" with "passive." Approaching a goal from tranquility doesn't mean you don't care or won't work hard. It means the fuel for your effort is aspiration rather than shame. The actions can look identical from outside. The internal experience — and the sustainability — are completely different.

Assuming you have to resolve the external situation before the internal work matters. You don't need to quit your job, leave the relationship, or escape the obligation first. The internal compass comes first. The external navigation follows from that.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the masculinity audit. Write down every trait you associate with being a man. For each one, notice: does thinking about this produce shame, or can you think about it neutrally? The shame-producing ones are rooted in insecurity and should be approached with curiosity rather than striving. Keep the list somewhere you can return to.

  2. Ask "do I want this, or do I want to want this?" For any major goal or obligation in your life, sit quietly and ask that question honestly. The answer often surfaces quickly and changes how you relate to the goal.

  3. Observe the crossing. For one week, practice noticing the moment when something external becomes internal. Someone says something and you feel a pang — pause and watch it. Where did that come from? Did you choose to let it in? This builds the discrimination muscle.

  4. Do one selfless act per day with no acknowledgment sought. Take out the trash. Help a stranger. Do something small and good and genuinely let it go. Don't tell anyone. Don't check whether it made you feel better. Just do it and move on. Consistency matters more than magnitude.

  5. Name your patterns. When a habitual shame spiral or identity collapse starts, label it: "this is the groove, not me." You don't have to believe it fully at first. The labeling creates distance, and distance creates space for choice.

  6. Go for a walk and think about who you actually want to be. Not what you think you should be. Not what would make others approve of you. What kind of person, specifically, do you want to become? Let that question breathe for an hour. Notice which answers feel expansive and which feel like obligations.

  7. When you receive criticism, run this process: Listen genuinely. Try to understand where it's coming from. Remove ego from the assessment — your worth is not tied to your performance. Then decide honestly whether the feedback is accurate. Incorporate what's true, release what isn't. Neither reflexively accept nor reflexively reject.
