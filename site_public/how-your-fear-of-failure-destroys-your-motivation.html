Excitement Is Lighter Fluid, Not Firewood

Think of building a fire. You pour on lighter fluid and it roars to life — but that's not what keeps the fire burning. The fire goes out because of rain and wind, because there's nothing solid underneath to sustain it.

Excitement works the same way. It can absolutely jolt you out of a rut. But excitement is an emotion, and emotions naturally ebb and flow. You cannot stay perpetually excited about anything. The trap many people fall into looks like this:

  1. A burst of excitement or inspiration
  2. A period of real effort and progress
  3. Excitement fades; old patterns creep back in
  4. Back to square one

The cycle repeats not because you didn't feel excited enough, but because the underlying negative forces — doubt, fear, self-defeating stories — were never dealt with. Every small setback becomes evidence that you were right to expect failure, and that thought is what smothers the flame.

The Real Work: Clearing the Negative Weight

If your fire keeps going out, the answer isn't to douse it with more lighter fluid. The answer is to get the rain and wind out of the picture.

For someone who has a history of flunking out, getting fired, and watching opportunities collapse, the dominant internal voice isn't "I'm excited for this new job." It's a quieter, more persistent one that says: you've done this before, you know how it ends. And when something at the new job goes imperfectly — a coworker outperforms you, a promotion goes to someone else, a bad week happens — that voice gets loud: See? There it is. You knew you'd screw it up.

The problem is that this voice treats normal fluctuations as proof of terminal failure. No job goes perfectly. Performance varies. Promotions sometimes go to someone else this cycle but open up next cycle. When you're carrying a heavy load of "I'm destined to fail," every bump in the road feels like confirmation of the worst story you tell about yourself.

The work, then, isn't about generating more positive feeling. It's about honestly examining the fear, the doubt, and the self-concept that says failure is your destiny — and dismantling it.

Authentic Doubt Beats False Confidence

There's a temptation, when you know your track record isn't great, to psych yourself up with hollow positivity. This time is different. I've totally got this. I'm going to crush it. The logic makes sense — why dwell on the negative?

But false confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → is fragile. The moment reality pushes back, it shatters. And when it does, you're left with nothing to stand on — worse off than if you'd been honest with yourself from the start.

The alternative isn't pessimism either. It's authentic doubt — a clear-eyed acknowledgment of where you actually stand.

"Authentic doubt is better than false confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →."

If you've struggled in the past, you shouldn't pretend otherwise. You should take it seriously. But taking it seriously doesn't mean accepting it as destiny. It means: I know I have real vulnerabilities here. I know I've screwed things up before. That's worth respecting — and respecting it means working on it.

Think about how an underdog team competes at its best. They don't pretend their opponent is weak — that's a lie the back of their mind doesn't believe anyway. What they do instead is enter the game knowing: I'm not favored, but I'm not beaten. There is a path to winning this, and I need to look for it. They're going to make mistakes, and I'm going to be ready when they do.

That posture — honest about the difficulty, not surrendered to it — is far more durable than manufactured hype.

Your Past Doesn't Guarantee Your Future

One of the most persistent lies that past failure tells you is that it's predictive. You've failed before, therefore you will fail again. You've screwed up jobs, therefore you'll screw up this one.

But past performance is not a guaranteed indicator of future results. A stock that has declined for years isn't mathematically doomed to keep declining. A person who has struggled through their twenties isn't sentenced to keep struggling.

What the past does give you is information — specific, honest data about where you're vulnerable. That information is actually valuable, if you use it right. The question to sit with isn't will I fail again? but what specifically has caused me to fail before, and what do I need to address?

When you treat your past problems as things to understand and work on — rather than evidence of a fixed character — you stop running from them and start gaining actual traction.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down your actual vulnerabilities. Not in a self-flagellating way — specifically. What patterns have caused problems in the past? Showing up late? Checking out when things get hard? Avoiding conflict? Name them clearly, because vague fear is more paralyzing than a concrete list.

  2. Audit your inner monologue around the new opportunity. For one week, notice every thought you have about it that contains a prediction of failure. Write those thoughts down. You can't work on what you can't see.

  3. Replace false confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → with honest-but-open self-talk. Instead of I'm totally going to nail this, try something like: I've struggled before, and that's real. I also haven't been in this exact situation before, and I don't know for certain that I'll fail. Let me look for the ways I can win this.

  4. Separate setbacks from destiny. When something goes wrong at work (and something will), practice the discipline of asking: Is this a one-time event, or is this actually proof that I'm doomed? Most setbacks are the former. Give yourself the chance to recognize that.

  5. Work on one specific vulnerability at a time. Don't try to overhaul everything at once. Pick the single pattern most likely to derail you — the one you most recognize from past failures — and make that your focus first.
