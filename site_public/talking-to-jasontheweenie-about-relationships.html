Summary

TL;DR — The way you judge your own wants, bottle your emotions, and measure your life against the wrong standard is quietly costing you more than you realize.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between a psychiatrist-turned-streamer and a 20-year-old Vietnamese-American content creator navigating the pressures of rapid online success, a complicated first relationship, aging immigrant parents, and the near-total absence of people he can talk to honestly. What emerges is less a checklist and more a gradual excavation — peeling back the reflexive self-judgments and "shoulds" to find out what someone actually wants, and why they can't let themselves have it.

The Reflex Nobody Teaches You to Notice: Self-Judgment as a Way of Life

One of the most consistent patterns in this conversation — and probably in your life too — is the speed at which a genuine feeling gets followed by a verdict.

It goes like this: something comes up, you notice it, and then almost immediately you tell yourself why it's wrong to feel that way. Jason does it constantly throughout the conversation, and he doesn't even catch it until it's pointed out.

He says he chose streaming over someone he loved. Then, unprompted: "That's kind of depressing… am I a [ __ ] up person for that?" He says he cancels on college friends. Then: "I know, I know, it's bad." He admits he sometimes wants someone to FaceTime after stream. Then, within the same breath: "But I know that's a want, not a need."

Each time, he runs the same sequence — acknowledge the thing, then immediately prosecute it.

"You make lots of choices and then you have this part of you that's like: this is bad. And then we ask you would you change that — and you're like no. But I should be someone who would change that."

This is worth sitting with. The judgment doesn't actually change the behavior. It just adds shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → on top of it. And as long as that cycle is running, you never get to the more useful question: what do I actually want, and what am I willing to do about it?

The invitation here isn't to stop evaluating yourself. It's to notice the reflex when it happens — to catch the moment you say "I know, I know, it's bad" and ask whether that verdict is doing anything useful or just making you feel guilty about something you're going to keep doing anyway.

Bottling Emotions Isn't a Character Flaw — But It Has Costs

Jason grew up in a household where emotional expression wasn't really on the menu. When his parents argued, and he brought it up, they'd say "we're good, that's just how we are." When he had problems, he dealt with them himself. That's not a failure of his parents — it's just how a huge portion of the world actually operates.

"Bottling your emotions is bad for other people. They're not you. They don't have your parents."

More than half the planet handles difficult feelings the same way. This isn't a uniquely broken thing — it's a cultural norm across most of Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, and beyond. The idea that you should be emotionally expressive is mostly a Western European and North American assumption, and acting like everyone who doesn't do it is damaged is both inaccurate and condescending.

That said, bottling does produce a specific and predictable cost: delayed detonation. Jason describes it himself — he streamed the day after a breakup, smiled through it, and then one random Tuesday a month later it hit him like a wall. The grief didn't disappear. It just got rescheduled.

The other cost is relational. When you grow up learning that the people who love you don't really hear you when you bring them hard things — when your parents' response to "why do you argue so much?" is a cheerful "we're fine!" — you quietly learn that opening up doesn't work. That lesson doesn't evaporate when the situation changes. It travels forward with you into every relationship you try to build.

What's Actually Happening When Someone Is "Hard to Convince"

Jason mentions wanting to talk to his dad — to tell him that the reason he's grinding so hard is because his parents are older than most, and he's terrified of running out of time to give something back. But he predicts his dad will just say "don't worry about me, focus on your future."

This gets framed as his dad being stubborn or dismissive. But there's something more interesting going on underneath.

When your dad tells you not to think about him, he's not dismissing your feelings. He's trying to protect himself from his own. An older parent who knows he won't have the normal decades with his kid — who has already thought about his own mortality — can barely hold it together when his son looks at him and says "I'm scared of losing you." Deflecting to "focus on your future" isn't coldness. It's the only way he knows to push those emotions away before they become too much to manage.

"He does the same thing you do — when a lot of negative emotions come up, he does whatever he can to push them away."

This is a useful reframe for any conversation where you feel like you can't get through to someone. When someone is persistently "hard to convince," the issue usually isn't that they haven't heard a good enough argument. It's that they're not able to fully receive what you're saying because of what it would mean for them emotionally if they did. The useful question isn't how do I make a stronger case? — it's what makes it hard for this person to hear this?

For Jason's dad, the answer is: hearing his son's love and gratitude out loud makes his own grief and fear feel real. That's not a wall. That's just a person trying to cope.

The workaround isn't manipulation — it's connection. You can say: "This conversation matters for me too. I need to say this. Hearing that I shouldn't worry about you doesn't make me stop worrying about you." That reframes the conversation from something being done to the parent to something both of you need.

Language as a Substitute for Action (and Why Telling Someone You Love Them Actually Does Something)

There's a principle from psychoanalysis — often attributed to Freud — that language and action are, at some level, interchangeable. The brain doesn't fully distinguish between doing something and talking about doing it. Both generate a version of the same reward.

This is why people who talk loudest about their plans are often the ones who do the least. The talking gives them the neurological payoff of the doing without requiring any actual change. You've probably seen this — the person who announces their new workout routine to everyone they know and then doesn't start it, because the announcement already felt like progress.

But this dynamic runs the other way too, and in a more useful direction. When you tell someone something that's been sitting inside you — especially something loving, something that's been weighing on you — there's a real and measurable shift. The weight doesn't disappear entirely, but it changes.

"When someone really loves you — telling your parents that you want to repay them, that you're doing all of this because of them — that signals the love that is in the actions of doing it."

Jason has been carrying the pressure to be successful quickly, to retire his parents, to give something back before it's too late, completely silently. He has never told them any of that. The result is that he's bearing that weight alone, and they don't even know it's there. Saying it out loud — even once — doesn't replace the action, but it does let both of them share the load.

The Problem with Living by Someone Else's Rulebook

Jason isn't a regular 20-year-old. He streams six or more hours a day, seven days a week. When he's not streaming, he's thinking about streaming — content ideas, analytics, growth, collabs, sponsors. He has people to manage, inbound to field, and an audience whose expectations shape every decision he makes.

His college friends, meanwhile, have club meetings and classes and the occasional party.

These are genuinely different lives. Not better or worse — different. But Jason keeps measuring himself against the standards of the life he's not living. He feels bad about not texting friends back. He feels bad about prioritizing a stream over someone who's "in town." He thinks he should be making time for people he genuinely has no energy left for.

"You're living your life. You can't judge your life by average standards."

This applies well beyond content creators. Anytime you're in an unusual situation — caring for a sick family member, building something from scratch, going through something others around you haven't experienced — using the expectations of a typical life as your benchmark will reliably make you feel like you're failing at something you were never actually set up to succeed at.

The more honest question is: given the life I'm actually living, what do I want? What am I willing to put in? What am I okay not having right now?

Wanting Something and Needing It Are Not the Same — But Devaluing It Is Still a Trap

Jason admits that after streaming he sometimes misses being able to call someone and just talk. He had that with his ex. He misses it. And then, almost immediately, he categorizes it as a "want, not a need" and uses that to dismiss it.

There's a well-worn defense mechanism at work here. When something you want is hard to have — or feels incongruent with the identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → you're building — devaluing it is the path of least resistance. It's the same thing that happens when someone gets rejected and says "I wasn't that into her anyway." It protects you from the sting of not having the thing. But it also slowly disconnects you from your own interior life.

"We end up being dishonest with ourselves. We end up kind of where you are — where there's a lot of stuff you want but you're not fully honest with yourself about it."

The distinction that matters here isn't want versus need. It's: what am I actually willing to prioritize? Right now, for Jason, the answer is: the career comes first. That's fine. That's honest. But framing it as "I don't need connection" to make the sacrifice feel more manageable is a slow erosion of self-awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

You don't have to act on every want. But knowing what you want — and being honest that you're choosing something else over it — is very different from convincing yourself the want was never real.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "I shouldn't want this" with "I don't want this." The pretending causes more long-term damage than the wanting.

Thinking you need to convince someone to feel differently. When someone deflects an emotional conversation, the problem usually isn't the argument you're making. It's that receiving the message is hard for them. Address what's hard, not what's incorrect.

Assuming bottling emotions makes you broken. It makes you like most people on earth. The question isn't whether it's ideal — it's whether you know what the cost is and whether you're willing to pay it indefinitely.

Saying "I know, I know, it's bad" is not the same as understanding why you do something or deciding whether to change it. The judgment loop is often a way of feeling like you're dealing with something without actually dealing with it.

Measuring your life against the standard of a life you're not living. If your circumstances are genuinely unusual, peer comparison becomes actively misleading. The right question is what your life actually requires of you, not what someone else's would.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the judgment audit for one day. Every time you say (internally or out loud) "I know, I know" or "I shouldn't" about something you're doing or feeling, write it down. At the end of the day, look at the list. Notice whether the judgment ever actually changed the behavior — or whether it just added a layer of shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → on top of it.

  2. Say the thing you've been carrying. Identify one person — a parent, a close friend, someone you've been wanting to say something to — and actually say it. Not the diplomatic version, not the version edited for their comfort. The version that's true. Give it a real sentence or two. Then pay attention to what shifts afterward.

  3. Before your next hard conversation, ask: what makes it difficult for this person to hear this? Don't start with your argument. Start with their emotional position. What would it mean for them to fully receive what you're saying? What feelings would that create? Build your approach from there rather than from the logical case for your position.

  4. Make one honest inventory of your wants. Write down things you actually want right now — not what you think you should want, not what would make you a better person to want, just what you notice yourself wanting. Then, separately, write what you're currently prioritizing. The gap between those two lists is useful information, not a moral verdict.

  5. If you're feeling behind on a friendship, don't default to "I should text more." Ask first: do I actually have time and energy for this? If the honest answer is no, that's worth sitting with rather than papering over with guilt. If the answer is yes, make a specific plan — not a vague intention.

  6. When someone you love deflects your affection or concern, try naming the dynamic rather than repeating the argument. Something like: "I know this is hard to hear, and I know you're going to tell me not to worry. But I need to say it anyway — for me." This reframes the conversation and removes the deflection's power without demanding they respond any particular way.
