How the Chaos Cycle Gets Started

It begins in childhood. You try. You fail — not because you're stupid, but because a neurodivergent brain in a neurotypical world will inevitably fall short of neurotypical expectations. Over time you notice the pattern: even when you try hard, you still screw up. Worse, the act of desperately trying not to screw up makes you more likely to. Your attention splits between the task and the fear of failing the task, and you can't do both at once.

So your brain, being smart, does a cost-benefit calculation and lands on an uncomfortable but logical conclusion: if failure is inevitable, why carry the stress of trying to prevent it? The problem is that this adaptation, practiced over years, teaches you something dangerous: chaos is where I thrive, so I should manufacture chaos.

The Esports Strategy That Changes Everything

A useful example comes from working with a competitive gaming team. They were talented but inconsistent — dominant in some matches, falling apart in others. The pattern was familiar: once they read the game state and concluded they were probably going to lose, they stopped playing seriously. Why try when the math is against you?

The shift was this: instead of aiming for victory or conceding defeat, the goal became losing as slowly as possible. Make the other team earn every inch. Don't GG out at 25 minutes just because the game looked over at 20. Thirty minutes. Forty minutes. Make them bleed for it.

Two things happened. First, the game-time extended and performance stayed higher longer. Second — and this is the surprising part — they started winning games they had no business winning. Comebacks that shouldn't have been possible started happening 10, 15, even 25% of the time when they'd already mentally written the game off.

You can apply exactly this logic to your ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition →. When you feel the familiar spiral starting — I'm going to screw up, I'm going to screw up — don't try to talk yourself out of it. Instead, say:

"Yeah, there's a decent chance I'm going to screw up. But I'm going to do as good a job as I can for as long as possible."

That's it. No toxic positivity. No fighting against a belief your brain has held for years. Just: delay the inevitable.

Why does this work? Because delay creates time, and time builds habits. The longer you stay in consistent, structured behavior before things unravel, the more repetitions you give your basal ganglia. Those repetitions start forming grooves. The grooves start becoming actual habits. And habits, once they exist, catch you when your attention wanders — which is exactly when you need to be caught.

  1. Identify your chaos triggers. Spend a few days noticing the moment you shift from "trying" to "might as well blow it up." What thought precedes it? What does the stress feel like just before you give in? Naming the pattern is the first step to interrupting it.

  2. Pick one tiny, non-negotiable habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → to anchor. What matters is training the basal ganglia with consistent repetition. One habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → done reliably is worth more than ten habits attempted chaotically.

  3. Practice the "delay" reframe. Next time you feel the spiral starting, say out loud or in writing: "I might screw this up. I'm going to go as long as I can anyway." Don't argue with the fear — just shift the question from "will I fail?" to "how long can I hold on?"

  4. Measure time, not outcomes. For a week, track not whether you completed tasks perfectly, but how long you stayed in the attempt before chaos took over. Extending that window — even by ten minutes — is genuine progress and directly feeds habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → formation.

  5. Design your environment for consistency, not rescue. Since your brain is stimulus-bound and externally motivated, use that. Put visual cues where you need them. Set up the environment so the right behavior is the path of least resistance, rather than relying on attention or willpower to override chaos in the moment.

  6. Notice what you're actually good at. Your damage-control and adaptation skills are real advantages in genuinely chaotic environments. Acknowledge them without using them as justification to manufacture crises. The goal is to keep the skill and lose the trigger.
