Summary

TL;DR — Mental illness exists across all cultures, but looks different depending on where you are; and much of what religion historically did for mental health can be understood in secular terms without losing the underlying value.

This is a wide-ranging exploration of two connected questions: how universal is mental illness really, and what role did religion play in mental health before modern psychology came along? Both questions matter because they push back on the assumption that mental health is purely a Western, modern problem with purely Western, modern solutions.

Mental Illness Is Universal, But Wears Different Clothes

Depression rates appear higher in North America and Europe than in other parts of the world — but that's largely a measurement problem, not a real difference.

The tools used to diagnose depression (instruments like the PHQ-9 or the Beck Depression Inventory) were built inside a particular cultural context. When you take them somewhere else, they don't necessarily capture how distress actually shows up there. In Indian cultural contexts, for example, depression often manifests as physical complaints — stomach problems, cognitive fog — rather than the sadness and hopelessness that Western diagnostic criteria expect. The concept of a discrete "mental illness" simply doesn't exist in the same way across all cultures.

So the numbers look lower elsewhere, but that's the instrument failing, not depression being absent.

When more advanced cross-cultural research controls for this — asking whether the percentage of people experiencing these kinds of suffering is roughly comparable across cultures — the answer is broadly yes. The neurochemical and psychological processes that produce mental illness seem to be pretty well conserved across humanity. Schizophrenia, for instance, appears in every culture studied. (One strange finding: immigrants develop schizophrenia at higher rates than both the population they left and the one they joined — something that isn't fully understood yet.)

The exportation of Western culture is a real variable here. There's solid evidence that body dysmorphic disorder and eating disorders travel with Western cultural export. Countries that receive heavy Western media influence see rises in these conditions. Japan and South Korea, for example, have seen eating disorders take root and begin propagating on their own. This suggests that while some forms of mental illness are cross-culturally universal, others can be introduced or amplified by cultural contact.

The honest summary: mental illness is a human universal. It just presents differently depending on cultural context, and our measurement tools aren't culturally neutral.

What Religion Actually Did for Mental Health

Before there were therapists, there was the priest, the rabbi, the monk, the village elder. Religion handled what psychotherapy now handles — and it did so more comprehensively than we tend to give it credit for.

Consider grief. If you lost someone and there was no therapist, you went to a religious figure. That person helped you integrate the loss, make sense of it, and find a way to carry it. Whether they said "they're in heaven" or "they will be reborn," the mechanism was similar: an internally consistent framework, delivered by someone who was deeply fluent in it and genuinely confident in it.

This maps directly onto something discovered in psychotherapy research: the type of therapy matters less than the therapist's mastery of it. A therapist who deeply understands CBT and a therapist who deeply understands psychodynamic therapy will get roughly comparable outcomes. Both will outperform a therapist who only sort of knows their approach. What produces results is comfort and competence within a coherent system — not the system itself.

Religion provided that. An internally consistent worldview, delivered by someone who lived inside it completely.

Religion also supplied something harder to replace: purpose, direction, and a framework for meaning. Different traditions did this differently. Judeo-Christian traditions tend to be more prescriptive — here are the commandments, here is what you should do. Karmic traditions (Buddhism, Hinduism, Jainism) work differently: there's cause and effect, karma, and the value judgment comes from the individual, not from divine command. There's no intrinsic morality handed down — just the reality that your causes have effects. That's a genuinely foreign concept in the Western frame.

The Hidden Psychotherapy Inside Religious Practice

One of the more useful things to extract from this: religious traditions contain working psychological interventions that we've only recently started understanding scientifically.

Acceptance is the clearest example. "It is God's will" is, functionally, acceptance. Surrender to a higher power is, functionally, acceptance. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) is built on the evidence that acceptance — genuinely letting go of the need to control outcomes — reduces suffering. Religion was teaching this for thousands of years without calling it ACT.

From the science side: it's not the deity that produces the therapeutic benefit. It's the act of acceptance and surrender. Deeply religious people will dispute this and say that surrendering to a false god doesn't help — but that's a theological claim, not a scientific one. The measurable variable is acceptance itself.

Meditation is the same story. Fifty years ago, suggesting that meditation could treat mental illness would have gotten you dismissed as a religious crank. Now there's substantial evidence it helps. The practice came from Buddhist tradition — what the West classifies as a religion — but the practice itself requires no belief system. We extracted it, renamed it "mindfulness," and ran clinical trials.

This is a pattern worth noticing. There's a section of the Geranda Samhita — a classical yogic text — that reads:

"The process of controlling the mind through the medium of the senses is called pratyahara... if the mind is separated from those sensory feelings and experiences, their effects will automatically stop. In this way an internalized state of mind will be achieved."

That doesn't read like a religious text. It reads like a psychology manual. The yogic and karmic traditions in particular are deeply concerned with how the mind actually works — the nature of thoughts, the nature of suffering, the mechanics of attention and sensation. They were doing phenomenological psychology inside a framework the West labeled "religion."

The Cost of Stripping Religion Down to Its Parts

The secular extraction of religious practices isn't without loss.

When we take mindfulness out of Buddhism or acceptance out of Christianity, we filter it through our own biases. We take the parts that feel scientifically respectable and quietly drop the parts that seem strange or culturally uncomfortable. But that filtering isn't neutral — we're just moving the line based on what we currently understand, and history suggests we've moved that line before and will move it again.

Fifty years ago the line excluded meditation entirely. Now it includes mindfulness but probably still excludes things that will eventually have their own clinical trials. We've been wrong before about what counts as legitimate.

The religious context also provided more than just isolated techniques — it provided community, social support, regular ritual, a shared framework for suffering, and a reason to show up every week. Extracting one technique loses everything that surrounded it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming lower measured rates of depression elsewhere means less actual suffering. The instruments were built in the West. They undercount differently-presenting distress elsewhere.

  • Assuming the decline of religion is simply a mental health crisis in the making. That doesn't follow. It means we have to find other ways to address the things religion used to address — but those answers don't have to come from religion specifically.

  • Assuming that because a practice comes from a religious tradition, it either requires that religion or can be fully understood without it. Both extremes miss something. The practice may work on its own, but stripping all context away risks discarding things we don't yet understand.

  • Treating Western diagnostic categories as objective and universal. Depression, anxiety, and related conditions manifest differently across cultures. Measuring them with Western tools produces Western-shaped results.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice where you're using cultural proxies for measurement. If you catch yourself assuming that someone from a different background "seems fine," consider whether the signals you're reading for distress are culturally specific to your own frame.

  2. Trace your existing coping practices back to their origins. If you meditate, journal, practice gratitude, or go to a support group, look into where those practices came from. You may find there's more depth to explore than the stripped-down secular version you're using.

  3. Experiment with acceptance deliberately. Pick one ongoing frustration or worry — something you've been trying to think or work your way out of — and practice setting the outcome aside for 24 hours. This isn't resignation; it's a test of whether the rumination is actually helping.

  4. If you've left a religious tradition, ask what you've lost, not just what you've gained. Community, ritual, a shared framework for grief, a coherent worldview — these things don't automatically transfer elsewhere. Identify which of those you're still missing and look for secular structures that provide them.

  5. When evaluating any mental health claim that seems strange or "unscientific," apply a historical check: ask whether it resembles something that was dismissed 50 years ago and is now mainstream. That won't tell you the claim is true, but it'll keep your skepticism honest.
