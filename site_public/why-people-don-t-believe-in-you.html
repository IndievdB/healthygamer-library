Summary

TL;DR — Why criticism lands hard on some days and rolls off on others isn't random. It depends on how much unprocessed emotional weight you're already carrying — and that weight usually traces back to a specific psychological scar.

This is a live coaching session exploring why the same comment, troll, or criticism can be completely devastating on one day and totally ignorable on another. Through the conversation, a deeper pattern emerges: the variability in your emotional reactions isn't really about what other people say. It's about what you're already carrying — and where your oldest wounds are.

The Receiver, Not the Signal

When someone's words hurt you on Tuesday but the same type of comment doesn't register on Friday, your instinct might be to blame the comment or the person. But the comment is the same. What changed is you.

Think of it as a broadcast signal and a receiver. The signal stays constant. But something in the receiver changes day to day — and that receiver is you. This reframe matters because it means the answer to "why do I get triggered?" isn't out there in the world. It's in you, which also means it's actually something you can work on.

Your Inventory Is Already Full

A useful way to picture this: imagine your emotional capacity as an RPG inventory with a weight limit. You can carry a certain amount before you slow down, before you can't pick up anything new without dropping something else.

On a hard day, your inventory is already near capacity before the first troll even types. The notification glitch on your stream, the back injury that's kept you from the gym, the self-criticism about your diet — each of those things takes up a slot. By the time someone says something annoying on Twitter, there's no room left. You snap. Not because of the tweet, but because the tweet arrived when you had nothing left to give.

On a good day, your inventory is light. The same tweet barely registers.

"You're carrying too much, and you don't have space for anything else."

The key insight here: a lot of what fills that inventory is subconscious. You've actively pushed difficult feelings away because sitting with them hurts. But suppressed emotions don't disappear — they just move to the background and quietly eat up your capacity, shortening your fuse without you realizing it.

Psychological Scars and Why They Form Where They Do

Everyone has trigger points. The location and shape of yours is determined by where you've been injured before.

A war veteran hears a truck backfire in a parking lot and is suddenly back in combat. That specific sound — not a dog barking, not a fire alarm — opens a door and a flood of memories, emotions, and fear rushes through. The muffler is the trigger because the muffler resembles the original wound.

Your triggers follow the same logic. If people not believing in you has been a source of pain since you were fifteen — writing "believe in me" on your wall, watching close friends doubt a path you knew was right, being dismissed by people you loved — then that specific thing is going to cut deeper than most. Every time it repeats, the scar thickens. "With each injury, that scar gets thicker and bigger and dominates more of your psychological process."

This is also why the same wound gets re-opened by things that seem unrelated. A DMCA strike that forces you to delete six years of stream clips hits the same nerve as the pizzeria conversation, which hit the same nerve as the sophomore year doubting. The content is different. The emotional frequency is identical.

The Pattern Under the Pattern: Isolation and Belief

When you dig through specific incidents — the botted account, the DMCA clips, the Twitter pile-on — a single theme keeps appearing: being alone with something important that no one around you can understand or believe in.

This lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → isn't just social. It's the particular ache of trying to share something that genuinely matters to you and finding the people closest to you can't or won't meet you there. When your best friends laughed at streaming in a pizzeria, they weren't being cruel. They probably thought they were looking out for you. It still hurt, because what you needed in that moment wasn't their wisdom — it was their faith.

The problem with wounds like that is they don't announce themselves. They just make you feel like no one can ever fully understand you. They make you assume, before you've even tried to explain, that your words won't be enough. And then — here's the subtle, important part — that assumption starts acting in your present-day conversations, making you feel inarticulate about things you can actually describe quite clearly.

"You think that you're not describing it well, but the very voice in your head that tells you no one can understand is also telling you that your words are insufficient."

Why You Defend People Who Are Doubted

There's a pattern worth noticing: intense loyalty to figures who get doubted publicly, who prove people wrong, who keep going when everyone's counting them out. When you defend someone like that ferociously — more than seems warranted by basketball statistics — it's worth asking why.

When something real is at stake, they show up completely. They're not going to let you fight alone. They feel, many of them, the same thing you feel — and that's part of why they're there.

The Mourning You Skipped

When the DMCA strikes hit and you had to delete years of clips, there was no time to grieve. The stream had to go on. Chat wanted to be entertained. You flipped into performance mode.

But deleting those clips wasn't just a data management problem. It was losing a record of real moments with real people — memories you'd planned to look back on, to show to people you love someday. That deserved to be grieved. You gave it no room.

Skipping that mourning didn't make the loss disappear. It moved it underground, into the subconscious inventory, where it kept taking up weight. Months later, a notification glitch landed on top of it, and the feelings from both collapsed into the same dark place.

"I feel like if every single streamer this happened to, it'd be like, okay, we're all in this together. But I was just over here like... damn."

Feeling alone in a loss doesn't require anyone to have done anything wrong. It just means you didn't have someone on your side of the wall.

How Healing Actually Works

Processing suppressed emotion is not comfortable. It involves deliberately moving toward the thing you've been trying not to think about, sitting with it, and letting yourself feel it rather than routing around it.

This feels bad in the middle. That's not a sign something is wrong — it's what the process looks like. The "weirdly good" feeling that comes after is what emotional metabolism feels like. You've taken in something difficult and your system has actually digested it, rather than just storing it in the back of the freezer.

Each time you do this, the dark place gets a little smaller. The fuse gets a little longer. Trolls start to land differently — not because they've changed, but because you're not carrying as much when they arrive.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking the problem is what people say. The content of criticism isn't usually what makes it hurt. What makes it hurt is the emotional state you're in when it arrives, and the specific nerve it happens to touch.

Assuming you just "shouldn't" be affected. Telling yourself to toughen up doesn't work because it doesn't address what's actually in the inventory. It just adds self-criticism to the pile.

Comparing your pain to others' and deciding yours doesn't count. You can hold space for the fact that other people have it harder and still acknowledge that your losses were real and that you're allowed to grieve them.

Skipping the mourning. Pushing through and "snapping back into stream mode" is a very human and understandable response to loss. But the emotion doesn't get processed by being ignored — it just goes underground and increases baseline volatility.

Confusing "I believe in myself" with "I don't need anyone to believe in me." These are different things. Self-belief is real and valuable. But the need for people who matter to you to see you clearly is also real, and not a weakness. Acknowledging the hurt of not having that is not the same as doubting yourself.

Waiting until you're in crisis to process. The work of emotional digestion is most effective when done regularly and proactively, not only when things are falling apart.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your inventory before reacting. When something hits harder than expected, pause and ask: what am I already carrying today? List the things that were bothering you before this moment arrived. Recognizing a full inventory doesn't make the upset go away, but it gives you context and removes the confusion about why this thing is hitting this hard.

  2. Name the core wound, not just the symptom. Next time you feel a spike of frustration or a mood drop, ask yourself: is there a part of me that feels like someone who should believe in me doesn't? Follow the anger down to the hurt underneath it. The hurt is where the actual information is.

  3. Stop running from the uncomfortable memory. Pick one unprocessed loss — something you decided not to think about because it was too sad. Spend ten minutes with it deliberately. Write about it, talk to someone about it, or just sit with it. You're not wallowing; you're metabolizing.

  4. Have the conversation you've been avoiding. If someone important to you dismissed something that mattered — even years ago, even with good intentions — consider telling them what it actually felt like. Not to assign blame, but to take down a brick from the wall. The goal isn't to prove them wrong; it's to let yourself be known.

  5.   7. Notice who you defend fiercely and why. When you find yourself unusually invested in defending someone who's being doubted, get curious about it. What do you recognize in their situation? What would you have wanted someone to do for you?
