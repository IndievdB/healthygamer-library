Why Addicts Taught Us This First

Consider what opioid withdrawal actually feels like. People who have survived both opioid withdrawal and stage-four cancer have described the withdrawal as worse. Every signal your body can send is screaming for the substance. And yet, 12-step programs help people beat it. How?

Step Nine is the key: make amends. Participants write down every person they've wronged — stolen from, lied to, abandoned — and then contact each one to own the mistake and apologize. It's uncomfortable. Some people yell back. Some are grateful. But when it's done, sobriety gets dramatically easier.

The reason is cognitive. Your brain doesn't passively forget the people you've wronged. It keeps a running background process for each one — a tab that never closes. Every unresolved wrong is consuming RAM. When you're already running at capacity just from guilt and shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, you have almost no cognitive resources left to resist cravings, make decisions, or follow through on intentions. Making amends isn't just emotional catharsis; it's closing tabs, freeing up the processing power needed to actually change.

The Same Mechanism Runs on Dreams

Yogic philosophy identified the same phenomenon at a broader level and called it incomplete karmaThe fundamental principle that every action has a cause and every cause produces an effect — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition → — any desire, intention, or commitment that was started but never resolved. This isn't limited to wrongs you've done. It includes:

  • A dream you've held since childhood but never pursued
  • A birthday card you said you'd send but didn't
  • A friendship you meant to repair
  • A career path you fantasized about but never started
  • A promise to yourself you quietly abandoned

Your brain treats all of these the same way: as open loops that need to stay in working memory until they're resolved. The more you accumulate, the heavier the load — and the less cognitive capacity you have available for actual action.

"The more incomplete dreams that you have, the more things that you have dreamed for and fantasized about that you have not achieved — your mind stores all of those in your RAM."

This is why people with enormous ambition often feel the most paralyzed. It's not lack of motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →. It's overload. Wanting a thousand things isn't energizing; it's suffocating. And every time you try to pursue one dream, your mind immediately surfaces the other nine hundred and ninety-nine.

The Counterintuitive Move: Subtract First

The instinct is to add: add discipline, add motivation, add a better system. But the actual leverage point is subtraction.

The starting exercise is simple and emotionally confronting: write down every dream, regret, and incomplete intention you're carrying. Everything. Then go through the list and do one of two things with each item — check it off (complete it) or strike it through (consciously let it go).

Letting go of a dream is genuinely painful. You've been taught your whole life to never give up. But holding onto a dream you will never realistically pursue isn't hope — it's cognitive debt you keep paying interest on. Consciously choosing to release it, grieving it briefly, and crossing it off closes the tab for good.

"Chasing a thousand dreams sabotages your ability to achieve any single one."

The same dynamic applies everywhere. The pressure you feel trying to be everything at once is the weight of unclosed loops, not a sign that you're dreaming big enough.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "letting go" with "giving up forever." Striking something off your list is a deliberate, conscious act of releasing a specific version of a dream — not a moral failure or a permanent closing of all doors. The point is to stop letting it passively drain you while you do nothing about it.

Keeping the list vague. If you do the exercise in your head, it won't work. The act of writing is part of what closes the loop for the brain. Vague mental acknowledgment keeps things fuzzy and unresolved.

Skipping the emotional pain. The reason these lists haven't been made before is that looking at them hurts. But the cringe and discomfort you feel when you think about an unresolved mistake or abandoned promise is exactly the signal that it's consuming RAM. Avoiding it doesn't make it disappear — it just keeps the tab open.

Trying to complete everything at once. After clearing cognitive load, the temptation is to attack every remaining goal simultaneously. This recreates the same problem. The framework below deliberately guards against this.

Being too rigid with the daily structure. Doing the 1-5-20 rule seven days a week leads to burnout and resets progress. Rest is part of the system, not an indulgence.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write the full list. Set aside an hour with pen and paper. Write down every dream, regret, unresolved relationship, broken promise, and incomplete intention you're currently carrying — no matter how small or how old.

  2. Sort ruthlessly. Go through each item and make a binary decision: Will you actively pursue or resolve this? Or will you consciously release it? There is no third option of "keep it vague and hope." Draw a single line through anything you're releasing; circle or star anything you're keeping.

  3. Grieve what you cross off. This is not optional. Acknowledge that the dream mattered, that letting go is a real loss, and then let it go. The emotional processing is what actually closes the tab.

  4. Make at least one amend. Pick one person you've wronged or one promise you broke — even something that feels small — and reach out to acknowledge it. Don't over-explain; just own it. Notice how your mind feels afterward.

  5. Apply the 1-5-20 rule, five days a week. Each day, complete: - One task that takes less than one minute - One task that takes less than five minutes - Work on one task that takes more than 20 minutes (you don't have to finish it — just work on it)

  6. Trust the momentum. The goal of 1-5-20 isn't heroic productivity. It's building evidence that you can close loops. Each completed task gives you a small confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → signal. These compound — like dominoes — into genuine forward movement.
