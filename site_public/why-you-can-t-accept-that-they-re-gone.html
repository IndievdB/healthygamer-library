The Karmic Frame: Effects That Outlast the Person

A complementary way to understand this comes from Buddhist thought on karmaThe fundamental principle that every action has a cause and every cause produces an effect — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition →. The Buddhist view (simplified) isn't that your soul carries debts from life to life — it's more like the butterfly effect applied to human action. Every action you take creates ripples. Those ripples affect other people for years, decades, sometimes a lifetime.

Think of a human life as thousands of karmic seeds sown in every direction. When that person dies, they are gone — but every seed they planted is still growing. The money they left you (or didn't). The words that inspired you. The wounds they inflicted. The habits they modeled. The love they showed or withheld.

"This person is gone — that person will never be there ever again. But it's actually kind of scientifically easy to say that their effects linger on. Their matter still exists, their energy still exists, and even their actions literally exist."

Grief, through this lens, is the work of reckoning honestly with all of those seeds — the good ones and the painful ones. Maybe they were an absent parent. Maybe they struggled with addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →. Maybe they said things that still echo in you. Acknowledging that they're gone and their karmic imprint remains is what lets you start to process both the love and the damage honestly, without pretending either doesn't exist.

This framing holds together something that feels contradictory: you can fully accept that the person is gone and fully accept that they are still affecting your life. Both are true. Neither cancels the other.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → for remembering. Thinking about someone constantly isn't the same as grieving them. If all you're doing is replaying memories of the person — without asking what those memories mean for who you are — you're stuck. The loop runs but nothing integrates.

Thinking "letting go" means forgetting. It doesn't. Letting go of the active grief is not the same as erasing the person. Integration means carrying them forward in a way that's built into you, not consciously maintained through effort.

Waiting for grief to resolve on its own. RuminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → is the mind's default. Integration requires something more active — it requires you to turn the question from "why did this happen / what did I lose" toward "who am I now because of this, and how do I want to move forward?"

Expecting the grief to be clean. The karmic seeds someone leaves aren't all good ones. Healthy grief means working through the damaging ones too — the ways a difficult parent, a lost relationship, or a complicated person shaped you in ways you're still untangling. That's part of the integration, not a detour from it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Shift the ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → question. When you notice yourself thinking about the person you've lost — replaying memories, revisiting what happened — consciously redirect: How did knowing them change me? What do I carry because of them? You're not suppressing the thoughts; you're pointing them in a more integrative direction.

  2. Write out their "karmic seeds" in your life. List concretely what this person left behind in you: valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition → they instilled, habits you picked up, wounds that still affect you, things they said that you still hear. Both the positive and the painful. This is the raw material of integration.

  3. Identify one way they've already shaped who you are. Not something you're still trying to figure out — something already true. Maybe you're more careful with your words because of them. Maybe you work harder. Maybe you're still learning to trust because they let you down. Name it plainly.

  4. Ask the forward-facing question. Given everything this person was and everything they left in you — good, bad, complicated — who do you want to be going forward? That's not betraying them or forgetting them. That's how you carry them in the healthiest way.

  5. Notice when you're stuck in the loop. If you find yourself thinking about a lost person and nothing new ever comes from it — no shift in perspective, no change in how you see yourself — that's a signal you're ruminating rather than integrating. Bring a therapist, a journal, or a trusted person into the process to help you move from memory into meaning.
