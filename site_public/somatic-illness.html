Why "It's All in Your Head" Was Both Wrong and Harmful

For decades, when doctors couldn't find a clear physical cause for a patient's symptoms, they labeled the illness "psychosomatic" — and by that they meant purely psychological, essentially imaginary. Patients with fibromyalgia or IBS were told their pain wasn't real, referred to psychiatrists, and often dismissed.

That framing was wrong in both directions. These conditions are genuinely physical — inflammation is measurable, pain receptors are genuinely sensitized, the gut is genuinely misfiring. But they are also genuinely psychological in origin. The more accurate modern understanding is that mind and body are not separate systems that occasionally influence each other; they are deeply, continuously entangled. An emotional assault registers physically, and a physical problem changes emotional experience.

A useful framework for understanding this entanglement comes from the Indian tradition of Ayurveda: Panchakosha theory, which describes a human being as five layered "sheaths" — the physical body, an energetic body, an emotional mind, an intellectual mind, and a spiritual self. The key insight isn't the specific layers; it's the principle they illustrate: a shock to one layer reverberates through all the others. Emotional bad news measurably suppresses immune function. Physical exercise measurably helps people recover from heartbreak. The layers support each other, and they damage each other.

This isn't just philosophy. Studies show that emotional state affects how robustly you respond to vaccines. People under chronic stress get sick more easily. The body keeps a running account of what the mind experiences.

What Actually Helps

There is genuine good news here. Because we understand the mechanisms, we have real interventions. None of them are magic, and none replace seeing a physician for your specific diagnosis — but several are things you can begin doing without a prescription.

1. Reconnect with your body safely.

The core task is recalibrating your relationship to bodily signals — not numbing out, not catastrophizing, but making contact with the body in conditions where that contact is manageable and non-threatening.

Yoga and Tai Chi are particularly well-suited for this. The goal in yoga isn't flexibility for its own sake — it's to stretch to the point where your attention is drawn into the body. Not so little that you can zone out, not so much that it becomes painful. A balance pose, for example, demands concentrationThe active, deliberate practice of fixing your attention on a single point — distinct from meditation as a state, Dharana is the technique, the verb, the thing you actually do.Full definition → on bodily sensation in a safe, controlled way. This is literally a practice of learning to inhabit your body again. Research shows these practices outperform standard physical exercise for several inflammatory and pain conditions — possibly because they work at this energetic and attentional level, not just the muscular one.

Other good options: swimming, hiking, rock climbing, or any activity that puts you genuinely inside your physical experience. The common thread is embodied attention in a non-threatening context.

Common Pitfalls

  • Taking "psychosomatic" to mean fake. These are real physical conditions with measurable inflammation, real nerve sensitization, and real functional impairmentThe degree to which a condition disrupts a person's ability to carry out the basic tasks of daily life — getting to work, feeding themselves, managing emotions, or surviving independently.Full definition →. The psychological component explains their origin; it doesn't diminish their reality.
  • Waiting for a purely physical fix. If you've cycled through medications for IBS or fibromyalgia without lasting improvement, that's not unusual — there often isn't a discrete physical lesion to correct. Treatments that address the nervous system and emotional layer may work where purely physical treatments haven't.
  • Treating yoga as flexibility training. The therapeutic mechanism isn't the stretch itself — it's the attentional reconnection with the body that the stretch induces. Pushing into pain or treating it as a workout misses the point.
  • Assuming emotional numbness means you're fine. The immune system is still activated. You can be symptom-free emotionally and still be accumulating physical damage.
  • Giving up after years of failed treatment. These conditions were poorly understood until recently, and many physicians still trained in an era when "psychosomatic" meant "refer and dismiss." A long history of unhelpful treatment doesn't mean the condition is untreatable — it may mean you haven't yet encountered the right framework.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. See a doctor first. If you have symptoms consistent with fibromyalgia, IBS, POTS, or similar conditions, get a proper diagnosis. These conditions have specific treatment protocols beyond what's covered here, and ruling out other causes matters.

  2. Start a body-reconnection practice. Commit to yoga or Tai Chi two to three times per week — not for fitness, but specifically for the practice of noticing bodily sensation without reacting to it. If classes aren't accessible, even a simple daily balance pose (stand on one leg for 30–60 seconds) begins the recalibration.

  3. Do a body scan daily. Once a day, sit quietly and move your attention slowly through your body from feet to head. Don't try to fix anything. Just notice what's there. The goal is to rebuild the habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → of receiving low-level signals from your body before they reach crisis point.
