Summary

TL;DR — Isolation and difficulty caring about others aren't character flaws — they're the logical result of being hurt when you opened up, never being shown how to care, and learning that the only safe move is to feel nothing.

Jacob is 27, socially isolated, and struggling to build friendships and romantic connections. He knows something is off — he catches himself not caring about people, then judges himself for it, then wonders if he's fundamentally broken. What unfolds across this conversation is a more coherent picture: a kid who never had caring modeled for him, who was betrayed by the people closest to him at exactly the moments he was most vulnerable, and who built a very rational wall around himself as a result. The "problem" isn't Jacob. It's that the wall has become invisible to him, and everything that bounces off it lands back on him as self-blame.

The Pattern of Self-Referential Blame

One of the first things worth noticing in Jacob's story is where his explanations tend to end up. When his work friendship feels off, he concludes: "Maybe I'm asking for too much." When he doesn't connect with new people, he wonders what's wrong with him. When his friend snaps at him, he walks away thinking he was being annoying.

Every incident, whatever its actual cause, gets routed back to Jacob as the explanation.

This isn't a random habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition →. It makes sense given his history. But it's worth naming clearly: self-referential blame is what happens when you can't read what's going on with the other person. If you can't locate the problem "out there," the only place for it to go is "in here." We'll come back to why that happens, but first — how did the wall get built?

How Trust Gets Broken Early

Jacob's instinct to isolate didn't come from nowhere. Two experiences stand out.

The first: around age 10 or 12, after his mother told him he had "no life," he wrote in a private notebook about feeling sad and wondering if life was worth living. His mother found it. When she asked him if he meant it, he said he was "just venting" — and absorbed all the discomfort of the moment himself, letting her off the hook entirely.

The second: in high school, a friend he'd known for years — someone who had been to his house, played games with him, been genuinely close — stood by and did nothing (or worse, laughed) when others sprayed him with body spray and a teacher yelled at him for the resulting disruption. His friend didn't defend him. He didn't say a word.

In middle school, the bullying was actually worse. But the resolution was better — apologies were forced, things got better, one of the bullies eventually became friendly. This matches a well-documented psychological principle: people tolerate more suffering when there's improvement over time than when suffering is static, even if the static version is objectively milder. The high school situation still carries more heat precisely because it ended with nothing — no resolution, no repair, just betrayal and silence.

What Avoidance of Emotion Actually Does to You

Avoiding emotion feels like a solution. In the short term, it is one. But it has three major long-term costs.

First: it makes caring harder. You can't selectively numb. When you shut down the circuits that let pain in, you also shut down the circuits that let warmth out. Jacob noticed this himself — he knows he should care, he wishes people well, but actually generating the feeling requires what he described as "reaching within myself," like digging for something that's been buried.

Third: it makes empathy nearly impossible, which then causes self-blame. Empathy isn't just understanding that someone else is having a hard time (that's sympathy). Empathy is actually feeling what the other person is feeling. When you can't do that — when the other person's emotional state doesn't register in you — you can't attribute the problem to them. Their bad day doesn't "go over there." It comes in here and fills the vacuum. So when Jacob's coworker snaps at him, on an intellectual level he suspects she might just be overwhelmed. But he can't feel that she's overwhelmed, so he can't locate the problem in her. It lands in him instead. He walks away.

The Anger That's Still There

About halfway through, something shifts. Jacob says people don't deserve his care. Not because he doesn't have anything to give — but because they weren't there when he needed them. Why should they get it now?

"I just want it for myself. I feel like some reason keeping my piece of myself makes me happier. Other people can't take it."

This is the thing that makes the confusion make sense. It's not purely that Jacob doesn't know how to care. It's that a part of him doesn't want to. And that part has a completely coherent argument: you didn't earn it. You weren't there. You don't get access to me.

The anger is reasonable. But there's a problem with what's being done with it. Anger turned against the self is the engine of depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → — this is one of the oldest observations in psychotherapy, noted by Freud and confirmed repeatedly since. When you stop being angry at the world and redirect all of it inward, you become depressed. Jacob has been doing exactly this: taking the unfairness of his situation, refusing to point it outward (because that might hurt someone, or because he's accepted responsibility for everyone else's behavior), and letting it compress inward into something like a neutron star — tiny, dense, extremely heavy.

There's also a related dynamic at play: parentification. When a 10-year-old tells his mother "I was just venting" to protect her from the implications of what she found, the child has become the parent. He's managing her emotions instead of having his own attended to. This has a name, it's documented, and it messes people up in exactly the ways Jacob describes — because you spend your life accepting responsibility for things that aren't yours, which means you can never fix them, which means you can never feel confident.

Why You Can't See the Exits

Jacob used an image that captures the situation well: a roundabout. You're going in circles, but it doesn't feel like circles — it feels like forward movement, until tomorrow when you're going the other direction and that also feels like forward movement. The exits are there. The compensations we build around unresolved emotional problems often do more damage than the original problem would have. The issue isn't the wound — it's the layered, rickety armor you've built to protect it, which has its own costs: difficulty with relationships, self-blame, confusion about who you are, a life that feels oddly colorless.

The good news: Jacob is already in therapy, and people around him have noticed he seems happier, even though he doesn't feel it from the inside. This is actually normal at the start of emotional work. The stuff that was dormant becomes active. You feel more, but in a contained space, and you carry less of it around unconsciously the rest of the time.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "I don't know how" with "I'm broken." Jacob kept circling back to wondering if he was fundamentally defective. The more accurate frame: he wasn't taught. You can't model what you were never shown. His grandmother was emotionally absent with his mother; his mother, doing the best she could, didn't have a full toolkit to pass on. This is generational, not genetic.

Taking all the responsibility. When you accept blame for things that belong to someone else, you can't fix them — because they're not actually yours to fix. In a 2v2 match, if your teammate is throwing and you also accept that it's your fault you're losing, you will never win, because you've made the actual cause of the problem invisible. Responsibility has to go where it actually lives.

Thinking empathy is just understanding. You can intellectually know your friend is having a bad day and still have no idea what to do about it, because the knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → doesn't connect to feeling. The missing step is being able to actually sense what's happening in the other person, not just reason about it.

Interpreting ambiguous social signals as rejection. When you can't feel what's going on with someone else, even a neutral response can read as hostile. Your friend seems distracted, you hear rejection. The antidote is buying yourself a moment: "Hey — is everything okay?" — before walking away with a conclusion.

Running from the anger. Jacob's instinct, when something painful surfaced during the conversation, was to deflect to a joke. That's fine sometimes. But anger that never comes out doesn't disappear — it compresses.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the self-referential pattern in real time. When something goes wrong in a social interaction, before concluding you screwed up, ask: "What might be going on with the other person right now?" You don't have to know the answer. Just hold the question for thirty seconds before landing on a conclusion about yourself.

  2. Practice the pause before walking away. When someone seems off, try: "Hey, is everything okay?" That's it. No fixing required. This creates space for the real explanation to surface instead of leaving you to fill the vacuum with self-blame.

  3. Stop blocking one exit from the anger. You don't have to yell at anyone. But find one outlet — exercise, music, journaling, a session with a therapist — where the resentment can actually leave your body rather than compress further inward. The goal is not hurting others and not hurting yourself. That middle path exists.

  4. Have one conversation with your mother about what her childhood was like. Not necessarily the big conversation about your notebook or your pain. Start smaller: ask her what growing up with her parents was like. Hearing her as a full person — not just your parent — loosens the grip of a lot of stored resentment, and it also shows you where some of the missing tools went.

  5. When you notice yourself describing your own life in the third person or as a hypothetical ("that would be really hard for someone"), stop and re-say it in first person. "That was really hard for me." It sounds trivial. It isn't.   6. Continue therapy. Especially given the dissociationA protective fragmentation of the self in which the brain severs communication between its emotional and analytical hemispheres — numbing feeling so that overwhelming experience becomes survivable.Full definition → piece — the disconnection from your own emotional life is something that benefits enormously from a sustained, private, one-on-one relationship where you can keep showing up.
