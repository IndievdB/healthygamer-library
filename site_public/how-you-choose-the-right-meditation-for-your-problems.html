Your Cognitive Fingerprint Determines Your Technique

Every brain processes information differently. Some people focus most naturally through the visual cortex, others through the auditory or somatosensory cortex. The same way everyone has a unique face and unique fingerprints, everyone has a unique mind — and almost nobody talks about this.

The implication: try different categories of technique, not just different variations of the same one.

For ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition → minds: Don't try to force stillness from the start. Go to any busy environment — a train station, a cafeteria — close your eyes, and try to register as many sounds as possible, as fast as possible. Don't analyze any of them. Just move from stimulus to stimulus to stimulus. This works for the same reason you exhaust a toddler at the playground before expecting them to sit still: the mind burns through its kinetic energy, and then — naturally — it slows down. For investigative or analytical minds: An introspective technique works better. Try this:

  1. Close your eyes and notice the temperature of your breath as it enters your nose.
  2. Follow that coolness — where exactly do you feel it? The tip of your nose? Your sinuses? Your throat?
  3. Notice what happens in your mind during the breath itself — thoughts cease. Then they return. Then another breath, another gap.

That gap is dhyanaA state of mind — not an action — in which the usual mental chatter falls silent and the sense of self temporarily dissolves.Full definition →, even if it only lasts a second. With practice, you're training yourself to widen it. Slow breathing expands the space between thoughts naturally.

The investigative technique works because your curiosity about your own experience becomes the anchor. You're not suppressing thought — you're redirecting it inward in a very specific, granular way.
