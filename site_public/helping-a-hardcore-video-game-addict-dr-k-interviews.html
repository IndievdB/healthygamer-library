Summary

TL;DR — Gaming addiction isn't a character flaw or a failure of willpower; it's the brain's best available solution to a real problem. The actual work is figuring out what problem it's solving.

This talk follows a live coaching session with Scott, a young man who plays World of Warcraft and other games for 18–20 hours a day. Through careful questioning, a clearer picture emerges: the gaming isn't random or senseless. It's doing a specific job — protecting Scott from a deep sensitivity to rejection and a persistent fear that he isn't good enough. The session then broadens into a wider lesson about why so many heavy gamers share this pattern, and what it would actually take to move forward.

Gaming Is a Solution, Not the Problem

The first reframe matters a lot: gaming is not the problem. It is the answer to a problem.

Scott's brain has two options. Option A: go out into the real world, apply for jobs, face rejection, feel shattered, crawl back home. Option B: log into Warcraft, rank up, feel a genuine sense of progress, accomplishment, and wholeness — guaranteed, every session.

"Your brain is smart. Your brain is like, Scott, why the f*** are we working on this CV? It's never gotten you anything. Instead we've got this other thing that gives you happiness, accomplishment, and feeling whole."

From a pure cost-benefit standpoint, the brain's choice is completely rational. The word "escape" gets rejected here because it implies failure. The word "comfort" gets rejected because it implies weakness. The better words are protective and adaptive. Gaming is adaptive because it genuinely works — it reliably delivers what the brain needs.

The problem isn't that gaming is bad. The problem is that it has become maladaptive: a short-term solution that, over time, prevents the conditions that would make it unnecessary.

The Pit, the Planks, and the Healer You're Missing

A useful image runs through the session. Imagine a pit. Gaming is a set of planks nailed over the top of that pit. The planks aren't the problem — the pit is. And the pit got dug somewhere.

In a raid, you need a tank, DPS, and a healer. Scott has a tank (gaming). He never had a healer.

What fills the pit? Rejection. More specifically: a sensitivity to rejection so acute that a single unanswered job application can send him spiraling back into a 20-hour gaming session. The question the session keeps circling is: where did that pit come from?

Several candidates surface: - Childhood eczema and hay fever that made Scott self-conscious about his appearance at school - Being called names and bullied at 13–14 - Moving countries at age 11 and being the new, visibly different kid - Growing up with a father whose alcoholism made home life unpredictable - His father's death the previous year — and the loss of the chance to make him proud

None of these alone is "the root." The honest answer is that the pit was dug by a collection of smaller rejections accumulating over years with nowhere to go.

How an Alcoholic Parent Quietly Destroys Confidence

One of the more clinically interesting moments in the session is the discussion of what an unpredictable parent does to a child's sense of confidence.

Confidence, at its core, is the ability to predict outcomes. A child becomes confident in walking because they can predict what happens when they take a step. They become confident in a caregiver because the caregiver behaves consistently. Order is the source of confidence — not success or failure.

"Can you imagine how anxiety-provoking it would be if we did not know which direction gravity went? If gravity sometimes went up and sometimes went down, how confidently do you think I would move through life?"

A parent who is one person sober and a different person drunk is, for a child, gravity that goes both directions. You don't know what you're going to get. You can't build predictions. You can't build confidence.

The consequence: the child grows up scanning for certainty, deeply unsettled by disorder, and gravitating hard toward any environment that offers reliable, predictable outcomes. Enter: video games. WoW is the same game every single time. The honor system behaves exactly as advertised. The rules do not change based on whether someone has been drinking.

This also explains a pattern observed across many gamers: roughly a third of those seen in individual coaching had a parent with an alcohol problem.

The 0% vs. 50% Problem

Here's one of the most counterintuitive observations in the whole session:

Many heavy gamers would rather spend their hours in a game with a 0% chance of real-world success than spend those same hours on an activity with a 50% chance of success — because the 50% scenario includes the possibility of failure, and that possibility is intolerable.

"You would rather waste your time playing video games all day long rather than waste your time on an application that may not work out. You would rather give yourself a 0% chance of success and waste your life."

This isn't laziness. It's aversion to uncertainty. What looks like avoidance is actually a logical response to an environment where unpredictable outcomes have historically meant pain.

The deeper confusion is between confidence and guarantees. Most heavy gamers don't actually want confidence — they want a scenario where confidence is unnecessary. They want the guarantee. And video games uniquely provide that: effort in, reward out, every time. Real life doesn't work that way, so real life feels unsafe.

The Numbness That Makes Everything Worse

Over time, repeated rejection doesn't just hurt — it trains the nervous system to go flat. Scott describes feeling "shattered" by rejections, but describes them in language that's vague and distant: "uncomfortable," "fine," "okay." He's not hiding — he genuinely doesn't have easy access to what he feels.

This is alexithymia: difficulty identifying and describing emotional states. It's a common adaptation when someone has spent years learning to absorb emotional hits without processing them.

The metaphor used: each rejection adds trash to your inventory. Instead of vendoring (clearing out) that trash, you close the bag. Over time, your subconscious is so full of unprocessed garbage that there's no room for anything good. The numbness isn't peace — it's a full bag that's been zipped shut.

The breathing exercise done with Scott — imagining each inhale as clean water flushing through the body, each exhale wringing out dirty water — is a simple somatic practice for beginning to process what's been stored.

The Two Scotts

Toward the end of the session, a specific dynamic gets named that feels true for many people with this pattern:

There are two versions of Scott. One knows he has potential, feels capable, applies for a job with genuine hope. The other believes, at some level, that he is a failure. Every rejection is terrifying not just because it hurts — but because it threatens to prove that the failure version is the real one.

"What is very scary for you is that you're not sure which of those Scotts is real. And every time you get rejected, maybe it terrifies you a little bit that the one who's a failure is actually the real Scott."

The goal isn't to get the "successful Scott" to win. The goal is to recognize that both of those Scotts are constructs — dirty water and clean water — and that what you actually are is the rag. The rag isn't defined by whether it's holding clean water or dirty water right now.

Common Pitfalls

Treating gaming as purely bad. Calling it an addiction, a failure, or an escape loads it with shame that doesn't help and isn't accurate. Gaming is adaptive until it isn't — understanding that distinction matters.

Looking for a single root cause. The expectation that there's one traumatic event at the center of the problem can cause people to dismiss real contributing factors ("I don't think there was one big thing") and stop digging. The pit is usually built from accumulated small injuries.

Mistaking numbness for resilience. Building resistance to rejection by going numb is not the same as becoming more confident. It just means the garbage bag fills up more quietly.

Applying for jobs one at a time and waiting. Each application becomes a referendum on your worth as a person. The cycle of hope and disappointment repeats in isolation, making the process more painful each time.

Conflating "doing something" with "working harder." The path forward isn't grinding harder at real life the way you grind a game. It's changing the system — applying more broadly, depersonalizing the process, front-loading effort to enable ease later.

Believing you know what you'd be good at or enjoy. Most predictions about enjoyment happen in the head, not in actual experience. With little real-world exposure, your estimate of your own capabilities is probably a significant underestimate.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Stop calling it an addiction (internally). When you notice yourself labeling gaming as a failure or a weakness, replace that with a question: what is this protecting me from right now? Identifying the actual need is more useful than condemning the behavior.

  2. Do the dirty-water breathing exercise. Sit up straight, close your eyes. Breathe in slowly and imagine vitality — clean water — filling every cell. Breathe out and imagine wringing dirty water (fear, rejection, shame) out of your body like twisting a rag. Do 5–10 cycles. Do it after rejection specifically, not just when you feel good.

  3. Batch your job applications. Instead of applying to one job, waiting, feeling the verdict, then recovering — apply to 10–20 at once in a single session. This depersonalizes each outcome. You are not that application. You are running a process.

  4. Do not stop applying once you get an offer. Getting one job is not the goal. Getting several options and choosing the best one is.

  5. Locate the rejection in your body. Next time something stings — a rejection, a comment, a disappointment — pause and ask: where do I feel this physically? What does it feel like in my chest, my stomach, my throat? Just noticing, without trying to fix it, is the beginning of processing rather than suppressing.

  6. Try something you can't predict. The only way to update your self-estimate is to do things with uncertain outcomes. Internships, volunteer projects, new environments — pick one thing in the next month that you genuinely don't know how you'll do, and do it not to succeed but to find out.

  7. Consider seeing a therapist again. Not because something is catastrophically wrong, but because processing the accumulated inventory of unvendored rejections is hard to do alone. A therapist is closer to the healer role the session identifies as missing.
