The Three Layers of Reality: Matter, Energy, and Experience

Most people think of the world as primarily material — things you can see, touch, and measure. But there's a layer of existence that doesn't reduce to physical objects: experience itself.

When you taste a strawberry, yes, taste buds fire, signals travel through the gustatory cortex, the thalamusThe brain's sensory gateway — every signal from your eyes, ears, nose, skin, and internal organs passes through the thalamus before reaching the rest of the brain.Full definition → processes them, and something happens in the brain. Science can map every step of that chain. But the actual feeling of sweetness — what philosophers call qualia — is categorically different from the nerve signals that produce it. Experience isn't just complicated matter. It's a different kind of thing, in the same way that matter and energy are different kinds of things even though one can be converted into the other.

Here's a useful analogy: matter can be assembled at different scales. Molecules become cells, cells become organs, organs become a person. The complexity changes with the arrangement, but it's all still matter at the bottom. Experience works the same way — it can take many forms, but it has its own irreducible nature underneath all of them.

The Ego as a Localizer — and a Limiter

Here's the structural idea that ties everything together.

Cosmic Consciousness (what Sanskrit calls Brahman) is the name for whatever this background field of awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → is at its largest scale — not localized to any particular body or perspective. Your individual experience arises when that awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → gets localized: into a body, an energy system, a particular vantage point. The result of that localization is what we call the ego (or in Sanskrit, Ahamkara — roughly, the "I-maker").

The ego isn't evil or wrong. It's a natural consequence of being a particular person in a particular body. But it comes with a cost: bias. When the ego is strongly active, you're not seeing clearly — you're seeing through the lens of "how does this affect me, what does this mean for me, what am I in relation to this?" That lens distorts.

The practical implication is counterintuitive: the more you can quiet the ego — not suppress it, not fight it, but let its grip loosen — the more clearly you perceive reality. This is why transcendent performers often describe their best moments as ones where they "got out of their own way." That's not poetic — it's structurally accurate. The self-referential noise of the ego dropped, and perception sharpened.

Flow is one step in this direction. The ego partially dissolves. Turiya is further along the same continuum. Common Pitfalls

Confusing autopilot for mastery. High speed and low apparent effort look the same whether someone is on autopilot or in flow. The internal difference is the direction of awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →. Chasing autopilot as the goal — just drilling until something is "automatic" — may produce competence but won't produce the occasional transcendent performance that flow enables.

Using "quantum" as a shortcut. But most people who reach for quantum physics to validate spiritual ideas don't know enough physics to use it carefully. Thinking the ego needs to be destroyed. The ego is a localization of consciousness, not a malfunction. The problem isn't its existence — it's being over-identified with it, treating the localized view as the only view. Treating "knowings" as always reliable. Pure awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → states can produce a felt sense of certainty — what's described here as "knowings." But that felt certainty isn't automatically accurate. Pattern recognition (pareidolia, apophenia — seeing ferrets in clouds, finding meaning in coincidences) operates in ordinary waking consciousness and can masquerade as deeper insight. The honest check is: does the knowing hold up when the mind does its follow-up work?

Thinking flow requires a dramatic context. Flow isn't only available in high-stakes athletic or creative performance. You can enter something like it watching a sunset, in deep conversation, in any absorbed engagement. The key variable is not the drama of the situation but the quieting of self-referential ego chatter.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Distinguish autopilot from flow in your own experience. After a skilled performance (gaming session, music practice, sports, coding), ask yourself: was awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → higher than normal, or lower? Did you "forget yourself" because you were absorbed, or because you were running on habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition →? Naming the difference builds the ability to aim for one over the other.

  2. Use absorption as a training ground. Pick one activity this week that you find genuinely beautiful or absorbing — a piece of music, a sunset, a view — and let it take over without immediately narrating it to yourself ("this is nice," "I should take a photo"). Practice staying in the direct experience before the mind labels it.

  3. Notice the moment "knowing" arrives before language does. In conversation, in a creative problem, in a decision — pay attention to whether there's a beat of clarity before you've formed a sentence about it. Don't force it. Just notice when it happens, and notice what the mind does with it afterward.

  4. Audit your ego activity during performance. During any skill-based activity, notice how much of your mental bandwidth is going to self-referential thoughts: Am I doing well? What does this say about me? Are they judging this? That's ego-load. It doesn't have to be eliminated — just noticed.   5. Experiment with the subject-object boundary. In your next deep conversation, instead of tracking "what I want to say next," try resting in genuine curiosity about the other person — letting them fully occupy your attention. Notice whether the quality of connection shifts. This is a low-stakes way to practice the ego-softening that underlies flow.

  6. Hold the quantum-consciousness parallel loosely. If you're interested in the physics angle, pick up a grounded text on quantum mechanics (something like In Search of Schrödinger's Cat is mentioned as a relatively accurate lay introduction). Let the ideas inform your thinking without using them as proof of anything. Good metaphors are useful; claimed proofs based on misunderstood physics aren't.
