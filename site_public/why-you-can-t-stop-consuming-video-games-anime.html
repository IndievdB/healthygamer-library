The Compulsion to Consume Isn't Random

Dom doesn't casually play a game or watch an episode. He devours. He read all of Berserk in a weekend at age thirteen. He played through both Knights of the Old Republic games back-to-back — 41 hours, then 36 — without coming up for air. He binged all of Attack on Titan across a single weekend instead of doing assigned training, then immediately jumped to the manga when the show ended because he couldn't stop.

"I feel like I'm just trying to fill something that can't be filled."

When he's inside a binge, it feels natural — just watching, just playing. When he surfaces, he looks around and asks himself what happened. It maps closely onto how addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → works: a cloudy, hedonistic state followed by a kind of hangover, self-loathing, and then, eventually, diving back under.

Part of what makes this so sticky is when the habit started. The earlier someone is exposed to a highly stimulating behavior, the more powerful the pull becomes — because the developing brain builds its baseline reward circuitry around that stimulus. Dom was gaming with his dad until 3 a.m. at age eleven. That's not just a memory; it's a neurological foundation.

What the Media Is Actually Doing

The consumption isn't filling empty time. It's doing several things at once:

  • Regulating emotion. After his parents' divorce at age ten, Dom threw himself into Lord of the Rings discs, cycling through every special feature, every movie in the house — anything to not feel the unease of a split family, an empty father's apartment, and a mother who had gone quiet.
  • Substituting connection. Gaming was how Dom bonded with his dad. It's how the whole family bonded. The computer became the mode of transportation to belonging.
  • Avoiding identity friction. When Dom is between worlds — not quite the athlete, not quite the nerd — the screen is a place where that tension disappears temporarily.
  • Escaping a self he doesn't recognize. In his darkest moments, Dom looks in the mirror and sees someone he doesn't know. The consumption is a way to not have to sit with that.

Being Stuck Between Two Worlds

Dom is genuinely caught between two identities and two sets of people. The jocks don't see him as one of them because he'd rather play Hearthstone. The nerds don't look like him or move through the world the way he does. He mentioned Naruto in a group of athletes once and immediately got sorted into the "loser" category — then drifted away from the nerds too because their ambitions didn't match his.

This in-between state shows up everywhere: in his goals (professional runner vs. Twitch streamer), in his family (Black kid in a jigsaw family with a white stepfather), in his social life (chad-touched, but with the heart of a weeb), even in something as small as wanting Justin Bieber's hair as a kid because everyone else had it.

The mistake is trying to resolve the tension by becoming one thing or the other. Playing the half-orc like an orc means losing. Playing the half-orc like a human also means losing. The identity work isn't about picking a side — it's about recognizing that the combination is the thing.

"You are not what you want to be. What you want to be is not who you are."

The Real Source of the Anger

Dom describes an anger that doesn't attach to any one thing — it finds something to latch onto, escalates, and spirals: parents, politics, the Smash Bros. meta. The anger isn't caused by those things. Those are triggers for something that already exists inside him.

That something has a shape. Dom didn't want his parents to divorce. He didn't want to shuttle between two houses on Christmas morning and watch a parent wave from the sidewalk. He didn't want to feel like an outsider in his own skin growing up in a predominantly white area. He didn't want to spend hundreds of hours on games when he could have been building something. He didn't want to lie to his coach.

He acknowledges his stepfather's love — genuinely and movingly. But love and harm aren't mutually exclusive. A parent can love you completely and still pass on patterns — the late-night gaming sessions, the emotional unavailability, the model of using screens to check out — that you'll spend years trying to undo. Recognizing the harm doesn't erase the love. It just makes the harm visible enough to actually work on.

The "What's the Cost of a Weekend?" Trap

One of the subtler traps Dom is caught in is the logic of the long horizon. When you have four years to reach a goal, the cost of one weekend is essentially zero. No one studies for a test a month away. No one trains on the day that doesn't matter.

"Your brain calculates behavior in the long term, not in the short term — which is why procrastination is conserved across the human race."

The problem is that every day is that day. The binge never happens in one sitting — it's one day that bleeds into the next because the math always says tomorrow is fine. This is why thinking about the goal is actually counterproductive. The goal is too far away to make today feel costly.

The reframe that helps: instead of asking what does this do to my five-year plan? ask who do I want to be today? Not who do I want to become — who do I want to be right now, in this hour. That question has a real answer. The five-year version doesn't.

The Suicidal Ideation Underneath

Dom is candid about something he'd never quite named before: a recurring wish to not exist. Not active planning — more like a passive fantasy of escape. Wishing a car would hit him on the way to a track meet he didn't want to attend. Speeding toward a stone wall after a winning race, feeling utterly alone despite the trophies. Looking in the mirror during dark stretches and not recognizing the person looking back.

"I don't want to wake up tomorrow — and I'm just going to stay up, but I don't want to stay up, I just want this night to be over."

This tends to get triggered by interpersonal letdowns — a friend who bails, a New Year's Eve spent alone after three people cancel. The intensity of the reaction is disproportionate to the event, which signals something older underneath: the original loss of the stable, unified family. When someone Dom loves isn't available, it doesn't just feel like disappointment — it feels like the ground opening up.

He's already doing something right: he stops himself from drinking in those moments (understanding from an earlier talk that alcohol dramatically shortens the gap between passive ideation and action), goes outside, feels the wind, eats candy, writes in his blog. These aren't cures, but they're real harm-reduction strategies that he built himself.

Common Pitfalls

  • Framing it as a willpower problem. If you think you just need more discipline to stop gaming, you'll keep trying to white-knuckle your way through — and keep failing — because the behavior is meeting a real need. The need has to be addressed.
  • Giving the people who shaped the habit a complete pass. It's possible to love someone deeply and still honestly name the ways their behavior affected you. Skipping that step means you can't actually fix what needs fixing.
  • Chasing the goal rather than inhabiting the day. Nobody wants to write a book; everyone wants to have written one. The people who fixate on the destination rarely arrive. The ones who let go of it sometimes do.
  • Treating the two worlds as a binary. Trying to become fully one thing — the athlete, the nerd, the streamer, the professional — and feeling like a failure for not getting there fully is the wrong game. The mixture is the actual identity.
  • Dismissing passive suicidal ideation as "not serious." Not having a plan doesn't make it harmless. The research is clear: these decisions are often impulsive, and the gap between passive wishing and action can close very fast under the right conditions (intoxication, acute emotional pain). Taking it seriously early matters.

  3. Deal with emotional residue as it arises, not at the end of the weekend. When something stings — a friend cancels, you feel left out, you skip a workout — write about it, sit with it, name it. Don't let it accumulate and then crash over you on Sunday night. Each piece handled in the moment is a piece that doesn't pile up.

  4. Have the honest conversation with your dad. Not an accusation — a genuine question: Did gaming ever get in the way of your life? Because I'm noticing it getting in the way of mine. This opens a real conversation instead of protecting a fiction that everything was fine.

  5. See a therapist if you can access one. Especially if you experience acute feelings of not wanting to exist after interpersonal letdowns, that's worth working through with someone specifically trained for it — not because you're broken, but because understanding why those moments hit so hard will matter in every close relationship you ever have.

  6. Stop managing two parallel lives; start living one. The bass guitar, the track meets, the anime nights, the friends who show up to your meets — none of those are contradictions. You don't have to earn the "nerd" stuff by performing enough "chad" stuff first. The integration is the destination.
