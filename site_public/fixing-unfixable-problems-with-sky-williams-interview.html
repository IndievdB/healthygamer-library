Summary

TL;DR — The patterns ruining your life aren't character flaws; they're lessons you learned from people who hurt you. You can't fix what you haven't found yet.

Most people who feel like they're fundamentally broken — like they corrupt everything they touch, like they'll inevitably disappoint everyone who believes in them — aren't broken at all. They're running on old programming installed by real wounds that were never actually healed, just walled off. This conversation with Sky Williams, a content creator who came in describing his problems as "unfixable," is a masterclass in what that walling-off looks like and why simply "moving past" trauma isn't the same as healing it.

The Difference Between "Moving Past" Something and Actually Healing It

Sky came in believing his toxic patterns — taking from others, never following through, burning people who invest in him — were simply who he was. Hardwired. Unfixable.

But there's a crucial distinction between surviving something and healing from it. Sky had done extraordinary work surviving. He'd built a personality that was warm, funny, larger-than-life, surrounded by people who loved him. By most external measures he'd moved on. But the wound underneath was still there, walled off and running the show from the inside.

"You moved past it instead of fixing it. You moved on with your life without actually addressing the underlying issue."

The medical analogy that makes this concrete: when your body can't fight an infection directly, it sometimes walls it off into an abscess — a sphere of scar tissue that contains the infection but doesn't eliminate it. The infection keeps running rampant inside. Nothing gets in, nothing gets out. Your body treats this as a solved problem, but it isn't. The only way to actually heal an abscess is to open it.

Humor, deflection, "no regrets" philosophies, keeping yourself constantly surrounded by people — these can all be forms of abscess tissue. They let you function. They don't let you heal.

How a Core Wound Becomes a Worldview

Sky's story revealed two core beliefs that were quietly controlling his entire life:

  1. "I will inevitably disappoint anyone who believes in me."
  2. "Some hurts are so deep they can never be repaired."

These weren't conclusions he reasoned his way to. They were lessons — taught repeatedly, by people he loved: a brother who betrayed him after a sexual assault, a mother who didn't know who to believe, a father whose conditional love came with conditions Sky could never meet. The same message, from multiple trusted sources, over years: you are a disappointment, and some damage cannot be undone.

"They just rammed that into you, over and over again — that there is hurt in this world that nothing can fix. And so that became truth."

Once that belief is installed, it starts generating evidence for itself. Sky would receive support from his community, feel genuinely motivated to honor it, go to sleep — and wake up the next day with that motivation gone. Not because he was lazy or sociopathic, but because somewhere in the night, the old belief reasserted itself: it doesn't matter, you're going to let them down anyway. The hopelessness produces the failure, which proves the belief, which deepens the hopelessness.

This is what a self-fulfilling prophecy looks like from the inside.

What Looks Like "No Empathy" Is Often a Protection Against Too Much of It

One of the most striking moments in this conversation is when Sky described himself as probably "one of the most toxic people on earth" because he lacks empathy — he takes from others and doesn't deliver in return.

But look at the actual evidence: he worked three jobs to house his best friends. He feels genuine guilt about letting people down. He described feeling sick watching his viewers keep supporting him while he failed to deliver. He used the word corrupt — not "inconvenience," not "disappoint." Corrupt. That's not the vocabulary of someone who doesn't care about other people.

"A sociopath doesn't think they're corrupting anyone. They just don't give a damn."

What looked like a lack of empathy was actually the protective layer over an enormous amount of it. When you feel deeply and you've also learned that the people you love will hurt you in ways that can't be fixed, one very logical response is to construct a rule: I don't regret my decisions. If you don't regret your decisions, you don't have to feel the guilt. If you don't feel the guilt, you don't have to feel the hurt underneath it.

The protection is real. The empathy underneath it is also real. The "no empathy" diagnosis — whether it came from a clinician at 15 or from Sky's own self-assessment — was describing the armor, not the person wearing it.

Detection Before Control

There's a principle from contemplative traditions that applies directly here: awareness precedes control. You cannot change a pattern you cannot see.

Sky's core wound had been operating like a stealth unit — invisible, running through his subconscious, sabotaging intentions he genuinely had. He'd make a real commitment, feel it slip away, and have no idea why. That's what stealth does. You can't counter what you can't detect.

The first practical move isn't fixing the wound. It's learning to see it in action. Specifically, to catch the moment when the old belief surfaces and starts making decisions on your behalf — usually not at the moment you accept someone's help or make a promise, but the next day, when the genuine intention starts to fade and something hollow replaces it.

In that moment, the question to ask yourself is: What is running in the background right now? Where is this feeling coming from? Is this actually true, or is this the old lesson reasserting itself?

That moment of recognition doesn't fix anything by itself. But it breaks the automation. It takes something that was happening to you and turns it into something you can observe — which is the necessary first step before any real change.

"Repair" Doesn't Mean What You Think It Means

One thing that keeps people locked in this pattern is a misunderstanding of what healing would even look like. Sky couldn't imagine repairing his relationship with his brother because that would mean... what, exactly? Forgiving the unforgivable? Pretending it didn't happen? Acting like they're fine?

That's not what repair means.

"Repair means that you're gonna engage with it, process it, grieve it, and then let go."

It doesn't require the other person to apologize. It doesn't require reconciliation. It doesn't require the harm to be undone, because the harm can't be undone. What it requires is that you stop carrying the unprocessed wound forward — that you let it be finished inside you, rather than running quietly in the background forever.

The belief that some wounds are permanently irreparable is itself the wound. And it was installed by people who never repaired what they did to you. You learned the lesson they taught by example.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the armor for the person. A diagnosis of antisocial personality, or a self-image of "I just don't have empathy," can become a story you live inside of rather than a thing you examine. The patterns are real; the explanation for them might not be.

Thinking "moving on" means "healed." Functioning well, being funny and likeable and productive, does not mean the underlying wound has been addressed. The abscess can stay walled off for years while you build an entire life around it.

Confusing the consequences of a belief with the belief itself. Sky spent a lot of time focused on his YouTube numbers, his failed friendships, his inability to follow through. Those are real problems. But they're downstream of something more fundamental. Fixing the symptoms without finding the source is why the same patterns keep returning.

Using self-awareness as another deflection. There's a version of "I know I'm toxic" that functions as a way of not actually doing anything about it — a performance of honesty that lets you off the hook because at least you're not lying to yourself. Naming the problem isn't the same as working on it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Find your core belief. Ask yourself honestly: what do I believe about myself that feels so true it never needs to be questioned? Common candidates sound like: "I'll always let people down," "I don't deserve good things," "people who love me eventually get hurt." Write it down.

  2. Trace it to its source. Who taught you that belief? Not who "made you feel bad" in a vague sense — who specifically, and how specifically? You're looking for the lesson, not just the emotion.

  3. Practice stealth detection. For the next week, when you notice yourself about to repeat a pattern you don't like — avoiding a follow-through, pulling back from someone, self-sabotaging — pause and ask: What belief just activated? What does the voice in my head think is going to happen here? You don't have to fix it yet. Just name it.

  4. Distinguish the armor from the wound. If you have a coping strategy you're proud of — a philosophy, a sense of humor, a way of framing painful things — sit with the question: Is this genuinely how I feel, or is this how I survive feeling something else? You're not trying to dismantle the armor; you're trying to know it's there.

  5. Redefine "repair." If there's someone you've hurt, or someone who hurt you, and you've been avoiding that because "it can never be fixed" — consider that repair might not mean restoration. It might just mean engaging with it honestly, grieving what was lost, and releasing the story that this wound defines you. That work may be best done with a therapist, but it starts with recognizing that "unfixable" might be a story, not a fact.
