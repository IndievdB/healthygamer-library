Summary

TL;DR — You can't find peace by walling off pain and performing a better version of yourself. Healing requires facing what's buried, not burying it deeper.

This is a live coaching session exploring why people split into a "public self" and a "private self," why that split is so hard to close, and what it actually takes to move toward peace. The conversation centers on a streamer who built an enormously successful online persona while quietly grieving a family torn apart by illness — but the core dynamic plays out in countless lives that have nothing to do with streaming.

The Vacuum Problem: Who Are You Without a Role?

When you don't have a clear sense of who you are on the inside, the outside world rushes in to fill that space. You become whoever you're around, whatever goal you're chasing, whatever role gets the most applause.

This isn't weakness — it's just what happens when the interior is empty. Nature abhors a vacuum. The danger is that the role starts to feel like the self. You go all-in because going all-in is the only thing that drowns out the question of who you actually are underneath.

How a Persona Gets Built

The persona doesn't come from nowhere. It usually starts from something real — a genuine trait, a talent, a way of making people laugh. But then something goes wrong in life, and the persona becomes more than an act. It becomes armor.

The pattern looks like this:

  1. Something painful happens that's hard to face.
  2. You discover that performing a version of yourself gets positive responses.
  3. That positive reinforcement becomes, in the words used here, "oxygen and water and lifeblood" — not just nice to have, but necessary.
  4. The more dependent you become on that response, the more completely you inhabit the role.
  5. Your sense of worth becomes tied to how you're perceived, not who you are.

"Who you are becomes determined by how you are viewed."

The cruel irony is that the positive reinforcement that felt like salvation was actually one of the worst things that could have happened — because it made the persona feel essential.

The Authentic Self Gets Pushed Into the Shadows

Every spotlight has a shadow. If Mitch Jones is in the light — clowny, entertaining, performing — then David is in the dark. And David is where all the real stuff lives: sensitivity, empathy, grief, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →.

The reason you keep the authentic self hidden isn't stupidity or cowardice. It's logic. Every time vulnerability showed up publicly, it got punished. Showing emotion led to ridicule. So the mind learns: keep David offstage. Mitch Jones is safe. David is not.

But here's what that costs you:

  • The moments when real human connection is possible — meeting someone in person, a quiet conversation — you're still performing. People see the persona, not you.
  • The loneliness that drove you into the persona gets worse, because now even the moments that could break through it are blocked.
  • You live in a permanent tug-of-war, exhausted by the switching.

"Mitch Jones is your savior and he's what's holding you back."

Complex Trauma Doesn't Work Like Simple Grief

There's a difference between getting hit once and getting hit over and over. Simple traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → is a single event you can grieve and (eventually) move past. Complex trauma is ongoing — a loved one deteriorating slowly, a wound that reopens every time you go home, every time you see how much has been lost.

You can't "process" that and move on because it isn't over. You're not grieving something finished. You're grieving something that keeps changing, keeps taking more. That's not a failure to heal — that's just what the situation actually demands.

This matters because if you blame yourself for not being "over it," you're misunderstanding the problem entirely.

The Fidget Mechanism

When the mind approaches something unbearable, it finds something to do instead. It opens a new browser tab. It shifts to talking about a safer kind of pain. It floods with thoughts about anything other than the real thing.

The persona is the ultimate fidget. When you go all-in on streaming, there is literally no room left to think about anything else. You can't hold grief and hold a live performance simultaneously. The performance requires everything — which is precisely the point.

Recognizing this doesn't mean the persona is fake. The clowny, warm, social part of the self is real. But it's being used as an escape hatch, and that use is what keeps the deeper wound from healing.

What Healing Actually Looks Like

Healing does not look like health bars refilling. It looks like the thing you most want to avoid.

During a brief breathing exercise, the emotions that surfaced — helplessness, grief, heaviness in the head, tightness in the chest — felt overwhelming enough to stop. Afterward: a strange lightness. That lightness is the healing. The white blood cells only reach the wound if you stop covering it up.

"There is no way you can attain peace while that is in there."

The goal isn't to wallow permanently in grief. It's to stop interrupting the process. Let the emotion move through. Don't rush back to "normal" — because rushing back to normal is exactly what prevents resolution.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the persona for the problem. The clowny, social, entertaining part of the self is real and worth keeping. The goal isn't to become a solemn, stripped-down version of yourself — it's to stop using the persona as a hiding place.

Believing that healing will destroy your edge. People almost universally fear that if they fix the underlying wound, the drive and the creativity will go with it. This doesn't happen. Healing removes weight, not capability. You become lighter to work harder, not softer.

Treating brief emotional contact as full grief. Feeling something once, getting overwhelmed, and then returning to normal is not grieving. It's visiting the wound. Actual processing requires staying with it rather than quickly recovering composure.

Engaging with every wave. Trying to convince negative people they're wrong, or prove something to critics, nearly always comes from ego rather than from any genuine desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → to help them. Diving through the wave — moving past without engaging — is not passivity; it's stability.

Expecting online connection to be "not real." It isn't nothing. But it's also not enough on its own. Both things can be true.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice when you're fidgeting away from something. The next time your attention suddenly jumps — phone, tab, subject change — pause and ask what you were just about to feel. Don't force it. Just notice.

  2. Try the breathing pattern introduced here. Inhale through the nose for 3–5 counts; exhale through the mouth for 5–9 counts (exhale longer than inhale). Sit up straight, hips higher than knees if possible. When an emotion surfaces, breathe into the physical sensation of it instead of thinking about it.

  3. Attend a yoga or Tai Chi class at least three times. Not for the stretch. For the mind-body contact. The physical postures ground attention in the present and let stored emotion release without the mental rush that pure meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → can trigger.

  4. Find a therapist and go more than once. One session rarely gets far enough. The emotional material is close to the surface; a consistent, private relationship with a therapist gives it somewhere to go safely.

  5. Practice the wave image for online negativity. When criticism or trolling pulls at you, visualize diving through the wave rather than pushing against it. Engaging to prove you're right agitates the water. Passing through it restores stillness.

  6. Audit the black-and-white swings. When you notice yourself at either extreme — relentless positivity or complete withdrawalThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → — recognize both as the same mechanism. Ask: what am I trying not to sit with right now?

  7. Schedule time to grieve, not just to cope. "Facing it" doesn't mean briefly feeling sad and then getting back to work. Set aside protected time — with a therapist, in a journal, or in stillness — where there's no expectation of returning to normal at the end of the hour.
