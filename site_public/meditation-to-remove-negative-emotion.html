Emotions as Digestive Blockages

Think about what happens in successful therapy. A person carries something heavy — grief, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, fear — for months or years. Then, in a session, something breaks open. Emotion rushes up. It's intense and uncomfortable. And yet, people consistently describe feeling lighter afterward, not worse.

The same thing happens at funerals. Everyone gathers and deliberately enters grief together. It's painful. But most people leave feeling more at peace than when they arrived.

What these two experiences have in common is that the emotion is actually processed — moved through — rather than blocked. The Manipura chakra, located at the navel, is understood in yogic tradition as the energetic center responsible for this kind of digestion, both physical and emotional.

When you're stuck in a loop — avoiding the outside world, chained to a screen, unable to act on what you know you should do — there's often an emotional energy underneath that you haven't been able to digest. You haven't analyzed your way out of it because analysis isn't the tool for this job. The body is.

The Three-Phase Practice

This is a guided meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → you can do on your own. Sit up straight, close your eyes, and move through the following phases.

Phase 1 — Belly Breathing (5 breaths)

Breathe in slowly and push your belly outward as the air comes in. As you exhale, contract the abdomen. This is diaphragmatic breathing, and it anchors your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → to the navel center. Do five full rounds, keeping your attention on the physical sensation of the belly expanding and releasing.

Phase 2 — Following the Channel

Continue breathing at a comfortable pace, but now track the breath as a continuous channel of sensation. As you inhale, feel the cool air enter at your nose and follow it all the way down through your chest, through your torso, and into the point of your navel. As you exhale, follow the reverse: warmth rising up and out of you. Breathe slowly enough that you can actually stay with this sensation the whole way. The slower, the better.

Phase 3 — The Mantra: Ram

Now add the mantra RAM (pronounced "rum") to your exhalation. There are three options depending on what feels natural to you:

  • Listen for it — as you breathe out, simply hear the sound of ram in your own exhalation, as though the breath itself is making the sound.
  • Repeat it silently — on each exhale, mentally say ram to yourself.
  • Chant it aloud — take a deep slow breath in, feel the belly expand, and on the exhale, sound out ram audibly and feel the vibration resonate in your abdomen.

All three are valid. Different cognitive temperaments will find different options easier. Some people naturally prefer listening; others need to actively vocalize. Try each and notice which one pulls your attention most completely into the practice.

What You're Doing When You Practice This

The point is not to think your way out of an emotion or to achieve some intellectual understanding of why you feel what you feel. The point is to create the conditions for emotional movement.

"For most of our life we are filled with emotions — they swirl through us, wreaking havoc on our mind, on our behaviors, on our relationships. We try really hard to block out the torrent of what we feel. We numb ourselves with shows, games, drugs — all to deal with these little things we call emotions."

The practice gives those emotions somewhere to go. When you breathe into the navel and use the mantra, you're activating the body's own capacity for digestion — not just of food, but of everything you've been carrying.

After the practice, you may notice that your anxieties, fears, and even strong positive emotions feel distant. Not suppressed — just quieter, less urgent. That's not a side effect. That's the whole point.

The Long Game: Bringing Meditation Into Life

There's a meaningful sign that the practice is working: you won't want to come back when it ends.

If you sit with this for even a few minutes and feel reluctant to re-enter ordinary life, that's not a problem — it's information. It means you've touched something real. The instruction then is simple: return to life anyway, and practice again tomorrow.

Common Pitfalls

Expecting it to work like analysis. This practice isn't about gaining insight into your emotions. If you sit down hoping to understand why you feel anxious or stuck, you'll likely feel frustrated. The mechanism is physical and energetic, not cognitive.

Treating it as a one-time fix. One session of this can produce a noticeable shift, but the real transformation happens through repetition. A single funeral helps, but showing up to the practice consistently over weeks and months is what gradually changes how emotions move through you.

Choosing the wrong delivery for your temperament. If chanting out loud feels forced or embarrassing, it will pull you out of the practice rather than into it. If silent repetition feels too abstract, try chanting. There's no hierarchy — use what actually works for you.

Mistaking the post-meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → reluctance for failure. Not wanting to return to ordinary life after meditating is a sign the practice is working, not that you've become avoidant. The instruction is to return anyway and come back the next day.

Assuming it needs to be long. The practice as described involves five belly breaths, a few minutes of channel-following, and a handful of mantra repetitions. You don't need an hour. You need consistency.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Set up the basics. Find a quiet place where you can sit with a straight spine. You don't need any equipment. Even a firm chair works. Commit to doing this once a day — ideally at the same time — for two weeks before evaluating whether it's helping.

  2. Do Phase 1 alone first. Before adding anything else, spend a few days just practicing belly breathing — five slow breaths where the belly expands on the inhale and contracts on the exhale. Get comfortable anchoring attention to the navel before adding the other layers.

  3. Try all three mantra options in separate sessions. On day one, listen for ram in your exhalation. On day two, repeat it silently. On day three, chant it aloud. Notice which one keeps you most present and connected to the sensation in your belly. Stick with that one.

  4. After each session, sit for 30 seconds before opening your eyes. Notice what's different. Are your anxieties closer or farther away? Does anything feel lighter? You're not looking for dramatic results — just building the habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of noticing.

  5. When you don't want to return after meditating, note it — and return anyway. That reluctance is a signal that you've touched the stillness the practice is pointing toward. Write it down if it helps. Then re-enter life and come back to the practice the next day.

  6. Use this specifically when you feel emotionally stuck. If you notice you're procrastinating, numbing with screens, or feeling vaguely trapped by an emotion you can't name, that's exactly when to do this practice rather than reaching for the usual distraction.
