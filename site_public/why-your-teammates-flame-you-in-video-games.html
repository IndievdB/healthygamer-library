Summary

TL;DR — When you feel happy watching someone fail, it almost always means they've hurt you somehow, even if they didn't mean to.

Most people assume that delight in another person's suffering comes from jealousy, insecurity, or a bad character. It's actually simpler and more sympathetic than that. The root is almost always pain. Understanding where that pain comes from — and owning it honestly — is the way out.

The Real Reason You Want People to Fail

There's a circuit in the human brain that essentially says: if you hurt me, I want to hurt you back. This isn't a character flaw unique to toxic people. It's a deeply conserved survival mechanism. Animals fight over territory; humans do the same thing emotionally.

The problem is that in modern life, "hurt" and "territory" are no longer simple physical things. You can be hurt by someone who never laid a hand on you, someone who doesn't even know they've done anything wrong. And yet the retaliatory impulse fires anyway.

This is why people who are very angry — whether it's online toxicity, intense political hatred, or feeling satisfaction when a rival stumbles — are, in the vast majority of cases, people who have been hurt themselves. The default human response to seeing someone else suffer is compassion and empathy. What overrides that default is your own unprocessed pain.

"People who delight in the suffering of others are usually people who have been hurt themselves."

Think about why revenge narratives are so satisfying in movies. The bully gets humiliated at the end, and it feels good — universally, across cultures. That's not because audiences are cruel. It's because everyone knows what it feels like to be hurt by someone, and everyone knows how much they'd like to hurt that person back. We just call it "justice" when the story packages it that way.

Unintentional Hurt Is Still Real Hurt

Here's where it gets complicated: the person hurting you may have no idea they're doing it.

That doesn't make the pain disappear. The damage is still real even when there's no villain to blame. But people resist acknowledging this. If someone hurt you without meaning to, it feels unfair to hold it against them. So instead of processing the pain, you dismiss it entirely — I shouldn't feel this way, it's not their fault — and the hurt just sits there, unexamined and festering.

This is a trap. You can simultaneously recognize that someone didn't intend to hurt you and acknowledge that you're genuinely hurting. Both things are true at the same time. Skipping the second part doesn't make you a more reasonable person; it just means the feeling goes underground and starts leaking out sideways — as irritability, withdrawal, or that quiet satisfaction when the other person fails.

The Specific Flavor of the Pain Matters

When you notice yourself feeling glad that someone stumbled, it's worth asking: what specifically is hurting me? Because there are several distinct things that could be going on, and they each point toward something different.

A few possibilities:

  • You want what they're giving to others. You watch them laugh and connect with someone else and feel a sting of I want that — why isn't that me? The pain is about yearning, not about them being bad.
  • You envy what they are, not just what they have. Maybe you see their warmth or ease with people and think: if I were like that, things would be different for me. The hurt is wrapped up in a story about your own inadequacy.
  • You feel the asymmetry. You're treating them as special — giving them weight, attention, emotional energy — and they're treating you exactly like everyone else. That imbalance hurts in a particular way, especially when you can't even call it unfair, because they don't know about your side of the equation.

"It can be so painful to be treated just like everyone else."

That last one is especially sharp. When someone you care about deeply treats you with the same ordinary warmth they give to strangers, it can feel like a kind of erasure. You're not special to them. And because you can't blame them for not knowing, you end up blaming yourself for having feelings at all — which compounds the pain rather than resolving it.

Why You Dismiss Your Own Feelings (And Why That Backfires)

There's a specific loop that keeps people stuck:

  1. Someone hurts you unintentionally.
  2. You tell yourself you can't blame them because they didn't mean it.
  3. You conclude you therefore shouldn't feel hurt.
  4. You disregard the feeling entirely instead of accepting it.
  5. The unprocessed hurt expresses itself in other ways — withdrawal, quiet resentment, or satisfaction at seeing them fail.

The guilt makes it worse. You feel something, then you feel ashamed of feeling it, then you suppress it harder. None of this moves you forward.

The alternative is to accept the feeling without making it a verdict on either you or the other person. I'm hurting. I don't need to assign blame for that to be true. I can feel this, look at it clearly, and work with it.

"If I can't blame him, I shouldn't feel this way — and so you disregard all of your feelings instead of accepting them and owning them."

Owning your feelings isn't the same as acting on them or dumping them on someone else. It just means not lying to yourself about what's happening inside you.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the symptom for the cause. Jealousy, insecurity, and the urge to see someone fail are symptoms. The cause underneath them is almost always hurt. Trying to fix the symptom — telling yourself to "stop being jealous" or "be a better person" — doesn't touch the root.

Thinking unintentional harm doesn't count. It does. Someone can hurt you without trying to, and that pain is valid and real. Refusing to acknowledge it because "it's not their fault" is a form of self-abandonment.

Treating your feelings as a moral verdict. Feeling glad when someone you resent has a bad day doesn't make you a bad person. It makes you someone who is in pain. Treating it as evidence of your own badness just adds another layer of guilt that makes the feelings harder to look at clearly.

Assuming this only applies to romantic situations. The same dynamic — hurt producing the desire to see someone fail — shows up in friendships, family relationships, competitive settings, and online spaces. The context changes; the mechanism doesn't.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the moment of satisfaction. The next time you notice yourself feeling glad that someone failed or struggled, pause. Don't judge it. Just ask: where is this coming from? Has this person hurt me in some way? Even if it was unintentional.

  2. Trace the specific hurt. Ask yourself which flavor of pain is operating. Is it yearning — you want something they're giving to others? Is it envy of who they are? Is it the asymmetry — you're treating them as special and they're not returning it? Getting specific is more useful than a vague sense of "I feel bad."

  3. Separate blame from acknowledgment. Practice saying to yourself: This person didn't mean to hurt me, and I am still hurting. Both sentences can be true. You don't need to assign fault in order to recognize and validate your own pain.

  4. Pay attention to what triggers you. If being around a specific person consistently leaves you feeling drained or resentful, sit with what exactly is happening in those moments. What do you want that you're not getting? What do you see that reminds you of something you lack or long for?

  5. Work with the feelings rather than suppressing them. Journaling, long walks, talking it through with someone you trust, or working with a therapist or coach are all legitimate ways to process pain rather than let it calcify into resentment. The goal isn't to perform equanimity — it's to actually feel and move through what's there.
