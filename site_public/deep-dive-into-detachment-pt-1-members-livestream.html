The Anatomy of Grasping

Grasping is what happens in the seconds immediately after a pleasant experience ends. You finish a great bite of food and the hand reaches for another before you've even swallowed. Stage Two: "Tat Tvam Asi" — The Advanced Ground of Detachment

Stage one says: I can't control the outside world, so I devote myself to my actions. This is true and useful. But there's a deeper layer.

Stage two recognizes that the division between "you" and "the outside world" is itself the misidentification (avidyā). The Sanskrit mahāvākya "tat tvam asi" — that thou art, or I am that — points to this directly. Everything in the world around you is not separate from you. It is all one system. Your ego is not the self; it is one expression of something larger, like a sock puppet operated by the same hand that operates every other sock puppet.

This sounds abstract, but it has a concrete psychological effect: when you stop experiencing the world as something happening to you, you stop needing to defend against it. "Why would you burn your hand? Why would you hurt another human being? Y'all are all part of the same system."

The compassion that emerges from this realization isn't even really compassion in the usual sense — it's self-care at a scale that includes everyone.
