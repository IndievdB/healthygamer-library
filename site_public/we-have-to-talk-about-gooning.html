Summary

TL;DR — The brain's sexual pleasure system can be "min-maxed" the same way junk food min-maxes hunger, and doing so carries real risks for your ability to connect with other people.

A new practice called gooning — essentially taking masturbation to its physiological extreme through elaborate setups, multiple screens, incense, and prolonged edging — is spreading online. To understand why it works and why it might be harmful, you first need to understand the basic neuroscience of sexual pleasure, how drugs interact with it, and what happens when any biological system gets deliberately over-exploited.

How Sexual Pleasure Actually Works in the Body

Sexual arousal is unusual because it requires both branches of the autonomic nervous system working together — something that almost never happens elsewhere in the body.

  • The parasympathetic nervous system (rest-and-digest) drives the initial arousal phase: blood flow, engorgement, erection, clitoral vasodilation. Relaxation is a prerequisite, not an accident.
  • The sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight) then activates to trap that blood flow and push toward orgasm.

You can visualize the whole sexual act as a curve: stimulation increases along the x-axis, and there's a threshold of orgasm — a critical point that, once crossed, triggers climax. A few things shift where that threshold sits:

  • Sexual abstinence lowers the threshold (easier, faster orgasm).
  • SSRIs raise the threshold (harder to orgasm — sometimes used clinically for premature ejaculation).

The important insight is the area under the curve. That area roughly represents the total pleasure extracted from the sexual act. A quick session produces a small area. A longer, slower build — with more stimulation before the threshold is crossed — produces a much larger area. This is the literal geometry behind why foreplay feels so much better than skipping straight to the end.

Why Drugs, Foreplay, and Fetishes All Work the Same Way

CNS depressants like alcohol and cannabis activate the parasympathetic nervous system and slow neural firing, which raises the orgasm threshold. This means:

  • The sexual act lasts longer before climax.
  • The area under the curve — total pleasure — grows significantly.
  • Go too far, though, and you push the threshold so high that orgasm becomes impossible altogether.

Foreplay and fetishistic behavior do something structurally similar without substances. Activities that don't directly stimulate the genitals — BDSM, erogenous-zone play, flirtation — keep you moving along the early, low part of the curve without triggering the critical threshold. Pain stimuli in BDSM can actually raise the threshold further, enabling even more buildup. Then, once direct stimulation is added, the payoff is dramatically larger.

"The reason that we want to do foreplay — or people enjoy engaging in fetishistic behavior — is because this is kind of what the curve looks like."

Masturbation without any of this context is the opposite: a steep, fast climb to orgasm with a small area under the curve. It's efficient but not particularly rich.

What Gooning Actually Does Neurologically

Gooning is a deliberate attempt to engineer the largest possible area under the curve through edging — repeatedly approaching orgasm without crossing the threshold — combined with a hyper-stimulating environment (multiple screens, sensory cues, ritualistic setup).

Each near-orgasm that doesn't complete appears to gradually raise the threshold further, allowing the next approach to involve even more accumulated stimulation. The result is a dramatically amplified total dopamine and pleasure release — far beyond what ordinary masturbation or even typical partnered sex produces.

This makes gooning genuinely effective at what it's trying to do. That's exactly the problem.

The Twinkie Problem: Exploiting a Biological System

Every system in your body was shaped by evolution to pursue a specific goal. High-fat, high-sugar foods taste incredible because calorie-dense food was rare and survival-critical for most of human history. The system worked perfectly — until someone invented the Twinkie, which delivers the reward signal so efficiently that it decouples pleasure from the underlying need. The result is obesity, metabolic disease, and a palate that finds vegetables boring.

The same logic applies to other brain circuits:

  • Video game addiction exploits the identity and achievement system — you get the neurological feeling of accomplishment without the real-world outcome.
  • Social media exploits social validation circuitry.
  • Gooning exploits the dopaminergic and sexual pleasure circuitry.

"Generally speaking, when we exploit our body by finding particular systems and hyperactivating them, it usually does not end well."

The sequence historically has been: first we unlocked the food exploit, then the identity/achievement exploit, now the sexual pleasure exploit.

The Loneliness Risk Nobody Talks About

The drive to have sex with another person is, evolutionarily, the primary engine that pushes humans toward connection. It's uncomfortable, it requires vulnerability, it means tolerating rejection. But that discomfort is part of what makes relationships form.

When you can fully satisfy that drive alone — and at a higher intensity than any partner realistically provides — the incentive to pursue real connection weakens. You've killed the hunger before you could be motivated to find actual nourishment.

"The moment that we start getting our needs met by ourselves, it disincentivizes us to connect with other human beings."

This doesn't mean everyone who masturbates is doomed to isolation. But a practice specifically optimized to be more pleasurable than partnered sex, conducted in a private "goon cave," is a meaningful step further down that road. Given how bad the loneliness epidemic already is, adding a high-efficiency substitute for the one drive that most reliably pushes people toward others seems like poor timing.

The Conditioning Problem

There's a second, more clinical concern. The body's sexual response system is highly trainable. People who have masturbated daily since early adolescence — using a specific grip, a specific type of stimulation, a specific category of pornography — sometimes find in adulthood that they cannot achieve orgasm through partnered sex. Their nervous system has been conditioned to a stimulus that a real partner cannot replicate.

Pornography already functions as a supernormal stimulus — everything is bigger, louder, more extreme, higher resolution than real life. Gooning takes that further by layering supernormal pornography onto a maximally engineered physical experience. If your baseline for sexual pleasure becomes "multiple monitors, hours of edging, perfect sensory environment," ordinary partnered sex is going to feel like a downgrade. That's not a metaphor; it's a conditioned response.

Common Pitfalls

  • "It feels good, so it must be fine." Twinkies also feel good. The question isn't whether something activates a reward circuit — it's whether doing so serves your broader wellbeing or undermines it.
  • "This is just sex-negative moralizing." The concern here isn't moral — it's mechanical. The same framework that explains why gooning works is the same framework that predicts its risks. This is physiology, not judgment.
  • "I can stop whenever I want." That's what makes conditioned responses tricky — they don't announce themselves. The person who can no longer orgasm through vaginal intercourse didn't decide to train their body that way.
  • "There's no research, so there's no problem." Absence of studies on a brand-new behavior isn't evidence of safety. The extrapolations from pornography use, compulsive masturbation, and supernormal stimulus research are the best available signal we have.
  • "Gooning is just an edgy internet joke." Many things that started as fringe internet culture became widespread behavioral patterns. Taking the underlying mechanics seriously now costs nothing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your own curve. Pay attention to whether your sexual activity tends to rush toward orgasm or involves a slow buildup. Notice what the difference in subjective pleasure actually feels like — this builds concrete intuition about the area-under-the-curve concept.

  2. Audit your baseline. Ask yourself honestly: has your threshold for sexual stimulation shifted over time? Are things that used to be exciting now boring? This is an early signal of conditioning.

  3. Notice what loneliness is driving. If you find yourself drawn to intensive solo sexual behavior, sit with the question of whether the activity is satisfying a drive that would otherwise push you toward real connection. Journaling for five minutes after can surface patterns you'd otherwise miss.

  4. Apply the Twinkie test to any habit. For any activity that feels good, ask: Is this feeding the underlying need, or is it a highly efficient substitute that kills the drive to meet the need naturally? This works for food, games, social media, and sex.

  5. Protect the incentive structure. If loneliness is a real problem in your life, be cautious about introducing more high-efficiency substitutes for the drives that would otherwise motivate connection. That doesn't mean abstinence — it means not optimizing those substitutes to the point where real connection can't compete.

  6. If you're already experiencing conditioning effects — difficulty achieving orgasm through partnered sex, inability to get aroused without specific stimuli — consider working with a therapist or psychiatrist who specializes in sexual health. This is a known, treatable pattern, not a permanent state.
