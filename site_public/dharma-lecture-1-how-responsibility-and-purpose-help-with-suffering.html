Summary

TL;DR — You can't force yourself to endure the pain of growth through willpower or good intentions alone. You need something bigger than yourself — a Dharma, a duty — that makes the suffering worth choosing.

Most people fail to change their lives not because they're lazy or broken, but because they're trying to motivate themselves with the wrong fuel. They set goals rooted in desire ("I want to lose weight," "I want financial independence") and then wonder why they can't stick to them when things get hard. The answer lies in a Sanskrit concept called Dharma — duty or responsibility — and understanding it reframes why change is so difficult and what actually makes it possible.

Growth Feels Bad, Not Good

Here's something worth sitting with: progress and growth are not pleasant experiences. The idea of progress feels good. The reward at the end feels good. But the actual process of climbing the mountain — submitting a resume when you don't know if it'll work, eating right when you hate salad, pushing through an uncomfortable social situation — feels bad. It's hard, uncomfortable, and often pointless-seeming in the moment.

This creates a cruel trap. You imagine the summit — the career, the independence, the healthy body — and your brain associates that goal with positive feelings. Then you start climbing and reality hits: it's exhausting, boring, and there's no guarantee the climb leads anywhere. So you stop. Not because you're weak, but because you signed up for a good experience and got a bad one.

"What holds you back in life is because life is crappy and you don't want to play that game."

This is especially visible in the pattern where someone would rather waste time doing nothing than waste time doing something that might not work. It sounds irrational, but it's completely consistent: if the goal is to avoid pain, a resume that might fail is just as painful as no resume, minus the false hope. Gaming, by contrast, offers reliable small rewards. The math makes sense — it's just pointed in the wrong direction.

The real goal, then, isn't to find a path without pain. It's to find a reason to choose the painful path.

What Dharma Actually Is

Dharma is the Sanskrit word for duty or responsibility. It's not about morality in an abstract sense — it's about what you're obligated to do by virtue of who you are and what you're responsible for.

The clearest way to feel what Dharma means: imagine someone points a gun at you. Your instinct is to back away. Now imagine someone points a gun at your child. Suddenly you're stepping into the path of the bullet. The gun hasn't changed. The danger hasn't changed. What changed is your relationship to the situation. You now have a reason to choose the pain.

That's Dharma. It's the thing that lets you face adversity — not by minimizing it or pretending it doesn't hurt, but by making the hurt worth it.

"Dharma is the ability to choose, tolerate, and even welcome both the good and the bad."

In the Bhagavad Gita (part of the ancient Indian epic the Mahabharata), a warrior named Arjuna is about to go into battle in a civil war and breaks down. He doesn't want to fight — he'd be hurting people he loves, including his teacher. Krishna, his charioteer, tells him he has to fight anyway — not because war is fun, but because it's his Dharma. His cause is just, it's his role, and withdrawing isn't an option. That conversation — a man paralyzed and a guide helping him find the reason to act anyway — is essentially the oldest recorded psychotherapy session.

Desires vs. Values: The Engine Problem

Most people set goals based on desires, not values. This distinction is the crux of why Dharma matters practically.

A desire originates in the senses — you see something, want it, and organize your behavior around getting it. Desires aren't evil. Wanting to lose weight, earn money, find a partner, feel less lonely — these are all reasonable desires. But desires have a fundamental weakness: they're not worth suffering for. When the going gets hard enough, the desire fades. It's not enough to override the brain's aversion to pain.

Values are different. They don't come from seeing something you want — they come from within. Helping a sick friend isn't triggered by walking past someone carrying soup. It comes from something deeper: care, responsibility, love. And crucially, values hold up under pressure in a way desires don't.

The medical school example makes this concrete: a patient who smoked was told repeatedly — by someone who genuinely wanted to help him — about lung cancer, emphysema, heart disease, strokes. He didn't quit. Then came one question: When you walk your daughter down the aisle, do you want to be wheeling an oxygen tank? He quit. Not because the information changed, but because the motivation did. That's what science calls a competing interest — something that makes you choose the painful road because the alternative is worse. Same concept, different vocabulary, as Dharma.

"Desires are not worth the suffering. That's why everyone isn't a millionaire — because everyone wants to be a millionaire but they're not willing to work for it."

The problem for a lot of people who feel stuck — unemployed, isolated, going nowhere — is that every goal they set is desire-based. Financial independence. Moving out. Getting fit. These sound like mature goals. They're actually all rooted in wanting things to feel better, which means they collapse the moment the process feels bad instead of good.

The Main Quest Problem

Think of your life like an RPG. Most people are treating side quests like main quests. Losing weight is a side quest. Making money is a side quest. Getting healthy is a side quest. You do them in service of something bigger — the main quest.

The reason the side quests feel impossible is that there's no main quest driving them. Without a main quest, losing weight is just suffering with no narrative payoff. With a main quest, it becomes something you need to do to get to the next stage. The suffering becomes purposeful.

Dharma is the main quest. And like any main quest worth caring about, it's bigger than you. It involves responsibility to something or someone outside yourself.

Frodo didn't want to go to Mordor. It was terrible the whole way. He went anyway because it was his to carry — and because the stakes were bigger than his comfort.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking your goals are values when they're actually desires. "I want to be financially independent" feels deep and important, but trace it back — where did it come from? Did you see someone else's life and want it? Did you imagine what it would feel like? That's a desire. Desires aren't wrong; they're just not enough.

Waiting until you feel motivated. Dharma is found through doing, not thinking. You won't discover what you care about by sitting with your thoughts. You find it through experience, contact with the world, showing up for other people.

Thinking "I don't care about anything" is a dead end. It's actually a diagnosis. If nothing competes with the pain of change, of course you're not changing. The task becomes finding or building something that does compete — not blaming yourself for the absence.

Treating the absence of Dharma as a character flaw. Not having a clear sense of purpose or duty isn't laziness or weakness. It's a gap that needs to be filled through experience and reflection, not self-criticism.

Looking for Dharma with your head instead of your life. You can't logic your way to a competing interest. It has to be real. It has to come from actual relationships, actual experiences, actual stakes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Make two lists. Write down everything you're currently trying to achieve or change. Then, for each item, ask: where did this come from? Did it originate in something you saw, felt through your senses, or imagined having? If yes — it's likely a desire. Mark it. Don't discard it, just recognize what it is.

  2. Find what you'd do for someone else. Think about the last time you went out of your way — genuinely, without being asked — for another person. What was the relationship? What made it feel non-negotiable? That pull is close to what values feel like. Follow that thread.

  3. Notice what happens when you restrain an impulse. When you have a strong urge to do or say something and you hold back — what do you feel? Anxiety? Anger? Relief? That emotional response tells you something real about your internal landscape. Sit with it rather than acting on it.

  4. Do something — anything — in the world today. Go for a walk. Text someone who's been having a rough time. Cook yourself a real meal. Volunteer somewhere. The specific action matters less than breaking the pattern of staying inside your head. Dharma is a product of experience, not logic.

  5. Ask yourself the hard version of the question. Like the smoking patient: don't ask "do I want to be healthy?" Ask "what does it look like if I don't change?" Make it specific and personal. Who's affected? What does that future actually look like?

  6. Treat yourself the way you'd treat a friend. If you currently have no Dharma — no duty to anyone or anything — start here: you are a person who deserves care. That's not a desire, it's a baseline value. Clean your space. Feed yourself properly. Get some sleep. Not because it feels good, but because it's what you'd do for someone you're responsible for.
