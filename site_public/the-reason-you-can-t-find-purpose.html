Summary

TL;DR — Purpose isn't something you find; it's something you build through specific, repeatable actions. And the internal feeling of having purpose is something you can actually measure and grow.

Most people treat purpose like a hidden object — something out there waiting to be discovered if they just try enough things or watch enough YouTube videos. That framing is wrong, and it's part of why so many people stay stuck. Purpose is an internal experience. It isn't correlated with having a career, a family, money, or any particular external marker. Plenty of people with all of those things feel completely directionless, and plenty of people without them feel a clear sense of meaning.

What's more, purpose isn't binary — you don't either have it or you don't. It's quantifiable, more like a percentage than a switch. That means it can be grown incrementally, which is the actually useful news here.

Purpose Is Built, Not Found

The language we use — "finding your purpose" — implies it's already out there somewhere and your job is to locate it. That framing leads people to keep looking outward: trying new hobbies, scrolling for inspiration, waiting for clarity to arrive. None of that works because it's aimed in the wrong direction.

Purpose is crafted internally through action. Three overlapping feelings — meaning, purpose, and direction — move together as one internal state. If someone feels one of them strongly, they almost always feel the others too. The practical question is: what actions reliably generate that internal state?

The Three Levers from Self-Determination Theory

Self-determination theoryMotivation that arises from novelty, genuine interest, and autonomy — not from rewards, emotions, or changes in biological state.Full definition → (roughly 30 years of research) identifies what people who feel purposeful actually do differently from people who don't.

1. Make choices — for yourself

People with a sense of purpose make choices. People without it have choices made for them.

Here's the subtle trap: if you're always asking "what's the right choice?" you're outsourcing the decision to the external world (which option pays more, which one society approves of, what your parents want). You're technically the one pressing the button, but the world is determining the outcome. That's not really choosing.

Making a genuine choice means deciding based on your own internal pull — I'm going for a hike because I feel like it. I'm reading this book because I'm curious about it. It doesn't matter how small the choice is. What matters is that it comes from you. Every time you do this, your internal sense of control, direction, and purpose increases.

2. Stretch your capacities

People who feel purposeful consistently push beyond what they think they're capable of. People who feel purposeless tend to play life on minimum effort — doing just enough to not get fired, optimizing for the path of least resistance.

The analogy is direct: muscles don't grow unless you stress them past their current limit. Self-esteemSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → and a sense of capability work the same way. If you always stay within what you already know you can do, your confidence in yourself never changes.

The size of the stretch doesn't matter as much as the fact that you're stretching at all. If you currently work out once a week, shooting for twice is a meaningful stretch. Waking up 30 minutes earlier counts. The goal is to push slightly past your current edge.

"The action that you take is not what's important. Whatever you are capable of, push yourself a little bit more."

Even when you fail, something important still happens: you register that you tried. You were a participant in your own life rather than a spectator. And more often than people expect, the attempt goes better than they predicted.

3. Relatedness

The first two levers you can pull on your own. This one is harder because it requires other people. A full sense of meaning involves being your authentic self and having someone genuinely see and respond to that.

This is increasingly difficult in a world of ghosting, AI filtering, and surface-level connection. It doesn't reduce to a simple technique, but it's worth naming: isolation is a real obstacle to purpose, and building even one or two relationships where you're authentically seen matters a great deal.

The Active vs. Passive Challenge Ratio

Here's a counterintuitive but important insight about how the brain perceives control.

A passive challenge is something difficult that happens to you — a breakup, a tax bill, a clogged toilet, a bad performance review. Most people who feel directionless experience life as an unending stream of these.

An active challenge is something difficult that you choose — training for a race, committing to a daily habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition →, setting a hard goal.

The brain doesn't calculate your sense of control based on the total number of things you're dealing with. It calculates a ratio: active challenges to passive challenges. The more things you're choosing to take on relative to things happening to you, the more in control you feel — even if the absolute number of stressors is high.

The instinct when life is overwhelming is to retreat — quit the job, ghost the person, reduce commitments, wait for the chaos to die down. But retreating from passive challenges usually creates more passive challenges downstream (the gap in your resume, the dwindling savings, the accumulating mess). The pile grows.

"Every time we try to retreat from passive challenges, we tend to create more passive challenges down the road."

The counterintuitive move: when life is coming at you hardest, add an active challenge. Start going to the gym. Pick up a meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → practice. Set a goal that matters to you. These are things you control. Adding them shifts the ratio, which shifts your brain's perception of control, which makes you less pessimistic and more capable of actually dealing with the passive stuff.

Two Techniques from Viktor Frankl

Viktor Frankl — neurologist, psychologist, Holocaust survivor — spent years thinking about how people find meaning under the most extreme conditions imaginable. He identified two practical techniques.

Dereflection

Most people who lack purpose are hyper-focused on themselves. Every bad thing that happens is processed as a statement about me — my worth, my failures, my ruined future. Frankl called this hyperreflection.

Dereflection is the deliberate practice of zooming out. Instead of "my life is over because of this breakup," the wider view is: this is a painful chapter in a longer story. It gave me experience. I have work to do now. I'll come out of this stronger.

This isn't toxic positivity or pretending things don't hurt. It's shifting from seeing individual experiences as the whole truth to seeing them as part of a broader arc. That shift, consistently practiced, is one of the ways people grow their sense of meaning.

Paradoxical Intention

We avoid the things we dread. And when we avoid them, the feared outcome becomes more likely — a self-fulfilling prophecy. Skipping the difficult performance review doesn't make the termination less likely; it makes it more likely.

Frankl's technique: deliberately look forward to the things you're dreading. Not just "white-knuckle through it" but actually change your mental attitude toward it. Frame the dreaded thing as something you have to work through to reach the other side — as evidence that you're taking control of your life rather than running from it.

"When you avoid a problem in life, the thing that you fear is more likely to happen."

The dread itself becomes a signal: this is exactly what I need to engage with.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for the right choice. Deliberating endlessly about which option is "correct" is not making a choice — it's letting external standards make the choice for you. Any genuine choice is better than perfect paralysis.

  • Thinking retreat will give you control. Reducing passive challenges by avoiding and quitting feels like gaining control. It usually does the opposite. Life keeps piling up whether you engage with it or not.

  • Setting goals from how you feel in the moment. Goals set from despair or excitement tend to be poorly calibrated — too ambitious, too rigid, abandoned when the feeling passes. Real progress is incremental: F to C, C to B, B to A.

  • Treating purpose as binary. You either have it or you don't is a bad frame. It's a quantity, not a category. Small increases in choices made, capacities stretched, and active challenges taken on all move the needle.

  • Conflating internal resistance with impossibility. Feeling embarrassed, anxious, or unmotivated when you try to implement these things is normal — it's your internal environment, not evidence that the approach is wrong. The resistance is the thing to work through, not the reason to stop.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Make one deliberate choice today — small counts. Pick something based purely on what you want, not what optimizes an external outcome. Go for the walk because you feel like it. Order what actually sounds good. Notice the difference between choosing for yourself and choosing to satisfy an external expectation.

  2. Identify your current capacity ceiling and set one stretch target. If you work out once a week, commit to twice. If you wake up at 7, try 6:30 for a week. The domain doesn't matter. What matters is picking something just past what you're confident you can do.

  3. Audit your active-to-passive challenge ratio. Write down the passive challenges currently hitting your life. Then write down the active challenges you're choosing. If you have zero or one active challenge and a long list of passive ones, add one deliberate commitment this week — something you choose, something that's difficult.

  4. Practice dereflection when you're spiraling. When you notice yourself catastrophizing about something personal ("I'm a failure," "my life is over"), try writing out a wider narrative. What does this moment look like as one chapter in a longer story? What might it be building toward?

  5. Pick one thing you've been avoiding and schedule a time to face it. Apply paradoxical intention: instead of framing it as something terrible to survive, frame it as something you need to go through to level up. The performance review. The hard conversation. The appointment you keep canceling. Set the date.
