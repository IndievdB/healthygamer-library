Summary

TL;DR — Habits don't work for everyone, and chasing them can actually keep you stuck. A better approach: make drastic changes, speedrun your basics every morning, and deliberately set up the conditions you'll wake up into tomorrow.

A lot of people struggle to build habits not because they're broken, but because their personality doesn't fit the habit model. The habit model assumes consistency, repetition, and gradual accumulation — a farmer's rhythm. But some people are more like hunters: dynamic, go-big-or-go-home, all-or-nothing. For them, trying to inch toward good behavior 1% at a time mostly produces failure, guilt, and a fridge full of rotting vegetables.

There's a different framework that works better for these people. It has two core moves: (1) make drastic changes instead of incremental ones, and (2) actively manage your "spawn point" — the conditions you set up today that determine what tomorrow morning feels like.

Why the Appeal of Habits Is Actually a Trap

Here's a counterintuitive truth: the desire to build habits is, at its root, a desire to stop struggling. A habit is an automatic action — it doesn't require effort or willpower. That's what makes it attractive. The fantasy is: once I've built all the right habits, I can be the lazy version of myself and still eat well, exercise, and get things done.

The problem is that building a habit requires the opposite of laziness. It requires struggle, every single day, for a long time. So if you're someone who's drawn to habits because you want to avoid struggle, you're likely to quit before the habit ever forms. You haven't failed at discipline — you've just been sold a path that doesn't fit how you're wired.

"The desire to create a habit-fulfilled life is a thirst for laziness, a thirst for a lack of struggle."

This isn't a moral failing. It's a mismatch between personality and strategy.

Drastic Change Works for More People Than We Admit

Society under-acknowledges a simple fact: for many people, sweeping, immediate change is more effective than gradual change.

Think about how people actually quit smoking — often cold turkey, not a 25% weekly reduction. Think about how people change their diet — not "I'll cut back on carbs," but "I'm going keto, I'm throwing out everything in my pantry, and I'm doing this for 30 days." Think about a bad relationship — not a staged breakup with decreasing text frequency, but a clean break.

The research on drastic environmental change supports this too. People who've joined the military, for instance, often report it as genuinely transformative — not because they loved every part of it, but because it imposed immediate, total restructuring of sleep, food, exercise, and routine all at once.

The key insight is that bad habits are synergistic, and so are good ones. When negative behaviors cluster together — poor sleep enabling junk food cravings, junk food causing an energy crash, the crash making exercise feel impossible — they're almost impossible to dismantle one at a time. You have to break the whole system at once.

The same logic works in your favor. When you exercise hard, you naturally crave water, not a milkshake. When you eat a real breakfast, you don't hit a desperate 2 p.m. caloric deficit that sends you toward the nearest greasy takeout. When you're genuinely tired from a full day of physical and mental effort, sleep comes easily. Good inputs chain into more good inputs. That's the synergy you're trying to activate — and drastic change is the fastest way to ignite it.

The Spawn Point Model

Every morning, you don't wake up as a continuous version of yesterday's self. You spawn into an environment that past-you created. That environment almost entirely determines what present-you will do.

"You are unlucky because the person that will inherit all of your hard work tomorrow will not be you. You will wake up as a different human being who will spawn into your life."

This reframe matters because it breaks the procrastination logic your brain uses against you. Your brain says: I can do it tomorrow, it'll be the same amount of work. That's a lie. Procrastination costs compound. Renewing something before it expires is far easier than reapplying after it does. Sending a difficult email today is far easier than sending it after three weeks of guilt have built up. Going through ten pieces of mail feels manageable; going through a three-month pile does not.

The question to ask yourself every evening isn't "did I do enough today?" It's: "What world do I want to spawn into tomorrow morning?"

  • Do I want to wake up to a clean kitchen or a pile of dishes?
  • Do I want eggs in the fridge or a takeout container from two days ago?
  • Do I want to feel hungry and ready to eat, or groggy from a late-night insulin crash?
  • Do I want my phone on the other side of the room, or within arm's reach to pull me into a 45-minute scroll before I've done anything?

Tonight's choices set tomorrow's spawn point. Every decision is really a decision about future-you.

The 90-Minute Morning Speedrun

Rather than trying to build habits you'll sustain indefinitely, the practical move is to speedrun all your basics in the first 90 minutes after waking up — before inertia, distraction, or decision fatigue can eat your day.

The rough structure:

  1. No phone within arm's reach — keep it off your nightstand; use a separate alarm if you need to
  2. Exercise — 30 minutes
  3. Meditate — 15 minutes
  4. Admin — 15 minutes (inbox, mail, bills, whatever's been piling up)
  5. Shower — 10 minutes
  6. Cook and eat a real breakfast — about 20 minutes (something simple: eggs, toast, fruit)

The total is roughly 90 minutes. This is not going to feel easy. The point isn't that it becomes automatic — it may never become automatic. The point is that by the time you sit down to work or face the day, the things that matter most are already done, and every subsequent choice (lunch, energy, sleep) becomes easier because of it.

"From the moment that you get up till 90 minutes into your day, you will have done it all."

This is why skipping breakfast hurts more than just your morning. When you enter the afternoon with a 1,200-calorie deficit, your body doesn't want broccoli — it wants the densest food available. The choices you made (or didn't make) at 7 a.m. are steering your 2 p.m. cravings.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until it feels easy. If you're telling yourself "I'll build the habit and then it'll stop being hard," you're chasing a fantasy. Accept that it will be a struggle, possibly indefinitely. The goal isn't to eliminate the struggle — it's to stop being surprised and derailed by it.

Trying to fix one thing at a time. Because good and bad behaviors are synergistic, isolating a single habit often doesn't work. Fixing sleep alone while keeping bad eating is like trying to treat depression while still drinking — the systems undermine each other. You often need to change several things simultaneously.

Trusting your brain's "do it tomorrow" logic. Your brain genuinely believes tomorrow will cost the same as today. It won't. Every delay makes the task harder — emotionally, practically, and sometimes materially.

Buying healthy food and calling it done. Having healthy food in the fridge helps, but when you hit a moment of low energy and don't want to cook, you'll order delivery anyway unless you've accepted that the struggle is the price. Buying the groceries sets the spawn point; actually cooking requires embracing the friction.

Keeping your phone next to your bed. This is the single most reliable way to burn 45 minutes before you've made a single intentional choice. Get an alarm clock. Your emergencies can wait.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Move your phone out of your bedroom tonight. Buy a cheap alarm clock if you need one. Do it before you go to sleep tonight, not "soon."

  2. Design your spawn point before bed. Spend five minutes asking: What do I want tomorrow morning to look like? Do the dishes. Lay out your workout clothes. Prep something for breakfast. Make it easy for tomorrow-you to do the right thing.

  3. Plan a 90-minute morning speedrun. Block the first 90 minutes of tomorrow for exercise, meditation, a quick admin task, and a real breakfast. Don't negotiate — just do it. It will be hard. Do it anyway.

  4. Make one drastic dietary change, not an incremental one. If you want to eat better, go to the grocery store and replace what's in your kitchen all at once. Clear out what you don't want to be eating. What's physically available is what you'll eat.

  5. Catch yourself the next time you think "I'll do it tomorrow." Ask: Will this actually cost the same tomorrow, or will it cost more? Usually it's the latter. Do a small version of it right now.

  6. Stop optimizing for habit formation. If you've tried to build a habit repeatedly and it hasn't stuck, consider that you might be a drastic-change person. Stop trying to change 1% at a time and ask: What would the military-style version of this change look like for me?
