How HG Actually Started and What It Was Trying to Solve

Dr. K trained as a psychiatrist, built a successful private holistic practice, and one day noticed the obvious: the people most in need of what he knew were not the CEOs in his waiting room. He started streaming. For the first six to eight months, HG was a Discord group of about thirty people meeting Thursday evenings — first-name basis, intimate, deeply personal. That was the origin.

Growth created a problem. At its peak, requests to work with Dr. K were running at 10,000 per month. He can see perhaps 30–40 patients per month in a traditional practice. The math doesn't work, which forced the question: can this be taught to others?

That question became the coaching program — not a cynical revenue play, but a response to a genuine bottleneck. The peer support model has solid grounding in public health research: the World Health Organization, the United Nations, and the US Surgeon General's office all point toward peer support as a scalable answer to the mental health gap, but at the time there were no peer support programs at scale for technology addictionSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition →. HG tried to build one.

Kruti's framing from her healthcare management background gave the work three constraints that became the North Star: anything HG builds must be affordable, accessible, and inclusive. A 20-year-old gamer sitting across from a 55-year-old therapist who has never heard of the game they're addicted to doesn't feel heard. That gap is what HG was trying to close.

The Economics of "Free"

Free content is expensive to produce. This sounds obvious but has non-obvious implications that even the founders didn't fully internalize at the start.

As the audience grows, costs scale with it. A Discord server with 87,000 members costs more to run than one with 10,000. Uploading years of stream content to YouTube, employing thumbnail editors, funding research, paying developers — all of this compounds. Ad revenue from YouTube helps, but HG's goal was never to be a profitable content channel. It's trying to do something harder: build the infrastructure to actually change mental health outcomes.

The financing options each have a serious downside:

  • Donations / nonprofit model — Donors get informal control. A million-dollar gift comes with an implicit expectation. The people with the most influence over direction become the fundraisers, not the practitioners. And you spend enormous amounts of leadership time soliciting money.
  • Venture capital / private equity — You give up a piece of the company in exchange for capital and a clear expectation of returns. At least the terms are transparent, unlike donor relationships. But the profit motive can warp the mission.
  • Sponsorships — HG has been offered deals that would pay in one month what YouTube memberships generate in an entire year. They've turned most of these down because the incentive for a sponsor is to influence the communityThe Sanskrit word for community, sangha refers to the group of people who support your spiritual or meditative practice — and according to the yogic tradition, it is considered vital to sustaining that practice at all.Full definition →, and HG has watched advertisers notice how much money is extractable from an engaged mental health audience. They've accepted a small number of carefully chosen sponsors (Babel, Factor) but categorically avoid categories like CBD companies, which offer the largest checks.
  • Platform exclusivity deals — Streaming exclusively on one platform pays very well and has been declined, partly because platform risk is real (see: what happened to Twitch streamers in Korea).
  • Patreon-style donation asks — Dr. K finds this model psychologically uncomfortable. When you're helping people who are already struggling, asking them for money without giving them something concrete in return puts everyone in a strange position. It also slides toward a guru dynamic that he explicitly doesn't want.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming paid content means the free stuff is going away. Memberships and guides don't replace free content — they fund it. The revenue from paid products is what makes continued free production possible.

Treating "Dr. K said X once" as official policy. The founders explicitly address this: when Dr. K floats an idea on stream, a slice of that gets clipped and circulated as a commitment. This has caused them to become more guarded over time, which is a loss for everyone. Ideas discussed in a two-hour conversation require the full context.

Putting HG on a moral pedestal. Dr. K says this directly: if you view HG as saintly, any future compromise — a sponsorship, a pricing change, a strategic pivot — will feel like a betrayal. The organization is run by ordinary people making difficult trade-offs with incomplete information. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your actual mental health resource consumption. Before criticizing what HG does or doesn't offer, spend 30 minutes searching the existing library (YouTube playlists, resource packs). A lot of what people ask for already exists.

  2. Engage the free content before evaluating paid content. If cost is a barrier, the resource packs are free and longitudinal. Use them first and form an opinion based on actual experience.

  3. If you want to support HG, buy something rather than donating. Pre-order the book, try a guide ($25, one-time, no subscription), or sign up for a month of membership. You get something concrete; they get funding that goes toward free content for people who can't pay.

  4. Apply the peer support insight to your own situation. If professional help isn't accessible, look for people who have been through what you're going through — not just people trained to help from the outside. The WHO backs this model. AA is one of the best-studied examples. It's not a lesser substitute; it provides something professionals structurally can't.

  5. If you're a mental health professional, look into HGI's continuing education modules. Accredited by the APA for CEUs.
