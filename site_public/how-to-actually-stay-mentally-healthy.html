Summary

TL;DR — Mental fitness isn't about making life easier; it's about building your capacity to handle difficulty, managing your energy like a finite resource, and learning to distrust your own mind when it leads you astray.

Most mental health advice focuses on optimizing your environment — remove friction, build habits, automate good behavior. There's a different approach worth considering: deliberately keeping some friction in your life, paying close attention to what actually drains and restores you, and treating the mind as a useful but unreliable instrument rather than a source of truth.

Staying Mentally Fit Means Training, Not Just Optimizing

There's a widespread assumption that the path to better output is making everything as frictionless as possible. The problem is that this quietly erodes your capacity. The more you offload self-regulation to your environment, the less capable you become at self-regulating when the environment doesn't cooperate.

A concrete example: instead of moving your phone to another room to make morning scrolling impossible, keep it right there — and choose not to use it. This is harder. You'll fail some days. But the act of resisting, repeatedly, is itself the training. Over about a year, this kind of daily struggle can drop your total phone use dramatically — not because you removed the temptation, but because you built real resistance to it.

The key insight is that willpower is trainable, and training it first thing in the morning carries over into the rest of the day.

One practice worth researching to support this: Pratyahara, a form of sensory withdrawal from yogic meditation. The basic idea is that your attention naturally gets pulled outward toward sensory objects — your phone, a notification, a craving. Pratyahara is a practice of consciously withdrawing that attention back inward. Training it reduces distractibility more broadly.

"The more you make things easy on yourself, the less capable you become."

Treat Your Energy Like a Battery, Not a Resource You Can Borrow Against

Many people manage fatigue by pushing through it — with caffeine, quick dopamine hits, and "breaks" that are actually more stimulation. This works in the short term but leaves you wrecked.

A clearer mental model: energy is a battery, and different activities charge or drain it. The important move is learning to tell the difference.

A few things that quietly drain more than people realize: - Caffeine — it doesn't give you energy. It blocks your perception of tiredness. It's a loan against future energy, and the interest compounds. - Scrolling as a break — pulling out your phone when you're tired feels like rest, but it doesn't recharge you. You come back to work just as depleted, sometimes more so. - Certain social obligations — some relationships and interactions leave you genuinely exhausted. You don't have to cut them off, but you can stop picking up every time they call.

A useful question to run on your daily activities: At the end of this, what will my energy level be?

Two things that actually restore energy: naps and walking. Short naps (20–25 minutes) are enough for physiological recovery. Set a timer — once you drift into deeper sleep stages, waking up becomes much harder and you'll feel worse. If you're currently sleeping badly or using a lot of caffeine, your naps may run long at first. That's a signal to address the underlying sleep debt and caffeine use, not a sign that napping doesn't work for you.

Stop Powering Through Entertainment That Isn't Working

This one sounds trivial but has a real payoff. There's a habit — very common — of forcing yourself through TV shows, video games, or other entertainment while waiting for it to "get good." You sit through episodes you don't enjoy, checking your phone in the boring parts, grinding through hours of a game that hasn't hooked you yet, hoping the promised payoff is coming.

This is a waste of time that also degrades your capacity for enjoyment. When you stop, stop immediately. The moment you pull out your phone while watching something, that's the signal — you're not having fun. Turn it off.

What happens when you stop chasing the hook? Your enjoyment of lower-stimulation activities starts to recover. Reading fiction becomes engaging again. Non-fiction feels rewarding. Your brain, no longer forced into high-dopamine loops it can't quite get, starts responding to things it had become numb to.

There's also a related sleep trick worth trying: when you're tired but your mind activates the moment you turn off the TV, don't immediately reach for your phone. Lie down on your side, close your eyes, and stay there for about 20 minutes. You probably won't fall asleep, but something resets — the boredom passes, the mind quiets, and you get up with noticeably more energy.

"If I don't force my brain into falling into dopaminergic crap, it actually works way better for other kinds of things."

Do Passion Work First, Not Last

The standard logic is: handle your obligations first, then do what you love with the time that's left. The problem is that there's almost never time left. The world takes everything, and passion projects get indefinitely deferred.

The reframe: do at least a small amount of passion work at the start of the day, before obligations consume your energy and attention.

This doesn't require enormous time — even 20 to 30 minutes of writing, practicing an instrument, or working on something you care about makes a measurable difference. The research on this is about perceived control and autonomy: feeling in control of your life isn't primarily a function of your circumstances. It comes from making choices. When three months go by and you've only responded to what the world demanded of you, the feeling of being trapped is almost inevitable — even if everything looks fine from the outside.

Spending even a few hours a week doing something you chose, growing in a direction you care about, counteracts that feeling in a way that finishing your to-do list never will.

Disbelieve Your Mind

The mind is not a neutral observer. It evolved to process sensory input and reach conclusions quickly, and it has systematic biases that distort what it tells you — especially under stress.

The severity of most mental illness can actually be mapped onto how much a person believes their own mind. In psychosis, someone is certain the FBI is following them. In severe depression, someone genuinely believes their family would be better off without them. The path from acute illness to recovery, across nearly every psychiatric condition, involves developing the capacity to hold the mind's outputs at some distance — to notice a thought without automatically treating it as true.

Two patterns worth watching for:

  1. Black-and-white thinking — whenever your mind jumps to "this is a disaster" or "this is going to be perfect," that's a flag. Neither extreme is usually accurate. When you notice this happening, step back, take a breath, take a walk, and revisit.

  2. Comparisons — your mind can always find someone doing better. The problem with comparisons is they strip away all context. The person you're comparing yourself to made specific tradeoffs to get where they are — tradeoffs you might not actually want to make. Whenever a comparison thought arises, the useful question isn't "why am I not like them?" but "would I actually want what they have, given what it costs?"

"My mind is definitely not an arbiter of truth. It's just an instrument designed to take sensory input and form some kind of conclusion."

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking willpower training means white-knuckling everything forever. The goal is to build capacity by choosing specific, manageable challenges — like one rule about your phone in the morning — not to make every moment a test of endurance.
  • Using caffeine as energy management. It feels like you're staying functional, but you're actually borrowing against tomorrow. Overreliance on caffeine also degrades sleep quality and makes napping difficult.
  • Treating doom scrolling as a break. A break is supposed to restore energy. If you feel just as depleted (or more so) after your break, it wasn't a break.
  • Waiting for passion projects to "fit." They will not fit. You have to place them at the front of the day deliberately.
  • Believing that disbelieving your mind means dismissing all emotion or thought. It doesn't. It means introducing a gap between what your mind says and what you act on — especially when you notice black-and-white thinking or comparisons.
  • Assuming these practices are sufficient regardless of circumstances. Stable housing, financial security, and healthy relationships are foundational to mental health in ways these practices can't fully substitute for. These habits help; they're not magic.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick one morning resistance target. Keep your phone near you, but commit to not using it for the first hour of the day. Don't change anything else. Expect to fail sometimes. Track loosely for a few weeks.

  2. Do the energy battery audit. At the end of each day for one week, write down two or three things that drained your energy and two or three things that restored it. Look for patterns you can act on.

  3. Set a nap timer. If you're exhausted mid-day, lie down and set a timer for 20–25 minutes. If your naps are running long, reduce caffeine and address your overnight sleep before trying again.

  4. Quit entertainment that isn't working. The next time you notice yourself checking your phone while watching something, turn it off. Don't finish the episode. Try lying down without a screen for 20 minutes instead.

  5. Schedule 20–30 minutes of passion work before your obligations start. This could mean waking up slightly earlier, getting ready faster, or just protecting the first usable chunk of your morning. Do this four or five days a week and keep it small enough that it's achievable.

  6. Start a thought-checking practice. When you notice a strong negative or positive thought, ask: Is this black-and-white? Is this a comparison? If yes, write it down and write one more nuanced version of reality beside it.

  7. Look into Pratyahara. Search for beginner-level guided practices in sensory withdrawal meditation if the phone resistance habit feels neurologically impossible right now — it addresses the pull toward distraction at a deeper level.
