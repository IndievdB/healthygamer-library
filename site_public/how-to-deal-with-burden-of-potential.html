Summary

TL;DR — Potential is not an advantage. The more capable you are, the harder your road is, and mistaking potential for a promise of easy success is what causes years of quiet disappointment.

Most people who grow up being told they're smart, talented, or "have so much potential" walk away with an unconscious belief: I won't have to try as hard as everyone else. When life turns out to be difficult anyway, the gap between that expectation and reality registers not as a neutral recalibration but as years of low-grade disillusionment. This talk uses a candid, wide-ranging conversation to work through exactly that pattern — touching on cannabis use, identity, ego, and finally landing on a reframe of potential that changes how the whole story looks.

The Hidden Trap of Being Told You're Special

When someone tells a child they have potential, the child rarely hears "you have the raw material to build something great if you work very hard." What they actually hear is: good things are coming and you won't have to do much to get them. That lands as a promise — something set in stone that will fall into place.

Years later, when things haven't fallen into place, the emotion that shows up isn't frustration with a false expectation. It's shame. It feels like squandering. And the word "squander" is telling, because squandering only applies to something that was an advantage.

This is the first mistake: treating potential as an advantage when it is, in fact, a burden.

Potential Is a Blueprint for a Mansion, Not a Shortcut

If you hand someone the blueprint for a shack and someone else the blueprint for a mansion, which has more potential? The mansion. Which is harder to build? Also the mansion — by a wide margin.

That is exactly what high potential means. It is a blueprint for something large and complex. It does not come with extra workers, extra materials, or extra time. It just means the structure you are supposed to build requires far more effort than a smaller one.

"The higher your potential is, the harder your road is. The more you're going to have to invest in realizing that potential."

Someone with fewer cognitive or material advantages has a smaller blueprint. They can finish it more quickly. That is not a failure on their part — it is just the scale of what they were handed. Meanwhile, someone with high potential who compares their progress to that person's is making a deeply unfair comparison. They are looking at a hundred-acre farm and asking why it isn't as far along as someone cultivating one acre.

Cutting yourself slack is not the same as making excuses. It is accurate math.

Why Privilege Can Make Things Harder, Not Easier

There is a paradox at the center of a comfortable upbringing: it removes the external pressure that normally forces people into motion.

If you have one day to finish an assignment, you do the assignment. If you have ten days, you have to manufacture the urgency yourself. In the same way, someone who grew up having to fight for basic stability has the world itself demanding effort from them. Someone who grew up secure has to generate that demand internally — and internal motivation is much harder to sustain than external pressure.

"External motivation is easier for our generation than internal motivation."

This is not an excuse for inaction. It is an explanation of why the person on the "easy" path sometimes struggles more than the person on the hard one. Recognizing that your advantages have actually made certain things more difficult — requiring more internal discipline rather than less — is more useful than the shame spiral of "I've been given so much and done so little."

Disillusionment Is the Loss of a False Expectation

"Disillusioned" is a precise word. To be disillusioned, you first had to be illusioned. The illusion was a specific false expectation about how life was going to go — vague but certain, good and arriving on its own. When life got hard, that illusion cracked. Then it cracked more. What can look like years of depression or failure to launch is often this progressive disillusionment: the slow-motion collapse of a promise that was never real.

The fix is not motivation or willpower applied to the same faulty map. It is replacing the map. Specifically: - Stop reading your current position as evidence of squandering - Recognize that difficulty is proportional to potential, not evidence against it - Understand that internal change — changing how you see yourself — is genuinely harder than any external accomplishment, and deserves the same respect you'd give any serious difficulty

The Spotlight, the Ego, and Why Introspection Works

Ego operates by comparison. The moment your mind is measuring you against someone else — why are they popular and I'm not, why have they built something and I haven't — the spotlight is pointed outward. When the spotlight is on other people, self-judgment is constant.

Introspection turns the spotlight inward. And here is the key insight: it may not matter what specific insight you gain from that introspection. The act of redirecting your attention inward is itself what reduces the comparison, the ego noise, and the anxiety.

External tools — a book, a conversation, a contemplative practice — can focus that spotlight more precisely, like a pressure washer compared to a sprinkler. But the mechanism doing the work is the internal focus, not the specific content of the insight.

"I think the key element is that your focus is on reflection and introspection. That's where the money is."

Intelligence Is One Stat on Your Character Sheet

Being told you are smart is not a compliment that requires a reaction. It is a description of one attribute — like height or eye color. It does not need to make you proud or ashamed. It does not need to be the thing that defines you or the thing you perform for other people.

What we call "intelligence" is usually an umbrella word for a whole cluster of things: practice, consistency, humility, curiosity, work ethic. When someone looks fluent in a skill and says "they're so smart," what they're not seeing is the thousands of hours underneath the fluency. Treating raw cognitive ability as the main variable makes you both overestimate and underestimate the wrong things.

"The dedicated person of average intelligence is capable of much more than the lazy or erratic genius."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the insight for the mechanism. People often credit a drug, a book, or a conversation with changing them, when what actually shifted was the act of introspecting — focusing inward — that the tool enabled. The risk is believing you need the tool to access that state, rather than training yourself to access it directly.

Using a diagnosis as an exemption. A label can be genuinely useful, and it can also become a quiet way to stop doing the difficult work of self-examination. These two things are not mutually exclusive — both can be true simultaneously. The question worth asking honestly is: what am I letting myself avoid by holding this label in front of me?

Treating internal change as easy because it sounds simple. "Just change your perspective" sounds trivial. It is not. Shifting how you fundamentally understand yourself is harder than most external accomplishments. Not acknowledging that difficulty — expecting it to happen quickly — is its own form of the potential trap.

Seeking the most advanced practice before mastering basics. The appeal of an intensive, direct path is partly ego. The desire to skip beginner's yoga and go straight to Kriya yoga, or to skip foundational habits and go straight to transformation, is a form of the same mistake: believing that your potential means you should be further along, faster.

Assuming your medical records speak for themselves. Especially in mental health, diagnoses travel forward through a system that does not always re-examine them. If substance use was involved around the time of a diagnosis, that context matters enormously — and it may not have been properly accounted for. You are responsible for communicating your full history to any new provider, not assuming they've read the chart.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Rewrite what potential means for your specific situation. Write down one area of your life where you feel you've "squandered" your potential. Then explicitly calculate the scale: what would it actually take to build what you're capable of? Compare that honestly to what you'd expect of someone with less to work with. Does your self-judgment still hold up?

  2. Catch ego comparisons in real time. For one day, notice every time your mind makes a comparison — "they can do this but I can't," "I should be further along than them." You don't need to stop it. Just notice it as ego operating, label it, and let it pass.

  3. Turn the spotlight inward instead of outward. When you feel anxiety about the future or shame about the past, ask: what is actually happening inside me right now? Not what you should have done, not what others are doing — just what is present in you at this moment. Do this for five minutes before reaching for your phone.

  4. Try a physically demanding balance posture as meditation. Stand on one leg, place your other foot on your inner thigh above the knee, and breathe slowly. Hold for as long as you can. Notice that when the body demands full attention, mental chatter stops — not because you forced it to, but because there was no room. This is what meditation is actually supposed to feel like: not a struggle to quiet the mind, but a natural quieting because something else has your full attention.

  5. Have an honest conversation with any treating provider about your substance use history. Don't assume they've read everything or connected all the dots. Give them the information directly and explicitly, and ask them to consider your diagnosis fresh with that full context.

  6. Treat your intelligence as a neutral fact, not a performance. When you notice yourself either puffing up around being smart or deflecting it with false modesty, recognize both as ego moves. Practice saying or thinking "that's just one of my attributes" and moving on without ceremony.
