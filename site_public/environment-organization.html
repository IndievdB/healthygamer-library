Environment - Organization

The challenge with getting organized is that we often have a choice: do the task itself, or get organized about doing the task. We can get work done, OR we can make a to do list for what we need to do. In this video, we'll explore why organization is important from a neuroscience perspective. Once we understand what organization does in our brain, we'll explore common pitfalls to getting organized. Lastly, we'll go over details about how to create an organizational system that protects us from forgetfulness and lateness.

Why Your Brain Struggles to Hold Everything at Once

The frontal lobesThe brain's master controller — the region responsible for planning, impulse control, directing attention, organization, and what we collectively call maturity.Full definition → govern what's called executive function — the ability to plan tasks, execute them, resist impulses, direct attention, and protect against distraction. The critical detail is that all of those jobs share the same limited resource pool.

Think of a two-year-old on a beach picking up shells. At some point, every time she reaches for a new shell, she drops one or two. She's not doing anything wrong — she's simply run out of hands. Your brain works the same way. Every task, appointment, errand, or nagging obligation you're trying to mentally track is something your frontal lobesThe brain's master controller — the region responsible for planning, impulse control, directing attention, organization, and what we collectively call maturity.Full definition → are "holding." The more they hold, the less capacity they have for everything else — including keeping you focused, on-task, and undistracted.

This is why the connection between a to-do list and the ability to concentrate on a textbook isn't obvious but is very real. They seem unrelated. The thing connecting them is the brain doing both.

There's a second problem, specific to distraction. When you're juggling tasks mentally and you get pulled away, your brain can't simply pick up exactly where it left off. It has to re-orient, re-map the landscape, and essentially restart. For someone whose mind is more prone to distraction, this means that every interruption doesn't just cost the time of the interruption — it costs the restart too. A task that could take an hour ends up consuming a whole afternoon.

"Every time I actually take one step forward towards the job search, I forget to do something else."

Organization is the backpack. It gets the shells off your hands so you can use them to pick things up.

The Real Cost of Skipping the System

There's a legitimate reason people abandon organizational systems early: at the start, the system costs more than it gives back.

Your brain has an existing autopilot — an informal, habitual way of juggling things. It's inefficient, but it's practiced. When you introduce a new system, your brain now has to maintain the old mental list and remember to use the new one. (Ever made a grocery list and still remembered everything from memory at the store? That's because your brain doesn't yet trust the list to hold things for it.)

The exhaustion you feel when first implementing a system is real. Your brain is learning something new while still running its old processes in parallel. This is normal. It passes.

The antidote is to anchor yourself to why you're building the system in the first place. Not the abstract "I should be more organized" — but the specific goal you haven't been able to reach with your current approach. Keeping that goal in mind is what gets you through the early inefficiency hump.

The Four Components of a Useful Organizational System

There's no single perfect system — everyone has a unique set of cognitive strengths, weaknesses, and vulnerabilities. But any good system needs to address four things.

1. Prioritization

The point isn't to have a ranked list for its own sake. The point is to stop forcing your frontal lobesThe brain's master controller — the region responsible for planning, impulse control, directing attention, organization, and what we collectively call maturity.Full definition → to constantly re-evaluate what's most important. That decision-making draws on the same resources as focus and distraction resistance.

A simple three-tier approach works for many people: - What has to get done today - What needs to get done soon (this week, roughly) - What would be nice to do eventually

The format matters less than the consistency. Whether you number things 1–10 or use broad categories, the goal is the same: look at the list and be able to act immediately without re-deciding anything.

2. The Temporal Dimension (When)

A calendar handles the "when" the way a to-do list handles the "what." Once something is slotted into a specific time, you can stop mentally circling it. You don't have to keep asking yourself, when am I going to deal with this? It's answered. That answer is stored somewhere outside your head.

"When I know something is gonna get taken care of, I don't have to worry about it anymore."

A physician managing a patient panel of hundreds doesn't mentally track every patient's follow-up needs. They make appointments. The calendar holds the information; the brain doesn't have to.

3. Operationalizing

Operationalizing means breaking an abstract goal into concrete, completable actions.

"Find a job" is not a task. It's a category. Your brain can't execute on it directly, which is part of why it feels so paralyzing. But "update the skills section of my resume" is a task. "Find three job postings to apply to today" is a task.

A useful thought exercise: imagine you're physically paralyzed but have full mental capacity, and you're directing a personal assistant who will do exactly what you describe, step by step. What would you tell them? Walk through it out loud or on paper. The granularity that exercise forces — sit down, open the laptop, navigate to email, check for responses — reveals exactly how finely you sometimes need to slice a problem.

You won't always need that level of detail. But it calibrates your sense of what a "completable task" actually looks like, and it trains the operationalizing habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition →.

4. The Knuckleheaded Stuff

Every system needs a dedicated place for small, low-stakes tasks that aren't urgent enough to feel important but still occupy mental real estate. Paying a bill, replacing toilet paper, filling out a form — none of these compete intellectually with your big priorities, but if they're unaccounted for, they leak into your attention at the worst moments.

Two practical approaches: - Schedule dedicated admin time — a regular block (daily or weekly) specifically for clearing this category. It's not glamorous, but ignoring it compounds. - Two-minute rule — if something takes less than two minutes, do it now. It's not worth the ongoing mental overhead of tracking it.

Electronic vs. Paper

Both have real tradeoffs.

Electronic tools offer reminders, notifications, scheduling links, and syncing across devices. For calendar management and appointment alerts, they're hard to beat — especially if forgetfulness is a real vulnerability for you.

Paper has one underrated advantage: opening a notebook doesn't expose you to TikTok notifications, Discord messages, or a friend texting you something funny. For to-do lists and task tracking specifically, pen and paper often wins on distraction cost alone.

A hybrid approach — paper for daily task lists, electronic tools for calendar and time-sensitive reminders — works well for many people. The right answer depends on experimentation and honest self-assessment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating organization as the goal. Making lists, color-coding calendars, and researching productivity systems can become a form of productive-feeling avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition →. The system serves your actual goals — it's not a substitute for working toward them.
  • Abandoning the system too early. The first weeks will feel less efficient than your old approach. That's expected. It doesn't mean the system isn't working; it means your brain hasn't calibrated to it yet.
  • Leaving no place for small tasks. Without a plan for low-priority items, they accumulate silently and drain the exact mental resources you need for bigger work.
  • Assuming one system fits everyone. Copying someone else's exact method without adapting it to your own patterns tends to fail. The principles are universal; the implementation is personal.
  • Operationalizing at the wrong level. "Clean the house" is not a task. Neither is "be healthier." If something feels overwhelming every time you look at it, it probably needs to be broken down further.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down your "why." Before touching any app or notebook, spend five minutes writing one to three sentences about what you're actually trying to achieve with better organization. Return to this whenever the system feels like more work than it's worth.

  2. Do a full brain dump. List every task, obligation, and nagging thought currently living in your head. Don't organize it yet — just get it out. Notice how it feels to have it on paper instead of in your skull.

  3. Sort that list into three buckets: must do today, should do this week, would be nice eventually. Don't overthink the categories.

  4. Pick one abstract goal you've been stuck on and operationalize it. Use the paralyzed-person thought exercise: describe every step out loud or in writing as if instructing a personal assistant. Then circle the two or three steps small enough to do today.

  5. Block admin time. Put a recurring slot — even thirty minutes — on your calendar each week specifically for low-priority tasks. Treat it as a real appointment.

  6. Run a one-week experiment with a paper to-do list if you normally use your phone (or vice versa). Track honestly: how often did you get distracted? Did the system actually help? Adjust based on what the data shows, not what seems like it should work.

  7. Apply the two-minute rule today. Any task you encounter that takes less than two minutes — respond to it, handle it, close it out immediately rather than logging it for later.
