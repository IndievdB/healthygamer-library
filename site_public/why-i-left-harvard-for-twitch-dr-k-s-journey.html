Summary

TL;DR — Defining success by external standards disconnects you from what actually makes you effective. The most meaningful decisions often come from walking away from objectively "correct" paths because they don't feel internally right.

This is a personal account of moving through multiple versions of "success" — pre-med student, aspiring monk, alternative medicine doctor, Harvard psychiatrist — and how each one was abandoned not from failure but from an honest internal signal. The deeper lesson isn't "follow your passion" as a bumper sticker. It's about learning to distinguish between goals that belong to you and goals that are borrowed from a social script, and understanding why suffering with purpose feels better than comfort without it.

The Trap of External Success

Success, as most people define it, has nothing to do with you as an individual. It's a checklist — a title, a school, a salary — that anyone can technically pursue regardless of who they are, what they care about, or what they're actually good at.

The problem is that when you chase an external target for purely external reasons, you have no real fuel. There's no intrinsic reason to push through the hard parts. That's exactly what happened in college: a 1.2 GPA, academic probation, working toward becoming a doctor mostly because strangers at parties looked impressed. It wasn't about medicine. It was about wanting respect in a world that hadn't given much.

"It had nothing to do with what I cared about. It had nothing to do with what I was aligned towards. It's all for the sake of external success."

External success metrics aren't bad in themselves. The problem is using them as a substitute for figuring out what you actually want.

False Spirituality and the Ego of Having No Ego

After abandoning pre-med, a years-long path toward becoming a monk looked, from the outside, like a complete reversal. But it was the same pattern: an external target, now just a more exotic one.

There's a particular kind of spiritual posturing worth recognizing — using the language of non-attachmentThe Sanskrit term for detachment — specifically, detachment from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → and anti-materialism as a way to avoid responsibility rather than transcend it. It shows up as people who lecture others about being money-obsessed while borrowing money they never repay. It's an ego that wraps itself in the appearance of having no ego.

"I used spirituality as an excuse to get away from my baser needs and instincts. It was a way to use spirituality to absolve myself of responsibility."

The tell is that real spiritual practice eventually exposes this. After years of rigorous training, the reasons for pursuing monasticism turned out to be just as ego-driven as the reasons for wanting to be a Harvard doctor. When that became clear, walking away — even after seven years of investment — was the honest move.

The Harvard Problem: When the Right Path Isn't Your Path

Psychiatry residency at Harvard Medical School was genuinely excellent. Great mentors, real research opportunities, a clear and well-supported path forward. And yet.

The deeper interest — understanding why young people were losing themselves to video games, why a generation was struggling and the field had no real tools to help — kept surfacing. When that question was brought to senior psychiatrists, some of the most respected minds in the field, the answers revealed a structural gap. These were people who had never played video games. The path they offered was the academically correct one: apply for a five-year grant, study the phenomenon, publish, apply for a bigger grant, repeat, and in fifteen to twenty years you might understand the problem well enough to build a clinic.

"By the time I get my research funding to study Diablo 3 addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, Diablo 3 is going to be dead."

The pace of technology and the pace of academic medicine are simply mismatched. Meanwhile, having worked informally with roughly 500 gamers during residency, the suffering was visible and immediate. The mismatch between institutional timelines and human need felt impossible to ignore.

This wasn't a rejection of medicine or science. It was a recognition that the specific tool being offered — a decades-long academic track — wasn't the right instrument for this particular problem.

Walking Away as a Strategy, Not an Excuse

The pattern across these decisions is consistent: monk, oncologist, Harvard faculty track. Each time, there was an objectively reasonable path on the table, and each time, something internal said no.

The key distinction is that this isn't permission to avoid difficulty or dodge responsibility. Every one of those "wrong" paths involved years of real work, real learning, and real sacrifice. The formal training in medicine, neuroscience, and psychiatry still happened. The meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → practice still happened. None of it was wasted.

"Even if I had set out to do this work, I still would have said I need some kind of formal training. You still have to do things you don't like. But when you do them in service to your own goals, your experience and performance will be so much higher."

The difference is whether the hard work is being done for your own purpose or instead of finding one. Difficulty in service of something meaningful is fundamentally different from difficulty in service of someone else's definition of success.

Why Suffering With Purpose Feels Better Than Comfort Without It

There's an experiment worth knowing about: two groups put their hands in painfully cold water. One group experienced a consistent temperature. The other started at a colder temperature and had it gradually warm to where the first group had been the whole time — objectively a worse experience. Yet the second group reported it as less painful and more bearable overall.

The direction of change matters more to our experience than the absolute level of discomfort. Coming back from behind feels better than an easy win that was never in doubt.

This has real implications for how to think about life satisfaction. A life optimized to avoid pain isn't actually more pleasant — it removes the contrast that makes good things feel good. The goal isn't to seek suffering for its own sake, but to ensure that when suffering comes, it's in pursuit of something worth pursuing.

"Don't avoid pain and don't avoid suffering. Build a life for yourself where you can succeed through pain and suffering, and then you'll have a life that's truly satisfying."

The gaming analogy is clean: the most memorable moments aren't the stomps. They're the comebacks.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking "follow your passion" for a free pass. This is probably the most common misreading of this kind of advice. Two kids need clothing and food. Passion doesn't pay for that by itself. The point is to be strategic about orienting your work toward things that matter to you — not to use it as a justification for avoiding responsibility.

Devaluing external achievement entirely. The flip side of chasing external success is dismissing it altogether and calling it shallow. That's just the ego moving in the opposite direction. The point isn't that Harvard is meaningless — it was genuinely excellent and formative. The point is that the credential is not the destination.

Confusing "satisfying life" with "comfortable life." Satisfaction isn't the same as pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition →. A life of low-stakes comfort can be quietly miserable. Having something to be proud of — something you worked toward, suffered for, and chose — is a different category of experience than simply avoiding pain.

Treating internal signals as always trustworthy. "Follow your instincts" doesn't mean every impulse is correct. The monk decision took years of actual practice to evaluate honestly. The test isn't whether something feels right on first impression, but whether it still resonates after real engagement with the work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down your current definition of success. Be specific. Then ask: whose definition is this? Would you still want it if no one could see you achieve it?

  2. Trace your past "wrong" decisions. List times you walked away from something or stayed on a path that felt off. What was the internal signal you ignored or obeyed? What did you learn from each?

  3. Separate the goal from the path. If you have a goal, ask whether the specific route you're pursuing is truly the best fit for you or simply the most socially legible one. Are there other ways to get to the same destination?

  4. Do a "false spirituality" audit. Identify any areas where you're using a high-minded principle — minimalism, anti-hustle, anti-ambition, work-life balance — as a cover for avoiding something hard. The tell: are you using the principle selectively, only when it's convenient?

  5. Find the purposeful suffering in your current work. If your work is painful, ask whether that pain is in service of something you've actually chosen. If yes, lean into it. If no, that's important information.

  6. Practice the contrast experiment in small ways. Voluntarily do something uncomfortable before something you enjoy — a cold shower before a warm one, a hard workout before a meal you like. Notice how the direction of change affects your experience.
