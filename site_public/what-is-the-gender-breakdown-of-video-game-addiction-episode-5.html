Summary

TL;DR — Video game addiction skews male, but internet and gaming-related compulsive use affects all genders; the full picture depends heavily on which platforms and behaviors you're measuring.

Among people under 18, roughly 8.4% show signs of problematic gaming overall. Break that down by gender and the split is approximately 11–12% of boys versus 6–7% of girls. That gap is real, but it's only part of the story.

The Numbers Don't Tell the Whole Story

The male-skew in video game addiction is fairly consistent across studies, but the research is still emerging and the picture shifts depending on what you're measuring.

Two factors complicate a simple "boys are more addicted to games" conclusion:

  • Type of addiction matters. When you broaden the lens from video games to internet-related compulsive behavior generally — social media in particular — women show higher rates than men.
  • Platform matters. Women are more likely to spend excessive time on mobile games than men are. If a study only looks at console or PC gaming, it will undercount problematic gaming among women almost by definition.

So the gender gap in gaming addiction is real but narrower than headline numbers suggest, and in adjacent categories it can reverse entirely.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the male-skew as absolute. Saying "gaming addiction is a guy problem" causes women who are struggling with mobile games or social media compulsion to go unrecognized — by researchers, clinicians, and themselves.
  • Conflating "video games" with all compulsive screen behavior. The research categories don't always map onto how people actually use technology. A woman spending five hours a day on a mobile game may not be counted in gaming addiction statistics at all.
  • Assuming the numbers are settled. This is explicitly an emerging area of research. Early findings shift as measurement tools improve and as gaming demographics continue to change.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what you're actually tracking. If you're concerned about your own or someone else's screen use, be specific: is it console games, mobile games, social media, or some combination? Each has its own pattern and its own research base.
  2. Don't use gender statistics to dismiss a personal concern. Being in a lower-risk demographic group doesn't mean you can't be affected. Use the numbers as context, not as a verdict on your individual situation.
  3. Stay skeptical of single studies. Because this research is still developing, look for consensus across multiple sources rather than anchoring on one statistic.
