Summary

TL;DR — What feels like pure lust is often something deeper, and the "reasons" you can't walk away are often your own mind protecting a secret desire.

When you develop strong feelings for someone you work with — or someone whose life you're embedded in professionally — the feelings rarely stay as simple as you tell yourself they are. The situation here is specific: someone editing videos for a streamer they're attracted to, hours every day, unable to "just quit." But the dynamics underneath apply broadly to anyone stuck in an emotionally charged situation they can't seem to leave.

Lust Is Often the Mind's Most Comfortable Lie

The first honest question to ask yourself is: is this actually just lust?

Lust is the easiest feeling to admit to. It feels physical, manageable, almost trivial — "I'm just horny, it's a body thing, no big deal." Compared to "I'm falling for someone I can never have," lust is a much softer landing. So the mind, trying to protect you from pain, will often hand you the lust diagnosis even when the truth is messier.

Here's a simple test: if all you felt were lust, would it bother you that this person is sleeping with someone else? Pure lust doesn't produce jealousy about who else someone is with. It only cares about your own access. But jealousy over a partner or boyfriend? That points to something beyond the physical — attachment, longing, the desire to matter to this person in a real way.

"The mind will tell you a lie to try to protect you. One of the most common lies it tells you is: when there's no opportunity for a relationship with someone, your mind will say — oh, you're just horny, that's it."

This matters because if you misdiagnose the problem, your solutions won't work. You can try every trick for managing lust and still feel stuck — because lust isn't actually what's running the show.

The Hidden Intimacy That Makes This Hard to Leave

There's something genuinely intimate about being someone's editor or close creative collaborator. Everyone else sees the polished, public-facing version of this person. You see the raw footage, the unedited photos, the behind-the-scenes reality. That access creates a real sense of closeness — and with it, a kind of significance. She needs me. I'm part of her success. I see her in a way other people don't.

That intimacy isn't imaginary. It's real — just not romantic. But the brain doesn't always make that distinction cleanly. The closeness feeds the feelings, and the feelings make the closeness feel even more meaningful. You end up in a loop.

Why "I Can't Leave" Is Worth Questioning

The mind is clever. When it has a secret desire — to stay close to someone, to keep that feeling of intimacy and significance — it will construct the most airtight logical argument it can to justify staying.

In this case: I get paid significantly above market rate. I can't just quit.

That might even be partly true. But it's worth sitting with honestly. A strong, demonstrable track record editing for a successful creator is genuinely portable. The skills transfer. The resume speaks for itself. The actual barrier to leaving may be much lower than the mind is making it seem.

"Anytime our mind is resistant to something, what it actually does is provide us with a very good logical reason — a justification — for not doing what we need to do."

The signal to watch for: you know exactly what you should do, and your mind immediately produces a compelling reason why that specific thing is impossible. That pattern — knowing the right move but having a perfect excuse not to take it — is almost always resistance, not reality.

The Void That Gets Filled With Sensory Input

One of the more useful ways to think about why these attachments take hold so strongly: when there's emptiness in your life — loneliness, lack of connection, an unfulfilling or absent relationship — the mind looks for something to fill it.

And if you're spending hours every day editing images and videos of one specific attractive person, your senses are getting flooded with input related to that person. The brain tries to use that input to fill the void. It never fully works — the emptiness isn't actually touched — but it keeps you coming back for more, the same way a substance might.

The practical implication: as long as your sensory world is saturated with this person, there's no room for anything else to grow. There's no space for new attraction, new connection, a relationship that could actually meet the need. You have to create that space deliberately, not just wait for the feelings to fade on their own.

Grief Is the Skill Nobody Teaches You

The final piece — and in some ways the most important — is learning to grieve something that was never real but felt like it could have been.

Grief isn't just for deaths and breakups. It's for possibilities that won't happen. For the version of reality where you and this person build something together — professional partners, maybe more — and it turns out beautifully. That fantasy is worth mourning, not dismissing.

"You have to sit there and think about all the things that you want, accept how you're never going to get them, and go through that process of grief. Even though it's really painful — because you're going to be uncovering all of your coping mechanisms and you're going to feel naked and terrified — all of that negative emotion that you're suppressing is actually going to come out. It'll overwhelm you for a while, but then it'll start to subside."

Grief done properly clears space. Grief avoided just drives the feelings underground, where they keep influencing your decisions without your awareness.

Common Pitfalls

Calling it lust when it's love. Accepting that you have deeper feelings is harder, but treating a love problem like a lust problem means your solutions will keep failing.

Taking the resistance at face value. "I literally cannot leave this job" deserves serious scrutiny before you accept it. The mind is motivated to keep you near the thing it wants.

Waiting for the feelings to dissolve on their own. They won't, especially if you're still spending hours a day immersed in that person's content and images. Passive waiting isn't a strategy.

Bringing it to the other person before you've worked it out yourself. They've spent zero hours thinking about this; you've spent hundreds. Dropping it on them without warning puts them in an impossible position and rarely goes the way you hope.

Trying to fill the underlying void with more of the same. Using the parasocial closeness to soothe the loneliness that created the attachment in the first place is a loop, not a solution.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do an honest emotional audit. Sit quietly and ask yourself: am I jealous when this person is with someone else? Do I fantasize about a relationship, not just sex? Write your answers down. You're trying to find out whether lust is the whole story or a cover story.

  2. Examine your resistance. Write out the reasons you believe you can't leave or create distance. Then argue against each one as if you were advising a friend. What would you tell them if they gave you those same reasons?

  3. Audit the empty spaces. Are you single? Lonely? Lacking close friendships? Identify what void this connection might be filling. That's the actual thing that needs addressing — the attachment is a symptom.

  4. Reduce sensory exposure where you can. Even within the constraints of the job, look for ways to reduce the amount of time you spend immersed in this person's content outside of actual work tasks. Notice what it feels like when the input decreases.

  5. Practice grieving the fantasy. Set aside 20–30 minutes in a private space. Let yourself fully imagine what you wanted — the closeness, the relationship, the partnership — and then consciously acknowledge it isn't going to happen. Don't rush past the pain. Let it land.

  6. Create space for something real. Actively pursue connection elsewhere — with friends, in dating, in new professional relationships. The goal isn't distraction; it's building something that can actually meet the need the infatuation is pointing to.

  7. Consider talking to someone who isn't invested in the outcome. A therapist, coach, or even a trusted friend who can be honest with you. The mind hides things from itself; an outside perspective helps surface what's actually going on.
