Summary

TL;DR — School shootings are a problem that demands everyone take personal responsibility, not just point fingers at institutions. Understanding the psychology of shooters, the mechanics of online radicalization, and the science of trauma can all help — but none of it works without individual accountability.

This talk covers four interlocking ideas: why labeling school shooters as mentally ill is both unfair and counterproductive, why gun control deserves a concrete answer from its opponents, how online platforms are quietly radicalizing ordinary people through a process called online drift, and what the actual science says you should (and shouldn't) do in the aftermath of trauma.

Mental Illness Is Not an Excuse

There's a reflex in modern culture to reach for psychiatric language whenever something horrific happens. Someone does something monstrous, and within hours the conversation shifts to: Was this person mentally ill?

As a psychiatrist, that framing is deeply frustrating. Every time you label a school shooter as mentally ill, you do two harmful things at once: you smear the vast majority of people who live with mental illness and never harm anyone, and you quietly hand the perpetrator an excuse.

The more honest framing is moral. As awareness of mental health has grown, we've lost a vocabulary that used to do real work — the language of good and evil, of sin, of duty and its violation. Those concepts got tangled up with religion and got quietly retired. But retiring them didn't make the underlying reality disappear; it just left us with psychiatry as the only tool, and psychiatry isn't built to handle evil.

"I believe firmly as a psychiatrist that it is not your fault if you are mentally ill. But it is absolutely your responsibility."

The analogy that makes this concrete: there is genuine sympathy available for someone struggling with alcohol addiction. That sympathy disappears the moment they get behind the wheel drunk and kill someone. The struggle is real; the excuse is not. If you have a mental health problem, it is on you to manage it. Failing that duty places the blame squarely on you.

The Gun Control Question That Deserves an Answer

This part is explicitly framed as a public health question, not a partisan one — though the acknowledgment is made that the two have become inseparable in American discourse.

Australia is demographically similar to the United States and has a fraction of the school shootings, largely attributed to strict gun control laws. There is counter-evidence: some U.S. states with strong gun laws haven't seen dramatic reductions in violent crime. Both data points are real.

But here's the actual question being posed — not as an indictment, but as a genuine ask:

"If you are not in favor of gun control, what's your plan? You don't get to say 'I don't know' or 'it shouldn't be this way,' because this is science — this is fact."

The alternative "plans" currently in play: schools being architecturally redesigned with curved hallways to reduce a shooter's line of sight. Costco selling bulletproof backpacks for children. These aren't rhetorical strawmen — they're real things happening right now. They might technically count as responses, but they're responses that accept child death as a baseline and engineer around it.

The ask is simple: if you oppose gun control, articulate what you're going to do instead. "Nothing" is not an answer when people are dying.

Online Drift and the Radicalization Nobody Notices

This is probably the most practically important section for most readers, because it describes something happening to your mind right now, invisibly.

Every major content platform is built to serve you more of what you engage with. That sounds neutral, even helpful. The problem is that engagement correlates with emotional intensity, not accuracy. The content that gets clicks is not the content that is most correct or most nuanced — it's the content that makes you feel something, especially anger or outrage.

Over time, platforms don't just show you more of what you like — they show you more extreme versions of what you like. This process is called online drift. You might start in a mainstream community and slowly migrate, click by click, toward more intense, more ideologically rigid, more emotionally extreme content. The shift happens gradually enough that you rarely notice it happening.

Safe spaces and niche communities accelerate this. A community built around a shared experience — loneliness, gender identity, political belief — often starts with genuine value. But when contrary voices are excluded (sometimes for good reasons, because many "discussions" are actually attacks in disguise), the community loses its immune system against extremism. What's left is a feedback loop that keeps amplifying its own conclusions.

"Content creation platforms, as they chase emotional engagement and dopaminergic clicks, are radicalizing us in the process."

The manifestos of mass shooters are filled with echo-chamber thinking. This isn't a coincidence.

What's important here is that blaming the platforms, while not entirely wrong, is also a way of letting yourself off the hook. You are the one clicking. You are the one who stays in communities that have drifted toxic. Pointing at Facebook or Reddit as the problem is the same move every other actor makes: "It's someone else's fault, so I don't have to change."

Responsibility Is Everyone's Job

There is a long list of parties that have refused to accept responsibility for the school shooting problem: the NRA, YouTube, Reddit, the government, legislators, individuals, parents, friends, bullies. Every single one of them defaults to pointing at someone else. Because every human mind defaults to pointing at someone else.

The way out of this is not waiting for the right institution to finally fix it. The way out is everyone doing what they actually can.

"Start by being a decent human being. That's your dharma. That's the beginning of it and the end of it."

Concretely, that means: don't bully. Don't dogpile someone who's already down. Treat people you disagree with like they're human. Expose yourself to contrary views with genuine curiosity instead of using them as target practice. Stop gravitating toward communities that traffic in contempt.

This isn't glamorous. It doesn't feel like saving the world. But the alternative — waiting for someone else to fix a systemic problem while doing nothing yourself — is exactly how the situation stays broken.

What to Actually Do After Trauma

If you or someone you know is in the aftermath of something traumatic, there's a strong instinct among well-meaning people (and historically, among some clinicians) to encourage immediate processing — talk about it, get it out, don't bottle it up.

The research says this is wrong, at least in the immediate aftermath.

After a traumatic event, the brain and body enter a protective dissociative state. Things feel numb, flat, muted. That's not a malfunction — it's the system doing its job, keeping you away from the full emotional impact while your physiology recovers from the enormous stress response that survival requires. Your cortisol spikes, your blood sugar spikes, your brain undergoes rapid rewiring. This is the neurological equivalent of emptying every drawer in the house to find your passport in three minutes — effective in the moment, and a complete mess to clean up afterward.

Forcing someone to re-experience trauma before they're ready reactivates all of that. It re-traumatizes. The same caution applies to certain open-awareness meditation practices for people carrying trauma — if the mind has carefully locked something away, forcing it open does damage.

What actually helps in the immediate aftermath? Dissociation. Letting the mind go somewhere else. There is genuine research showing that playing Tetris after a traumatic event provides measurable benefit. Video games — which get a bad reputation for being an escape — are in this context doing exactly what the brain needs: keeping you occupied, engaging your attention elsewhere, and giving the subconscious time to do its background work without interference.

"If you want to veg out and play a video game after a traumatic event, go for it. Let your mind do what it needs to."

This is one of those cases where what feels like avoidance is actually appropriate self-care.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating mental illness as an explanation for evil. The two can coexist, but one doesn't explain the other. Conflating them harms people who struggle with mental illness and provides unearned cover for people who commit violence.
  • Blaming institutions as a substitute for personal action. It's easy to say Facebook or YouTube is the problem. It costs nothing and requires no change from you. That's precisely why it's so popular and so useless.
  • Forcing trauma processing too soon. Encouraging someone to "talk it out" immediately after a traumatic event, or using intensive meditation practices with someone carrying unprocessed trauma, can worsen outcomes rather than help.
  • Treating online communities as neutral spaces. Safe spaces have genuine value, but unmoderated echo chambers reliably drift toward extremism. Staying in one without noticing the drift is not passive — it's participation.
  • Waiting for a grand dharmic purpose before doing anything. The impulse to wait until you can make a big impact often masks a reluctance to make any change at all. Small decency, practiced consistently, is the actual work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your content diet. Look at what you've been watching, reading, and clicking on for the past week. Ask honestly: is this content becoming more extreme over time? More emotionally charged? Less nuanced? If yes, deliberately seek out one source that challenges your current views — not to be persuaded, but to practice engaging with disagreement.

  2. Practice small-scale decency today. Don't wait for a meaningful opportunity to have impact. In the next conversation you have — online or in person — with someone you disagree with, try to engage them as a person rather than a position. Notice how hard it is. That difficulty is the work.

  3. Stop outsourcing the problem. Pick one thing within your actual reach — voting, donating, having a real conversation with someone radicalizing, moderating your own behavior in online communities — and do it. Criticizing institutions you can't control while ignoring what you can control is not a neutral position; it's a choice to not act.

  4. After a traumatic event, don't force processing. If you or someone close to you has just experienced something traumatic, resist the urge to "work through it" immediately. Let numbness do its job. Engage in absorbing, low-stakes activities — yes, including video games — and allow processing to happen gradually and at the pace the mind sets.

  5. Check whether you're in an echo chamber. If the community you spend the most time in online has no tolerance for dissenting views, ask yourself: have the views in this community become more extreme since you joined? Have yours? Noticing drift is the first step to resisting it.
