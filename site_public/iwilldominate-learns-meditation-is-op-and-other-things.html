The Activity Isn't the Problem — Your Attitude Is

Most people think of activities as inherently good or bad. School is boring. Gaming is fun. Work is a drag. But Dom's own experience breaks that model: he used to dread streaming the same game he now enjoys. The activity didn't change much. Something inside him changed.

This leads to a counterintuitive conclusion: your enjoyment of life is not primarily determined by what you do, but by the state you bring to it. The source of joy or suffering is mostly internal.

Dom's psychiatrist helped him see some glaring basics he had been dismissing: - SleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → schedule: streaming midnight to 8am left him perpetually groggy - Diet: not eating three times a day — not even "eating healthy," just eating - Exercise: moving his body again

These aren't dramatic interventions. But fixing them changed his baseline mood dramatically. It's a useful corrective to how most people think about unhappiness: I'm miserable because Reddit mischaracterizes me is the explanation that feels true, but I never eat and I sleep at the wrong hours is what's actually doing most of the damage.

How You Measure Success Determines How You Feel

For years, Dom used a single metric: average viewer count. Every decision — whether to take a collaboration, change content, go out with friends — got filtered through that number. Going out with friends felt like "losing a stream day." It felt selfish.

That external scoreboard made every plateau feel like failure and made every interaction transactional. He found himself calculating whether talking to someone was worth it in followers gained, not in the value of the conversation itself.

What changed wasn't willpower or discipline. It was reassigning value. Dom started looking for worth in the things he had written off as low-value:

  • A good conversation with a friend
  • A piece of content he felt proud of regardless of its numbers
  • A body of work he could point to and say I made this

Jay-Z Cole described something similar: even taking out the trash can have purpose if you let it. The task doesn't change. The attitude toward it does. And when you start finding value horizontally across activities — rather than chasing a steep vertical hierarchy of "high value" versus "low value" — life gets richer, and somewhat paradoxically, external success tends to follow.

Walking Away From Good Opportunities

There's a pattern worth naming: Dom's real turning point came when he walked away from a stable viewership to try content he actually wanted to make.

Most people trying to grow a streaming channel know this is a bad idea. Changing your content kills your numbers. Every experienced streamer would have called it a mistake. And by the conventional logic of optimizing for growth, it was.

But Dom had hit a financial floor — a number where he knew he could live modestly for the rest of his life — and that gave him permission to stop optimizing for the metric. He traded viewership security for personal fulfillment.

"I think success comes from walking away from good opportunities, not taking them."

The reason this works is that "good opportunity" is always defined by an external standard — what worked for someone else, what looks impressive on paper, what the communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → consensus says is the smart move. But that external standard has nothing to do with your unique circumstances. A good opportunity that isn't aligned with who you are isn't actually good for you.

The corollary: you can't just follow someone else's playbook. No one with Dom's specific history, skills, timing, and personality has ever existed. Playing to your uniqueness is not soft self-help advice — it's just accurate strategy.

Toxicity: What's Actually Going On

Dom's current challenge is that even though he's stopped typing toxic things to teammates, he still thinks them. The frustration is still there.

The root: when a game is unwinnable, he can't express his skill. There's nothing he can do to demonstrate competence. That helplessness converts directly to frustration, which converts to toxicity.

This matters because without voice chat, teammates get dehumanized — they become bots, obstacles, wasted space. And it's much easier to be cruel to something you don't recognize as a full human being. The toxicity in his chat works similarly: some fraction of viewers are there to provoke a reaction because getting a big streamer to react makes them feel like they influenced something real.

The deeper pattern is schadenfreude — pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition → derived from someone else's pain. That impulse is genuinely human. It doesn't always trace back to childhood traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → or deep psychological wounds. Sometimes people are just trolls. Acknowledging this honestly is more useful than forcing a neat causal story.

What is worth noting: the people sending toxic messages are also, in most cases, people who don't have a reliable place to express their own value. The frustration Dom feels on a losing game is probably close to what they feel in their daily lives. That doesn't excuse it, but it contextualizes it.

Meditation Is OP — But Almost Everyone Teaches It Wrong

Dom had tried meditation before and found it useless. The reason: he was trying not to think.

Trying not to think is the wrong goal. It creates internal conflict — one part of the mind generating thoughts, another part fighting them. That tension produces more mental energy, not less. In fact, the mechanics are structurally similar to a panic attack: high mental energy, fighting against your own thoughts. Trying not to think during meditation is essentially practicing the same mode of mind that causes panic.

The right approach, especially for someone with a highly active, driven, competitive mind, is to give the mind something demanding to focus on — not suppress it. A frame that concentrates mental energy rather than battles it.

For Dom, this meant triphasic (segmented) breathing: 1. Inhale into the belly 2. Inhale further into the chest 3. Raise the shoulders to take in a final bit of air 4. Release the shoulders, then contract the chest, then pull the navel toward the spine

The cognitive load of executing each phase correctly occupied enough mental bandwidth that ordinary discursive thought couldn't compete. The result: Dom lost his sense of time, felt spontaneous happiness, and smiled without knowing why.

"Meditation is OP. The problem is that they do such a shitty job teaching it that people don't get it."

The point of meditation is not anxiety reduction or clinical symptom relief — those are side effects. The point is the state itself: alert, present, quiet, and genuinely happy without an external reason. That state is accessible any morning you want it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking the activity is the problem. If you hate your job, it's tempting to assume a new job will fix everything. Sometimes it will help. But the same internal state that makes one job miserable will likely contaminate the next one too.

  • Trying not to think during meditation. This is the near-universal mistake. It turns meditation into a panic attack in slow motion. Give your mind something to do instead.

  • Optimizing for external metrics and calling it success. Viewer counts, salaries, follower numbers — these aren't bad things to track, but when they become the sole arbiter of whether something is worth doing, they hollow everything out. The 45-year-old investment banker at Goldman Sachs who dreads going to work every day got there by chasing good opportunities.

  • Looking for the source of unhappiness only in circumstances. Reddit is mean, the relationship ended, the career plateaued — these feel like the causes. But eating once a day and sleeping at 4pm in the sunlight are also doing work that's easy to ignore.

  • Copying other people's success formulas. What worked for someone else was calibrated to their unique combination of circumstances, skills, timing, and temperament. Borrowing it wholesale is likely to produce mediocre results and definite misery.

  • Attributing all toxicity to deep psychological wounds. Sometimes the explanation is simpler: schadenfreude is human, some people troll for fun, and forcing a childhood-trauma narrative onto every instance of online cruelty is both inaccurate and unhelpful.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your physical baseline before anything else. Sleep on a schedule that matches daylight. Eat three meals a day. Move your body in some form. These are not glamorous interventions, but they are probably doing more damage than anything Reddit says about you.

  2. Notice what your actual barometer of success is right now. Write it down specifically — viewer count, salary, approval from a particular person. Then ask: does chasing this make me feel more like myself or less?

  3. Try the value-finding exercise with a low-stakes task. Before you do something boring (dishes, trash, a tedious work task), take a quick mental snapshot of how you feel. Do the thing. Take another snapshot after. Study the difference. This is how Dom's Jay Cole example works in practice.

  4. Identify one "good opportunity" you've been pursuing that doesn't actually fit you. What would it look like to walk one step laterally instead — toward something adjacent that you'd actually want to do?

  5. Try triphasic breathing meditation. Sit up, close your eyes, and practice the four-phase breath: belly in → chest in → shoulders up → shoulders down → chest out → belly out. Repeat for 5–10 minutes. The goal isn't to clear your mind; it's to focus your mind so completely on the mechanics that ordinary thinking can't gain traction. When you finish, sit quietly for a moment and just observe your breath returning to normal. Do this at a consistent time — dawn works particularly well if you can manage it.

  6. Study your wins as carefully as your losses. Pick a day when you made a good decision — got off social media, had a productive session, treated someone well under pressure — and ask what was different about that day. The information in your successes is just as useful as the information in your failures, and most people ignore it entirely.
