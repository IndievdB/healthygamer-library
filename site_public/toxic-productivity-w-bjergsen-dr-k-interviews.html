Summary

TL;DR — A relentless drive to improve can be both the engine of your success and a quiet source of suffering. The key is learning to tell the difference between what you genuinely need to do and what you're doing to escape feeling weak.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Bjergsen (Søren Bjerg), a professional League of Legends player turned head coach. What starts as a practical question about season burnout and emotional flatness unfolds into something deeper: an examination of how early experiences of inadequacy can quietly wire a person's entire relationship to work, identity, and rest. The ideas here apply far beyond esports.

Two Different People: On-Season and Off-Season

There's a particular pattern that shows up in high-performance environments — a kind of split self. During the competitive grind, you go robotic. Emotions flatten, creativity disappears, awareness of yourself and others narrows. You stop noticing team dynamics, stop seeing problems from new angles, and start just going through the motions. Then the season ends and suddenly you can cry again, think creatively, and reflect clearly.

This isn't a personality quirk. There's a physiological explanation: sustained high stress keeps cortisol and adrenaline elevated, and those hormones are not designed for nuanced thinking. They're designed for survival. When your brain's fight-or-flight system is chronically engaged, creative and self-aware thinking gets deprioritized. The mind that can see the big picture at the start of the season gradually gets replaced by a tunnel-vision machine.

The cruel irony is that the grind you're doing to get better is, past a certain point, degrading the quality of the thinking that makes you actually good at what you do.

The Feeling That Never Catches Up

Here's a pattern that shows up across high achievers — in esports, investment banking, medicine, anywhere performance is intense and measurable. A person feels behind. They feel underdeveloped, inadequate, like they have catching up to do. So they work incredibly hard. They rise. They reach the top of their field.

And they still feel behind.

The problem is that the feeling of "I have catching up to do" is not actually tracking your real-world progress. It has its own internal logic, formed long before your career started. When the feeling drives behavior instead of reality driving behavior, you end up in what's essentially an unwinnable game — because you can never know when you've caught up, because the goalpost is emotional, not objective.

"I feel like I have a lot of catching up to do."

That sentence, said by someone who had already won a championship and was now head coach of a top-tier team, is the clearest possible illustration of how this works.

Where It Actually Comes From

Being bullied between fourth and eighth grade — badly enough to drop out — leaves a mark. Being physically small, thrown over a fence into the kindergarten yard, having your backpack stolen so you'd miss the bus and have to walk five miles home — those aren't just bad memories. They shape what you believe about yourself.

The word that surfaces for a lot of people who were bullied is weak. And a profound amount of adult behavior can be traced back to a single, fierce, largely unconscious decision: I will never be weak again.

"Like, you would never put yourself in that situation... you've climbed fully out of the pit, but you need reminders to keep you striving."

The skull on the piano — a physical object meant to remind you that life is short and you should make the most of every day — is a good symbol for this whole mechanism. On the surface it's a productivity tool. Underneath, it's fear. The fear that if you stop pushing, even for a day, you'll end up back in the pit.

That fear is not irrational. It worked. It genuinely drove the work ethic and resilience that produced real success. The problem is that it doesn't know when to stop. It keeps running the same program even after the original threat is long gone.

Toxic Productivity: What the Phrase Actually Means

Toxic productivity isn't just working too much. It's working from the wrong emotional source. There's a crucial distinction between:

  • Actions driven by genuine necessity (you have to watch film of tomorrow's opponent — that's just the job)
  • Actions driven by fear of feeling weak (watching film at midnight when you're burned out because stopping feels like letting everyone down)

These can look identical from the outside. They can even feel similar from the inside. But they produce very different results over time. Actions driven by fear of inadequacy tend to follow a boom-bust cycle: grind to exhaustion, burn out, stop, feel guilty, grind again. Actions driven by genuine necessity and care tend to be more sustainable and, paradoxically, more effective.

The burnout cycle isn't a discipline problem. It's a signal that the emotional driver has taken over from the rational one.

The Rigged Game of Reassurance

One of the most painful features of deep-seated inadequacy is that it becomes immune to evidence. Teammates tell you that you're doing great. Your CEO praises your work. A friend says you're enough. And each time, there's a voice that says: they don't really know me.

This is not ingratitude or arrogance. It's a structural problem. The feeling of inadequacy was formed in a context that had nothing to do with job performance, so no amount of job-related evidence can fully reach it. Compliments bounce off because the part of you receiving them is still, in some corner, the kid in the kindergarten yard.

"No matter who you hear it from... even if you heard it from the CEO... that voice is always going to tell you you're not enough."

This also creates self-fulfilling prophecies. In relationships, a person convinced their partner is dissatisfied will latch onto the 10% of feedback that confirms that fear and dismiss the 90% that contradicts it. Eventually the dynamic created by that anxiety produces the very outcome they feared.

The same dynamic can play out professionally.

The Paradox at the Center

Here's the uncomfortable loop that any honest version of this conversation has to acknowledge:

If you say "you need to accept yourself more," you've implicitly said there's something you need to get better at — which feeds the very engine you're trying to cool down.

If you say "you're fine exactly as you are, stop striving," you're also not quite right, because growth and care about your work are real and good.

The honest answer isn't to resolve the paradox but to notice it. The moment you can see that both paths — relentlessly striving AND forcing yourself to stop — can come from the same wound, something loosens. You don't have to choose a path. You just have to develop enough awareness to recognize which emotional state is behind a given action in a given moment.

"The actual action you take at the end of the day is not rewiring the neurons. What rewires the neurons is asking yourself the question and being able to authentically answer it."

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking drive for dysfunction. Not all ambition is toxic productivity. The problem isn't wanting to improve — it's when fear of weakness becomes the primary fuel and reason starts to take a back seat.

  • Using growth-language to avoid uncomfortable feelings. Framing everything as "I just want to get better" or "I always have room to improve" can be genuine — or it can be a way of not looking at what's actually underneath. The language of growth is easy to weaponize against yourself.

  • Expecting reassurance to fix the feeling. Seeking validation from teammates, partners, or coaches won't resolve a sense of inadequacy rooted in earlier experience. The person receiving the validation needs to do internal work — not collect more positive data points.

  • Assuming that letting go of the fear will destroy your work ethic. This is a very common and very understandable fear. But the evidence is the opposite: people operating from positive energy and genuine care tend to outperform people operating from anxiety and self-punishment, especially over the long term and especially at the highest levels.

  • Waiting until burnout to rest. If rest only happens after you've completely depleted yourself, you're still letting the feeling of inadequacy make the schedule. The boom-bust cycle is a sign of who's really driving.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ask the question before acting. When you're about to put in extra hours, watch more film, skip a break, or take on more than you planned, pause and honestly ask: Am I doing this because the work genuinely needs it, or because I'd feel weak if I didn't? You don't have to change what you do — just answer truthfully. Over time, the honest answer is what starts to reshape the pattern.

  2. Notice the burnout cycle. Track whether your effort on a given task (watching film, studying opponents, extra meetings) follows a boom-bust pattern — intense overwork followed by avoidance. If it does, that's almost always a sign the emotional driver has taken over. Aim for a steadier, lower baseline you can actually sustain.

  3. Sit with the feeling instead of acting on it. When the sense of inadequacy comes up — the "I need to do more or I'm failing everyone" feeling — don't immediately act on it or try to make it go away. Just notice it. Label it. "This is the feeling of inadequacy." You don't have to solve it. The awareness itself is the work.

  4. Try the doorway breathing practice. Every time you walk through a doorway, take one conscious breath. Inhale and exhale. You can pair it with any mental note that feels genuine to you — strength, presence, whatever is grounding. The goal isn't the words; it's using the doorway (which neurologically resets your thought process anyway) as a recurring moment of self-awareness throughout the day. It's harder than it sounds precisely because it's constant.

  5. Watch what happens when you receive a compliment. Don't just respond politely. Notice what happens inside after someone praises your work. Is there a quiet voice that says they don't really know me or they're just being nice? If so, don't try to argue with that voice. Just ask: Where is this coming from? Tracing it back — not necessarily to a specific memory, just to the feeling itself — is how it gradually loses force.

  6. Separate the two things. Practice distinguishing between "I care about my team and don't want to let them down" and "I'm afraid I'm fundamentally not good enough." Both can be true at once, but they're different. The first one is healthy. The second one can be worked with. Letting the second one masquerade as the first is where the trouble tends to live.
