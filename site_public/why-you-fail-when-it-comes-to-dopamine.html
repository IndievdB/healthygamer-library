What Are You Actually Expecting?

Here's the question most people never stop to ask before attempting a dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → detox: What do you think this is going to feel like?

If your brain has been trained on pornography, video games, and social media, and you sit down to read a book or go for a hike, it is going to feel awful. Boring. Pointless. Your mind will be counting down the minutes until you can go back.

And here's the honest truth: it was never going to feel any other way. Reading is not going to feel like pornography. A hike is not going to feel like a video game. No one gets addicted to cooking because cooking isn't designed to be addictive. YouTube and video games are taking over people's lives precisely because they are by design more dopaminergic and behaviorally reinforcing than almost any real-world activity.

Waiting for healthy activities to produce the same dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → hit as a video game is a trap. That moment will never arrive. If you're tolerating a walk in the park only because you believe it will eventually feel as good as your favorite game — you're going to quit, and you're going to quit soon.

The question to sit with is: what were you expecting, and is that expectation realistic?

The Real Choice: Brain vs. Soul

Once you drop the fantasy that healthy activities will eventually feel as stimulating as addictive ones, you're left facing the actual decision in front of you.

A dopaminergic life gives you: - Constant stimulation - An always-occupied mind - A short-term sense of entertainment

But underneath that, it produces: - Existential dread in the quiet moments - ShameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, envy, and emptiness that keep growing - A feeling that your life is moving in the wrong direction - Emotional demons you're perpetually pushing back into the closet with more stimulation

A soul-oriented life — making art, building relationships, doing things you're proud of — gives you: - Genuine fulfillment and self-respect - The possibility of real connection - Something to show for your time

But it costs you: - Constant stimulation - Easy entertainment - The ability to hit fast-forward on discomfort

Neither path is sold to you honestly. The dopaminergic path markets itself as fun and never mentions the emptiness. The other path gets marketed as rewarding without acknowledging that it involves real boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition →, frustration, and sustained effort.

You have to choose between them with open eyes. And critically — you have to choose before the healthy option starts feeling good, because it may not feel good for a long time.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting for the discomfort to end. The single most common way people fail is asking "how do I get over the pain?" It's a natural question, but it's the wrong one. It frames the discomfort of real life as a temporary obstacle to push through before things get comfortable again. They don't. A relationship doesn't become easy. Creative work doesn't stop requiring blood, sweat, and tears. If you're holding out for the day when the healthy path feels as effortless as scrolling, you'll fold every time.

Expecting real activities to compete with engineered ones. Books are not designed to be as gripping as Lost Ark or Fortnite. Human conversation is not engineered to be as stimulating as pornography. Measuring real life against algorithmically optimized content and finding real life lacking is not a discovery about real life — it's a discovery about how good engineers are at their jobs.

The decision has to include the cost, or it won't survive the first bad day.

Be honest. This is step one of making a real plan.

  2. Ask the genuine preference question. Sit quietly and ask yourself: A year from now, would I rather have spent the year losing myself in [YouTube/games/porn], or spent the year bored and making progress on something I care about? Write down your actual, gut-level answer — not the "right" answer.

  3. Name what you're choosing, including the cost. If you decide you want the soul-oriented path, write down explicitly: "I am choosing to be bored. I am choosing to feel like the healthy activity sucks, possibly for months. I am not waiting for it to stop sucking." Make the deal with yourself before you start, not in the middle of a hard moment.

  4. Drop the expectation that it will feel good soon. The next time you do something healthy — a walk, reading, cooking — go in with zero expectation of enjoyment. You're not doing it because it will feel good. You're doing it because it's the path you chose. Removing the expectation removes the constant disappointment that makes you quit.

  5. Separate your "shoulds" from your genuine wants. On paper, make two columns. In one, write what you think you should want or value. In the other, write what your actual behavior suggests you currently want. Notice the gap. The work of changing your life is moving things from the left column to the right — not by willpower, but by honestly understanding where the gap comes from.

  6. Stop asking "when does the pain end?" — and notice when you're asking it. This is the question that guarantees failure. When you catch yourself asking it — during a boring walk, during an awkward social interaction, during a creative session where nothing is flowing — recognize it as the signal that you're on the right path, not the wrong one.
