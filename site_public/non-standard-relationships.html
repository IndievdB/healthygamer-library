Summary

TL;DR — Whether it's a situationship, polyamory, or the friend zone, the common thread is that avoiding honest communication makes all of them worse, not easier.

Situationships

The people most likely to enter a situationship are those focused on career or financial goals who also have a high sex drive — essentially people who don't have the bandwidth for a full relationship. The irony is that the thing they're trying to avoid — communication — is exactly what makes these arrangements actually work.

Research on successful situationships points to a handful of upfront conversations most people dread having:

  • Negotiation from the start: What is this, and what isn't it? What does each person want?
  • Sexual boundaries: Exclusivity, contraception, safety.
  • Honest intentions: If one person secretly wants a romantic relationship, hiding that tends to poison things.
  • Logistics: How often do you contact each other? Is this secret or not?
  • An exit plan: Under what conditions does this end? If one of you starts dating someone else, what happens?

Situationships also carry a built-in biological risk: the same behaviors that build emotional connection in a full relationship — spending the night, physical closeness, regular contact — trigger the same bonding mechanisms here. Polyamory

About 4% of people are currently in polyamorous relationships, but around 20% have tried it and left. That gap matters: studies measuring happiness in polyamory only capture people who are still in it, not the ones who dropped out. So the finding that polyamorous and monogamous people report similar happiness levels should be read with that in mind.

One genuine upside the research does point to: multiple partners can distribute the weight of a relationship. Emotional needs, sexual needs, practical support — these don't all have to fall on one person.

The Friend Zone

People who get chronically friend-zoned are usually following the wrong formula. Fearing rejection, they avoid sending explicit romantic signals and instead pile on nice gestures — airport pickups, favors, gifts — hoping to gradually earn the other person's affection. The problem is that being liked and being found attractive are not the same thing. Over time the investment grows, resentment builds, and that toxicity moves things further in the wrong direction.

The better move is to say something. Not a grand confession that drops an emotional burden on the other person, but something honest and low-pressure:

"I know we've been friends for a while, but I'm starting to have some romantic feelings about you — I'm thinking about you in a different way."

Frame it as something the friendship can figure out together, then ask directly whether they'd want to explore it. If the answer is no, the hardest but most important step is to actually move on. Staying in the friend zone while carrying those feelings doesn't just hurt — it occupies the mental and emotional space where someone who is interested could otherwise exist.
