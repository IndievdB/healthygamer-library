Summary

TL;DR — Chasing achievements won't make you happy, not because achievement is bad, but because happiness doesn't live in outcomes. It lives in presence and unmet expectations.

Most people spend their lives assuming happiness is waiting at the next milestone — finish school, get the job, buy the house, then retire into contentment. But when the milestone arrives, nothing changes. The feeling is underwhelming, sometimes even emptier than before. Understanding why that happens — and where happiness actually comes from — is what this talk is about.

The Treadmill We're All On

There's a familiar pattern: finish primary school, aim for high school, finish high school, aim for college, finish college, aim for a job, get the job, aim for a promotion. At every stage the implicit promise is the same — the next one will be the one. It never is.

This isn't a personal failure. It's a structural problem with how most of us are taught to think about happiness. We're handed a script — do these things, follow this path — but the script never explains why those things are supposed to feel good, or what to do when they don't.

The result is what you could call the completionist theory of happiness: the idea that life is like building a house, and you can't feel at home until every room is finished. You don't get 10% happy when the frame goes up. You're waiting for 100% completion. The problem is that 100% never actually arrives, and even if it did, there's no guarantee it would deliver what you imagined.

Why We Keep Chasing Anyway

There are three real reasons the achievement-happiness myth is so persistent.

1. We can only see what people do, not what they feel. When you see someone who appears happy and successful, you observe their external life — their job, their degree, their lifestyle. You cannot observe the two years of therapy, the internal work, the shift in perspective that actually produced that contentment. So you copy what's visible. You chase the achievement because that's the part you can measure and replicate.

This is the same mistake as watching a monk eat simply and concluding that restricting food will make you spiritual. The monk doesn't eat simply in order to become free from desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → — they already conquered the desire through inner practice, and simple eating is just a byproduct. The causality runs the opposite direction to what it looks like from the outside.

2. Achievement and wellbeing aren't completely unrelated. There's a real, if limited, correlation. Being financially stable, having a degree, maintaining relationships — these things genuinely reduce certain kinds of suffering. So chasing them isn't irrational. The mistake is assuming they're sufficient for happiness, not just helpful for avoiding certain miseries.

3. An unhappy mind looks outward for solutions. When you're not happy, the mind does something very subtle: it looks at what you don't have and concludes that getting that thing will fix the feeling. If you're lonely, you assume a relationship will solve it. If you haven't graduated, you assume graduating will solve it. The unhappiness itself generates the belief that an external achievement is the answer. This is why people who are miserable keep chasing, and why the chase never ends — because the mind keeps generating new targets.

What Actually Creates Happiness

When you look at moments in your own life when you were genuinely happy — not just relieved, but actually lit up — two things tend to be present.

Low or absent expectations. Think about receiving a gift. The same mediocre gift lands completely differently depending on whether it's your birthday (where you expected something great) versus a random Tuesday (where you expected nothing). On your birthday it's disappointing. On a random Tuesday it's a pleasant surprise. The object is identical. The experience is opposite. Happiness isn't in the thing — it's in the gap between what you expected and what you got.

This is why doing something for the first time often hits harder than repeating it. The first time you hear the epic music at the end of a game, it's overwhelming because you didn't see it coming. The second time, even if everything external is the same, the expectation is already there and the ceiling is lower. Novelty itself isn't the key — you can replay the same game and still enjoy it. What matters is whether expectation has inflated the experience ahead of actually having it.

Full presence in the current moment. AnxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → lives in the future. Regret lives in the past. Neither of them can exist in the present moment. When you're genuinely immersed in something — in flow, fully engaged — there's no room for either. That's not a coincidence. That's where the enjoyment is actually happening.

This is why people love flow states, and why work can sometimes feel more alive than leisure. It's not about the activity itself. It's about whether your mind is here or somewhere else.

"You can't be anxious in the present, and you can't have regret in the present. Anxiety is in the future and regret is in the past."

The Counterintuitive Truth About Permanent Happiness

Here's the part that's uncomfortable but important: permanent happiness probably doesn't exist. Not as a stable state you finally arrive at and maintain. Most people, if you actually ask them, have never met someone who is permanently happy.

The pursuit of guaranteed happiness — building a life where contentment is automatic and protected — is actually one of the main things that prevents happiness. Because it keeps you living in the future, optimizing conditions, waiting for the setup to be complete.

"It's the guarantee of happiness that prevents us from ever being happy."

When you accept that happiness is temporary — that it's always been temporary, that it can only ever exist in a particular moment — something shifts. You stop managing for a future state and start actually inhabiting the present one. The moment with the epic music, your friend beside you, the boss fight clicking into place — you recognize it as a once-in-a-lifetime thing you'll never get back in exactly this form. So you're fully in it.

That intensity and presence, paradoxically, tends to produce better outcomes in the rest of life too. When you work with genuine engagement rather than anxious achievement-chasing, it shows. But that's a side effect, not the goal.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until conditions are right to "allow" yourself to be happy. The feeling that there's still too much left to accomplish, that you're not ready yet — this is the completionist trap. There's always another quest. If happiness requires finishing the game, you'll never get it.

Chasing novelty as a substitute for presence. It can look like the first experience was better because it was new, which leads to constantly seeking new experiences, new relationships, new games, new jobs. But novelty isn't the mechanism. Absent expectation and genuine presence are the mechanism. You can find both in familiar things.

Copying the external lives of happy-seeming people. The inner work that creates contentment is invisible. What you see is the degree, the job, the lifestyle. Copying those without doing the internal work produces the same empty result every time.

Thinking temporary happiness is lesser happiness. Accepting that happiness is temporary can feel like a depressing downgrade. It isn't. It's actually what makes it possible to fully inhabit the moments when it's present.

Assuming suffering is only for people whose lives are falling apart. If your life looks fine on paper and you still feel hollow, that's not ingratitude or a weird anomaly. The same structural confusion about happiness affects everyone. A smooth external life doesn't make the internal question irrelevant.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Study your own happiness today. Don't wait. Start paying attention right now to moments — even small ones — when you feel genuinely good. What's present in those moments? What's absent? Keep a rough mental (or written) log.

  2. Notice your expectations before an event. Before something you're looking forward to, catch yourself building an anticipatory picture of how it'll go. Just noticing this habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → starts to loosen its grip. You don't have to eliminate expectations — just see them.

  3. Practice present-moment check-ins during activities. When you're doing something enjoyable, periodically ask: where is my mind right now? If it's in the future (planning, anticipating, worrying) or the past (replaying, comparing), gently bring it back to what's actually happening in front of you.

  4. Ask someone you perceive as happy how they got there — and actually listen, without filtering their answer through what you already believe. If you can't find such a person, that itself is useful information.

  5. Resist the next milestone substitution. When you notice yourself thinking "I'll feel better when ___," pause. Ask whether you felt better after the last time you hit that kind of milestone. Use your own experience as evidence, not the assumption that this time will be different.

  6. Treat happiness as an experiment, not a destination. Approach the question of what makes you happy the way you'd approach any puzzle — with curiosity, not urgency. Each moment of genuine enjoyment is data. Collect it.
