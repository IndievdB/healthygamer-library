Summary

TL;DR — Passive suicidality — a quiet indifference to being alive rather than an active wish to die — is far more common than official statistics suggest, and it's driven by broken dopamine circuitry that can be deliberately repaired.

Most mental health resources are aimed at people in acute crisis: those with a plan, a means, and an intention. But there's a much larger, mostly invisible group who aren't trying to die — they just wouldn't mind if they did. They wake up with nothing to look forward to, fill their time with video games or pornography or binge-watching not out of enjoyment but out of a need to fast-forward through existence, and have stopped believing their lives can meaningfully improve. This state — passive suicidality — sits beneath a staggering number of modern complaints: addiction, burnout, anhedonia, and the vague sense that nothing matters.

This piece unpacks what passive suicidality actually is, why it's spreading, what's happening in the brain, and — most importantly — what you can concretely do about it.

What Passive Suicidality Actually Looks Like

Active suicidality is what the medical system is built to catch: a person with intent, a plan, and a means. Emergency rooms, locked psychiatric units, crisis lines — all of it is designed for that scenario. And that infrastructure is genuinely valuable.

Passive suicidality is something different. It sounds like this:

"It's not that I want to kill myself. It's just that if I went to sleep tonight and never woke up, I would be completely fine with that."

There's no yearning to live. No active desire to die, either — just a total absence of desire to continue. If a meteor hit, fine. If a terminal diagnosis arrived, whatever. Life has become something to get through, not something to inhabit.

This state is the undercurrent beneath a lot of behavior that gets labeled differently: video game addiction, pornography addiction, chronic procrastination, burnout. When you scratch beneath the surface of someone who can't stop gaming for 12 hours a day, you often find someone who isn't really enjoying the game — they're using it to hit the fast-forward button on a life that holds nothing for them.

Official statistics put lifetime suicidal ideation at around 10–11% of the general population. Among people with psychiatric diagnoses or mood/anxiety disorders — a group that's growing rapidly — that number jumps to around 47%. And those diagnostic categories are expanding every year.

Why the Brain Gives Up: The Downward Trajectory Calculation

Your brain is constantly modeling the future. It's good at it — the same way it knows whether you can hold off on finding a bathroom until the next exit, it looks at your life's trajectory and makes a forecast.

Passive suicidality is not, at its root, about a life that's objectively unsalvageable. It's about a perceived downward trend. Your brain sees where you were, where you are, and extrapolates forward — and the line is going down. No relationship ever worked. Every job application was rejected. You tried working out and got injured. You went to college and it didn't open the doors you expected. You've done the things society told you to do and gotten nothing back.

That's the experience of many people in this state. They're not delusional or weak. They learned from their experience, and their experience has been one of trying and failing, over and over, at a volume the human brain never evolved to handle. The sheer quantity of rejection modern life generates — hundreds of job applications, years of fruitless dating — can be 100 to 1,000 times what our brains developed to process.

"It's really easy to be hopeful about life when the work that you put into life gets you something in return. It's way harder to be hopeful about life when there's nothing to hope for."

Hope Is Not an Emotion — It's a Calculation

This is the most important reframing in the whole talk: hope is not a feeling you generate through willpower or positive thinking. It is a two-variable computation your brain runs automatically.

Research on psychotherapy with suicidal patients identifies two requirements for hope:

  1. A sense of agency — the belief that your actions can affect your situation. Not that everything will work out, but that you have some power in the world.
  2. A road map — a believable path forward. Not certainty of success, but a logical sequence of steps your brain can actually trace.

Both are necessary. Neither alone is sufficient.

This is why standard advice fails people in this state. When someone tells you "just put yourself out there" or "here's what you should do," they're handing you a road map without first restoring your sense of agency. When a road map lands in the hands of someone who doesn't feel powerful, it has the opposite effect — because when it doesn't work (as it often won't), the conclusion is: this works for everyone else but me. I'm unsavable.

Suicidality itself is, at its core, a control behavior. When someone keeps a noose in their basement and resists getting rid of it, it's not because they're planning to use it. It's because it represents the one last piece of control they have: I can make this stop if I need to. Taking it away — which is absolutely the right clinical move — produces a spike in anxiety, because the person has just lost their only perceived exit. Understanding this reframes suicidality from something frightening and irrational into something psychologically coherent, which is the first step toward actually addressing it.

The Neuroscience of Emptiness: Anhedonia and Dopamine

Anhedonia — the loss of interest or pleasure in activities — is the neurological engine of passive suicidality. And the mainstream psychiatric toolkit is poorly equipped to treat it.

Most antidepressants target serotonin. Serotonin is a molecule of contentment — it reduces wanting and craving. After orgasm, a surge of serotonin is what makes you no longer interested in sex. SSRIs are effective at reducing negative affect (the darkness), but they do little for anhedonia. If your life is empty and full of pain, removing the pain leaves you with an empty life. You've moved from suffering to neutral. That's not the same as having reasons to live.

The system responsible for wanting, craving, motivation, and reward is the dopaminergic system, centered on the nucleus accumbens. This is what video games, pornography, drugs, and binge-watching all tap into — reliably, efficiently, with minimal effort and guaranteed return. When your brain is doing the math on how to spend its energy:

"What about all this other stuff — have a family, have a career, have a life? That's a lot of work and it may not work out. Whereas this little bit of heroin I can inject into my veins — that's a sure thing."

The dopaminergic circuit learns through behavioral reinforcement: pleasure causes you to repeat the behavior that produced it. If the only things generating dopamine are passive entertainment, your brain gets wired to want more passive entertainment. Real-world progress generates little or no dopamine, so it doesn't get reinforced, so you don't do it again. This is not a moral failure. It's the predictable output of a circuit that's been trained on the wrong inputs.

Research on anhedonia also distinguishes between two subtypes: - Consummatory anhedonia: reduced pleasure during an experience - Motivational anhedonia: reduced anticipation of and drive toward rewarding events

Both need to be addressed. And the good news is that there are ways to do it.

The Goal Dilution Problem

Here's one of the most counterintuitive findings covered in the talk, drawn from computational modeling of anhedonic circuitry:

The more goals you have, the less dopamine you get from making progress on any one of them.

Imagine two people making identical progress in their career — both move from a 2 to a 5 on a 10-point scale. But one person has already taken care of their health and social life. The other has those goals also sitting at 0. The first person experiences the career progress as a large win relative to the remaining gap. The second person's brain registers the same progress as a drop in the bucket — because it's computing 50 units of progress against 150 units remaining across all dimensions.

Every incomplete goal in your mind dilutes the dopaminergic reward from progress in any direction. This is why people with long mental to-do lists often feel no satisfaction when they check something off. It's not a discipline problem. It's the math of how the brain processes reward.

This is also why video games generate such powerful dopamine: while you're playing, you're not thinking about your career, your relationships, or your physical health. All those incomplete goals disappear from working memory. So any progress in the game is rewarded against a near-zero baseline of competing demands.

"The more goals you have in your mind that are incomplete, every goal weighs down the dopaminergic activation from all the other progress. It's diluted."

How to Actually Start Rewiring This

Three concrete mechanisms that research on positive affect treatment has identified:

1. Anticipate internal rewards, not external ones

External reward framing: I'll study for three hours and maybe get an A in three months. This does almost nothing for anhedonic dopamine circuitry unless you already have high conscientiousness.

Internal reward framing: If I do the dishes now, when I walk into the kitchen tomorrow morning I won't feel that low-grade dread. The reward is the relief you're giving your future self. You're not accomplishing something grand — you're reducing a small, specific suffering.

The trick is to actively imagine that future moment. How will it feel to walk into a clean kitchen? How will it feel to wake up having already done the thing? This anticipatory imaging is what begins to activate the dopaminergic circuit in the direction of real-world behavior.

2. Bias toward bittersweet, not positive

Toxic positivity — converting all bad experiences into lessons or gifts — doesn't work and often makes things worse. But the opposite bias, which is the default in depression, is also harmful. The anhedonic brain filters out positive signals automatically. You have to consciously correct for this.

Research on healthy aging found that elderly people whose happiness increases over time share one trait: they see their lives as bittersweet. Not good, not bad — both. They don't chase happiness or flee pain. They can hold loss and warmth at the same time. People with severe depression, on the other hand, see almost exclusively the negative.

The practice: at the end of any day, identify three things that went reasonably well or that you did adequately, alongside three things that didn't. This isn't about pretending things are fine. It's about counteracting a filter that's silently removing positive signals from your perception.

3. Reduce goals ruthlessly — focus on one at a time

Given the dilution effect described above, the most productive thing you can do if you're in a passive suicidal state is to stop trying to fix everything. Pick the smallest, most tractable thing and focus there. Let other goals exist on paper somewhere, but don't let them live in active working memory while you're trying to make progress.

The dishes. The one hour of studying. The single document you open and write something in. The value isn't in what you accomplish — it's in giving your nucleus accumbens a clean, undiluted signal that you did something and it felt okay. Stack enough of those, and the circuit starts to change.

The Dopamine Timing Problem

One underappreciated reason motivation is so depleted in modern life: most people spend their first hour or two on a device.

Dopamine is packaged and stored during sleep, ready to be used the next day. It's a finite daily supply. When you spend the first 60–90 minutes scrolling, you burn through a large portion of that reserve on passive content consumption. By the time you get to work or any real-world task, there's very little dopamine left for the behavioral reinforcement that makes effort feel worthwhile.

Working first thing in the morning — before opening social media or video content — means you're spending that dopamine on something that can actually reinforce productive behavior. The same task, done at 7am versus 11am, will feel meaningfully different.

Sense of Agency: It Doesn't Require Power

A common misreading of agency is that it requires capability, resources, or success. It doesn't. It requires the belief that you have some influence over your own situation.

A patient who survived a genocidal concentration camp was asked how she endured. Her answer: every day, she focused on the one thing no one could take from her — the decision not to give up. She couldn't control the guards, the regime, or her location. She could control whether she quit.

"They can take everything away from me, but the one thing I can control is whether I give up or not."

That is agency. It lives in the mind, not in circumstances.

The practical implication: agency is built through doing, not through achieving. Signing up for the thing, making the call, starting the document — these actions are agency. The outcome is separate. The moment you act, you have taken control of something. That's where the sense of agency comes from, and that's where hope begins to grow.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting until you feel hopeful to act. Hope doesn't precede action — it follows from a developing sense of agency, which only comes from acting. Waiting for motivation before starting is waiting for something that only appears after you've started.

  • Treating every problem at once. If your life feels broken in five dimensions and you try to fix all five simultaneously, you will feel less reward from progress in any of them than if you focused on one. This feels counterintuitive but is neurologically well-supported.

  • Expecting external results to create internal motivation. "I'll feel better when I have a girlfriend / a job / an apartment." Anhedonic circuitry doesn't update on external events the way people expect. The internal experience of effort and small progress needs to come first.

  • Consuming advice without acting on it. Podcasts, lectures, videos — none of these appear in the computational model of what activates reward circuitry. Understanding the framework matters, but it's not one of the variables. Experience and iteration are.

  • Celebrating outcomes instead of process. Rewarding yourself only when you finish the thesis can accidentally wire you to love the party, not the work. The most durable reinforcement comes from noticing, during the process, that you're doing something.

  • Mistaking passive suicidality for a character flaw. People in this state are often remarkably resilient and capable — they're doing Herculean effort just to function, because their brain provides no dopaminergic return on any effort. This is a circuitry problem, not a willpower problem.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do one small thing tonight that your morning self will thank you for. Before you go to bed, identify one source of low-grade dread you'll face tomorrow (a sink of dishes, an unopened email, a task you've been putting off). Handle it. As you do it, spend 30 seconds actively imagining how it will feel to encounter that thing already done. Don't rush past this — the imagining is the exercise.

  2. Audit your goal list and cut it. Write down everything you're currently holding as an active goal or obligation. Then choose one — the smallest, most concrete, most doable — and consciously park the others. They still exist, but they're not competing for attention today. Notice whether completing something small feels different when it's the only thing on the board.

  3. At the end of today, name three things that were okay. Not great, not life-changing. Just things that happened or that you did that were neutral-to-positive. Do this alongside noting what went badly. The point is balance, not spin. Do this for two weeks and pay attention to whether your baseline perception of the day shifts.

  4. Change your morning device rule. For one week, do not open any app, video, or social feed for the first 30 minutes after waking. Use that window for something that requires mild output — writing a few sentences, stretching, making coffee without your phone in hand. Track whether your sense of accomplishment or motivation later in the day changes.

  5. Redefine what counts as success for one goal. Pick something you've been avoiding because you're afraid you'll fail or quit (a drawing challenge, a writing project, a fitness routine). Rewrite the success condition: not "complete 31 days" but "see how many days I can go before I stop, and notice what it feels like to keep going past day 5." Remove the all-or-nothing frame.

  6. If you're passively suicidal, tell someone in a clinical context. Not because it's an emergency (though if you're actively suicidal, go to an emergency room now), but because this is treatable and the treatment works better with another person involved. If calling feels like too much, call the office on a Saturday, leave a voicemail, and let them call you back. That's the whole commitment.

  7. When you notice yourself thinking "but I still have to do X, Y, Z," pause and name it. That thought is the dilution effect happening in real time. You don't have to solve it — just recognizing it as a neurological pattern rather than a factual assessment of your inadequacy is enough to start loosening its grip.
