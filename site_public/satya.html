How Truthfulness Teaches You to Let Go

Here's the part most people don't expect. At its root, lying is about control.

When you lie to someone, you're trying to manage how they respond to you. You're engineering their reaction, steering them away from judgment, disappointment, or conflict. Every lie is an attempt to control another person's behavior.

Which means every time you tell the truth instead, you are surrendering that control.

This is what "letting go" actually means in practice — not some internal mental state you achieve through visualization, but the concrete act of saying the true thing and letting someone respond however they're going to respond. You do what you do. You say what's real. You give other people the right to judge you, to be upset, to walk away. And you don't manipulate the outcome.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start the practice today. Set an intention to go as long as possible without telling a lie — including small ones, omissions, and social softening. Don't set a goal of 30 days; just see how long you can go and notice what comes up.

  2. Keep a brief log of the moments you catch yourself. When you're about to lie, or realize you just did, write one sentence: what was I trying to control? Over time, patterns will emerge about which situations trigger the most dishonesty.

  3. Before an action you'd normally hide, pause and ask: "Would I have to lie about this later?" If yes, that's information. Use it to make a different choice rather than relying on a cover story afterward.
