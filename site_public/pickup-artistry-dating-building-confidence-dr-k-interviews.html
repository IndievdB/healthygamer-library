Summary

TL;DR — The real reason pickup artist techniques fail for most people isn't a lack of skill or practice; it's that they're treating external symptoms of a deeply internal wound.

Alex is 20 years old, has spent $8,000 across seven dating coaches, worked through mountains of pickup artist (PUA) video programs, and approached roughly 300 women. He's never been on a date. What unfolds in this conversation isn't a critique of dating advice — it's a diagnosis of something much older and more fundamental: a self-image built from years of being bullied and excluded, carried silently into every adult interaction.

External Solutions Don't Fix Internal Problems

The most important insight here can be written on a sticky note:

"External solutions to internal problems — this is your problem. You look to dating coaches and websites and videos and things like that to fix what's inside, and that's why it doesn't work."

Alex is clearly intelligent and disciplined. He overcame shyness. He learned to walk up to strangers. He practiced relentlessly. By every metric the PUA community uses, he put in the work. And it didn't work — which is actually diagnostic information.

When a treatment fails despite being correctly applied, the most likely explanation is that you're treating the wrong thing. The problem Alex has isn't approach mechanics or conversation technique. It's what he believes about himself at a level below conscious awareness — and no number of scripts or "infield" practice sessions can reach that.

How a False Foundation Gets Built

Alex's story starts in elementary school: a small school, 15 kids against one, sitting alone at recess, called names he still won't repeat out loud. He eventually went back to sit at the table with the kids who bullied him — not because he gave up, but because being alone felt worse. He was trapped either way.

From experiences like that, a child builds a conclusion: I am someone who doesn't fit in with other human beings. That conclusion gets installed before the prefrontal cortex fully develops. It's not abstract — it becomes the lens through which everything else is filtered.

Then he walked into ninth grade at a new high school carrying that belief. This is where confirmation bias becomes dangerous. Before he even opened his mouth to a new peer, he already "knew" the answer. Every awkward interaction confirmed it. Every rejection confirmed it.

"You start with the get-go — before you even step in the door — Alex is a guy who doesn't fit in. Everything else you've tried to fix is laid on that foundation."

The problem isn't the skyscraper. The problem is what it's built on.

What Suppression Looks Like From the Inside

Alex says he used to hate himself as a freshman. Now he says he doesn't really have a relationship with himself. But when he looks in a mirror, he involuntarily cringes — a visceral, subconscious reaction he doesn't consciously choose.

This is the shape suppression takes: the pain doesn't disappear, it goes underground. It stops being active self-hate and becomes background noise — a persistent sense of being cringy, of being fundamentally inadequate. It shows up most clearly in unguarded moments: a mirror, an unexpected question, a genuine conversation with someone he's trying to attract.

The cringe isn't coming from his face. It isn't coming from anything the other person can actually see. It bubbles up from somewhere internal — from an echo of something that happened a long time ago.

"As long as you believe that's what you are, that's what they're going to see."

Why the PUA Framework Makes Psychological Sense (and Still Fails)

PUA language is striking once you notice it: women are "targets," they're rated as "HB 8s" or "HB 9s," and interactions are called "approaches" or "sarging" — military-sounding words. Falling in love with one person is called one-itis, literally framed as a disease.

This isn't random. If your deepest belief is that human connection gets you hurt, the PUA framework offers a clever psychological workaround: stop thinking of them as people. Play a game. Be an avatar — "a chad" — approaching a rating, not a human. The old programming (when I interact with humans, they treat me like garbage) doesn't apply to a game, because games have different rules.

It's genuinely appealing. And for some people, in some situations, it produces results. But it fails for the majority who use it — and especially for someone like Alex — because it never touches the root assumption. It's a costume layered over a wound.

The other issue is outcome-dependence. The entire approach framework is goal-oriented (get a number, get a date, get laid), yet the same communities will tell you "don't be outcome-dependent." That's an internal contradiction that's hard to resolve when your whole system is built around approaching women as missions to complete.

The Cringe Doesn't Come From Your Face

A short meditation exercise near the end of the conversation makes something concrete that's easy to miss intellectually: when Alex looks at himself on camera, a feeling rises. But when he tries to locate where it comes from — tracing it to its source — he can't find it in his appearance. It bubbles up from somewhere else.

This matters enormously for how he interacts with other people. The cringe he assumes others see when they look at him isn't coming from his face. It isn't an objective fact they can perceive. It's a subjective internal event that originates inside him.

Which raises two follow-up questions worth sitting with: 1. If the cringe does come from somewhere — a specific wound, a specific belief — then it can be worked on directly. 2. If it comes from nowhere — if it's just an echo of a past that no longer exists — then why let it define who you are today?

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming social skill is the problem. Alex improved his social skills substantially — he's no longer shy, he can initiate conversations, he can state his intentions. But it still doesn't work. Skill-building without addressing the underlying belief system is like renovating a house with cracked foundations.

  • Collecting techniques instead of changing the foundation. More coaches, more programs, more infield footage — none of it asks the foundational question of who do you believe yourself to be?

  • Dehumanizing the other person as a coping mechanism. Treating women as targets or ratings feels like it removes the stakes, but it also removes the possibility of genuine connection, which is ultimately what most people actually want.

  • Misreading "relief" as a sign you're on the right track. Alex felt relief when asked uncomfortable questions — not because they were easy, but because he was finally being seen without being punished for it. That relief is information about where the real work is.

  • Treating rejection volume as the solution. "Approach a thousand women and you'll get good" only works if your mechanics are the problem. If the issue is what you're unconsciously transmitting about yourself, more volume just means more rejection.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Trace the foundation. Think back to the earliest time you formed a conclusion about yourself in relation to other people. Write it down plainly: "I am someone who ___." Ask yourself: where did this belief come from? Is it actually true, or was it true for a specific environment at a specific age?

  2. Do the mirror exercise. Look at your face on camera or in a mirror — close up, unavoidably. Don't try to feel good. Just notice what arises. Then try to locate where the feeling comes from. Your eyes are just seeing a physical form. The reaction is being added by something else. Track it.

  3. Stop optimizing the approach; optimize the presence. For one week, try talking to strangers — not to get anything, not to practice a skill — just to be in conversation with another human. No target, no mission, no outcome. Notice what that feels like differently.

  4. Name what you feel before you suppress it. Suppression works by moving feelings underground before they can be acknowledged. When something uncomfortable comes up — embarrassment, rejection, loneliness — try to name it out loud or in writing before you push it away. Specificity matters: "I feel embarrassed" is more useful than "I feel bad."

  5. If you have one-itis, don't pathologize it — but also don't let it live only in your head. The PUA framing treats attachment as weakness. A more useful question is: is this based on who this person actually is, or on a fantasy you've built? If you can find out who they actually are — have a real conversation, take a real risk — that's far more informative than either pining indefinitely or forcing yourself to feel nothing.

  6. Get a good receiver. Alex couldn't name his own feelings but responded immediately and accurately when they were reflected back to him. Therapy, a trusted friend, or even journaling can serve this function — something that mirrors your inner world back to you clearly enough that you can start to see it yourself.
