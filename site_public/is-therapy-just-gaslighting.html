Summary

TL;DR — The negative beliefs you hold about yourself aren't objective truths; they're the product of gaslighting you absorbed growing up. Therapy's job is to remove that gaslighting, not replace it with positive spin.

A lot of people resist therapy because they suspect it's just a sophisticated form of self-deception — a compassionate professional convincing you that you're great, even when your life is objectively struggling. That suspicion makes sense. But it gets the direction of the gaslighting exactly backwards. The distortions aren't coming from therapy. They're already inside you, installed long before you ever considered making an appointment.

How to Tell a Real Thought from a Gaslit One

Once you accept that some of your beliefs about yourself may be distortions, the next question is: how do you tell which ones? Yogic psychology offers a useful three-part framework. Two of those parts are where most people actually go wrong.

The ManasThe emotional and reactive layer of the mind — the part that instantly stamps every experience with like or dislike.Full definition → (emotional mind): Emotion is always running. Your brain's emotional circuitry is active 24 hours a day — there is no version of you that feels nothing. The question is whether you're aware of it or not.

When you're not aware of the emotion shaping your thinking, you experience the output of that emotion as pure fact.

"Are you feeling sad? Are you feeling hopeless? 'No, this is truth. This is objective. I have evidence. It's not an emotion.'"

That's the trap. The person who's convinced they'll be alone forever isn't reporting a neutral fact — they're reporting a fact that hopelessness is currently painting for them. Hopelessness, by definition, makes everything look permanent and negative.

The fix isn't to argue with the thought. It's to name the emotion first: I'm feeling hopeless right now, so I'm probably perceiving things as worse than they are. Let me give it 24 hours. Simply acknowledging the emotion reduces its invisible grip on your reasoning.

The Ahamkara (ego): The ego, in this framework, isn't arrogance — it's any belief about yourself that comes after "I am..." Anything that places you on a scale relative to someone else. What Therapy Actually Does

Therapy, done well, is not a cheerleading session. It's not convincing you that you're secretly great. It's the patient, often painful work of identifying where your mental model was built on false premises — and then objectively examining your actual weaknesses from a cleared-out starting point.

The difference between "I've never been on a date and I'm 28" as a fact versus "I've never been on a date and I'm 28 because I'm a loser" as a belief is enormous. The first is a starting point for learning. The second is a destiny. When "I am a loser" is the frame, your past becomes your future — every setback confirms it, every delay proves it.

"As long as you feel like a loser, never having had a date in your life becomes a destiny. When you are a loser, your past becomes your future."

Remove the false frame, and the same factual history becomes just a history — one you can learn from and move past.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating objective bad outcomes as proof of a broken self. Your outcomes are real. The self-blame narrative attached to them probably isn't. These are two separate things.
  • Confusing "undoing gaslighting" with "being told you're fine." Therapy that just reassures you without examining the distortions is not the goal. The point is clarity, not comfort.
  • Believing you don't feel emotions. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the emotion before evaluating the thought. When a negative belief surfaces ("I'll never be able to do this," "they don't like me"), pause and ask: what am I feeling right now? Sad, anxious, hopeless, ashamed? Write it down. Just naming it changes its grip.

  2. Spot the comparison. When you catch yourself sizing yourself up against someone else — a friend's career, a stranger's dating life, a sibling's achievements — recognize that as the ego operating. Ask: what does the comparison actually tell me about what I can do? Usually, nothing.

  3. Separate the fact from the frame. Take one belief you hold about yourself and split it into two sentences. Example: Fact: I haven't had a romantic relationship. Frame: That means I'm fundamentally unlovable. Notice which part is objective and which part is an interpretation.

  4. Trace a belief back to where it came from. Pick a persistent negative belief — about your intelligence, your worth, your potential — and ask: who first told me this, directly or indirectly? Was that person a reliable narrator of your capabilities, or were they working through their own unhappiness?

  5. Try a 24-hour rule on hopeless thoughts. When something looks permanently bad, acknowledge the hopelessness explicitly and give it one day before drawing conclusions. Hopelessness distorts time — permanent-feeling things often look different by tomorrow.

  6. If you're on the fence about therapy, go once with a specific question. Not "fix my life" — that's too abstract. Go with: "I want to understand why I stop trying when things get hard." That gives both you and the therapist something concrete to examine, rather than a vague mandate to make you feel better.
