Summary

TL;DR — Identity rigidity is the main reason people can't change their minds, and the antidote is holding your beliefs as things you have rather than things you are.

This conversation between a psychiatrist and a long-time internet personality explores why people — especially public figures, politicians, and content creators — become locked into positions they can no longer update. It covers the psychology of ego, emotion, and identity that makes changing your mind feel like self-destruction, and what you can do to stay genuinely open while still holding a stable core.

The Real Danger of Growing an Audience

Spending over a decade building a public identity creates a specific trap: your audience starts to define you, and you start to internalize their definition. The longer this goes on, the more expensive any intellectual U-turn becomes.

Consider the example of a prominent conservative commentator waking up tomorrow genuinely convinced that welfare programs are essential. Even if the conviction is real, acting on it might be professionally ruinous — and more insidiously, the ideological pressure might prevent the thought from fully forming in the first place. That's audience capture: the crowd doesn't just respond to what you think, it gradually shapes what you're able to think.

The same dynamic applies to anyone who has staked their brand, their social circle, or their self-concept on a fixed position. Joining an ideological organization, building a following around a label, or simply saying "I am X" often quietly robs you of the ability to grow.

"I Like Capitalism" vs. "I Am a Capitalist"

The most practically useful idea in this conversation is a small language distinction with large consequences:

"I am a person that likes capitalism, but I'm not a capitalist. I might be a person that plays PC games, but I'm not a PC Gamer."

When you say I have this belief, the belief is something you hold and can put down. When you say I am this thing, the belief becomes structural — it's load-bearing for your sense of self. Attacking the belief then feels like attacking you, and your psychology will defend it the same way it defends your physical safety.

This is why political identity is so sticky. You don't just have Republican or socialist ideas — you are a Republican or a socialist. Changing your mind stops being an intellectual update and starts feeling like a kind of death, or at minimum a confession that you've been bad or stupid. Given that most people hold an almost binary self-image (I'm either a good person or a bad person), admitting even one significant error feels like tipping the whole scale.

Why Ego Rises — and How to Work Around It

Ego isn't vanity. Psychologically, ego is a defensive mechanism that activates in response to pain. When someone points out a mistake, the fact that you made it is painful — so the ego generates denial, deflection, or counter-attack to protect you from feeling that pain.

This is exactly what happens in addiction psychiatry. The person who says "I am not an alcoholic" isn't being dishonest in a calculated way — they're being defended against a genuinely threatening idea. The ego-protection kicks in before the reasoning does.

The clinical insight that applies far beyond therapy: remove the negative emotion from the equation, and people become able to admit things they couldn't admit before. This is why motivational interviewing works, and it's also why skilled interrogators don't hammer suspects with accusations — they build rapport, validate circumstances, and drain the emotional charge out of the room. Once the threat level drops, people volunteer information they would have furiously denied moments earlier.

What this means practically: if you want someone to update a belief, making them feel stupid or evil for holding it is almost always counterproductive. The shame just triggers more defensive ego, which hardens the position further.

Feelings Are Never Random

One of the strongest claims here is deceptively simple:

"People's feelings can sometimes be very irrational, but people's feelings are never, ever, ever random. They always feel something for a reason."

Even a belief that is statistically false — say, an assumption that a particular group of people is dangerous — didn't arrive from nowhere. It came from media consumption, personal experiences, family, or culture. Calling the person evil and moving on skips the actual question: why does this person feel that way?

This matters because the only path to genuine change runs through understanding, not condemnation. If you can identify the source of the feeling, you have something to work with. If you just label the person as bad, you've closed the conversation and hardened them further.

The same logic extends to trauma responses. A person who flinches at a loud noise and feels genuine fear of attack isn't being "irrational" in any meaningful sense — their brain formed an association between a stimulus and danger for a real historical reason. The response is rational given the information their nervous system encoded. Treating it as pure irrationality misses what needs to be addressed.

Changing Your Mind in Real Time Is Almost Impossible

"I'm probably not gonna do it, and I really don't expect anybody to do it. Usually if I'm having a conversation with somebody, I'm just there to kind of plant the seeds."

This is worth holding onto if you ever argue with anyone about anything. Real-time capitulation in a heated conversation is vanishingly rare and requires unusual emotional regulation. The normal human pattern is:

  1. Defend during the confrontation
  2. Cool off afterward
  3. Let the planted idea quietly take root
  4. Arrive at a private admission later
  5. Eventually acknowledge the shift, sometimes openly

If you expect someone to flip in the moment, you're setting yourself up for frustration. If you're content to plant a seed and walk away, you have a realistic chance of actually influencing someone.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking intellectual flexibility for having no core. Staying open to change on most things doesn't mean drifting completely. The goal is to identify the few things you're genuinely certain about and hold those firmly, while remaining honestly uncertain about everything else. Treating everything as provisional can be its own kind of performance.

Thinking shame is a useful motivator for change. Publicly humiliating someone for a past position, or making them feel stupid for holding a current one, triggers ego-defense and hardens their stance. It feels satisfying but it's almost never effective at actually moving someone.

Confusing the behavior of changing one's mind publicly with actually changing one's mind. A politician or pundit who shifts position for strategic reasons (like the abortion tweet discussed) isn't necessarily experiencing genuine intellectual growth — they're recalibrating for audience and outcome. Real updating looks quieter and usually comes after a period of sitting with discomfort.

Assuming people who disagree with you are just evil or stupid. This is the fastest way to stop learning from conversations. Almost everyone holds their beliefs for reasons — bad data, formative experiences, social environment — and understanding those reasons is more useful than dismissing the person.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your identity labels. Write down the things you currently call yourself ("I'm a libertarian," "I'm a vegan," "I'm a rationalist"). For each one, ask: is this something I have, or something I am? Practice rephrasing each one: "I find libertarian arguments compelling on issues X and Y." Notice how it changes what you feel you're allowed to question.

  2. Find your four or five unmovables. Identify the handful of positions you hold with very high confidence and have never meaningfully shifted on. Write down why you hold them, with actual reasons. Everything outside that list is fair game for genuine uncertainty — practice voicing that uncertainty aloud.

  3. Before your next disagreement, try to state the other person's view back to them accurately. Not as a debate tactic, but as a genuine test. If they say "that's not quite what I meant," keep going until they say yes. You're not conceding anything — you're making sure you're actually engaging with their real position instead of a version you find easier to dismiss.

  4. Ask "why do they feel this way?" before "are they right?" The next time you encounter a belief you find baffling or offensive, make it a practice to first trace the probable origins of that feeling — experiences, media, community. You don't have to agree. You're just looking for the reason, which will make any further conversation more productive.

  5. Plant seeds, don't demand harvests. When you're in a conversation where you can feel the other person digging in, lower your expectations from "I will change this person's mind right now" to "I will leave them with one idea worth thinking about." Say your piece clearly, without escalating emotion, and let it go. This also applies to yourself: after a conversation where someone challenges your view, give it 48 hours before deciding how you actually feel about it.
