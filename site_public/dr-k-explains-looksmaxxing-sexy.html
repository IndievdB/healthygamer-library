The Science (and Non-Science) of Mewing

Mewing — maintaining the entire tongue pressed against the roof of the mouth to supposedly reshape the jaw and face — is one of the flagship "hard" looksmaxxing practices. The orthodontic and maxillofacial surgical communities are largely united: there is no good scientific evidence that mewing produces the facial restructuring its proponents claim.

A few relevant facts: - Mewing is named after Dr. John Mew, who was stripped of his dental license by the General Dental Council for promoting theories not grounded in sound science. - Orthodontic force (braces, surgical hardware) is measured in newtons and applied persistently over years. The pressure a tongue can exert on the soft palate is orders of magnitude smaller. - The American Association of Orthodontists states that facial restructuring requires moving jawbones and soft tissue — not just repositioning the tongue. - Some evidence exists that in early childhood (roughly the first five years), environmental pressures can influence facial development. That does not scale to adults.

And yet people swear by it. This is not necessarily evidence that it works; it may be evidence of several other things — the placebo effect (documented cases exist of people requiring hospitalization after overdosing on placebo pills, with genuine physiological changes that resolved only once they were told what they had taken), the difficulty of isolating one variable when someone is simultaneously doing body recomposition, skincare, sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition → optimization, neck training, supplementation, and cold showers, and the psychological relief of doing something when you feel out of control.

The honest position: the medical consensus says mewing doesn't work, people feel like it does, and those two facts need to be held together rather than one being dismissed.

The Control Paradox: What Looksmaxxing Shares with Anorexia

A 1979 paper on anorexia nervosa describes what it calls the control paradox: anorexics experience themselves as entirely out of control (terrified of becoming fat, seeing themselves as gluttonous and immoral) while simultaneously achieving absolute control at two levels — the size and shape of their body, and the exercise of self-denial as a moral achievement.

"The battleground becomes an internal one. The battle is fought within the individual rather than between the individual and the world."

This maps almost perfectly onto a significant subset of looksmaxxing. The world in 2024 is genuinely chaotic for a lot of young men: AI is threatening careers, housing costs have broken the old social contract, dating apps have made appearance the primary selection criterion, and social media constantly displays the top 0.1% of physical specimens as though they were normal. You cannot control most of this. But you can control what goes into your body, how many hours you sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition →, what you inject, how you train.

The more chaotic and out of control someone feels externally, the more brutal their self-control tends to become internally. This is not a pathology unique to looksmaxxing — it is the mechanism behind anorexia, and it is one reason anorexia is so hard to treat. The person doing it doesn't experience it as an illness. They experience it as one of the few things that is working.

An important asymmetry: when we treat anorexia inpatient — inserting IVs, forcing nutrition — we are taking away the one thing the patient was successfully controlling. That is genuinely traumatic, even when it is medically necessary. Understanding that tension matters for understanding looksmaxxers.

Self-Objectification Theory and the Looking-Glass Self

Research from feminist psychology in the 1970s and '80s identified self-objectification: the process by which a person begins to view and evaluate themselves primarily through the imagined eyes of an observer. The sequence looks like this:

  1. External pressure — society signals that appearance matters and rewards it materially (jobs, dating outcomes, social status)
  2. Compliance — you begin optimizing your appearance in response
  3. Internalization — you start to believe that this external standard is your standard
  4. IdentityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → incorporation — the way other people see you becomes the way you see yourself

This was originally documented in women. What the data increasingly shows is that men are catching up — not because the underlying psychology has changed, but because men are now being subjected to the same objectifying forces that women were in the '70s. Dating apps select heavily on appearance. Social media rewards physical aesthetics. The "male ubermensch" ideal — wealthy, jacked, in control, desired — is now a coherent cultural image that didn't exist in the same concentrated form before the internet.

Social Media and the Collapse of Normal

One in 40,000 women meets the physical requirements of a model's body type. If you spend six hours a day watching the most beautiful people on the planet, curated by algorithms optimized for engagement, your brain recalibrates what it considers normal. The result: 39,999 people think they are abnormal.

This is being compounded by automatic AI filtering on social media platforms — applying beauty filters to photos without explicit user consent — and by the fact that most platforms are image-based. The research finding matters here: frequency of use of image-based, but not text-based, platforms is significantly associated with body dysmorphic symptoms, and this association is amplified in people who are dispositionally perfectionistic.

The old social environment included grandparents, overweight neighbors, plain-looking colleagues — a realistic distribution of human appearance. The new social environment selects for the top fraction of a percent and delivers them at scale, daily, to everyone's phone. You used to have to go to Harvard to feel like an idiot by comparison. Now feeling inadequate by comparison is the default.
