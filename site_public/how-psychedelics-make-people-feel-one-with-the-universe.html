Summary

TL;DR — Psychedelics dissolve the sense of self by pushing the brain into a high-entropy state, which is the same neurological mechanism behind mystical experiences of "oneness." That's fascinating science — and also a reason to treat these substances with serious caution.

The neuroscience of psychedelics turns out to map surprisingly well onto ancient concepts like ego death and enlightenment. A paper on the "entropic brain" theory provides a concrete framework for understanding why people on psychedelics report feeling merged with the universe — and why that experience can go badly wrong.

The Brain as a System That Can Be More or Less Ordered

To understand what psychedelics actually do to the brain, it helps to think in terms of entropy — a measure of disorder or uncertainty within a system. In everyday waking life, your brain operates in a relatively low-entropy state. That means it's organized, predictable, and constrained in how it processes information.

Your sense of self is one of the clearest expressions of this low-entropy organization. "I am this person. I have this identity. I am successful / a failure / funny / anxious." Whatever the content, the fact that there is a stable "I" making those judgments is itself a feature of an ordered, low-entropy brain state.

Psychedelics disrupt that order. They push the brain toward a high-entropy state — one characterized by disorder, diffuseness, and unpredictability. The result, subjectively, is that the sharp boundary between "me" and "everything else" starts to dissolve.

Why People Feel One with the Universe

When the brain's entropy rises, the self doesn't just get fuzzy — it can effectively disappear. This is what people mean when they describe feeling connected to other people, to nature, or to the universe itself. It's not a metaphor or a mood; it reflects a measurable shift in the brain's organizational state.

"When people talk about being connected to other people and their sense of self kind of breaks down, and they realize they're connected with the universe — that's a high-entropic state of consciousness."

This is also what spiritual traditions have called ego death — the experience central to concepts like moksha (liberation) in Hindu and Buddhist thought, or mystical union in other traditions. The self that normally mediates your every experience temporarily ceases to function as a boundary.

"You are no longer you, but you are like one with the universe. We can say those words, but it doesn't make any sense until you experience it in a state of consciousness."

The neuroscience doesn't invalidate the experience — if anything, it deepens its significance. The fact that there is a measurable brain-state correlate for what meditators and mystics have described for thousands of years is genuinely striking. It suggests that "ego dissolution" isn't just poetic language; it describes something real happening in the brain's organization.

Why This Is Also a Reason for Caution

Here's the thing: shattering your sense of self is not automatically a healing experience. The same mechanism that produces feelings of unity and transcendence can also produce lasting psychological damage.

"Shattering your sense of self can sometimes leave you traumatized."

There's an important selection bias to keep in mind when you read testimonials about psychedelics. The people for whom an experience goes well generally don't end up in emergency rooms or psychiatric clinics. The people for whom it goes catastrophically wrong do. That doesn't make all positive outcomes fake — it just means the population you hear from in enthusiastic online communities is not a representative sample of everyone who uses these substances.

The high-entropy brain state is powerful precisely because it dismantles something fundamental. The self isn't just an ego to be shed like a coat; it's the organizing structure through which you navigate reality, relationships, and time. Temporarily dissolving it under the wrong conditions — wrong set, wrong setting, wrong dose, wrong personal history — can leave that structure damaged in ways that don't simply resolve when the drug wears off.

The Microdosing Question

A natural follow-up is whether smaller, sub-perceptual doses — microdosing — sidestep these risks while delivering benefits like improved focus or mood. The honest answer is: we don't know.

Microdosing is relatively new as a widespread practice. There simply hasn't been enough time or research to understand what repeated low-level exposure does to the brain over months or years. The relevant questions haven't been answered yet:

  • Does it increase the risk of anxiety or depression over time?
  • Are there cumulative neurological effects?
  • Who is most vulnerable, and why?

The fact that many people are doing something and reporting feeling good is not evidence of safety. It's worth remembering that cocaine was once an ingredient in popular soft drinks — it felt good, gave energy, seemed to have no obvious downsides, and was widely consumed. "Popular and pleasant" and "safe" are not the same category.

"Your brain absolutely loves it, which is why everyone's doing it. So it can't be bad, right? Kind of like when cocaine was in Coca-Cola — tastes delicious, gives you energy, there are no downsides. Let's do it."

The appropriate response to genuine uncertainty isn't to assume the best. It's to wait for science to study large populations systematically, so that patterns — benefits and harms — can actually surface.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking "interesting" for "safe." The neuroscience of psychedelics is genuinely fascinating, and reading about it can create a sense that these are well-understood, controllable tools. They're not. Fascinating and dangerous aren't mutually exclusive.

Survivor bias in testimonials. If you mostly hear from people who had transformative positive experiences, you're not hearing from people whose sense of self didn't reassemble cleanly afterward. That group exists; they're just less likely to be posting.

Assuming microdosing is low-risk by default. The word "micro" implies something minimal and harmless. But the long-term effects of regularly perturbing brain chemistry — even subtly — are simply unknown. "Low dose" doesn't automatically mean "low risk over time."

Treating community consensus as scientific consensus. A substance being popular in a particular community, or having a long history of use in some contexts, is not the same as it being studied, understood, and validated as safe by rigorous science. These are different evidentiary standards.

Thinking you can fully prepare for ego dissolution. High-entropy brain states are, by definition, disorganized and unpredictable. Reading about what to expect doesn't put you in control of an experience that operates precisely by dismantling the faculties you'd use to stay in control.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Sit with the entropy concept before seeking the experience. Spend a few minutes reflecting on what your sense of self actually consists of — your roles, your self-descriptions, your habitual thoughts about who you are. This makes the concept of ego dissolution more than abstract, and gives you a clearer sense of what you'd actually be dissolving.

  2. Apply the cocaine-in-Coca-Cola test to anything you're considering. Before adopting any new substance or practice because it feels good and is widely used, ask: "What would I need to actually know to be confident this is safe long-term?" If the answer is "more than I currently have," treat that as meaningful.

  3. If you're drawn to the underlying experience — not just curiosity about substances — explore non-pharmacological routes first. Long-form meditation, breathwork (like holotropic breathing, ideally with a trained facilitator), or serious contemplative practice can produce states of reduced self-referential processing without the same unpredictability. They're slower, but the risk profile is far better understood.

  4. Before making any decision about substances, consult an actual medical professional — not a forum, not a podcast, not a friend's positive report. A professional can account for your specific mental health history, current medications, and vulnerability factors that general advice cannot address.

  5. When you encounter research or anecdotes about psychedelics, notice who's in the sample. Ask: are these people who had good outcomes self-selecting into the conversation? What happened to the people who aren't talking? This epistemic habit applies far beyond this topic.
