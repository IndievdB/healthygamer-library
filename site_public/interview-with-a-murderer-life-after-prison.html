Summary

TL;DR — A man who killed someone at 16 and served 20 years in prison shares his life story, and the conversation reveals that most of what holds him back isn't a lack of ability — it's the survival strategies he was forced to develop, and has never been able to put down.

Anthony, now 41, grew up with a drug-addicted, physically abusive mother, no father, a brother the state eventually took away, and a childhood interrupted by a serious injury that left him in casts and wheelchairs for years. He was bullied, never learned to make friends, was working full-time and paying rent at 12, and was jailed at 15 by his own mother after she lied to police. At 16, he shot and killed a man — motivated partly by a genuine sense of protecting his brother and partly, as he came to understand years later, by jealousy. He received the maximum sentence: 20 years in adult prison.

He's been out for about five or six years. He's a 4.0 GPA computer science student. He hasn't been in a relationship or had a real friend in decades. He spent much of the past year and a half on the edge of suicide. He describes himself as not knowing who he is, not knowing what he wants, and feeling like he'll never catch up to whatever normal adult life is supposed to look like.

The conversation that follows is less a therapy session than a live excavation — trying to figure out not what's broken, but why things are the way they are, and what might actually change.

The Real Diagnosis: Shit Life Syndrome

The cleanest way to understand Anthony's situation doesn't come from a diagnostic manual. An ER nurse once described patients like him as having "shit life syndrome" — not depression, not bipolar disorder, not trauma alone, but all of those things sitting inside a larger container of a life that just went badly wrong from the very start.

That framing matters because labels like "felon," "trauma victim," or "loner" each capture something real but none of them capture the person. The risk of over-pathologizing is that you start treating the label instead of understanding the human. Anthony's situation makes more sense when you recognize that his early years denied him things most people absorb without noticing:

  • How to read relationships and emotions (taught to us by early caregivers)
  • How to form and maintain friendships (learned during the school years he spent isolated)
  • How to be in a romantic relationship (requires emotional templates he was never given)
  • Who he is as a person (identity forms through the reflections of people around us — and his reflections were cruel or absent)

When a six-month-old is hungry, it's the people around them who teach them what that feeling is called and what to do about it. Strip that away and the damage isn't just emotional — it's structural. It shapes how the nervous system learns to make sense of the world.

Adaptations That Saved You Then, Trap You Now

One of the most important ideas in this conversation is that the things holding Anthony back aren't weaknesses or character flaws. They are solutions — strategies his brain built to survive specific circumstances. The problem is he's no longer in those circumstances, but the strategies are still running.

A few examples:

Resting blank affect. Being expressionless and unreadable is an asset when you're in prison and showing emotion is a liability. It's also what happens when you grow up in a home where being noticed means getting hurt. It helped him survive. Outside prison, it reads as intimidating or closed off, which makes connection harder.

Fight response to threat. After 20 years in a place where flight isn't an option, his nervous system adapted to respond to perceived danger with aggression. He describes pulling over at a McDonald's and screaming at a car that honked at him — then realizing it was a woman with her daughter and immediately calming down. That's not who he is; that's a survival circuit misfiring in a context it wasn't built for.

Killing hope. This one is the most important. When you're 16 and you get a 20-year sentence, and 20 years feels like a life sentence, the only way to survive your 18th birthday in prison, your 21st, your 30th, is to stop wanting things. You pull up hope like a weed every time it sprouts. You get very, very good at it. Then you get out, and people ask you what you want from life, and you come up empty — not because you have no inner life, but because you spent two decades systematically destroying access to it.

"You spent 20 years doing your level best to get nothing out of life."

Gifting as connection. Anthony notices that he tries to keep friendships by buying people things or doing extravagant favors, and that friendships end the first time he says no. He learned this model of love from a mother who was the only person he had — someone who was also his abuser. When love and harm come from the same source, the blueprint for relationships gets permanently strange.

The Friend Zone, Rejection, and a Rigged Game

Anthony mentions he's never successfully asked a woman out, has been friend-zoned consistently, and had exactly one sexual encounter in his life — at his brother's house, with someone who was, as he put it, the local welcoming committee. He frames this as evidence that he is fundamentally undesirable or broken.

But there's a more precise explanation. The pattern looks something like this:

  1. He's afraid of rejection, so he stacks the deck by being maximally helpful and accommodating
  2. This emotional attentiveness without any stated romantic intention reads as friendship
  3. He ends up in the friend zone
  4. This confirms his belief that he is unlovable as himself
  5. Repeat

The deeper issue is that he's never played a fair game. He's never been in good shape, done emotional work, felt reasonably secure in himself, and asked someone out. Every rejection has happened within a rigged context. Treating those outcomes as evidence of something fixed and permanent about him is a logical error — and one his brain is strongly motivated to keep making, because as long as the game is rigged, the real self never has to be tested.

There's also something worth noting about how he screens for partners. He rates himself as a three but says he's looking for an eight. Asked why, he eventually lands on: because having an eight would impress other people. That's not attraction — that's the same impulse as the compulsive lying about a 120 mph fastball when he was 11. It's trying to construct an external image that compensates for an internal sense of worthlessness.

You Can't Find Yourself if You Keep Pulling Up the Weeds

Anthony keeps asking some version of the same question: Who am I? What do I want? What's my purpose?

The honest answer is that discovering what you want from life isn't really a search. It's more like a stopping. His brain is wired — after 41 years of chaos, abuse, incarceration, and survival — to constantly assess for threat and deprive itself of anything that feels like hope or desire. The things he actually wants keep trying to surface. He just keeps cutting them down before they can grow.

He doesn't need to go to a mountain retreat to find himself. He needs to stop weeding.

"I don't think you need to actually discover what you want from life. I think all you've got to do is stop pulling out the weeds."

What he clearly loves: learning things deeply, thinking through complex ideas, having his thoughts challenged, and sharing what he knows. That's not nothing. That's actually a complete personality. A philosopher, a thinker, someone who wants to put something excellent into the world and have people recognize it as excellent. The dormant version of that person has been there the whole time.

Common Pitfalls

Treating adaptations as identity. "I'm lazy" or "I have no personality" aren't accurate self-assessments — they're descriptions of what happens when a person has been optimized for survival rather than living. Labeling yourself this way makes change feel impossible and obscures the actual mechanism you'd need to address.

Using the "common denominator" insight to confirm shame. Anthony is right that he's the common denominator in his social failures. But that insight cuts both ways: if you're the common denominator, the power to change is also in your hands. The brain tends to hijack that observation and turn it into there's something fundamentally broken in me, which is not the same thing and not useful.

Forgiving yourself (or others) on demand. There's a cultural pressure to forgive, and a tendency to treat the inability to forgive as a moral failure. Anthony never received an acknowledgment of wrongdoing from his mother — not a real one. Forgiveness without acknowledgment is being asked to do extra emotional labor for someone who hurt you and never took responsibility. You can make peace with something without forgiving it.

Mistaking security for happiness. Because Anthony never had safety, his brain optimizes for it constantly — in career choices, relationship choices, daily routines. Safety and happiness aren't the same thing. Recognizing when a decision is driven by the need to feel secure (rather than by genuine desire) is a useful internal signal to develop.

Concluding you'd be happier alone before you've tried. Anthony raises this toward the end — maybe he's just better off alone. That might even be true. But he doesn't get to know yet, because he hasn't actually attempted connection from anything close to a fair starting position.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start living before you start optimizing. If you've missed years of normal experience — relationships, failures, triumphs, embarrassments — your first job is to accumulate experience, not find a purpose. Go eat somewhere new, join an in-person class, talk to a stranger. You don't find out who you are by thinking about it; you find out by doing things and noticing what happens.

  2. Practice catching external motivations. When you want something, ask: Am I doing this because it genuinely appeals to me, or because of how it will look to others? You don't have to act on the answer immediately — just notice. Over time, this builds the muscle of moving toward authentic preferences rather than performed ones.

  3. Question your conclusions before they close. When your mind says I can see where this is going, pause. Ask: What's the specific thing I'm afraid will happen here? Often you'll find the fear is general and unspecific — a residue of old programming rather than an accurate read of the present situation. Your threat-detection is calibrated for environments you no longer live in.

  4. Get a physical check-up, specifically including testosterone levels. Low testosterone contributes to low libido, low motivation, and low mood. If you've had significant leg muscle atrophy from injury, this matters more — the quadriceps are a major source of endogenous testosterone production. This is actionable and doesn't require emotional work first.

  5. Find a therapist — not because you're broken, but because you're cerebral. The goal isn't to be told you're a victim. The goal is to have someone devoted to helping you understand the patterns in your own thinking, one hour a week. People who are good at introspection and conversation tend to get disproportionate value from therapy. Look for someone who treats you as a survivor, not a patient to be pitied. Community mental health centers and student services at universities often offer low-cost or sliding-scale options.

  6. Use the hitchhiking memory as a compass. There was a period when you had nothing to offer anyone, no status, no gifts, no history — and strangers helped you anyway, and you connected with them. That happened because you were just yourself. When you're trying to figure out whether you're being authentic or performing, ask: Am I being the person from the highway, or am I trying to impress someone?

  7. Don't wait until you're "ready" for relationships — but go slowly. You don't have to open up completely to someone right away. The goal of early social contact isn't to find a partner; it's to practice being around people without running the full "how do I get them to like me" program. Conversation is enough. Friendship is enough. Let it be low-stakes.
