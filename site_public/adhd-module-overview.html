The Real Difference Between Doing and Trying

Resistance: The Part of You That Sabotages Yourself

One of the most disorienting experiences in trying to get things done is this: you genuinely want something, and yet when the moment comes to act, you don't. The job application sits there. The project file goes unopened. The gym shoes stay by the door.

This isn't laziness. It's resistance — and it's a real psychological phenomenon. Parts of the mind actively work against goals that another part has consciously committed to. The "you" that wants something and the "you" that avoids it are both real.

"I can want to do something, but then when I try to do it, I don't want to do it — which is kind of weird, right? 'Cause I want to do it, but I also don't want to do it. So who is the me that wants to do it, and what the hell gets in the way?"

Understanding resistance — where it comes from and how it operates — is a prerequisite for getting past it. Trying harder in the face of resistance usually doesn't work. Understanding it does.

Common Pitfalls

  • Attributing the gap to willpower. The assumption that people who accomplish things are just more disciplined leads you to try harder at the wrong thing. The actual levers are attention, environment, resistance, and goal design.
  • Treating your goals as given. Most people never question whether their goals are actually theirs, actually achievable, or framed in a way that produces action. Unexamined goals are often the ones that go nowhere.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ask where your goals came from. Pick one major goal you're currently pursuing and trace it back. Did it originate from something you saw, something someone told you, something you genuinely want? Is it yours, or did it come in through your sense organs as exposure to someone else's life?

  3. Audit your attention environment. For one day, track every time your attention gets pulled away from something you intended to focus on. What pulled it? Phone? Notification? Social feed? Build a picture of where your attention is actually going versus where you want it to go.

  4.   5. Redesign one environmental variable. Pick one context where your motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → consistently fails and change something physical about it: where you work, what's accessible, who you're around.   6. Reframe an overwhelming goal into a near-term action. Take a goal that currently feels paralyzing and ask: what is the smallest action that would be a real step toward it? Not a plan, not preparation — an action. Make it small enough that resistance can't justify avoiding it.
