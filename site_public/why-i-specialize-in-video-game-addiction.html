Summary

TL;DR — A Harvard-trained addiction psychiatrist explains why he, specifically, is positioned to help people struggling with video games: he was one of them.

Most clinicians treating gaming problems have never picked up a controller. This talk is the origin story of someone who built expertise from the inside out — starting with his own near-academic-collapse over gaming, running through his psychiatric training, and arriving at a frank admission that the field itself is still young. The throughline is simple: understanding a problem from the inside matters, and the mainstream mental health system often misses what's really going on with heavy gamers.

The Insider Credential Nobody Talks About

There's a quiet credibility problem in mental health: the experts are often decades removed from the experiences their patients are living. The leading psychiatrists in depression, anxiety, and addiction research are typically mid-to-late career — brilliant, well-published, and, in many cases, people who have genuinely never played a video game.

That gap is not trivial. When a clinician has no felt sense of what it's like to lose track of time in a game, to prefer a virtual world to the one outside, to feel the pull of one more match at 3 a.m., they're working from the outside in — pattern-matching symptoms to categories rather than understanding the texture of the experience.

The alternative is someone who nearly failed out of college because of video games, who slept through a Spanish final after an all-night gaming session, who graduated with a 2.5 GPA and spent years trying to make sense of it. That personal history isn't a liability to hide — it's information.

"The leaders in the field of psychiatry are all in their 50s, 60s, and 70s, and they've actually never played a video game."

How Real Understanding Gets Built

Psychiatric training — even elite psychiatric training at places like Massachusetts General Hospital and McLean Hospital — doesn't automatically produce someone who can help a person whose life is being consumed by gaming. The formal knowledge base about video game addiction is genuinely thin. There haven't been decades of rigorous clinical trials the way there have been for depression treatment or cancer care.

So when the textbooks and mentors run dry, you go to the source. Rather than stopping at "my teachers don't know," the response was to start talking directly to gamers — not just locally, but from across the world: North America, South Korea, the Middle East. People with different cultural contexts, different games, different life situations, all sharing a common pattern of struggle.

That kind of accumulated, direct, cross-cultural conversation builds something that a literature review can't fully replicate: a working model of why people play, not just how much they play. The "why" turns out to be the clinically important part.

What the Standard System Gets Wrong

Here's where a lot of well-meaning clinical care breaks down: a person who is gaming excessively goes to a therapist or psychiatrist, gets evaluated, and walks out with a diagnosis of depression or anxiety — because those things are genuinely present. They get prescribed an antidepressant. They go home, take their medication, and keep gaming.

Nothing changes.

The problem isn't that the diagnosis is wrong, exactly. It's that it's incomplete, or pointed at the wrong target. Depression may be real and present, but the primary driver of the dysfunction is the gaming itself. Treating the depression without addressing the gaming is like treating the smoke without touching the fire.

"While they are depressed, the problem really isn't that they are depressed. Their problem is that they play too many video games."

This is a subtle but important clinical distinction. Depression and excessive gaming frequently co-occur, and they feed each other — but if you only treat one while ignoring the other, outcomes are poor. Someone who has spent years being handed partial solutions — pills that address mood but leave the behavioral pattern untouched — isn't being failed by medicine in general. They're being failed by a system that hasn't developed the specific tools to address this particular problem.

The Honest State of the Field

One of the more useful things here is the candor about limits. Video game addiction is a relatively new clinical concern. The research base is thin compared to most established psychiatric conditions. Treatment approaches are still being developed, refined, and debated.

This isn't a reason to despair — it's context worth carrying. If you or someone you love has struggled to find help, and found that the help available felt generic or off-target, that's not just a personal failure to find the right clinician. The field as a whole is catching up.

What does exist is years of direct clinical experience working with gamers — spouses of gamers, parents of gamers, the gamers themselves — and a framework built from that experience rather than from inherited assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the symptom for the cause. Depression, anxiety, and social withdrawal are real when they appear alongside heavy gaming, but they're often downstream of the gaming pattern rather than the root of it. Treating the mood without addressing the behavior tends to leave the core problem intact.

Assuming any psychiatrist or therapist can address gaming addiction. Mental health credentials are not interchangeable here. A clinician who has no personal or professional familiarity with gaming culture is missing context that shapes both the assessment and the therapeutic relationship. This isn't a knock on their general competence — it's a specific gap in a specific area.

Thinking the research base is mature. It isn't. If you approach this expecting the same depth of evidence-based guidance that exists for, say, alcohol use disorder or major depression, you'll be disappointed. The honest position is that the field is young, knowledge is accumulating, and anyone claiming certainty about treatment protocols should be viewed with some skepticism.

Waiting for the official system to catch up before seeking help. The mainstream mental health system is behind on this. Waiting for it to fully catch up before addressing a gaming problem is its own form of avoidance.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit the "why," not just the "how much." Before counting hours or making rules about screen time, spend time genuinely asking yourself — or helping a loved one ask — why the games are so compelling right now. Boredom? Social connection? Escape from specific stressors? The "why" points toward what actually needs to change.

  2. If you're seeking clinical help, screen your clinician. It's fair to ask a therapist or psychiatrist directly whether they have experience working with gaming-related issues. You're not being difficult — you're trying to find someone with relevant expertise rather than someone who will fit your problem into the nearest familiar category.

  3. Don't let a partial diagnosis satisfy you. If you've been told you're depressed or anxious and given treatment that targets only those symptoms, but gaming is still dominating your life, push for more. The mood issue and the behavioral issue may both need to be addressed — separately and deliberately.

  4. Seek out community-level understanding. Some of the most useful insight in this space came from listening to gamers across different cultures and contexts. Online communities, forums, or groups specifically focused on gaming and mental health can offer perspective that a single clinician appointment can't.

  5. Hold your expectations loosely. Because the field is still developing, progress may be slower and messier than you'd like. That's real, and it's worth accepting rather than letting it become a reason to give up on getting help altogether.
