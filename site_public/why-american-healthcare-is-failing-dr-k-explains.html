Summary

TL;DR — American healthcare costs so much because every player in the system has an incentive that works against the patient, and the patient is the only one with no power to push back.

The U.S. healthcare system is a multi-layered structure of misaligned incentives. Insurance companies profit by collecting high premiums and minimizing what they pay out. Doctors, insulated from patients' actual costs, have incentives to order expensive tests and treatments. Employers choose your insurance for you, stripping away your ability to vote with your wallet. And the uninsured quietly raise everyone else's costs through a backdoor subsidy mechanism most people never see. The person who ends up absorbing every financial shock in this chain is the patient — the one person with the least control over any of it.

How a Normal Market Works — and Why Healthcare Breaks It

In a healthy market, money and service flow directly between buyer and seller. If the apple is bad, you complain, or you go somewhere else. That feedback loop keeps sellers honest and prices grounded in reality.

Healthcare breaks this loop at every step. You don't pay your doctor — your insurance company does. You don't choose your insurance company — your employer does. And you don't get to fire your insurance company if it's terrible, because switching would cost you the bulk-discount that makes employer-sponsored insurance affordable in the first place.

"The basic problem with this is that this person should be empowered to make the most decisions. These three people should have the least power in the relationship. This person should have the most power."

When the consumer has no power in a market, costs rise without consequence — for everyone except the consumer.

The Insurance Company's Incentive Problem

An insurance company makes money one way: collect as much in premiums as possible, pay out as little in care as possible. Those two goals are in direct conflict with your health.

This plays out concretely in something called prior authorization. When a doctor prescribes a treatment — say, medication A — the insurance company can say: we'll only pay for medication B, because it's cheaper. The doctor believes medication A is better. The patient wants medication A. But the insurance company holds the money.

To fight this, doctors hire administrative staff dedicated entirely to battling insurance denials. Insurance companies hire staff dedicated entirely to processing (and often denying) those appeals. Neither of those employees delivers a single minute of healthcare. They exist solely because of friction in the system — and their salaries raise costs for everyone.

"We both hired an additional person to get the person the apple, so now we've just increased costs across the board with no improvement in care."

The administrative overhead of private insurance runs around 25–30 cents on every dollar. Medicare's administrative overhead, by comparison, is around 3–4 cents on the dollar. Most of that difference is corporate salaries and the machinery of claim-fighting.

The Doctor's Incentive Problem

Doctors aren't blameless either. When a patient pays out of pocket, a doctor has a natural reason to consider cost — a patient who goes bankrupt stops being a patient. But when a third party (insurance) is paying, that brake disappears.

If a CT scan pays the hospital $100 in margin and an MRI pays $300, and the patient never sees the difference in their bill, the financial incentive is to order MRIs. Not out of malice — the doctor can even rationalize it as the patient deserves the best care — but the aggregate effect is that insurance gets billed for more expensive procedures, and premiums go up in response.

The insurance company then requires prior authorization for expensive imaging, adding another layer of paperwork — and the cycle repeats.

The Employer Problem

Because insurers give bulk discounts to large groups, individual insurance is dramatically more expensive than employer-sponsored insurance. The result: you are effectively forced to get insurance through your job.

The numbers are striking. Paying out of pocket for a family plan that is worse than an equivalent employer-sponsored plan can cost $4,500/month versus roughly $2,800/month through an employer. That gap alone traps people.

But your employer's incentive is to get the cheapest plan that keeps employees from quitting — not to get you the best care. They adjust premiums, deductibles, copays, and out-of-pocket maximums against each other in ways that are genuinely hard to compare. The more variables in a financial instrument, the harder it is to understand — and the easier it is to get a bad deal without realizing it.

The Hidden Cost of the Uninsured

Here's the part most people miss. When an uninsured person shows up at an emergency room with a heart attack, the hospital is legally required to treat them. The hospital absorbs the loss — but it doesn't just eat it quietly.

It does two things: 1. Goes to the government and requests subsidies to stay solvent (funded by taxes). 2. Charges insured patients and their insurance companies more to make up the gap.

A 20-minute psychiatry visit billed to a private insurer might bring in $250. The same visit billed to Medicaid might bring in $70. The hospital makes up that difference by charging the private insurer more, which raises your premiums.

"The problem with our healthcare system is that this guy's getting screwed all the time. No matter what happens, this is the guy that gets it."

People who oppose universal coverage on the grounds that they don't want to pay for others' healthcare are, in practice, already paying for it — just through a more expensive and chaotic mechanism.

What a Better System Looks Like

The core fix isn't political — it's structural: put financial power back in the consumer's hands.

Singapore's system offers one model. Citizens are required to save a percentage of income into a personal healthcare savings account. When you control the money, you become a real consumer — you ask about costs, you compare options, you push back on unnecessary procedures.

An HSA (Health Savings Account) works on the same principle and exists in the U.S. already, though it's underused and limited.

There's also an older incentive model worth thinking about: in ancient China, physicians were reportedly paid a monthly retainer — and not paid in any month a patient got sick. The entire incentive shifts from treating illness to preventing it. That's a system organized around health, not around sick care.

The current U.S. system is, functionally, sick care — it's built and paid around treating disease after it happens, not preventing it.

Common Pitfalls

  • "The uninsured are someone else's problem." They're not. Their emergency care costs get laundered back to you through hospital pricing and government subsidies.
  • "My employer gives me good insurance, so I'm fine." You still have no negotiating power, no ability to switch, and no visibility into what you're actually getting — until you need it.
  • "A complicated plan with a high maximum coverage must be good." The more levers in a plan (premium, deductible, copay, out-of-pocket max, coverage caps), the easier it is to hide the ways it fails you. A $5M coverage ceiling means nothing if the deductible is $50,000.
  • "Doctors always order tests because they care." Sometimes, yes. But the financial structure of insurance removes the cost-consciousness that would otherwise exist in a direct patient-doctor relationship.
  • "Universal healthcare means government waste." The data points the other direction — public insurance programs run at roughly 10% of the administrative cost of private insurers.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Read your actual insurance plan. Find the four numbers that matter: monthly premium, annual deductible, copay per visit, and out-of-pocket maximum. Calculate what you'd pay in a bad year (a hospitalization or surgery), not just a normal year.

  2. If your employer offers an HSA-eligible high-deductible plan, compare it seriously. Run the math: premium savings + employer HSA contributions vs. your expected annual healthcare spending. For healthy people, HSA plans often win.

  3. Ask for itemized bills. Hospitals are required to provide them. Errors are common and often large. Disputing charges is possible and sometimes successful — especially if you're uninsured or underinsured.

  4. When a doctor orders a test, it's okay to ask: "Is there a less expensive alternative that would give us similar information?" Doctors respond differently when patients signal they're paying attention to cost.

  5. Check cms.gov for data before forming strong opinions on healthcare policy. Primary sources — CMS, New England Journal of Medicine, Health Affairs — are more reliable than partisan news coverage on this topic.

  6. If you're self-employed or between jobs, compare marketplace plans carefully against short-term coverage and direct primary care (DPC) arrangements, where you pay a monthly fee directly to a doctor. DPC removes the insurance middleman for routine care entirely.
