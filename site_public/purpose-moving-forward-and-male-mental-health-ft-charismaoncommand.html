The Arc Most Men Don't Recognize They're On

Charlie's story has a shape that shows up constantly in men who eventually do deep inner work — and recognizing the shape matters because it stops you from thinking something is wrong with you when you're in a particular phase.

The pain is real, but it's also a signal: something in you wants more connection.

Phase two is strategic extraversion. The antidote most men find is to build a system. Phase three is the collapse of sufficiency. At some point you have the things you were working toward — the subscribers, the money, the relationship — and you're sadder than you've ever been. What Codependency Actually Feels Like From the Inside

Most descriptions of codependency are clinical and behavioral. Charlie describes it from the inside, which is far more useful.

The core confusion is between closeness and love — treating them as synonyms. If love means closeness, then any distance feels like rejection, and creating appropriate distance feels like abandonment or cruelty. So even when a relationship is generating resentment, you stay. Even when someone can't meet a need of yours, rather than naming it or stepping back, you develop indirect strategies to squeeze it out of them.

What makes this especially hard to catch is that the mechanism often looks like care. You're helping, advising, supporting. But underneath, you're managing someone else's emotional state because you can't tolerate how their distress lands in your own body.

"I could confuse my feelings and my needs with someone else's very easily — very, very easily."

This confusion has roots. Charlie traces his back to enmeshment with an anxious mother — not a villain, just a deeply loving parent whose emotional weather became his weather. He learned to adjust himself to prevent her distress. He quit wrestling not because he didn't like it, but because he could feel her panic in the audience and didn't want to be the cause of it. That pattern — taking responsibility for what other people feel — became the operating system underneath all his relationships.

The adult version: never breaking up with anyone, never stepping back from a friendship even as resentment built, staying in close contact with everyone because distance felt like failure. Love conceived as a maximum-closeness project.

The healthier version isn't coldness — it's acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → as the primary act of love, not closeness. You can love someone and see clearly that they're not able to meet a particular need. You can communicate that. And if they can't or won't, you can, with love, create more distance. The love doesn't have to die. The resentment doesn't have to build.

The Trap Inside "Finding the Productivity in Rest"

There's a subtle pattern worth naming carefully because it's very easy to fall into when you're a high-achiever trying to do inner work.

Charlie had built his life around optimization — four daily goals, grading himself each night, the full Tim Ferriss architecture. When he started recognizing that this was exhausting and possibly a coping mechanism, he began finding value in space, stillness, and non-doing. Good. But notice the framing: "space is actually productive." "Stillness generates better ideas." "Rest has ROI."

This is the productivity mind co-opting the spiritual insight. You haven't actually let go of the need to produce — you've just reclassified rest as a form of production. The furniture has moved. The belief hasn't.

The deeper invitation — and this is genuinely hard — is to be okay with time that has no value. To take an L and not find a silver lining in it. To do nothing and not need it to count. As long as rest only becomes acceptable when it earns its keep, you're still in the same structure.

"You should be okay taking space if there's no value there. If we're really talking about acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition →, you should have no repulsion towards wasting your time."

This isn't a criticism of anyone doing inner work — it's a description of where the next layer of freedom tends to be hiding.

Self-Medicating With Other People

One phrase Charlie uses that deserves to stand on its own: self-medicating with other people.

It names something that looks like relationship but is actually closer to addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →. You're not primarily with this person because of who they are — you're with them because of what they regulate in you. The closeness quiets a specific internal discomfort. When they're unavailable, the discomfort surges. When they behave in ways that don't serve the regulation function, you panic, control, or resent.

This is distinct from healthy interdependence (which involves directness, named needs, genuine mutuality). The tell is indirectness — you're not saying "I need reassurance right now," you're engineering situations where the reassurance will appear to happen naturally, so you don't have to acknowledge the need.

The treatment isn't to need people less. It's to get accurate about what you actually need, locate how much of that is yours to tend to internally, and communicate the remainder clearly instead of maneuvering for it.

Touch, Men, and the Body They Live In

There's a brief but important exchange about how men in many Western cultures receive physical contact primarily in one of three ways: violence (hitting, rough play), high-stakes assessment (sports physicals, medical exams), or violation. Affectionate, caring, non-sexualized touch between men was largely taboo through most of the 80s and 90s — in no small part because of pervasive homophobia.

The result is a generation of men who are profoundly touch-starved without knowing it, because they were never given a language for that hunger. Many men will hug strangers more readily than the people they love most — because with strangers, there's less at stake, less history, less of the old confusion between touch and harm.

Charlie describes something he calls confused love: staying in your own physical island not because you don't want to connect, but because some part of you is afraid of what might come through you to someone you care about. The mechanism that was supposed to protect others is also sealing you off from them.

The data on this isn't soft. Isolation and touch-starvation have measurable health consequences. And the healing Charlie describes — a transformed hug from his father — wasn't dramatic. It was just contact, finally received, that let something shift.

Purpose, Presence, and the Difference Between Meaning and Flow

Near the end of the conversation there's a philosophical exchange worth capturing cleanly.

There are two things that can make suffering feel meaningful. The first is referential meaning — this painful thing connects to something larger; this struggle will produce growth; this experience is building toward something. Viktor Frankl's work, and most Western therapeutic approaches to traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, operate here. There's good science behind it.

The second is harder to articulate: transcendent purpose. The sense that you don't need to extract meaning from the present moment because the present moment is already exactly where it's supposed to be. Not "this is useful" but "this is it." The Daoist sense of flow. The experience of being in the right place on the right path without needing to engineer it.

What's interesting is that these two modes aren't necessarily sequential — but for most people, the referential meaning phase comes first. You need to find a use for pain before you can accept that pain doesn't need a use.

"Being in the now is actually the first step. But the further down you go, that duality gets dissolved."

The practical upshot: if you're currently at "I need to find the lesson in this," that's not a lesser spirituality — it's probably exactly where you need to be. The transcendent thing, when it arrives, tends to arrive on its own once the referential scaffolding has done its job.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the phase for the destination. The outward-focused, high-achievement phase of young adulthood is developmentally appropriate. The mistake isn't doing it — it's assuming it will eventually produce inner peace if you just get more of it.

Reforming rather than releasing. Calling rest "productive" or reframing stillness as good strategy is easier than actually letting go of the need to produce. Watch for the productivity mind recruiting spiritual concepts to its own agenda.

Engaging with emotions rather than sitting with them. Sitting with an emotion decreases it. Engaging with it — stalking your ex online, rehearsing an argument, catastrophizing — energizes it. Most people who say "I've tried sitting with my feelings and it doesn't work" are actually doing the latter.

Sitting with the wrong layer. If sitting with sadness or anger doesn't move anything, you're probably not at the actual emotion. Anger often sits on top of fear. The body scan will usually find what the narrative misses.

Assuming enmeshment requires a bad parent. You can have an extraordinarily loving, present, caring parent and still develop patterns of confusion between your emotional state and others'. The love and the wound can be the same thing.

Waiting to be ready. Specifically around major life decisions — the preparation mindset can be infinite. You don't need to have resolved your emotional history before you're allowed to build a life. People learn from being in it, not from being ready for it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your emotional translation. For one week, every time you notice irritation or anger, pause and ask: what else could this be? Run through the list — shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, fear, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, grief, powerlessness. You don't have to answer correctly. Just practice asking.

  2. Start with the body, not the label. When something feels emotionally charged, sit down, close your eyes, and describe what you feel physically. Location, texture, temperature, movement. No emotion names. Just sensations. Do this for two to three minutes before you try to name the feeling.

  3. Rotating body scan to reduce emotional intensity. Slowly move your attention through your body — feet, calves, knees, thighs, hips, belly, chest, shoulders, arms, neck, face — without staying in any one place. This technique has neuroscience support for reducing both chronic pain and emotional reactivity. Do it when an emotion feels too big to sit with directly.

  4. Notice your codependency signature. Ask yourself: when someone close to me is struggling, what do I do? Immediately offer advice? Try to fix it? Dismiss it? The urge to intervene before being asked is worth tracking. Practice sitting with someone's difficulty without saying anything. Tolerate your own discomfort at their distress without acting on it.

  5. Try the vertigo grounding exercise. Spin slowly in place until you feel slightly dizzy, then lie down on the floor. Notice the solidity of the ground against your back while the world is still moving. This is a felt, physical sense of what "being grounded" means — not a metaphor.

  6. Locate the indirect need. When you find yourself engineering a situation to get a particular response from someone (reassurance, praise, attention, comfort), stop and name the need explicitly to yourself. Then decide: is this something I can give myself? Or is this something I can ask for directly?

  7. Distinguish sitting from engaging. The next time you're in emotional pain, set a timer for ten minutes. During those ten minutes, notice the physical sensations (see item 2), and any time you find yourself telling a story about the emotion — replaying events, predicting outcomes, blaming someone — gently return to the sensation. That's the difference between sitting and engaging.

  8. One honest conversation with someone you usually perform for. Pick one person in your life with whom you typically present only the functional, capable, fine version of yourself. Tell them one true thing about how you're actually doing. Observe what happens in your body before, during, and after.
