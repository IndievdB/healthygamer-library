Summary

TL;DR — Trying harder to become who you're supposed to be is often the thing that prevents it. The most meaningful things in your life arrived without forcing them.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Bobby, a veteran of the competitive Smash Bros. Melee community who now works at Twitch. Bobby came in with questions about purpose, belonging, and why he keeps running into friction with other people despite caring deeply about doing good work. What unfolds is a layered exploration of inherited anxiety, the difference between authentic belonging and effortful self-improvement, and why the drive to "do great things" can quietly turn you into someone people don't want to be around.

The Melee Community as a Mirror for Belonging

Bobby's life organized itself around two themes: communication — how hard it is for people to actually hear each other — and belonging — the hunger to be accepted as yourself, not as a performance.

Most of his childhood involved neither. He didn't fit with his family, left his religion without a replacement, lost friendships, and generally felt like something was missing without knowing what. Then came Melee.

What the game gave him wasn't just skill or status. It gave him a context where showing up as himself was enough. That's the distinction between fitting in and belonging:

  • Fitting in is becoming someone you're not in order to be accepted
  • Belonging is being fully yourself and still being welcomed

Fitting in is corrosive because it forces you to choose between authenticity and acceptance — two things you're hungry for simultaneously. Go too far toward pure authenticity without the social awareness to connect, and you end up alone. Go too far toward acceptance at the cost of yourself, and it will come for you eventually.

The game also provided something rare: an objective shared reference point. When everyone agrees to the rules of a game, you can actually communicate — you can believe what the game tells you even when you can't articulate it verbally. Without that shared object, people can be having completely different conversations and never realize it.

Inherited Anxiety and the Feeling You're Hiding Something

Bobby describes a lifelong sense that he has something to hide — something that, if discovered, would get him cast out. He's never been able to find what that thing actually is.

His father and grandfather both have a degenerative nerve disease (CMT), and both went to extreme lengths to conceal it. Bobby didn't inherit the disease, but he seems to have inherited the psychology around it — the constant vigilance, the sense of something shameful that must be managed.

There are a few ways this kind of inheritance can happen:

  1. Epigenetics — stress and anxiety can alter which genes are activated, and those patterns can be passed to children. The science is still emerging, but it's a real field.
  2. Social modeling — children of anxious parents learn anxious behaviors. Treating a parent's anxiety disorder can measurably improve a child's.
  3. Direct upbringing — if your father modeled hiding as a coping strategy, you'll likely learn to hide too, even if you have nothing to hide.

The result for Bobby: a fully intact hiding mechanism, searching for something to conceal. Over time he filled it in with "the way I think" — a belief that if people really saw how his mind works, they wouldn't accept him.

"I have this sense that I don't want other people to know the way that I think."

"Not Good Enough" as Incomplete Dharma, Not Inadequacy

Bobby says he has a deep, persistent sense of not being good enough — but interestingly, it doesn't cause him suffering. He doesn't feel inadequate. He just feels... unfinished.

This is an important distinction. For most people, "not good enough" is a wound that drives compulsive striving. For Bobby, it functions differently — it's more like a compass pointing toward something not yet done.

The framing offered here is that this feeling isn't a deficiency. It's the signal of an incomplete Dharma — a sense of purpose that hasn't fully expressed itself yet. Like seeing a flower bulb that hasn't bloomed. The bulb isn't broken. It will bloom when it blooms.

"A flower is gonna bloom when it blooms. You can see the bulb and say it hasn't bloomed yet, and that doesn't have to be a problem."

The trap is assuming that "not good enough" means you need to try harder to get better. That assumption turns incompleteness into a project, and the project takes you further from the thing you're actually after.

Why Trying Is the Problem, Not the Solution

Bobby has a vision: create the conditions for radical belonging, the kind Melee gave him, and extend it to other communities. It's a genuinely meaningful goal. But the way he pursues it keeps generating conflict.

The pattern looks like this: he shows up with deep knowledge, high stakes, and a strong sense that others don't understand what's actually at risk. When someone pushes back or doesn't "get it," he gets angry — not because he's wrong about the content, but because he's trying so hard to make something happen that anyone in the way feels like an obstacle.

"It's like you're trying to get someone to love you — I want you to love me, I want you to love me — and then someone gets in the way of that and you get mad at them for blocking this great work."

What they're responding to isn't his authentic self. They're responding to the frustrated, trying version — the one hammering nails that don't want to be hammered. The authentic Bobby didn't build belonging in the Melee community. He was it. He showed up, and it happened.

The paradox here is familiar to athletes and meditators: if you're trying to win, the best strategy is to stop trying to win and just play. Trying is the interference. The thing you want — being yourself, creating genuine connection — can't be manufactured through effort. It emerges when effort stops.

Parenting as a Practice Ground

Bobby is worried he'll pass his anxieties to his three-year-old. This is worth addressing directly: you cannot craft a child into something, and you cannot ruin what they fundamentally are. You control the Dharma (what you do); you don't control the Karma (what becomes of them).

When his son asked him to be a shark, Bobby's authentic preference was to rest. He chose to be a shark anyway — not out of obligation, but because at some level he genuinely wanted to. That small moment is the actual practice. Not engineering the perfect parenting philosophy, but showing up for the thing that's in front of you.

It's also healthy to tell a child when you're tired. That teaches him that other humans have feelings and limits, which is the beginning of compassion. Trying to be the perfect parent every moment is both exhausting and useless — it's another form of the same trap.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking incompleteness for inadequacy. If "not good enough" causes you real suffering and compels desperate effort, it's functioning as inadequacy. If it's just a quiet sense of something unfinished, it may actually be pointing you somewhere meaningful. These feel similar but require opposite responses.

Thinking authenticity means unfiltered output. Being yourself isn't the same as saying whatever comes to mind. The authentic self isn't trying — it's being. When you're trying to be authentic, you're already performing.

Assuming the grand mission justifies the behavior. The road to great work can trample people. When you're so convinced of the importance of your mission that others become obstacles, you stop being the person whose mission was worth following in the first place.

Over-processing into conclusions before sharing. Bobby had a habit of presenting polished conclusions rather than raw data. This makes conversations more efficient but also makes it harder for others to actually follow his thinking — and harder for him to be challenged in useful ways.

Trying to fix a wound by finding its source. The hypothesis that there's "something deep I need to hide" can perpetually self-justify — if you don't find it, it just means you haven't dug deep enough yet. That's not a falsifiable belief, and chasing it can become its own trap.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Watch your child sleep (or find your equivalent). Bobby was given this as a direct practice: sit with your child while they sleep and notice the complete absence of trying. Notice what it feels like to just be present with someone you love, with no agenda. Hold that feeling as a reference point.

  2. Take a snapshot of the trying. In your next moment of conflict or frustration — especially when someone isn't responding the way you want — notice what trying feels like in your body and behavior. Then compare it to the snapshot above. You're not analyzing, just noticing the difference.

  3. Stop digging for what you're hiding. If you have a persistent belief that there's something shameful inside you that would cause rejection if revealed, consider that the hiding mechanism itself may have been inherited — not the content. The anxiety is real; the secret may not exist.

  4. When you feel the need to make people understand, pause. Ask yourself if you're trying to get them to love you, agree with you, or respect you. If so, notice that the trying itself is what they're actually responding to — not your ideas.

  5. Let the important things arrive. Make a short list of the most meaningful things in your life. Ask honestly: did you engineer them, or did they happen to you? Use that as evidence that striving harder is not your most reliable strategy.

  6. Follow the "why" to the ground floor. Bobby found that with Melee, the why question had no answer below it — he just wanted to play. When you're in your head asking "why am I doing this?", keep asking until you hit something that has no further justification. If you can't find it, that absence is useful information.
