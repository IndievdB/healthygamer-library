Summary

TL;DR — Loneliness born from abandonment can't be fixed by becoming more independent; it heals through consistent, reliable connection over time. This conversation digs into fear of abandonment, the self-fulfilling cycle of neediness it creates, and what actually breaks that cycle — along with a brief but important note on psilocybin use and self-reflection.

The backdrop is a candid exchange between a psychiatrist and Reckful (Byron Bernstein), a streamer who grew up losing both brothers — one at age six, one at age fifteen — in ways that felt like disappearances rather than normal departures. What unfolds is less a clinical assessment and more a real-time demonstration of how unmet childhood needs shape adult behavior in ways that are rational, not shameful.

The Aching Loneliness That Can't Be Named

There's a particular kind of loneliness that the word "lonely" barely touches. It's not simply being alone — it's a felt sense that something is structurally missing, a hunger that you've tried to fill yourself for years and simply cannot. When asked to locate it in his body, Reckful described it as the feeling of really wanting a hug and not being able to get one.

This kind of loneliness typically has a specific origin. When someone leaves your life early and without adequate explanation — especially when you're five or six years old — you don't have the cognitive equipment to understand that they left because of their circumstances. Your child brain concludes: something is wrong with me. That conclusion doesn't automatically dissolve when you grow up and intellectually understand what happened. The belief was installed before you could reason about it.

"I still never believed that sentence" — the sentence being: they left because of them, not because of you.

The wound isn't a character flaw. It's an unmet developmental need.

The Self-Fulfilling Cycle of Abandonment Fear

Here's where it gets painful in a mechanical, almost predictable way. When you're terrified someone will leave, you hold on tighter. You text more. You show up with walls of text. You want to make plans spontaneously because waiting feels unbearable. From your side, you're doing what any reasonable person does when they're afraid of losing something — you try harder.

From the other person's side, the intensity registers as "too much." They start pulling back. You feel that pullback and the fear spikes higher, which makes you hold on tighter, which makes them pull back further — until they're gone.

"You can see it happening and it terrifies you because you can't stop it and you know what's gonna happen."

And every time the cycle completes, two things get worse: your nervous system accumulates another data point confirming that people leave, and your hunger for connection in the next relationship becomes more desperate. The wolf, as it's put in the conversation, gets hungrier each round.

This pattern maps closely onto what clinicians see in people with borderline personality disorder — not as a label to apply, but as a framework that reveals something important: the behavior that drives people away is not irrational. It's a completely logical response to a genuine threat. The problem is that the behavior makes the threat real.

What Actually Fixes It (And What Doesn't)

The conventional wisdom — that you need to be comfortable alone before you can be in a healthy relationship — sounds reasonable on the surface. But think it through:

If the core wound is fear of abandonment, the mechanism that heals it is not being abandoned. You cannot think your way out of it. You cannot meditate your way out of it quickly (meditation is valuable, but it's a long-term maintenance practice, not an acute intervention — the analogy used is yoga versus surgery after a car accident). You cannot bootstrap yourself into feeling secure through willpower.

What the research on BPD suggests — and it's a striking finding — is that if someone can sustain a stable, non-abandoning relationship for two years, there's roughly a 50% chance the abandonment-fear patterns resolve substantially on their own. The nervous system slowly learns that the threat it has been bracing for isn't coming.

The analogy with a two-year-old child is clarifying. When a toddler becomes clingy after a disorienting change in the household — a new sibling, a parent's absence — you don't tell the child they need to work on their independence. You give them more consistent, reliable attention. You show up. Over time, the clinginess naturally settles as the child internalizes: these people are here, they're not going away.

The child didn't need to fix themselves. They needed someone to stay.

Why You Can't Buy Your Way to Belonging

One of the most honest moments in the conversation is the acknowledgment of a pattern: paying people's rent to have a roommate, offering people jobs so they'd be around more, restructuring situations to keep people close. These aren't cynical moves — they come from genuine desperation for human contact.

But there's a corrosive side effect. When you engineer someone's presence in your life, you can never be fully sure whether they're there because they want to be or because of what you've provided. That uncertainty poisons the thing you were trying to create.

"What you really need is someone who wants you for you, and you make it hard on them by giving them things they may need in life."

This doesn't mean you can never have mutually beneficial relationships — of course you can. The distinction worth noticing is the difference between genuinely caring for a friend and strategically keeping someone close because the loneliness has become unbearable. Both can look similar from the outside. The internal difference matters, and it's worth paying attention to which one is driving a given decision.

Rerouting the Thought Trails

The brain builds habitual thought pathways the way animals build trails through a field: repeated passage wears a groove, and new thoughts naturally slide into the same ruts. Negative thought loops — I'm too much, I'm unlovable, I drive people away — don't stay active because they're true. They stay active because they've been traveled thousands of times.

You don't break those loops by medicating them away (medication doesn't re-route the trail, it just changes how you feel while you're on it). You build new trails by thinking in new ways, repeatedly, until the new path becomes the easier one. Gratitude journaling is one approach because it literally practices a different cognitive direction. Reframing is another — recognizing when a familiar self-critical thought is about to fire, and consciously choosing to take a different road.

The specific reframe that matters most here: you don't need to learn how to be independent. You need more love than you got. That's not weakness; it's an accurate description of a deficit. Telling yourself you should be fine alone — after thirty years of that instruction producing no results — is the definition of trying the same failing treatment indefinitely.

Common Pitfalls

Conflating neediness with weakness. The need for connection after early abandonment is a rational adaptation, not a character defect. Treating it as something to be ashamed of and suppressed makes it worse, not better.

Trying to fix the problem alone. The wound is relational. It heals relationally. Trying to bootstrap yourself into security through self-improvement projects misses the mechanism entirely.

Assuming psilocybin (or any substance) replaces the underlying work. The conversation is non-judgmental about substance use, but the key principle is: don't do it on autopilot. The only real mistake is avoiding honest reflection about what you're using, why, and whether you're genuinely exploring alternatives or just reaching for the easier state.

Tainting relationships by engineering them. When you manufacture someone's presence in your life, you undermine your own ability to trust that presence. It feels like a solution but compounds the core problem.

Chasing only the "happy" version of yourself. Happiness is worth pursuing, but trying to exist only in that mode means the difficult, quieter version of you never gets enough support to actually become less painful. Both selves need to be livable.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the loneliness without judging it. When you feel the pull toward frantically reaching out, pause and try to locate where that loneliness lives in your body. Name it as what it is — not neediness, not weakness, but an unmet need for consistent love — and let that naming be neutral.

  2. Audit one relationship for tainting. Pick one significant relationship and ask yourself honestly: am I in this because I genuinely care about this person, or because the loneliness is driving me to keep them close? You don't have to end anything — just notice.

  3. Start building a new thought trail. Identify the specific self-critical loop you return to most often (e.g., "I'm too much for people"). When you catch yourself on that trail, consciously reroute: what's the alternative framing you've been given or discovered? Practice thinking the new thought, even briefly, each time.

  4. Find one person who is "currently okay but has been there." The sweet spot for connection — someone who can understand where you are without being pulled under by it — is real and findable. Invest deliberately in those relationships over ones where you bond purely over shared suffering.

  5. Try a structured settling practice. After a draining conversation or when your thoughts are looping, try a short chanting or breath-focused exercise. The goal isn't silence — it's interrupting the loop long enough to notice that your baseline, without active thought, is more stable than your thoughts suggest.

  6. Before reaching out in a moment of high need, ask one question: Is this the loneliness talking, or do I genuinely have something to say to this person right now? You can still reach out — but doing it consciously rather than reactively changes the quality of the interaction.
