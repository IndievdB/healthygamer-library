TL;DR — Nobody fully understands what dreams are, but they're almost certainly not meaningless noise. And if your partner is angry at you for what you did in their dream, that anger is pointing at something real in them — not in you.

This covers two interlocking ideas: the relationship problem of being held responsible for dream behavior, and the deeper question of what dreams actually are and where they come from. When You're Blamed for What Happened in Someone Else's Dream

Before getting into the science, there's a practical relationship problem worth addressing directly.

If someone is angry at you for behavior in their dream, the most useful move is to ask open-ended questions rather than either apologizing or getting defensive:

  • "Help me understand what I did that was wrong."
  • "Am I responsible for what your mind creates?"
  • "Can I control what I do in your dreams?"

These aren't rhetorical gotchas — they're genuine invitations for the other person to examine their own reasoning. Often people haven't actually thought it through.

The more important warning: don't apologize for things outside your control. If you apologize the first time, and the second time, you've implicitly agreed that you are responsible. You've set an expectation. By the fifth or sixth apology, it's not unreasonable for the other person to believe — on some level — that you really do owe them something here. Apologizing reinforces the pattern. Be careful about what behaviors you're willing to take ownership of, because that shapes the relationship's norms going forward.

The deeper question, though, is why someone keeps dreaming about their partner cheating. That's where the science and psychology get interesting.

What Science Actually Knows About Dreams (Which Isn't Much)

The honest starting point: we don't really know what dreams are or why we have them.

This isn't false modesty — it reflects a genuine research limitation. Neuroscience typically advances through invasive animal studies, but you can't ask a dog what it was dreaming about. Tools like fMRI and EEG give us imprecise, low-resolution data. And unlike most brain functions, dreaming doesn't appear to be localized to a single region, so there's no "dream center" to point a scanner at.

What qualitative research (asking people about their dreams) has established:

  • Dreams are built from waking experience. People blind from birth don't see in dreams — they dream in sound, touch, and smell. Whatever your brain has never processed, it can't reconstruct at night.
  • Dreams are predominantly emotional. The most common emotion in dreams is anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →. Negative emotions overall are more common than positive ones, though the full emotional range is possible.
  • Dreams are bizarre and stitched-together. They take fragments of plausible reality and combine them in nonsensical, amplified ways.
  • Sexual content is less common than people assume — roughly 8–10% of dreams.
  • We don't know what purpose they serve. People with brain injuries who lose the ability to dream seem to function reasonably well, though that data is complicated by the brain damage itself.

The Main Theories: RNG, Subconscious, or Housekeeping?

Three broad hypotheses explain what dreams might actually be:

1. Pure randomness (the RNG hypothesis) Dreams are a byproduct of whatever restorative maintenance the brain performs during sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → — neurons firing, adenosineA metabolic waste product produced in the brain as neurons burn energy throughout the day.Full definition → clearing, memory pathways resetting. The emotional content feels meaningful the same way tea leaves or cloud shapes feel meaningful: your pattern-seeking mind assigns significance to noise. The dream itself has no intrinsic meaning.

"The meaning that we see in dreams is assigned by our mind, as opposed to having some kind of intrinsic meaning."

This is intellectually coherent, but it's hard to fully accept given that essentially nothing else in the body is random. Evolution doesn't tend to preserve elaborate, regular processes for no reason.

2. The subconscious (Freud and Jung) Freud's biggest contribution to Western psychology wasn't the oral phase or the Oedipus complex — it was the idea that the mind is not entirely conscious. There is a subconscious layer, and during waking life the conscious mind acts as a gatekeeper, suppressing what's down there through defense mechanisms like denial, intellectualization, and repression.

During sleep, the gatekeeper goes off dutyDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition →. Dreams, in this view, are unfiltered subconscious thought — the subconscious throwing a party with no adult supervision.

Where Freud and Jung diverge is on how subconscious content appears in dreams:

  • Symbolic representation (Freud): things in the dream stand for something else entirely. Losing your teeth is a symbol for fear of death. A bear is a symbol for your father. The content is coded, and requires interpretation to decode.
  • Thematic representation: the dream isn't a substitution cipher — it's more direct than that. If you're afraid of abandonment, you might dream of being left behind at a circus. The fear maps onto your real fear; only the setting is scrambled.

The case against rigid symbolic interpretation: symbols aren't universal. There's no strong evidence that "losing teeth = fear of mortality" means the same thing to someone in Papua New Guinea as it does to a Freudian analyst in Vienna. If symbols aren't universal, you can't read a symbols dictionary and reliably decode anyone's dream.

3. Mental housekeeping A more modest hypothesis: dreams are a side effect of memory consolidation and emotional processing. If you spent the day at a theme park, your brain consolidates that experience at night — and the process of re-encoding it generates dream-like stimuli. Emotions or events that went unprocessed during waking hours get their time in the queue.

This isn't mutually exclusive with the subconscious hypothesis; it's just shorter-range. It's about what happened in the last day or two, not decades-old buried conflicts.

Dreams in Psychotherapy

Dream analysis is more central to some therapeutic traditions than others. Psychoanalysts (Freudian and Jungian) work extensively with dreams and symbolic interpretation. Contemporary therapists, especially those practicing third-wave approaches like DBT or mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition →-based therapy, tend to use dreams less.

A practical approach that avoids the pitfall of over-interpreting symbols: rather than translating the content of a dream, focus on the emotions.

For each thing that happened in the dream, ask: what did you feel? Then ask: is this emotion relevant to something in your actual life?

This turns the dream into something like a projective test — an inkblot that reveals not some universal symbolic code, but this particular person's emotional preoccupations. The dream provides raw material; the meaning emerges from the conversation, not from a decoder ring.

"I think about dreams as almost a projective test — it's really in the interpretation of it that you do with a patient that you figure out what has value and what doesn't."

There's also evidence that doing dream work can help when therapy feels stuck. If you've hit a wall and don't know where to go next, dreams can open up emotional territory that wasn't accessible through direct conversation.

The Eastern View: Dreams as a State of Consciousness

Eastern frameworks, particularly from yogic and Vedic traditions, approach dreams differently. The content of the dream is less interesting than the fact of the dream as a distinct state of consciousness — alongside waking, deep sleep, and meditative states.

One relevant concept is the samskaraA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from past experiences that gets stored in the mind and automatically activates in present situations, distorting your perception of what's actually happening.Full definition →: a stored, unprocessed emotion. When something happens and the emotion it generates isn't fully metabolized, it goes dormant — until something (a triggering encounter, or a dream) reactivates it. The reactivated emotion then floods the present moment, feeling much larger than the current situation warrants.

"This emotion is not coming from our current interaction — it's projected from the past onto the present."

In this framework, the girlfriend who keeps dreaming about her partner cheating isn't making things up — she has a stored emotional wound (possibly from a past relationship, possibly from something older) that her sleeping mind keeps reprocessing. The dreams are her samskara looking for a way out.

There's also a karmic perspective worth noting: the intense fear or anxiety experienced in dreams feels completely real — indistinguishable in quality from waking emotion. One interpretation is that this real suffering serves a real function, a kind of debt-paying or emotional processing that the waking mind couldn't complete.

Common Pitfalls

  • Apologizing as a reflex. When someone is upset, apologizing feels kind. But apologizing for behavior in someone else's dream implicitly concedes that you're responsible for it. Over time, this creates an expectation and a pattern that becomes very hard to walk back.

  • Taking symbolic interpretation too literally. Dream dictionaries and universal symbol systems have real appeal — they promise a clean decoder. But symbols are not universal across cultures or individuals. A symbol that "means" one thing in one tradition or one person's life may mean nothing or something entirely different in another's.

  • Assuming dreams are either totally meaningful or totally meaningless. The most honest position is: we don't know. Treating dreams as a complete oracle leads to over-interpretation; dismissing them as pure noise probably misses something real about emotional processing.

  • Focusing on the content rather than the emotion. The bizarre imagery in dreams — the circus, the plane, the president — is often a distraction. The feeling underneath the image is more likely to be clinically and personally useful.

  • Blaming the dreamer. If a partner keeps dreaming about betrayal, the instinct may be to wonder what you're doing to cause suspicion. More often it reflects an unprocessed wound in them that predates you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you're in the dream-blame situation: instead of apologizing or getting defensive, ask open-ended questions. "Help me understand what I did wrong." Let the other person work through the logic themselves. Don't concede responsibility for things genuinely outside your control.

  2. Keep a brief dream journal. For each dream you remember, don't focus on interpreting the images — write down the emotions. What were you feeling at each point? This builds the habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of tracking emotional content rather than narrative.

  3. Do the emotion-mapping exercise. Take the emotions from a recent dream and ask: Is there anywhere in my waking life where I feel something similar? You don't need a therapist for this. Just sit with the question and see what surfaces.

  4. If you feel stuck in therapy (or stuck in general self-reflection), bring a dream to the next session, or journal about one. Describe the sequence of events, then go through each event and name the emotion. Use that as a starting point rather than trying to decode symbolism.

  5. If your partner keeps dreaming about betrayal, resist the urge to prove your innocence. Ask, with genuine curiosity, what they were feeling in the dream — and whether that feeling shows up anywhere else for them. The dream is probably not about you.
