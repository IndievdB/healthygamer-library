Abusive Systems Run on Positive Emotions, Not Just Negative Ones

Western culture tends to sort emotions into "good" and "bad." The Greatest Scam a Cult Can Pull

This is the central mechanism worth understanding clearly:

Cults hold people emotionally hostage by tricking them into believing they are responsible for other people's suffering.

Here's how the logic is constructed for the person in this situation:

  1. If I leave, my parents will have to shun me (their religion demands it).
  2. If I stay, my parents won't have to suffer that loss.
  3. Therefore, my parents' suffering is in my control.
  4. Therefore, I am responsible for it.
  5. Therefore, I must stay.

Every step feels emotionally true. But step two is where the manipulation happens. The parents don't have to shun their child because the child left. They have to shun their child because the religion is commanding them to. The cult is causing the suffering. The person leaving is not.

"This is the biggest scam that's ever been pulled by a cult — because you're not causing their suffering. It's the religion that's causing their suffering. But the religion has brainwashed you into thinking that you have this in your control."

This pattern appears everywhere, not just in cults. A gaslighting partner who says "I cheated because you worked too much and weren't there for me — so it's your fault." A parent who says "if you don't clean your room, I'll lose my temper and hurt your sibling — so that'll be on you." An addict who leverages a parent's love to extract financial rescue after rescue. In each case, the person doing harm places the responsibility for their behavior onto someone else. Once that transfer sticks, the other person is trapped — not by chains, but by love and guilt.

Responsibility Has a Boundary

There's a genuine philosophical question underneath all of this: whose responsibility are someone else's feelings?

The useful framing isn't "your actions have no effect on others" — of course they do. The question is who is ultimately responsible for managing the feeling that results.

If you give someone a birthday cheesecake and it accidentally triggers grief over a loss you knew nothing about, your action produced the pain — but you can't reasonably be held responsible for it. If you deliberately bully someone, you are responsible because the intention was harm. The action matters, and intention matters, but the person experiencing the feeling retains responsibility for how they manage it internally.

"I can't control what they say, but it is to a certain degree within my control how I manage my feelings."

Viktor Frankl discovered this inside Nazi concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → camps. Marcus Aurelius built a philosophy around it. The stoic and yogic traditions converge on the same insight: there is always a layer of internal independence that no external force can fully take from you. Protecting that independence — rather than outsourcing control of your inner life to whoever might be upset by your choices — is not selfishness. It's psychological survival.

Applied to the cult situation: yes, leaving will cause your parents sadness. That is real. You can acknowledge it, grieve it, and try to stay connected if they allow it. But their decision to shun you, and the pain that produces for them, belongs to the religion that commands it and to the choice they make in response to it. Not to you.

What Leaving Actually Looks Like

Think of it as a life transplant. Not just moving out of a house — being cut out of the entire organism you've grown inside: family, friends, communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition →, shared language, shared meaning. You lose everything at once and are placed somewhere foreign where you have to learn to function from scratch.

Transplants are hard. They require time, adjustment, and support. But they can be lifesaving. And most former Jehovah's Witnesses, by their own account, are glad they left.

When the confrontation comes, the most honest and dignified position sounds something like:

"This religion isn't right for me. Any religion that forcibly separates parents from their children is not one I want to be part of. I understand this is a big part of your life. I don't blame you. I forgive you. I love you, and if I'm never allowed to talk to you again, I love you from the bottom of my heart. I'm going to go live a happy, healthy life, and I wish the same for you."

That's not a cold exit. It's taking your action — clearly, with love — and then releasing control over what other people do in response. That's all that's ever actually in your hands.

One more thing: don't assume the outcome is fixed. Sometimes when one person leaves, others follow. Sometimes a parent quietly admits they haven't believed in years either. Sometimes letters sent into silence get answered five years later. Life is long. The world changes faster than anyone expects. Today's permanent-feeling rupture is not necessarily permanent.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking fear is the main obstacle. If you've been in a high-control religion, you may spend a lot of energy preparing to overcome fear and shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → — only to find that guilt and love are what actually stop you at the door. Name the positive emotions explicitly, because they require different work.

  • Accepting responsibility for what the cult is doing. The moment you catch yourself thinking "if I leave, I'll destroy my parents' lives," pause. Ask: who is actually making that happen? Your departure isn't the cause. The religion's rules are the cause.

  • Confusing empathy with obligation. You can understand why your parents would shun you, feel deep compassion for them, and recognize them as victims of the same system — and still leave. These are not in conflict. Compassion doesn't require you to stay trapped.

  • Assuming the outcome is permanent. People change. Circumstances change. Don't make a lifelong decision based on how things look at the moment of maximum pressure.

  • Waiting for permission or the right moment. The person in this situation has a concrete opportunity — a job he loves, some savings, and a therapist. The "right moment" is often just the moment when the cost of waiting finally exceeds the cost of acting.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name which emotions are actually holding you back. Write down, honestly, whether it's fear, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, guilt, love, or loyalty. Treat positive emotions with the same seriousness as negative ones — they can bind just as tightly.

  2. Trace responsibility back to its source. When you feel guilty about potential harm to your family, ask yourself: who is actually making this happen? Write out the causal chain and identify where the cult's rules enter it. This is not to dismiss your feelings — it's to see clearly who owns what.

  3. Build your external support network before you need it. Identify your therapist, any family outside the religion, and any new connections through work or other interests. The transplant is easier with a receiving organism ready to receive you.

  4. Prepare what you want to say. Write out the conversation you'd have with your family if it came to a confrontation. Aim for honesty, love, and clarity — not blame. Practice it until it feels like yours.

  5. Separate "causing sadness" from "causing harm." Ask yourself: is there a version of this where I do the kind, honest thing, my parents feel sad, and that's okay? The goal isn't to eliminate their pain. The goal is to act from a place of non-violenceA Sanskrit term meaning non-violence or non-harm — the principle that you should do your best not to hurt any living being, and that this commitment ranks above all other moral values.Full definition → and integrity, and allow them to manage their response.

  6. Think one generation forward. If you have (or might have) children someday, consider what world you're building for them by the choice you make today. This isn't a guilt exercise — it's a dharmaDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition → exercise. What are you actually responsible for, over time?

  7. Keep a line of communication open, even if it goes unanswered. A letter, a card, a photo of your life. You cannot control whether it's received. You can control whether it's sent.
