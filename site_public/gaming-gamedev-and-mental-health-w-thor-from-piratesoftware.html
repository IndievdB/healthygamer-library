Summary

TL;DR — A psychiatrist and a veteran game developer trace how a person's inner architecture — emotional reactivity, analytical capability, stored emotional wounds, and ego — determines whether failure demoralizes or teaches. Along the way, the conversation becomes an unexpected introduction to the science and philosophy of mind, ending in a live meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition →.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between two people with genuinely complementary lenses: one who has spent 20 years studying and treating the mind, and one who spent 20 years building things, failing, rebuilding, and teaching others to do the same. What starts as a question about game developer mental health becomes a surprisingly deep map of how human beings process experience — and why the same failure can destroy one person's will to continue while barely registering for another.

Thor's Inner Architecture: Analytics at the Top

Thor's background covers game testing at Blizzard, application security, offensive hacking for the federal government, and now full-time game development and streaming. The throughline isn't the jobs — it's a consistent decision-making style he can trace to a single inflection point: being homeless in his early 20s after a business partner opened-sourced all of their shared work.

"It was never one of those things where it's like oh screw that person I'm just going to wallow in sadness. It was like: we need to solve this puzzle. This is a puzzle that needs to get solved and it has to be solved by me changing my behavior."

His response wasn't to suppress emotion — he's explicit that feeling sad, angry, and upset is fine — but to refuse to let those feelings be the only thing happening. He describes a two-layer system: emotional input arrives first, and then something else steps in to ask whether acting on that emotion is actually useful right now.

When asked why he does this when many people don't, he points to his mother, who was intensely emotionally reactive. Watching chaos produced by unmanaged emotion — specifically, how the outcomes became random and people got hurt — made him not want to be that way. He chose his father's house in the divorce, partly for this reason. The adaptation was deliberate, even as a child.

This matters because his advice to aspiring game developers flows entirely from this temperament. "Make a bunch of bad games. Fail fast. Don't tie your personal value to your first creation's performance." It works for a large chunk of his audience — but the conversation honestly confronts why it can't simply be handed to everyone.

The Stack: Why Failure Three Feels Like Failure Ten

Each unprocessed failure adds emotional residue. When the next failure arrives, the new emotional charge combines with everything stored from before. This is why patterns of repeated failure become so overwhelming — the person isn't managing one failure, they're managing an accumulated stack without ever having discharged any of it.

"You're not demoralized because of one thing. You are demoralized because of the storehouse of failures — and today when you fail, the reason you're demoralized and you give up is not because you failed today. It's because you add the energy of all of your previous failures onto this failure."

This is also where the nocebo effectThe nocebo effect is the mirror image of the placebo effect: if you believe something isn't going to work, it doesn't work.Full definition → enters: if you believe something isn't going to work, it is significantly less likely to work. Placebo and nocebo effects account for somewhere around 30–50% of outcomes in well-studied conditions. A string of unprocessed failures, interpreted through an ego lens as personal inadequacy, produces a genuine physiological change that makes the next attempt harder.

There is a brain region called the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) that integrates information and guides present-moment decision-making. Normally, memories of past experiences migrate to the hippocampusThe brain's central hub for memory and learning — and, critically, the structure that consolidates short-term information into long-term understanding.Full definition → — they become past events that can be recalled without the full emotional charge of the original moment.

The memory stays in the PCC — in the present — which is why traumatic experiences don't feel like memories. They feel like things that are happening now. This is the neuroscience of why someone who "should be over" something isn't over it. The experience is literally not in the past in their brain.

Effective psychotherapy moves the memory from the PCC back to the hippocampusThe brain's central hub for memory and learning — and, critically, the structure that consolidates short-term information into long-term understanding.Full definition →. When someone tells the full story of a difficult experience — not editing out the parts that were good, not flattening it into a morality tale — the emotional energy rises, the analytical mind engages, and the memory files back into the past. This is what emotional processing actually is:

"Reliving the memory, reliving the emotion, reframing it, and then filing it back away again using a more analytical approach rather than just having it up in the forefront of your mind."

Common Pitfalls

Treating advice as universally applicable. Thor's "make bad games fast" advice works for people whose buddhiThe intellect or analytical faculty of the mind — the part of you that does logic, reason, and deliberate thought.Full definition → is already stronger than their ahamkara. For people in the other situation, the advice can actually reinforce negative loops — they try, they fail, and the failure confirms the story they already believed. Advice should be calibrated to the person, not broadcast as a universal fix.

Confusing emotional suppression with emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that promote adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition →. Thor explicitly does not suppress his emotions. He feels them, checks whether acting on them is useful right now, and then processes them through introspection later. Suppression means skipping the processing step. That's how samskarasA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from the past that lingers in the unconscious mind and automatically shapes how you think, feel, and respond to situations in the present.Full definition → accumulate.

Believing your analytical mind is actually analyzing. The ego can take over the buddhiThe intellect or analytical faculty of the mind — the part of you that does logic, reason, and deliberate thought.Full definition → silently. When you're rationalizing why your ex was actually terrible, or why that person who didn't swipe right was obviously wrong for you anyway, you feel logical. You are not. The test: would you say the same thing to someone else in the same situation? If not, your analysis is being run by your ego.

Equating demoralization with the present failure. The failure in front of you is rarely what's actually crushing you. It's the unprocessed stack underneath it. Trying to fix the feeling by fixing the present failure misses the actual problem.

Idealization of creators. Watching someone who has it figured out and concluding you should already be there is a perceptual error fueled by both ego (comparison requiring a me and a you) and incomplete information (you're seeing their output, not their history of failure and processing).

Action Items & Exercises

  1. After a failure, notice the language you use. Did the project fail, or did you fail? Practice replacing "I failed" with "this project didn't land." The linguistic shift is not denial — it is the first step toward accurate analysis rather than ego-driven overgeneralization.

  2. Process failures before stacking a new attempt. Before moving on from something that didn't work, sit with it long enough to ask: what specifically went wrong? What was external to me? What can I actually change? Do this while letting the emotion be present, not while trying to talk yourself out of feeling bad. Emotional processing requires the emotional circuitry to be active.

  3. Use the comparison test for self-critical thoughts. When the internal voice says something harsh about your work or your ability, ask: would I say this exact thing to a friend or colleague in the same situation? If no, the voice is being run by ahamkara, not buddhiThe intellect or analytical faculty of the mind — the part of you that does logic, reason, and deliberate thought.Full definition →. You don't have to silence it — just don't take its conclusions as facts.

  4. Build a narrative around significant failures or painful experiences. Write or speak the full story — including what was good about it, not just what went wrong. Let the emotion come up while you tell it. This is the mechanism by which memories move from the present into the past and lose their grip.

  5. Practice locating the emptiness within. Make a circle with your arms. Look at the space inside — not through it at objects, but at the space itself. Close your eyes and try to feel the corresponding space within your body, roughly at the solar plexus. Notice where sensation stops. Spend a few minutes there. This is the beginning of the practice described toward the end of the conversation.

  6. Inventory your diet and substances relative to your actual baseline. If you're relying on caffeine, stimulants, or other inputs to function, you may not have a clear read on what your natural capacity is. This isn't about eliminating anything — it's about understanding what your actual floor looks like without the scaffolding.

  7. When giving advice to others, get information first. The advice that works for you works because of your specific history and mental architecture. Before applying what worked for you to someone else, spend a few minutes understanding where they are. The same input produces different outputs depending on the substrate.
