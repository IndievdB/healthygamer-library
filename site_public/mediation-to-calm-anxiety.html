What Kapalabhati Actually Is

Kapalabhati (also called bellows breath) is a breathing pattern built around forceful exhalations and passive inhalations.

Here's the basic mechanics:

  • You sharply contract your stomach to push air out — a forceful, active exhale.
  • You don't actively inhale. Instead, you let the inhale happen on its own as a rebound or recoil after each exhale.
  • The inhale is passive; the exhale is where your attention goes.

The rebound inhalation is the trickiest part to find. It's not a deliberate breath in — it's more like what happens to your lungs after you squeeze the air out. Give yourself about half a second and let it come naturally. If you run out of breath, go ahead and inhale so you can keep exhaling — just keep your focus on the exhale, not the inhale.

How to Practice It

Start with sets of 10 exhalations, then build up to 24, then 30.

  1. Close your eyes.
  2. Begin the forceful exhalations — sharp, rhythmic, stomach contracting each time.
  3. Let the passive inhalation rebound between each exhale.
  4. If you feel like you're running out of breath, take a deliberate inhale, then continue.
  5. After the final round of 30, stop and sit with how you feel.

That last step is not optional — it's the actual point.

"The space after the practice is the whole point. It's actually not the exhalations. After you're done with that last breath of 30, just sit with the way that you feel."

The breaths themselves are preparation. The stillness that follows — that shift in how you feel — is what the technique is trying to get you to.

Why This Works for Anxiety Specifically

Anxiety isn't just a thought pattern; it has a physical rhythm. An anxious mind moves fast, and anxious breathing moves fast to match it. Trying to impose slow, calm breathing on a mind that's already running hot is like trying to get an overexcited dog to sit — it doesn't have the energy for it yet.

Kapalabhati works differently. It meets the mind where it is — fast and energized — and then naturally slows as the practice winds down. Your nervous system follows that deceleration.

"You don't want to force the anxious mind to be calm. You kind of want to run with it for a little bit, and then as you start to slow down, your anxiety is gonna slow down too."

The dog analogy makes this concrete: if your dog is running in circles and you try to make it sit still, it won't cooperate. But if you start running with it and then gradually slow your own pace, the dog slows with you. The anxious mind responds the same way. You can't strong-arm it into stillness — you have to join its pace first.

What to Do If You Feel Constriction

Not everyone comes out of kapalabhati feeling relaxed. Some people feel tightness — in the chest, the throat, the back of the neck. That's okay and worth knowing about in advance.

If you feel constriction after the practice:

  • Take a slow, deep breath in.
  • Actively breathe into the area of tightness — if it's your chest, direct your breath there; if it's your neck, imagine your vertebrae expanding with each inhale.
  • Don't fight the sensation. Let it be there and breathe around it.

The physical sensation of expansion — even just the sense that your spine lengthens slightly with a full inhale — can help release that tightness gradually.

Common Pitfalls

Focusing on the inhale instead of the exhale. The technique is exhale-driven. If you're actively trying to breathe in, you've lost the thread. Let the inhale take care of itself.

Stopping too early. The payoff isn't during the exhalations — it's in the stillness that follows the full practice. Cutting the session short before you reach that post-practice moment means you've done the work without collecting the result.

Trying to slow down before you've sped up. If you skip the active phase and go straight to slow breathing, you're back to forcing calmness on a wired mind. Let the fast-paced exhales happen first.

Mistaking "not getting it" for doing it wrong. The rebound inhale is genuinely hard to describe and harder to find at first. Confusion early on doesn't mean you're failing — it means you're learning a physical skill, and it takes a few attempts to feel it.

Forcing stillness on a racing mind as a general strategy. Kapalabhati reflects a broader principle: anxiety has momentum, and meeting it with resistance usually adds to it. Joining the pace and then decelerating is the actual mechanism — and it applies beyond just breathing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try a set of 10 exhalations right now. Close your eyes, forcefully contract your stomach to push air out, and let the inhale happen passively between each exhale. Don't chase the inhale — just exhale sharply and wait for the recoil.

  2. Build to a full session. Once you can find the rebound inhale, do a set of 10, then 24, then 30 consecutive exhalations. This progression is the actual practice.

  3. Sit in silence after your last exhale. Don't skip this. When the 30-breath round ends, just stay still with eyes closed and notice what's happening in your body and mind. Give it at least 60 seconds. That state is the destination.

  4. If you feel tightness post-practice, take a deliberate slow breath and direct it into the tight area — chest, throat, or neck. Repeat two or three times and notice whether the constriction shifts.

  5. Use this technique the next time anxiety ramps up — not as a last resort, but early. Because it works by matching your energy level first, it's more effective before you're completely overwhelmed than after.
