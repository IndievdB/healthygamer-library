Why Progress Feels Like Running in Place

One of the most disorienting experiences in personal growth is doing everything right and still feeling terrible. Asriel named it clearly: he was doing school, practicing better self-care, showing up — and the emotions didn't change.

There's a useful analogy here. In online matchmaking, your win rate hovers around 50% even as you get better, because the difficulty of your opponents scales with your skill. Life works the same way. As you level up, the challenges you face level up with you. You're not fighting the same monsters — you're deeper in the game, where the air is thinner.

This isn't failure. It's the texture of actual progress. The feeling of "nothing is working" often means you are, in fact, climbing. The mistake is interpreting that feeling as evidence you haven't moved.

Where the Belief Comes From

Asriel traced the feeling of being unimportant back to fourth grade, the day he showed up at school and was told he was no longer enrolled there. His mother had moved him to his brother's school — the brother who had gotten into the gifted program — without telling anyone. He found out at the school door.

That moment isn't traumatic because of the school change itself. It's traumatic because of what it communicated: you weren't worth thinking about. There wasn't even a conversation. He was an afterthought in a logistical decision.

This sat inside a broader pattern: - Parents splitting up, living between two homes - A father who worked constantly, who responded to struggle with "you need to do better" rather than understanding - Siblings who were objectively more academically gifted and received more recognition - A stepfather whose mood determined whether the household was safe, making Asriel's sense of worth unstable and contingent

None of this required anyone to be a villain. His father is described as someone he deeply respects — hardworking, sacrificial, a scout leader. And yet:

"Did he care about you? Yes. Did he love you? Obviously. Was he there for you emotionally? Not really."

The distinction that matters most here is love versus pride. His father demonstrated care through action and sacrifice. But Asriel had never once heard his father say he was proud of him. Those are not the same thing, and the absence of the second one leaves a specific kind of wound.

Intrinsic Value vs. Conditional Worth

The core belief Asriel is carrying isn't "I'm bad at things." It's something more fundamental: my worth depends entirely on what I accomplish and what I can do for others. Strip away the achievements, and there's nothing left worth valuing.

"I think I have value — it's what I can accomplish and do for people around me."

This is conditional worth. It means every failure is an identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → attack, not just a setback. It means no achievement ever fully lands, because the baseline assumption ("I'm in the bargain bin") keeps reasserting itself. When the job site didn't call after he didn't show up, his mind's immediate translation was: see, you don't even matter enough to fire.

Intrinsic value — the idea that you are worth something simply by existing and showing up — is not something Asriel was taught. It's a foreign concept, genuinely confusing when someone tries to offer it. When that recognition was extended in the conversation, he felt something but couldn't quite name it, had never quite felt it before.

"To be honest, I try not to think about it. That just makes me nervous."

That nervousness is what it feels like when a belief system encounters information it wasn't built to process.

The Dad Conversation

Late in the conversation, Asriel mentioned something he'd almost glossed over: he'd recently told his father about his struggles with a job, and his father's response was "Where did I go wrong with you?"

On the surface, this looks like self-blame — the father taking responsibility. But look at the assumption buried inside it: something went wrong with you. Even a compassionate, self-critical framing can reinforce the child's sense that they are a problem to be explained.

This is not an indictment of the father. He's growing too. But the pattern is worth seeing clearly: the opposite of pride isn't contempt. It can also be a worried, caring disappointment that still communicates you are not quite right.

Asriel had also deflected constantly throughout the conversation — offering reasons why his stepfather drank, explaining his mother's emotional state, contextualizing his father's busyness. This is worth naming:

"You're putting a hundred points of blame on yourself."

Protecting everyone else from accountability is another form of self-erasure. It's hard to believe you matter when you reflexively make everyone else's behavior understandable and your own experience incidental.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking external progress for internal healing. Finishing school, getting into therapy, joining a group — these are real and meaningful. They don't automatically resolve the underlying belief driving the distress.

Treating shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →-as-fuel as the only option. People who use this strategy often believe that if they stop beating themselves up, they'll stop doing anything at all. Sometimes that's temporarily true. But it's not evidence that self-attack is necessary — it's evidence that no other motivational structure has been built yet.

Confusing love with pride. A parent can love a child completely and never communicate pride in who that child is. These are distinct emotional messages, and a child who receives one but not the other will carry a specific unresolved question about their worth.

Deflecting credit when it's offered. Asriel described making contributions in his group, then immediately qualified that they might not be as valuable as others'. The mind that has been taught it's in the bargain bin will contort any incoming positive signal to fit the original belief. Noticing this pattern is the first step to not fully trusting it.

Assuming nihilism means meaninglessness. Nihilism, properly understood, means you can't rely on an external objective standard for meaning — not that there isn't any. Meaning still has to be carved out, but the carving is yours to do.

  4. Track where you assign blame. When something goes wrong, notice whether your first instinct is to find reasons why the other people involved were understandable, reasonable, or justified. That's not always wrong — but if it's always the move, it's worth asking what's getting explained away.

  5. Ask yourself the fourth-grade question. When a negative emotion comes up — particularly around worthiness or not being enough — see if you can locate when you first felt this way.   6. In your support group or close relationships, let someone else be proud of you. Don't immediately qualify it. Let the statement land for a second before the deflection reflex kicks in. You don't have to believe it yet. Just don't dodge it quite so fast.

  7. Consider whether there's a conversation to have with your father — not to assign blame, but to hear something you may have needed for a long time. It doesn't have to happen now, and it may be hard for both of you. But the question are you proud of me is not asking for validation. It's asking to receive something that was always supposed to be there.
