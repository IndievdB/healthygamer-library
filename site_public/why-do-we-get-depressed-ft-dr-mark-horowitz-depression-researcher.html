The Serotonin Hypothesis Didn't Hold Up

For decades, patients were told depression is caused by low serotoninA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition → — a chemical imbalance the medication corrects, much like insulin corrects diabetes. That story has now collapsed under its own evidence.

A landmark umbrella review published in Molecular Psychiatry (co-authored by Dr. Horowitz) synthesized the existing research and found no consistent support for the low-serotonin theory of depression. The chemical imbalance framing was never as solid as it was presented. Studies comparing serotonin levels, serotonin metabolites, and serotonin transporter activity between depressed and healthy volunteers show flat, non-significant differences.

The analogy that makes this concrete: social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → disorder improves with alcohol. No one concludes that social anxiety is caused by an alcohol deficiency. The drug has an effect on the experience without that effect revealing anything about the underlying cause.

"I always say, what would your grandmother say? She would say people get depressed, demoralized, despairing, and hopeless when things are very hard, overwhelming, and exhaust your capacity to cope. And that's what the research shows."

The data that actually predicts depression most reliably: the number of stressful life events in the past year. Job loss, bereavement, financial precarity, relationship collapse — these correlate with depression onset in near-linear fashion. Neuroimaging findings and genetic markers, by contrast, show effects close to flat. One long-running New Zealand study followed a birth cohort for 45 years and found that 86% had met diagnostic criteria for a mental illness — mostly depression or anxiety — at some point. Depression, framed this way, looks less like a discrete pathology and more like a near-universal human response to hard enough circumstances.

What Depression Actually Is (And Isn't)

The diagnosis of Major Depressive Disorder was created in the 1980s when a committee of psychiatrists debated whether the threshold should be four symptoms, five, or six. One participant compared the process to a group of friends arguing over which restaurant to go to. The line between MDD and adjustment disorder — low mood tied to identifiable life circumstances — is blurrier than clinical training implies.

This doesn't mean suffering isn't real. It can feel like your brain is physically broken. Psychomotor retardation, inability to move, all-black thinking — these are genuine experiences. The argument isn't that the suffering is imaginary. The argument is about the level at which it makes sense to intervene.

The software/hardware analogy is useful here: Microsoft Word runs on circuits, but when it crashes you call the software team, not the circuit board technician. Of course the mind is instantiated in the brain — of course grief changes your neurochemistry — but that doesn't mean the right intervention is at the chemical level. When a friend tells you their mother just died, responding by ordering an amygdalaThe brain's negative emotional center — the region that generates fear, anxiety, and survival responses.Full definition → MRI would be sociopathic. The meaningful level of analysis is still: what is happening in this person's life?

An important wrinkle: resilience itself is a variable. Two people can face identical circumstances and respond very differently. This is why circumstances alone don't fully determine whether someone gets depressed — temperament, emotional needs, support structures, meaning, and coping capacity all matter. But the conclusion that individual variability implies a chemical cause is the logical leap that generated the serotonin hypothesis in the first place, and that leap has not held up.

How Antidepressants Actually Work

If the drugs aren't correcting a serotonin deficiency, what are they doing?

The most consistent finding across studies: emotional numbing. Somewhere between 50–70% of people on antidepressants report that both positive and negative emotions are flattened. A study giving healthy volunteers three weeks of escitalopram (Lexapro) demonstrated this numbing in people who weren't depressed at all — ruling out the idea that blunting is just a feature of the depression itself.

SSRIs affect the serotonin transporter in a manner similar to MDMA, only at a much lower dose. This is not a fringe claim — it's the basic pharmacology. Taking a small dose of a substance that acts somewhat like a recreational drug every day carries the same class of long-term concerns we associate with recreational drugs: tolerance, dependence, disrupted sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → architecture, impaired memory and concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition →, sexual dysfunction.

"If you're suicidal or panicked or anxious and you're given an antidepressant that has a mild numbing effect, you might feel a lot better. I'm not taking that away from anyone. But getting better in the short term may not mean getting better in the long term."

The disease-centered model says: the drug fixes a broken system. The drug-centered model says: the drug has a psychoactive effect that changes how you experience things, without necessarily addressing underlying causes. Both can produce the subjective report "I feel better." Only one of them tells you anything about what will happen in year five.

This also explains why antidepressants are prescribed for roughly 45 different conditions — depression, anxiety, pain, PMDD, fibromyalgia, post-stroke recovery. A drug with a highly specific mechanism (insulin for diabetes, antibiotics for a bacterial infection) doesn't work for 45 unrelated diagnoses. A drug with a non-specific numbing or blunting effect might produce some relief across all of them, without that breadth revealing anything about mechanism or cause.

The clinical implication: there are people for whom that numbing provides enough reduction in acute suffering to make therapy and life change accessible. That's a real use case. The problem is that the drug is often prescribed indefinitely, framed as treating a chronic illness, which it was never designed or proven to do — and which sets up the withdrawal problem described below.

Why Stopping Is So Much Harder Than Anyone Said

The guidelines in most English-speaking countries historically said antidepressants can be stopped over four weeks, halving the dose twice. That protocol came not from studies of tapering but from a study showing abrupt cessation was too fast — so a committee picked a number. The characterization of withdrawal as "mild and self-limiting" traces back to papers funded by pharmaceutical companies in the 1990s, written partly to distinguish antidepressants from benzodiazepines (which had acquired bad press for dependence), and the term "discontinuation symptoms" was coined specifically to replace the more accurate "withdrawal symptoms."

The actual pharmacology explains why the standard approach fails:

The dose-response curve is hyperbolic, not linear. The biggest effects on the brain occur at the smallest doses. Two milligrams of citalopram has roughly half the brain effect of twenty milligrams. This means:

  • Going from 20mg to 10mg is a small reduction in brain effect.
  • Going from 10mg to 5mg is a slightly larger one.
  • Going from 5mg to 0mg — which sounds tiny — throws the brain off a cliff.

Equal reductions in brain effect require increasingly small reductions in dose as you approach zero. This is called hyperbolic tapering: the steps get smaller and smaller toward the end, often requiring doses below one milligram. Achieving this often requires liquid formulations of the medication.

Withdrawal symptoms from antidepressants can include: low mood, anxiety, panic attacks, insomnia, crying spells, suicidal ideation, as well as physical symptoms like dizziness and brain zaps. Because these are psychological symptoms, they're frequently misread as relapse of the underlying condition — and the patient is put back on the medication. The data from studies used to justify long-term antidepressant use often involve abrupt cessation of the drug followed by mood monitoring, with no measurement of withdrawal symptoms. What looks like evidence that stopping causes relapse may largely be evidence that stopping causes withdrawal.

"You could do the same thing with cigarettes — you could say if people stop cigarettes they become anxious and irritable, therefore people must need cigarettes to prevent anxiety and irritability. That's absurd."

The UK's NICE guidelines and the Royal College of Psychiatrists have already updated their guidance to reflect this: taper over months or years, not weeks; reduce to very small final doses; adjust rate to the individual's experience of withdrawal symptoms. This has not yet reached North American guidelines.

Randomized controlled trials of hyperbolic tapering are still in progress. The existing evidence is observational and biological — strong basic science, growing clinical observation, no published RCTs yet. One Dutch group found that 85% of patients who had been unable to stop antidepressants using conventional tapering were able to stop using gradual compounded tapering strips.

Depression as Signal, Not Malfunction

One framing that shifts how to think about all of this: depression as a signal that something in your life needs to change, not a malfunction to be suppressed.

If you cover the signal — numb it down — the underlying conditions remain. For some people, that numbing provides enough relief to address those conditions. For many others, it simply extends the period of not addressing them, while the brain adapts to the drug and makes eventual stopping harder.

Therapy helps in part by allowing access to painful emotional material in a safe context, processing it, and releasing its hold. That mechanism requires the ability to feel the difficult thing — which is precisely what numbing agents reduce. Studies suggest that people on long-term antidepressants have a worse response to therapy than those not on them. The short-term studies (six to twelve weeks) don't capture this; the effect appears over longer timeframes.

The 19 treatments listed as equally effective and cost-effective to antidepressants in UK national guidelines include: mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition →, exercise, several forms of psychotherapy, and problem-solving therapy — which was the single most cost-effective treatment for severe depression in a nine-year government analysis.

What keeps suicidal people alive is rarely medication. In clinical experience, it's the people, animals, or responsibilities in their lives that make existence feel necessary. Common Pitfalls

Mistaking withdrawal for relapse. When you taper off an antidepressant and feel terrible a few weeks later, the instinct is to conclude the drug was necessary. This may be true — but it may also be withdrawal, especially if the symptoms include anxiety, panic, insomnia, and suicidality, which are common withdrawal presentations. Without careful symptom tracking and a slow-enough taper, these are easily confused.

Assuming "sub-therapeutic dose" means no effect. The word "therapeutic" in pharmacology refers to the dose the manufacturer got approved with regulators — not a chemical threshold below which nothing happens. Very small doses can have large effects on the brain.

Conflating short-term improvement with long-term benefit. Most antidepressant efficacy data comes from six-to-eight-week trials. Long-term outcomes are much murkier, and the side-effect and dependence profiles that emerge over years are not captured in those studies.

Treating the publication record as complete. Publication bias means negative studies frequently go unpublished. The evidence base for any treatment likely overstates its effectiveness because only positive results tend to make it into journals.

Thinking the easiest-to-study treatments are the best ones. Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you're considering starting an antidepressant, ask your prescriber specifically about the mechanism of action, expected effect size versus placebo, typical timeline for stopping, and what the withdrawal process looks like. Informed consent should include these facts before you begin.

  2. If you're currently on an antidepressant and want to stop, research hyperbolic tapering and bring the concept to your doctor. The key principle: reductions should get smaller as the dose gets smaller, often requiring liquid formulations to achieve sub-milligram steps. Go slowly enough that withdrawal symptoms are manageable — this may take months to years, not weeks.

  3. Track your symptoms against events. For two weeks, keep a simple log: what happened today, and how did you feel? This isn't therapy — it's data. Many people discover a clearer connection between life circumstances and mood than they previously recognized, which opens up a different class of interventions.

  4. Examine whether your depression is being treated at the right level. Ask yourself: Is there something in my life circumstances that I've been numbing rather than addressing? What would need to change — in relationships, work, meaning, environment — for my baseline to shift?

  5. Invest in finding purpose, not just managing symptoms. Identify one thing that gives you a reason to do something hard. It doesn't need to be grand. For someone suicidal, it might literally be a pet. Start there, and build upward. What competing interest could make the destructive behavior less necessary?

  6. If you've been told you have treatment-resistant depression, consider the possibility that the resistance is in the treatment approach, not your brain. Seek out a clinician who integrates psychotherapy, lifestyle, and careful medication review — not just medication escalation.

  7. Read the patient information leaflet for any psychiatric medication you're prescribed. Don't skip it because a clinician told you not to worry about it. It was written by people with legal and ethical obligations to tell you the truth.
