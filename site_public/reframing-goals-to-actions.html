Summary

TL;DR — Goals are things that happen; actions are things you do. Your brain can only reinforce the second kind.

Society trains you to be outcome-oriented — your résumé, your salary, your accomplishments are treated as your worth. The problem is that your brain's reinforcement system runs on visible feedback. When you set a big abstract goal like "get in shape" or "be sober for a year," your brain goes to the gym on day one, sees no change in the mirror, and quietly concludes the effort isn't paying off. Over time it sabotages your motivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition →. This is why New Year's resolutions die and why people stuck in life can still binge video games for hours — instant gratification gives the brain immediate proof that the behavior was worth it.

The reframe is simple but uncomfortable: you cannot "be sober for a year." That's not an action, it's an outcome. What you can do is say no to the drink being offered to you right now. The people who succeed at sobriety aren't the ones counting toward twelve months — they're the ones asking, what do I do in this moment?

"They're not worried about the outcome, they're focused on the action."

The Paralysis Exercise

Here's a concrete way to force this shift. Imagine you're paralyzed from the waist down and have an assistant who will do exactly what you tell them — but nothing more. They have no judgment and no initiative. Now describe how they would accomplish your goal.

"Find a job" is useless instruction. But this works: - Open the laptop - Navigate to the résumé file in this folder - Double-click it - Change the address on line one - Update the education section with these specific entries

Notice how many steps just updating a résumé actually contains. Each one is a discrete, completable action. Once your brain can see "résumé finished in one hour," it has a real reward to work toward — and that recruits the same circuitry that makes instant gratification so sticky.

The more abstract the goal stays, the more stuck you stay. The more you dissolve it into tiny sequential actions, the more your brain can actually work with you.

Action Items

  1. Pick one goal you've been stuck on.
  2. Write out every instruction you'd give a literal-minded assistant to accomplish it — specific enough that they couldn't misinterpret a single step.
  3. Do only the first action on that list today. Don't think about the full goal; just complete that one step.
