You Own Your Likeness — Full Stop

There's a basic principle in how society already operates: you own your own face, your voice, your image. Companies can't put your face on an advertisement without your permission. That's not a new or radical idea.

Deepfake pornography violates that principle directly. It takes someone's likeness and uses it for something they never consented to. The fact that someone is a public figure, a streamer, or a celebrity does not change this.

"It comes with the territory" is not an argument. What comes with the territory is what you actually signed up for.

Streaming on Twitch or building a YouTube audience is not consent to have pornographic content made about you. These are categorically different activities. The reasoning that fame or visibility licenses others to violate your rights isn't just logically weak — it's not how any other area of society actually works. Being wealthy doesn't mean people can steal from you. Being visible doesn't mean people can use your body without permission.

The Parasocial Trap — How Deepfakes Make It Worse

Here's the part that often gets missed: the people consuming this content are also being harmed.

Human bonding runs on a handful of mechanisms. Witnessing someone's genuine vulnerability, seeing their daily highs and lows, being acknowledged by them — these also create real feelings of connection.

Streamers and influencers, unlike traditional celebrities, deliver exactly that. You see them tired, frustrated, excited, embarrassed. They talk directly to camera, respond to chat, share personal moments. Your brain registers this as a real relationship because, neurologically, it uses the same machinery. This is the parasocial relationship — a one-sided bond that feels mutual.

The problem is what it can't give you back:

  • It doesn't listen to your vulnerabilities
  • There's no physical presence, no touch
  • The "other person" will never actually know you

And yet it scratches enough of the itch that people keep coming back. Over time, this can make genuine relationships feel harder — more effortful, less reliable. The parasocial substitute becomes a crutch, which makes real connection even more difficult, which makes the substitute feel even more necessary. It's a cycle, and it traps people.

Deepfake pornography accelerates this cycle dramatically. It takes an already addictive parasocial bond and adds a physical, sexual dimension. Now the brain has checked even more boxes — intellectual familiarity, emotional pseudo-intimacy, and sensory gratification — all tied to a person who doesn't actually know you exist.

This is part of why this content is paywalled and sells — it's more addicting than ordinary pornography, precisely because of the parasocial layer on top. Where the Cruelty Comes From

A lot of the dismissive, hostile responses to victims of non-consensual pornography come disproportionately from men. That pattern isn't random, and it isn't simply explained by malice.

When someone is suffering and the most common response you've ever received for your own suffering is "stop complaining," "you're privileged," "that comes with the territory" — you learn that pain doesn't deserve acknowledgment. That lesson doesn't just make you stoic. It makes you resentful. And when someone else shows up expressing pain, you reflexively say the same thing back to them.

Hurt people hurt people.

The people most likely to dismiss victims of deepfakes or revenge porn are often the same people who have been told their own pain doesn't matter — and weren't given any tools to process that. The resentment calcifies, and it gets redirected at whoever seems to be getting sympathy they were never given.

This matters practically: if you're a man who struggles with lonelinessA painful emotional state in which you feel disconnected from other people — distinct from simply being physically alone.Full definition →, failed relationships, or feeling like the world is against you, the reflexive lack of empathy is not just morally wrong — it is actively part of why connection keeps failing. Compassion isn't a strategic tool, but it's worth recognizing that emotional unavailability and contempt drive people away. The problem isn't "women don't give me a chance." The problem is often the internal emotional state that contempt reflects.

The Wider Spiral

Zoom out and there's a feedback loop operating at scale.

As certain communities teach men emotional manipulation tactics ("game," pickup techniques, red-pill frameworks), women's mistrust of men increases — adaptively, rationally. That increased mistrust then feels like rejection to men who weren't using those tactics, deepening their resentment. Which pushes more men toward those communities. Which increases manipulation. Which increases mistrust. And so on.

No one in this loop is thriving. Women are being manipulated and violated. Men are lonely, emotionally stunted, and increasingly isolated. And technologies like deepfakes are accelerating the damage by making parasocial addictionSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition → cheaper and more potent, while simultaneously brutalizing the people who become targets of it.

The human brain was built for a social world of roughly 150–300 people. When hatred or sexual humiliation scales to tens of thousands of simultaneous sources, there is no psychological immune system for that. That's why the psychiatric outcomes are so severe.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Check your dismissal reflex. The next time you feel the urge to say someone "shouldn't" feel hurt or "signed up for" what happened to them, pause and ask: where did I learn that pain doesn't deserve acknowledgment? Is that a rule I actually believe, or one I absorbed defensively?

  2. Audit your parasocial relationships. Make a list of creators, streamers, or influencers you feel genuinely close to. Then honestly assess: do those feelings of connection substitute for real relationships? Are they making it easier or harder to pursue actual intimacy and friendship?

  3. Notice the asymmetry. In your parasocial relationships, map out what you know about that person versus what they know about you. That asymmetry is a useful reality check on how "real" the relationship is.

  4. If you consume deepfake or non-consensual pornography, ask what it's actually doing for you. Not morally — practically. Is your life genuinely better because of it? Or is it filling a need that it can never fully satisfy, while making other needs harder to meet?

  5. Practice extending compassion to someone you disagree with or resent. This is genuinely hard. It doesn't mean agreeing with them or excusing harm. It means recognizing that people express cruelty from pain, and responding to the pain rather than matching the cruelty.

  6. If you're a man struggling with lonelinessA painful emotional state in which you feel disconnected from other people — distinct from simply being physically alone.Full definition → or repeated rejection, consider getting real support. Parasocial relationships, deepfake content, and online communities that frame women as adversaries are all substitutes that worsen the underlying problem.
