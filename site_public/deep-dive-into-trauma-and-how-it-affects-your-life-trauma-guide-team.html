Why Getting Emotionally Aware Can Make You Feel Worse (And What to Do About It)

Once you start doing emotional work — journaling, therapy, introspection — you may notice that you feel more sad, not less. And then the thoughts loop. Nothing seems to resolve. This is a real and common stall point.

The key distinction here is between awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → and processing. Awareness means you've uncovered the emotion. Processing means you've actually moved through it. These are not the same thing, and most of what society tells you to do (journal, talk about it, validate it) gets you to awareness without giving you the tools for what comes next.

Perseveration — just repeating the same painful material in your head — does not heal anything. The emotion doesn't dissolve from being revisited. It needs to be met with something that changes it.

Three things that actually move the needle:

  1. Cognitive reframingDeliberately shifting how you interpret an event to change its emotional impact — not by forcing positivity, but by questioning whether your automatic reading of the situation is actually accurate.Full definition →. Changing the story you tell about what happened. Not pretending it wasn't bad, but finding an interpretation that opens a path forward rather than closing one. "I missed the boat, so I'm screwed" and "I missed the boat, so I have to make up for lost time" are both reasonable conclusions from the same facts — but they lead to completely different futures.

  2. Somatic and movement-based approaches. Trauma is physiological, not just mental. Hiking (especially alone, in nature), yoga, tai chi, breathwork, and EMDR all work by interrupting the automatic link between a trigger and its physiological response. When you're physically active while thinking the difficult thoughts, the nervous system gets a different message. The emotion can move through without triggering the full spiral.

  3. Working with another person. A therapist or coach can do the cognitive reframing work with you — helping you see from outside your own loop in a way that's very hard to do alone.

Nature counters this through plant aerosols that chemically induce relaxation when they hit your olfactory receptors, through physical activity that uses up the stored stress energy instead of letting it spin in your mind, and through deeper breathing patterns that reverse anxiety-induced hyperventilation.

How to Cry Again When You've Lost the Ability

If you haven't been able to cry in years, the block is almost always learned — and usually learned from pain. Something happened that taught you crying was dangerous, shameful, or useless.

A few ways back in:

  • Let someone validate you without you intellectualizing it away. Notice when you rationalize ("it is what it is"), change the subject, or get suddenly curious about something tangential. Those are your escape routes. Stay in the emotional lane.
  • Use sad music or film. Pay attention to the exact moment you feel yourself suppressing the natural response. That moment of suppression is the thing to soften.
  • Find privacy. A lot of the block is shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. Being alone removes the audience.
  • Think back to when you learned not to cry. Then sit with how genuinely sad it is that the younger version of you — already hurting — was surrounded by people who punished them for expressing that hurt.

"It's bad enough that you're hurting, but when people punish you for hurting, that's even sadder."

That last step isn't a trick. It's real. And it can crack something open.

The Real Reason Self-Compassion Is So Hard

Self-compassion fails most often not because people don't know they should have it, but because they're too angry at themselves to allow it.

The logic is airtight from the inside: your life is in a bad place, and you made the choices that led here, so you're to blame, so you don't deserve kindness. Arguing with that logic doesn't work — you can't convince someone they deserve compassion when they feel certain they don't.

The bypass: stop arguing about what you deserve, and ask a different question. What kind of relationship do I want with myself? Do you want to hold yourself in contempt indefinitely because of mistakes from years ago? Is that a life sentence you actually want to impose?

"It's not about what they deserve. Can I be kind to this person even though they don't deserve it? Kindness is going beyond what people deserve."

The fear underneath — that allowing even a little self-compassion will turn you into someone pathetic and self-pitying — comes from the sheer amount of emotion you've been holding back. It feels like if you open the dam one inch, the whole thing floods. But floods don't last forever. The emotion will eventually equilibrate. The fear of what's behind the dam is bigger than what's actually there, precisely because you've been working so hard to contain it.

How to Rebuild a Sense of Self

Trauma hollows out identity by severing you from your own emotional experience. Without emotional engagement, experiences don't stick as formative. You become a chameleon — picking up whoever's in the room, feeling okay when someone treats you well, devastated when they don't, with no stable internal reference point.

Identity forms through two things:

1. Emotionally charged experience. Not routine — the mundane doesn't build identity. What builds identity is being tested emotionally and discovering what you're made of. DissociationA protective fragmentation of the self in which the brain severs communication between its emotional and analytical hemispheres — numbing feeling so that overwhelming experience becomes survivable.Full definition → cuts you off from this. You have to stop dissociating, which means tolerating — and staying with — your emotions instead of suppressing them.

2. Narrative. The story you tell about your life, its arc, where you've come from and where you're going. Research on trauma consistently shows that healing involves constructing a narrative — making meaning from what happened, not just cataloguing it.

The Japanese art of kintsugi — repairing broken pottery with gold so the cracks become part of the object's beauty — is a useful frame. Being broken is one piece of who you are. It's only a problem if it's the only piece. Adding the pieces of survivor, of someone who rebuilt, of someone whose difficult path produced something unique — that's what makes a whole identity.

"You can't have a redemption arc without being broken. And the redemption arc is really what makes you who you are."

A low GPA can become a more interesting story than a perfect one, if it's part of a real arc. The brokenness isn't the enemy of identity — the absence of the rest of the story is.

Trauma's Effect on Future-Planning

One largely overlooked consequence of childhood trauma is the atrophying of the ability to plan for the future. When you grow up in an environment where someone else controls your life unpredictably, planning becomes genuinely useless. There's no point wanting things that can be taken away without warning. So the brain stops doing it.

The problem is that the brain doesn't automatically restart this capacity once you're out of the traumatic environment. It never learned to. It was optimized for survival today, not investment in tomorrow. This shows up later as an inability to set goals, stick to plans, or feel genuinely motivated by future outcomes — even when the person intellectually understands why those things matter.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking awareness for processing. Journaling, talking, and uncovering feelings are a start, not a finish. Without reframing, somatic work, or someone to help you, you can loop in the awareness stage indefinitely.
  • Assuming "ignorance is bliss." Emotional suppression doesn't produce bliss — it produces numbness on all frequencies, including joy. And it still costs you.
  • Thinking self-compassion requires deserving it. This framing guarantees you'll never get there. Compassion is, by definition, giving more than what's deserved.
  • Treating trauma as purely mental. Trauma lives in the body. Talking alone — especially for people who process physically rather than verbally — may not be enough without somatic intervention.
  • Fearing the flood. The terror that opening up even slightly will release everything at once keeps people permanently sealed off. The flood, when it comes, passes. Emotions equilibrate — that's what they do.
  • Confusing self-blame with responsibility. You're not at fault for what happened to you. You are responsible for whether you stay stuck in its pattern. Those are different things, and only the second one gives you any power.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your suppression moves. In your next emotionally charged conversation, watch for when you rationalize ("it is what it is"), intellectualize, change the subject, or get suddenly curious about something else. Those are exits from the emotion. Practice staying in it for thirty more seconds.

  2. Try emotion-plus-movement processing. Next time you're carrying a heavy or looping thought, take it outside. Walk, hike, or exercise while allowing yourself to think about it. Don't force insight — just let the body be active while the mind holds the material.

  3. Work on one cognitive reframe. Pick one belief about your past that forecloses a future (e.g., "I missed my chance, so I'm behind forever"). Write down one alternative interpretation that's equally true but opens a path instead of closing one.

  4. Trace the moment you stopped crying (or stopped feeling). Sit quietly and ask: when's the last time I cried? What happened around that time? What did I learn about what crying or feeling meant? Then sit with how hard that must have been for the younger version of you — no argument, no fixing, just acknowledgment.

  5. Audit your relationship with yourself. For one day, notice every time you speak to yourself in a way you would never speak to someone you care about. Don't try to fix it yet — just observe the pattern and see what it actually looks like.

  6. Ask the identity question differently. Instead of "who am I?", ask: "What are the moments in my life that shaped me, and what did I learn about myself in them?" Write them down, including the painful ones. Look at the arc they form. That arc is the beginning of narrative.

  7. Explore a somatic intervention. If you haven't tried breathwork (particularly slow, deep triphasic breathing), yoga, or tapping (EFT), give one of them a sustained try — not one session, but a few weeks. Pay attention to how it affects your baseline emotional state, not just the moment itself.
