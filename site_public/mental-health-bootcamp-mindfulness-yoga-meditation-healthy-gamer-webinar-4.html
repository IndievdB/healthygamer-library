Why Yoga Exists (It's Not About Flexibility)

Yoga's real purpose is to anchor your mind in the present moment so you can't think about anything else. It is preparation for meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →, not an end in itself.

The word yoga comes from the Sanskrit root yuj, meaning union or yoke. It points toward two things simultaneously: the process of becoming free from what binds you to ordinary life, and the process of joining your individual consciousness to a larger, infinite consciousness (called Brahman in Hindu philosophy).

When you're doing poses, the body becomes an anchor. There's a sweet spot you're looking for:

  • Too easy — your mind wanders
  • Too hard — you can't sustain it

The target is a posture that's demanding enough to hold your attention in the present but stable enough to maintain. The measure of whether you're doing yoga correctly isn't how straight your back is or how far you reach. It's entirely this: what is your mind doing?

What Meditation Actually Is

Attention can go in two directions: outward into the senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste) or inward into thought. These two are in a roughly antagonistic relationship — when you're deep in thought, your sensory experience dulls; when you're fully engaged with a sensation, thoughts quiet down.

PratyaharaThe fifth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed yoga, pratyahara is the deliberate withdrawal of attention away from external sensory objects and back inward.Full definition → works by systematically withdrawing from the senses one by one. Yoga starts this process by closing the eyes and turning attention to bodily sensation. As each channel of outward attention closes, what's left?

The answer resists language, which is partly the point. One way to describe it: the state is advaita — non-dual, without qualities. Not good or bad, not past or future, not pleasant or painful. Just is-ness. It is a state that you have to experience to understand, the same way you can't fully understand being underwater from a verbal description.

MindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition →, by the way, is an early step toward this — not the destination. It's the practice of noticing that self and mind are separate things; that you are not your thoughts. That gap between the observer and the observed is the beginning of the path, not the end of it.

Action Items & Exercises

  2. Try the "charging the laser beam" practice. Hold your middle finger half an inch from the center of your forehead, between the eyebrows. Don't touch. Hold it there for 30–60 seconds, then lower the hand and keep your attention on that spot. Do this three times before sitting to meditate.
