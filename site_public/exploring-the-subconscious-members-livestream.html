Why You Keep Repeating the Same Patterns

The unconscious forms conclusions from experience the same way a scientist forms hypotheses — except you don't get to review the data or challenge the methodology. Three bad experiences become "I am a loser." That generalization then filters every subsequent experience to confirm itself. A square-shaped piece of evidence enters your mind and comes out triangular, because your unconscious has learned to reshape squares into triangles.

This is why people don't learn from their mistakes. The mistake isn't being made by the conscious self who "knows better." It's being generated by an unconscious that has its own theory of reality and bends incoming data to fit.

Relationship patterns are the clearest example. An anxiously attached person and an avoidantly attached person don't stay together because they like each other. Why Psychology Alone Can't Fix This (And What Can)

Here's a structural problem with conventional psychology: it was developed by external observers studying other people. Psychological defense mechanisms are described in the language of outside observation. But the only person who can actually see your mind is you.

This creates a fundamental translation problem. Scientific psychology, by design, tries to remove subjectivity — large sample sizes, controls, elimination of individual variation. But when you're trying to change yourself, your individuality is the entire point. The thing science eliminates is exactly what you need.

This is partly why contemplative traditions (yoga, meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition →, Vedic practice) can be more effective for inner work than conventional psychology as a self-help tool. These systems were built from the inside out — they give the practitioner techniques to observe and work on their own mind, without needing an external therapist to do the work for them.

Psychotherapy still works — but it works by doing, slowly and externally, what these internal practices do directly.

Your Mental Diet Is Programming You

Every moment you spend consuming content, you are being conditioned. This is not metaphor. The inputs your unconscious receives become the conclusions it draws, the motivations it generates, and the reality it constructs.

Radicalization through the internet follows this mechanism exactly: feed a person a certain diet of salient content, and their unconscious will build a worldview from it — a worldview that feels completely self-evident to them, because they didn't notice it being installed. Governments and platforms have known this for a long time. What's changed is that most individuals have lost conscious awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → that it's happening.

Spending four to six hours daily on a phone is not procrastination. It is programming. The unconscious doesn't distinguish "real life" from "content." It learns from all of it equally.

This also works positively. Immersive environments — a writer's workshop, a peer support meeting, sustained exposure to people who embody what you want to become — shift your inputs and thereby shift your unconscious. Sobriety correlates with meeting attendance. Creativity correlates with reading volume. The solution to writer's block, according to one mentor quoted in the talk, is simply: read more.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming willpower can override the unconscious. It can't. Willpower is a conscious tool fighting an unconscious juggernaut. The goal is to work with the unconscious mechanisms, not to muscle past them.

Thinking "my experience is my truth" is the end of inquiry.
