The Mystery of Symptoms That Shouldn't Still Be There

Loan's story starts with a breakup at 21. The stress of it triggered a cascade of physical symptoms — brain fog, eczema, restless leg syndrome, heart racing, cold-induced itching (cold urticaria), early morning waking. Standard stuff for acute stress. What's strange is that the stress resolved. His ex moved on, got married, and Loan genuinely doesn't care. But the symptoms never went away.

This is the puzzle he's been trying to solve for six years: why do physical symptoms persist after the emotional cause is supposedly gone?

The tempting answer — "it's all in your head" — is both partially true and deeply misleading. What's actually happening involves psychoneuroimmunology: the study of how emotional states trigger real physiological changes. Chronic, unprocessed emotional stress releases cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition →, which causes demarginalization of white blood cells — essentially putting the immune system on high alert with nothing to fight. The immune system then starts attacking the body itself. It's like pulling the pin on a grenade and then just holding it.

The reason Loan's symptoms persist isn't that the original stressor is still there. It's that the emotional patterns that were activated then — suppression, toning things down, not fully processing what he feels — never changed. The grenade is still live.

How Emotions Get Learned — and What Happens When They Don't

A child doesn't know whether a fall is serious or funny until they look at a parent's face. If the parent rushes over in alarm, the child cries. If the parents laugh, the child laughs. The caregiver's emotional mirroringThe ability to literally feel what another person is feeling, rather than simply understanding it intellectually.Full definition → teaches the child what their own internal states mean.

Loan describes growing up in a household where his parents were largely absent or emotionally disengaged — not abusive, just not there in an emotionally reflective way. No one asked how his day was. Nothing good or bad was worth reporting. His mom's solution to his current suffering is "just relax and watch TV." When he corrects her, she just repeats it.

The result: Loan never really learned to read his own emotional states from the outside in. He has a internal experience, but the machinery for labeling, trusting, and expressing it was never fully built.

This connects to something concrete: validation. Loan uses the word several times. What he means is: I send something out into the world and I need the world to send something back. His doctors respond to his complex suffering with "let's adjust your meds." His therapist responds to his existential confusion by trying to diagnose him. His mom responds to his pain with dismissal. The pattern of his life has been: send something out, get nothing or the wrong thing back.

The Neutral Tone and What It's Hiding

One of the most telling patterns in the conversation is Loan's language. Nothing is great. Nothing is terrible. Things are "fine," "okay," "kind of a nightmare," "I guess I was crushed." He qualifies extreme words immediately after using them. He ends statements with "I don't know" or "maybe."

"I guess I was crushed."

This is not how people talk about genuinely resolved experiences. It's how people talk when they've learned — consciously or not — that their emotional reactions are too big, too inconvenient, or not welcome. The toning-down happens fast, almost automatically. The initial response surfaces and then something intercepts it.

This shows up physically too. When asked to smile big, Loan says "I don't want to do that." When he does smile for a moment, he reins it in immediately. He constrains his laugh the same way. Even when Twitch chat says something absurd and catches him off guard, a real smile breaks through — and then he pulls it back.

The suppression isn't just social shyness. It's a deep-seated pattern of not letting the emotion complete its arc.

The Social Catch-22

Here's where the dating and friendship problems become clearer. Connection between two people happens through shared emotional experience — the moment you and a friend are both outraged at the same bad teammate, or both laughing at the same stupid thing. That co-resonance is the glue.

Loan can't easily provide that. Not because he doesn't feel things — he does — but because he doesn't freely express them. When someone tells a joke he finds mildly funny, he doesn't give them the reinforcement they're looking for. They sense it and deflate. He notices their deflation (he's perceptive), interprets it as rejection, and retreats.

"You leave people hanging. They'll express and you're uncomfortable expressing with them, and then they're like, well, this guy doesn't think my joke is funny — when you think it's hilarious. You just feel embarrassed to laugh."

The people who get through to him are "oblivious extroverts" — people whose social momentum is high enough that they don't register his muted responses and just keep going. That creates space for Loan to warm up slowly, which he can do. But most people aren't oblivious extroverts, so most interactions stall.

Dating has the same structure, amplified. The women he ends up in situationships with are ones he's not that into — safe, because he won't have to be fully emotionally present. The ones he would want to be fully present with, he hasn't been able to get or keep. And when rejection comes, the suppression kicks in, the ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → starts, and the body registers what the mind won't fully acknowledge.

Visceral Hypersensitivity and the Isolation Loop

There's another mechanism worth understanding: visceral hypersensitivity. People with psychosomatic problems often have the volume turned way up on internal bodily signals. The more isolated and internally focused you become, the louder those signals get — because they're the only signals available. The more you stay home, the worse the eczema feels. The more you engage with the outside world, the more external stimuli compete with and drown out those internal signals.

This means social isolation isn't just emotionally painful — it physiologically amplifies the very symptoms that make going out feel hard. The loop closes.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking the original traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → is the problem. Loan spent time in therapy focused on the 21-year-old breakup. But he genuinely got over that. Chasing the original stressor misses the point when the patterns activated by it are still running.

Taking "it's all in your head" literally. This framing is almost always unhelpful and often wrong. The physiology is real. The symptoms are real. What's also real is that emotional processing has documented effects on immune function, and that's not the same as saying it's imaginary.

Assuming self-awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → means emotional fluency. Loan describes himself as self-aware — and he is, in some ways. He notices patterns in his behavior, tracks his social dynamics, understands he's making calculations. But awareness of behavior is not the same as awareness of emotion. You can be perceptive about everything except what you're feeling in a given moment.

Seeking a single diagnosis or answer. Loan has tried this. His therapist tried to diagnose him with something. No single label fits. This is frustrating but also useful — it means the interventions are multiple small levers, not one big switch.

Confusing "I'm entitled to my feelings" with "my feelings should run my life." Both the "validate everything" camp and the "push through everything" camp are incomplete. The real skill is learning to feel the emotion fully and choose your behavior in relation to it — not suppressing it, not being enslaved by it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do a nightly emotional rewind. At the end of each day, sit quietly and walk backward through the day chronologically — from right now back to when you woke up. Note what you felt at each point. Don't organize or analyze. Just inventory. The goal is to stop emotions from accumulating unacknowledged.

  2. Catch the initial response before it gets toned down. When something happens and you feel a reaction, notice what that first response is before the rational mind qualifies it. That initial flicker — the slight stomach drop, the impulse to say something, the flash of anger or sadness — is the data. Practice not immediately walking it back.

  3. Practice smiling genuinely in low-stakes moments. This isn't fake positivity. The exercise is to find something you actually find good — a memory, something on screen, a person you like — and let the smile happen without pulling it back. Practice not reining in the expression once it starts.

  4. Use the CPAP consistently. Sleep quality is upstream of mood, irritability, immune function, and emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition →. Apneic episodes are genuinely disrupting the physiological environment everything else depends on.

  5. Track what state you're in — internalizing or externalizing. On worse days, notice whether your attention has turned inward (toward your body, your thoughts, how you're being perceived). Deliberately shift outward — ask someone else a question, notice your environment. This isn't avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →; it's interrupting the loop that amplifies symptoms.

  6. Find a therapist whose goal matches yours. If your therapist is focused on finding a diagnostic label and you want to work on specific problems — emotional expression, social connection, understanding your physical symptoms — those goals are misaligned. Shop for fit, not credentials.

  7. Notice the pattern with "oblivious extroverts" and learn from it. The reason those friendships work is that you get time to warm up before your muted responses deflate them. See if you can manufacture that same runway intentionally — get into situations where there's a shared task or activity (a game, a project, a class) so connection can develop alongside action rather than purely through conversation.
