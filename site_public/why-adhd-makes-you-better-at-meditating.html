Summary

TL;DR — ADHD brains aren't bad at meditation; they're bad at the watered-down version of meditation most apps sell. The advanced, exploratory form of meditation that Himalayan masters actually practice turns the core traits of ADHD into genuine advantages.

Most people with ADHD have tried meditation, found it painfully boring, and concluded that something is wrong with their mind. That conclusion is understandable but backward. The real problem is that the meditation most of us have access to — breath-focused mindfulness apps, guided relaxation tracks — was designed by and for neurotypical brains. It's a stripped-down commercial product, not the real thing. Once you understand what meditation actually looks like in its more complete form, the ADHD brain stops being a liability and starts being an asset.

How Modern Mindfulness Got Watered Down

The mindfulness explosion that produced every app and corporate wellness program traces back to the Zen Buddhist tradition — an originally rigorous, even harsh, system. Zen masters used to walk around monasteries striking disciples with bamboo switches without warning, sometimes for months. The point wasn't cruelty; it was to force a student into genuine acceptance. You can't anticipate every blow, you can't retaliate, and eventually you realize the only sane response is to stop bracing — to treat the stick like a mosquito bite. That's non-attachment, learned through lived experience rather than conceptual understanding.

That practice would never survive today's subscription economy. So what emerged is a cleaned-up, inoffensive version: sit down, observe your breath, repeat. It's fine for neurotypical users. For anyone whose brain craves novelty, it's quietly torturous — and when those people drop off the app, they assume they failed.

"The meditation that we practice today is actually a watered-down version of a tradition that used to be so much more robust. This is like the difference between a cocktail and a mocktail."

The incentives made it worse. Meditation apps compete for subscribers. If the practice feels too hard, people cancel. So the practice gets easier, blander, and further from anything that produces real depth. A Zen master smacking you with a stick has no financial stake in whether you stay — they care about your actual development.

What Meditation in Its Advanced Form Actually Is

The people who have spent decades meditating in Himalayan caves are not watching their breath for thirty years. They are exploring an internal universe — and that universe turns out to be effectively infinite.

The practice works like this: you notice a thought, an emotion, or a sensation, and instead of labeling it and letting it go (as mindfulness instructs), you follow it inward. Where did this come from? What is underneath that? What is underneath that? You can trace a passing craving for food back through sense perception, through habit, through something you've never consciously examined before. The exploration never bottoms out.

"They're not sitting there doing the same rote thing over and over. They're literally out there — or in there — exploring the vastness of the internal universe."

This is closer to what a skilled wine taster does than to what a factory worker does. A factory worker repeats the same motion indefinitely. A taster closes their eyes, concentrates, and keeps discovering finer and finer distinctions — oak, fruit, something floral. Advanced meditators are doing the same thing with the texture of consciousness itself. The deeper you go, the more there is to find.

Why the ADHD Brain Is Built for This

Two traits that make standard mindfulness miserable for ADHD people are exactly what advanced, exploratory meditation requires.

1. Love of novelty. The ADHD brain gets bored doing the same thing repeatedly — which is a disaster if meditation means watching your breath for twenty minutes. But exploratory meditation never does the same thing twice. Every session goes somewhere new. Every layer you uncover reveals another layer underneath. The novelty is endless, and the ADHD brain latches onto it hard.

2. Heightened sensitivity (not "distractibility"). The clinical framing calls this distractibility, which makes it sound like a malfunction. Reframe it: the ADHD brain is highly perceptive — it picks up subtle signals that a neurotypical brain filters out entirely. That's a disadvantage when you're trying to read a textbook and someone drops a pen across the room. It's a massive advantage when you're trying to detect increasingly fine gradations of internal sensation. Neurotypical practitioners often take much longer to sense what's being pointed at; ADHD practitioners, once properly oriented, can advance faster precisely because their sensitivity is already calibrated high.

A Practice That Actually Works: Exploratory Om Chanting

Rather than breath observation, a practice well-suited to this approach is om chanting with the goal of internal exploration. The point is not to complete a number of repetitions as a task — the ADHD brain hates that framing. The point is to discover something you haven't noticed before.

Technically, "om" moves through three sounds: an open-mouth ah, a half-open uh, and a closed mmm — representing the full range of sound the mouth can produce. Each round takes roughly 9–12 seconds.

Here's how to engage with it:

  1. Sit upright, close your eyes, and take a deep breath.
  2. Chant one full round slowly, moving from open mouth to closed.
  3. Instead of focusing on the sound, track the vibration inside your body — where do you feel the ah? Where does the uh resonate? Where does the mmm travel?
  4. Notice that the ah and uh tend to resonate in the chest and throat, while the mmm moves from the jaw upward through the head.
  5. With each subsequent round, try to detect something you missed the last time — a finer vibration, a slightly different location, a quality you couldn't name before.

The experience changes substantially as rounds accumulate. The first few rounds, the mind is still settling. By the 15th or 20th round, you're detecting things the first round gave no access to. A practical progression:

  • Start with 7 rounds
  • Move to 9, then 21, then 33 as the practice becomes familiar

Thirty-three rounds sounds like a lot. It isn't a chore if your mind is genuinely exploring — and the difference between round one and round thirty-three, in terms of what you can perceive, is striking.

Common Pitfalls

Treating it like a task to complete. Counting down to a finish line — "five more and I'm done" — kills the practice. The frame should always be what can I discover, not when can I stop.

Giving up before the practice deepens. The first several rounds of any session feel flat, even for experienced practitioners. That's normal. The interesting perceptual territory opens up further in, not at the start.

Concluding "I don't feel anything." Early on, the vibrations are subtle. The instruction isn't to find something dramatic immediately — it's to keep refining your attention until something appears. Like wine tasting, it takes a few attempts before the distinctions become legible.

Thinking more rounds automatically means better outcomes. The number matters less than the quality of attention. Thirty-three distracted rounds is worse than ten genuinely exploratory ones.

Assuming that because standard mindfulness didn't work, meditation itself doesn't work. These are different things. The app is not the tradition.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try one round of exploratory om chanting right now. Sit upright, close your eyes, take a breath, and chant one slow ah-uh-mmm lasting about 10 seconds. Don't assess the sound — assess where you feel vibration in your body.

  2. Do a 7-round session this week. After each round, pause for five seconds and ask: did I notice anything different that time? Write down anything that surprised you, even if it feels trivial.

  3. Reframe "distracted" as "sensitive." When your attention gets pulled somewhere during a practice session, treat it as your high-sensitivity perception doing its job. Gently redirect it back — not as a failure to correct, but as perception being re-aimed.

  4. Progress the round count gradually. Once 7 rounds feel natural, move to 9. Once 9 feel natural, move to 21, then 33. Don't jump ahead; let each level consolidate.

  5. Apply the exploratory frame to any emotion this week. When you feel a strong reaction — frustration, craving, anxiety — close your eyes and follow it inward. Ask: where did this come from? What is underneath it? Don't try to fix or label it. Just look one layer deeper than you usually do.

  6. Notice the difference between early and late rounds. In a longer session (21+), pause briefly after round 7 and note your internal state. Pause again after round 21. The contrast itself teaches you something about how this kind of attention actually works.
