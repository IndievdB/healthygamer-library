Summary

TL;DR — You can't control outcomes, only actions. Most people operate on an implicit belief: if I take the right action, I will get the right result. When the result doesn't come, they blame themselves. This creates a negative feedback loop where disappointment leads to inaction, which leads to worse outcomes, which leads to more shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. Karma, understood correctly, breaks that loop — not by excusing laziness, but by clarifying what you actually control and what you don't.

The Universe Tends Toward Order — Even Where You Can't See It

A useful starting point is the history of science. Five thousand years ago, lightning was Zeus. Disease was divine punishment. These were things humans had no framework to understand, so they were filed under "chaos" or "god's will." Over time, science colonized those territories. What looked like chaos turned out to have structure.

The pattern keeps repeating: wherever humans have actually looked closely at something that seemed random, they've found order. This has happened with physics, chemistry, biology, psychology, and even behavioral economics and advertising. The domain of "unexplainable chaos" keeps shrinking.

This matters because the hard line many people draw — between things that can be explained and things that can't — is probably artificial. Karma is essentially the claim that cause and effect applies universally, not just to billiard balls and chemical reactions. It's Newton's third law extended to everything: every action has consequences, whether or not you can trace them.

Genetics is one of the clearest modern examples of this principle. A hundred and fifty years ago, the idea that who you are today is heavily shaped by things that happened before you were born would have sounded mystical. Now it's textbook biology. Genetics bridges the gap between "what happened before" and "what's happening now" in a way that's scientifically legible. If one such bridge exists, it makes sense to stay open to the possibility that others do too.

Karma Is Cultivation, Not Creation

Here's the core idea, stated plainly: you cannot make a plant grow. You can only water it.

You can put a seed in good soil, give it sunlight, water it consistently — and it might not grow. There are forces at work beyond your direct control. Your past experiences, your upbringing, your genetics, the circumstances you were born into — all of these are "prior karmas" that shape what's possible for you right now, often in ways you can't fully see.

This is not fatalism. It's precision about what you're actually responsible for.

When you don't pass a test, or can't bring yourself to leave the house, or keep skipping the gym — the usual move is to call yourself a failure and pile on blame. But that logic only holds if you had full control over the outcome. If someone skipped a test due to anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → around failure, that anxiety didn't appear from nowhere. It came from years of accumulated experience — neglectful parents, traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, social failure, never being taught how to handle pressure. The person in that moment is the product of all those prior causes. Blaming them entirely is like blaming someone for not having fruit trees in a garden where nothing was ever planted.

"Going to class is not about getting a grade. Going to class is about providing a buff for future you."

This reframe — from "I want to graduate" to "I want to set myself up to graduate" — is small in wording and large in effect. When you're attached to the fruit, every disappointing result breaks your motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →. When you're focused on the seed, the action itself becomes the point.

Detaching from Outcomes Goes Both Ways

There's a symmetry here that's easy to miss. If you let go of blame for your failures, consistency requires that you also let go of pride in your successes.

Advantages compound invisibly. Growing up in a household where certain concepts were spoken about naturally, having parents who could financially support you during your twenties, having access to particular communities or bodies of knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → — these are "race bonuses," factors that accelerate growth that had nothing to do with personal virtue or effort. Acknowledging them doesn't diminish what you've done; it just gives you an accurate picture of how things actually work.

Free Will and Destiny Aren't Actually in Conflict

A common objection: "If I'm not responsible for my results, doesn't that mean nothing matters? Can't I just do whatever I want?"

Karma resolves the free will vs. determinism debate cleanly. It's your choice whether to jump off a cliff. Once you jump, you're going to fall — that part is determined. Both things are true simultaneously.

Applied to daily life: you couldn't have done better than you did in the past, given who you were and what you were working with. That's genuinely not your fault. But that doesn't carry over to today. Today is a new day. The seeds you plant now are yours to plant. Whether they grow is not fully yours to control, but whether you plant is.

"The past and the future are not tied together. Today is a different day, and the seeds you sow today will shape what's possible for future you."

Responsibility lives with actions, not results. A "responsible person" is usually described by outcomes — mortgage, job, family. But that's the wrong definition. Responsibility is about what you do, not what happens.

Habits Are Karma Made Automatic

HabitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → and karma aren't competing frameworks. A habit is one of the fruits that karma can bear.

When you study at the library every day, that's a karmic action. If you sustain it long enough, one of the effects is that studying becomes easier — it requires less willpower, it becomes more automatic. That's the habit. But the habit is itself then a cause: it shapes whether you show up to class, whether you perform well on tests, whether you graduate. Effects become causes, which produce new effects.

You can't "have" a habit going forward. You can only observe one in retrospect — "I notice I've been doing this consistently without forcing it." You sow karmic actions in the present. The habit is what you find when you look back.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "it's not my fault" with "nothing is my fault." Releasing blame for the past doesn't excuse inaction in the present. These are logically separate. The past is not your fault. What you do today is a fresh set of causes with fresh effects.

Staying attached to specific outcomes. If you go to the library in order to pass a test, every failed test breaks your motivation. If you go to the library to set up future you, the action holds its value regardless of any single result.

Expecting visible cause-and-effect chains. Doing yoga every day doesn't obviously lead to a job or better relationships. The link is real but indirect. Compound interest is invisible until it isn't. Nine years of invested effort can suddenly become legible all at once.

Using karma to dodge accountability. The point is not "everything happens for a reason, so relax." The point is that your effort is always meaningful even when outcomes are uncertain, and that clarity frees you to act rather than spiral.

Judging yourself by a starting line you didn't choose. Some people had significant advantages (financially supportive parents, cultural context, stable environments). Comparing your outputs to theirs without accounting for inputs leads to unfair self-assessment.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reframe one goal you have. Take something you're currently pursuing for a specific outcome ("I want to get a job," "I want to lose weight") and restate it as a seed-planting intention: "I want to set up conditions where getting a job becomes more likely." Notice how the action feels different when the outcome isn't the measure of success.

  2. Do one small positive thing today with no expectation attached. Go to one class. Do ten minutes of movement. Read one chapter. Don't track it, don't celebrate it, don't assign it a goal. Just do it as an offering to future you.

  3. Audit your self-blame honestly. Write down one thing you blame yourself for. Then list the prior circumstances — the genetics, the upbringing, the experiences — that made you the person who ended up in that situation. Ask yourself: given all of that, could you really have done dramatically better? This isn't about excusing yourself; it's about accuracy.

  4. Separate past and present deliberately. When you catch yourself thinking "since I failed before, I'll fail again" or "since I didn't do anything about it before, I can't now," name the logical error: the past and the present are not the same garden. Plant something today.

  5. Track inputs, not outputs. For two weeks, keep a simple log of actions taken — not outcomes achieved. Did you show up? Did you do the thing? That's what you control. Review the log at the end of two weeks and notice how it feels different from tracking results.

  6. Practice compound patience. Identify one area where you've been expecting fast results and consciously extend your time horizon. If you've been at something for three months and it hasn't paid off, ask what it might look like if you stayed consistent for two years — and whether you'd be willing to find out.
