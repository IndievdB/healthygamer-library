Summary

TL;DR — AI mental health tools feel helpful, but the evidence suggests they may quietly erode the very skills you need to take care of yourself.

A wave of people are turning to AI chatbots for emotional support and mental health guidance — often because real professional help is hard to access. That instinct is understandable. But a closer look at how these tools actually work, and what the early research is showing, reveals a pattern worth taking seriously before you make AI a regular part of how you cope.

The Study That Should Give You Pause

Recent research used three clinical scenarios to test how ChatGPT handles mental health situations:

  1. A person experiencing everyday stress and sleep difficulty
  2. A person with clinical depression and work stress
  3. A person with postpartum depression — a genuinely serious condition linked in its most severe presentations to suicide and infanticide

What the study found was unsettling: the advice ChatGPT offered was essentially the same across all three scenarios. Whether you're a little burnt out or in the middle of a psychiatric emergency, the chatbot responds with roughly equivalent, generic guidance.

But the more important question isn't just that it got the answer wrong — it's why it got it wrong.

Why AI Can't Actually Diagnose or Help You

A competent clinician doesn't just dispense advice. More than half of the work is figuring out what's actually going on. When someone says "I'm having trouble sleeping," a psychiatrist is mentally running through a differential diagnosis — a list of possible explanations that could include:

  • Stress or anxiety
  • Clinical depression
  • A first manic episode (including postpartum mania)
  • Substance use
  • Thyroid dysfunction (especially relevant postpartum — the immune system can attack the thyroid gland after delivery, causing symptoms that mimic depression)
  • Genetic insomnia
  • Rare but serious conditions like fatal familial insomnia

To narrow that list down, a clinician asks questions — targeted ones, based on a working hypothesis about what might be happening. That hypothesis requires actual knowledge and reasoning.

AI can't do that. Not because it lacks data, but because of what it fundamentally is:

"These AIs are large language models. They don't have information — they make predictions about how people speak."

A large language model doesn't understand what words mean. It has studied enormous amounts of human language and learned to produce text that sounds right and that people respond well to. It's doing sophisticated speech mimicry — not analysis. There's no internal model of you, no working hypothesis, no clinical reasoning behind the response. It has learned to generate words you'll find satisfying. That's a very different thing from actually helping you.

Feeling Good Isn't the Same as Getting Better

Here's the uncomfortable part: the fact that AI feels helpful doesn't mean it is helpful.

Think about Michael Jackson's final months. He was being put under general anesthesia — propofol, a drug used in cardiac bypass surgery — every night to sleep. His physician was later convicted of manslaughter. But if you had asked Michael Jackson a month before his death whether his doctor was helping him, he would almost certainly have said yes. He might have said this doctor was the only one who truly understood his condition.

That's the trap. Things that feel good — that relieve discomfort immediately — get coded in your brain as "helpful." Addictive substances work this way. Maladaptive coping strategies work this way. And there's now an AI addiction scale being developed by researchers, because the patterns of dependence people develop around these tools are real.

The 2019 research on AI and psychological functioning backs this up: AI showed improvement in mental health outcomes only when compared to doing nothing at all. Once an active control (anything the person was actually doing, even something with no therapeutic value) was introduced, the effect disappeared. There's no solid evidence that AI meaningfully improves mental health.

The social dimension is worse. Studies on AI companionship and loneliness found that chatbots do provide a temporary sense of connection — they reduce the feeling of loneliness in the short term. But over time, they actually worsen social isolation. The tool does the job well enough that you stop building the human skills that would have eventually addressed the root problem.

The Skill Decay Problem

This is the thread that ties everything together. AI is consistently good at one thing: improving your immediate output while degrading your underlying capability.

Students who use AI to write papers get better grades on those papers — and worse at writing. People who use AI for companionship feel less lonely right now and become more isolated over time.

Apply that same logic to mental health:

"Is my mental health something that I want to be good at managing? Do I want the skill of regulating my emotions? Do I want the skill of self-reflection and introspection? If the answer is yes, you should not be using an AI."

Emotional regulation, self-reflection, sitting with discomfort long enough to understand it — these are learnable, practicable skills. Every time you outsource that work to a chatbot, you get a passable short-term result and miss an opportunity to actually build the capacity. Over time, you may become genuinely less equipped to handle your own inner life.

Common Pitfalls

"It gave me real insight, so it must be working." A sophisticated language model can absolutely produce text that sounds insightful and resonates emotionally — that's literally what it's optimized for. The feeling of insight isn't evidence that something therapeutic is happening.

"It's better than nothing." The research suggests this is only narrowly true — and "better than nothing" is a low bar that doesn't account for the opportunity cost (time not spent on things that would actually build skill) or the risk of dependency.

"I just use it to vent / organize my thoughts." This one is worth being honest with yourself about. There's a difference between occasionally using a tool and gradually shifting your default coping strategy away from humans and toward a machine. The dependency research suggests the line is easier to cross than people expect.

"It's fine because I know its limitations." Knowing AI has limitations doesn't protect you from the skill-decay effect. You can consciously understand that a GPS is imperfect and still find, years later, that your spatial navigation instincts have atrophied from disuse.

Conflating "this helped me feel better right now" with "this is good for my mental health." These come apart more often than we'd like to believe. The Michael Jackson example is extreme, but the psychological mechanism — feeling good → concluding the source is helpful — operates in much subtler situations every day.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your current AI use for mental health purposes. Write down honestly how often you turn to a chatbot when you're stressed, anxious, or struggling emotionally. You can't change a pattern you haven't clearly seen.

  2. Before opening a chatbot, try a timed self-reflection first. Set a 10-minute timer and write or think through what's bothering you without any outside input. Notice what comes up. Only then, if needed, use AI as a secondary resource — not the first port of call.

  3. Track whether things are actually improving over time, not just in the moment. Keep a simple log: date, situation, what you did, how you felt afterward, and — crucially — how you felt 24–48 hours later. "Better right now" and "genuinely improving" are different data points.

  4. Use AI for information, not for processing. Asking a chatbot "what are evidence-based treatments for social anxiety?" is categorically different from using it as a stand-in therapist. The first retrieves information. The second attempts to replace a relational, skilled process with a language prediction model.

  5. Invest in at least one human support structure. This could be a therapist, a coach, a trusted friend you talk honestly with, or a support community. The research on AI companionship suggests that the more you rely on AI for social and emotional needs, the harder it becomes to build these connections — which means the time to build them is before you need them badly.

  6. If you're in a serious situation — postpartum mood changes, thoughts of self-harm, significant functional impairment — treat it as a medical situation. Get a real clinician involved. AI cannot take a history, cannot order labs, and cannot differentiate a thyroid crisis from depression. The cost of a missed diagnosis here is not an incorrect essay grade.
