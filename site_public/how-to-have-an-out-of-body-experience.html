Summary

TL;DR — Your thoughts are localized in space, and an out-of-body experience is simply the practice of moving that localization outside of your body through deep meditative relaxation.

Most people have never noticed that their thoughts travel with them — they don't stay in the room you left. Once you see that thoughts occupy a location (roughly, inside your head), the next question becomes: can you move that location? That's the core of what contemplative traditions have called out-of-body experience or astral projection. This piece walks through the underlying idea, the basic practice, and what to realistically expect.

Thoughts Have a Location in Space

This is the foundational insight, and it's easy to dismiss before you've sat with it.

We generally assume thoughts have no location because they aren't physical objects. But pay attention for a moment: when you think, do your thoughts exist in the next room? In your hand? They don't. They seem to exist somewhere around your head, between your ears. They follow you everywhere — they don't stay at the traffic light or in your bedroom.

That's localization. Your experience of thought is situated in space, even if that space is non-physical. You've just never noticed it because the location has never changed.

An out-of-body experience is what it sounds like from this frame: your experience of thought — that subtle sense of "this is where I am" — shifts to somewhere outside your body. Not dramatically. Not like a movie. It just feels like your awareness is in the corner of the room rather than behind your eyes. The experience of thoughts in your head is so ordinary and taken for granted that their relocation feels similarly quiet, just deeply strange.

"You don't even realize they have a localization in space because it's always been in the same place."

The Role of Attention

Normally, attention is never free. It's always hitched to something — a sensation, a thought, a feeling of hunger or tightness. When you're hungry, your attention is on the hunger. When you're thinking, your attention is wrapped up in the thought. You don't experience attention as a separate thing because it's never separate from anything.

The practice works by removing the anchors one at a time.

When the body's signals quiet down — when you're not hungry, not cold, not tense — the body stops pulling your attention toward itself. When thoughts also quiet, the mind stops pulling attention there either. What's left is attention itself, unanchored. And that is what can float.

"As our body stops sending us signals, we will start to feel our consciousness more robustly."

The Practice Itself

Before you begin: - Lie down on the floor (not your bed — that will likely trigger sleep) - Wear light, comfortable clothing - Make sure you're not hungry, thirsty, cold, or needing the bathroom - A darker, quieter room helps - This is a practice to work up to over time; 10–15 minutes produces almost nothing — a genuine session is closer to an hour

The practice is a body scan into deep relaxation:

  1. Close your eyes and notice all the sensations your body is sending you — tightness, pressure, temperature
  2. Move through each region of the body (toes → feet → calves → knees → thighs → fingers → wrists → forearms → arms → shoulders → abdomen and back → chest → neck → head → face), and with each exhale, consciously release the tension held there
  3. With each breath, try to double your relaxation — let half of whatever tension remains go
  4. As you sink deeper, notice whether the body starts to feel heavy, then numb
  5. If numbness arises, stay with it without forcing anything
  6. At some point you may notice a subtle buoyancy — a sense of something straining gently against the surface of your body, wanting to float

"There's a part of you that's going to be like pulling outside of your body — and it's not bad."

The metaphor used is surfing: once that floaty sensation arrives, don't grab for it and don't push it away. Ride it. The moment you react — laugh, tense up, get excited — it collapses. The goal is to ride that wave as long as you can. An hour of riding is roughly when things start to become interesting.

What numbness means: Body numbness during this practice is a sign you're probably doing it right. The sensory inputs of the body are quieting, which is exactly what needs to happen. It's uncomfortable, and that discomfort is one of the main reasons people quit. Gradually build your tolerance for it rather than forcing through.

Expectation, Belief, and the Mind

Here's something counterintuitive: what you believe about this practice is almost completely irrelevant to whether it works.

That's not a motivational claim — it's structural. The practice works by moving you beyond the mind. Expectations, doubts, theories, and convictions all live in the mind. By the time the practice is doing anything, the mind is quieter anyway. Your skepticism can't protect you from an experience, and your belief can't manufacture one.

The only way your mindset matters is if it causes you to stop practicing. If your mind says this is stupid, I'm wasting my time and you listen to it and quit, the practice never gets a chance. But if you keep doing the practice, what you think going in doesn't determine what you experience.

"What you think and expect is irrelevant. That's what's so cool about meditation. If you do the practice, experience is going to be the ultimate thing."

The analogy: you can believe the water is hot or cold all you want. Jump in and you'll know.

On the question of placebo: expectation may actually work against this kind of practice. Priming the mind with expectations engages exactly the mental machinery you're trying to quiet. The spiritual experiences that arise from deep meditation, at least in this framing, don't come from believing hard enough — they come from letting go of the mind entirely.

Common Pitfalls

Quitting too early. Ten minutes of this practice in one sitting produces almost nothing except relaxation and possibly drowsiness. The minimum threshold for any meaningful experience is probably around an hour per session, and realistically a consistent practice of weeks or months before anything significant happens. Estimating roughly 90 hours of practice before having even a small chance of a genuine spiritual experience is not discouraging — it's just honest.

Forcing the float. When the buoyancy feeling arises, the instinct is to lean into it or push it further. That collapses it immediately. You have to ride it without grabbing at it.

Laughing, reacting, or getting excited. Emotional and physical reactions pull attention straight back into the body. If you feel something strange and giggle, tense, or mentally exclaim "this is it!" — it's gone. Part of the practice is learning to encounter unusual sensations without reacting.

Doing it in bed. The deep relaxation this practice creates is very close to the onset of sleep. Your bed is strongly conditioned to that transition. Use a floor or a chair with your back straight.

Confusing this with lucid dreaming. Lucid dreaming moves in a different direction — it uses imagination and thought to construct experience. This practice is about quieting thought, not directing it. There may be overlapping consciousness states between the two, but they are not the same thing.

Ignoring the safety caveats. If you have a history of trauma, depersonalization, derealization, or psychotic disorders, this practice carries real risk. Stop immediately if anything feels distressing, and consult a medical professional before trying it if any of those histories apply to you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Sit with the localization insight first. Before doing any practice, just notice for a day or two that your thoughts follow you around. They're in the room you're in. They're roughly in your head. Let that actually land rather than skipping past it.

  2. Run one 30-minute body scan. Lie on the floor in comfortable clothes, neutral temperature, no hunger or physical discomfort. Work slowly through each body part as described above — toes to face — and with each exhale, consciously release tension. Don't aim for anything unusual. Just practice the relaxation.

  3. Build up your tolerance for numbness. When body numbness arises during a session, sit with it for a little longer each time before coming out of the practice. This is the main barrier to going deeper, and it's trainable.

  4. Increase session length gradually. Go from 20 to 30 to 45 to 60 minutes over weeks. There's very little point in rushing this; the threshold effects seem to require sustained stillness.

  5. Practice without an agenda. Each session, notice when your mind is saying this isn't working or I should be feeling something by now — and recognize that as just another thought pulling your attention. You don't need to argue with it. Just return to the breath and the body.

  6. After a session, note what you noticed — not to evaluate progress, but to get familiar with what different stages of relaxation actually feel like in your body. Over time you'll develop a map of the terrain.
