Summary

TL;DR — You don't need to know how therapy works before you start; you just need to show up and stay engaged.

Most people who are skeptical about therapy aren't skeptical about whether it works — they're anxious about doing it wrong. They've never been taught the basic mechanics of how a therapy session functions, so the whole thing feels opaque. That anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → becomes a reason to avoid it altogether. These four principles cut through that confusion and give you a working model for how to actually participate in therapy effectively.

You Don't Have to Know Anything Going In

This is the most important thing to internalize before your first session: it is not your job to understand how therapy works. That's the therapist's job.

Think about taking your car to a mechanic. You don't walk in and say "I believe it's the carburetor." You walk in and say "it's making a weird noise" — and then the mechanic takes over. You're not expected to know the difference between a fan belt and an oil leak. You just have to bring the car to the shop.

Therapy works the same way. All you need is one sentence about why you're there:

  • "Life feels hard right now."
  • "I think I might be depressed."
  • "I don't even know what's wrong — I just feel stuck."
  • "I've heard therapy helps people, so I figured I'd try it."

Every single one of those is a perfectly valid entry point. A good therapist will orient you from there. Many therapists will explicitly ask on a first session whether you've been in therapy before and, if not, will take a few minutes to explain how it works. You're allowed to ask your therapist directly: "I've never done this before — how does this work?" That's not a weakness. That's exactly the kind of openness that makes therapy go well.

Therapy Is Not Something That Happens To You

Some people hear "go to therapy" and show up expecting to be therapized — to sit back and receive treatment the way you'd receive an X-ray or a cortisone shot. That's not how it works, and it's one of the most common reasons people walk away saying "therapy didn't work for me."

The better mental model is a personal trainer. You walk into a gym, tell the trainer you want to get into shape, and they figure out the program — the weights, the reps, the cardio, all of it. That's their expertise. But when they tell you to do the squat, you have to actually do the squat. They cannot do it for you.

In therapy, the more you think, the more you share, and the more honest you're willing to be, the more the process can actually work. That doesn't mean you need to arrive with perfectly articulated insights. It means you can't treat the session as passive time to fill. The rough rule of thumb: aim to be doing roughly half the work.

"You have to show up and do some of the work — ideally 50 percent of it."

Patience and persistence matter too. Progress in therapy rarely feels linear, and some sessions will feel like nothing happened. That's normal. Showing up anyway, and continuing to try, is itself part of the work.

If It's Not Working, Say So

This is probably the single biggest predictor of whether therapy succeeds or fails: whether the patient brings up their concerns directly with the therapist.

A lot of people, when therapy doesn't feel like it's helping, go quiet about it. They don't want to make the therapist feel bad. They assume the problem is themselves — that they're too broken to be helped, or that they're doing therapy wrong. So they keep saying "things are going fine" while privately feeling like nothing is changing.

This is exactly backwards. The therapist is not a mind reader. If something isn't landing, they need to know.

You don't have to have a sophisticated critique ready. You can say:

"I've been coming here for a month or two and I'm not feeling much better — can we talk about that?"

Or even just: "I don't know what I was hoping for, but it isn't this."

That's enough. It then becomes the therapist's job — not yours — to start asking questions and adjusting the approach. Think of it like being served a dish at a restaurant and telling the chef it tastes off. You don't need to know what's missing. You just have to say something. The chef asks: too salty? needs spice? a little citrus? And you respond to each suggestion until something clicks.

Therapy is a co-op game. Both people have to be playing, and both people have to be communicating.

Not All Therapists, or Types of Therapy, Are the Same

If you've tried therapy once and it didn't click, that's not evidence that therapy doesn't work for you — it might just mean you tried one variety and it wasn't the right fit.

If the fit feels wrong, that's worth exploring, either by raising it with your current therapist or by trying someone else.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting until you "know how" to do therapy before going. There's nothing to learn in advance. Show up first; understanding comes from doing it.
  • Being polite instead of honest. Telling your therapist things are fine when they're not protects their feelings in the short term and sabotages your progress in the long term.
  • Assuming passivity is appropriate. Therapy is not a procedure done to you. The work you do between and during sessions is what actually drives change.
  • Treating one bad experience as a verdict on all therapy. A bad fit with one therapist, or one modality that doesn't suit you, says nothing definitive about whether therapy in general can help.
  • Feeling ashamed that you don't know what you want from therapy. Not knowing is completely normal and explicitly within the scope of what a therapist is there to help you figure out.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Lower the bar to entry. If you've been putting off therapy because you don't feel ready, write one sentence about why you'd go. That sentence is all you need to book an appointment.

  2. At your first session, ask the therapist to orient you. Say: "I've never done this before — can you explain how this works?" Most therapists will be glad you asked.

  3. Track how active you're being. After each session, ask yourself: did I show up and try, or did I mostly wait for something to happen to me? If it's the latter, try sharing something you normally would have held back.

  4. If something feels off, name it out loud in the session. You don't need a polished critique. "I'm not sure this is working — can we talk about what I should be getting out of this?" is a complete sentence and a valid thing to say.

  5. If you've had a bad therapy experience, research a different modality before quitting. Even reading a short description of each might point you toward a style that feels more like what you need.

  6. Give it enough time, then reassess honestly. A few sessions isn't always enough to judge. But if after several weeks something still feels fundamentally off, that's real information — use it to have a direct conversation with your therapist rather than silently dropping out.
