Summary

TL;DR — Toxic behavior online isn't random cruelty — it usually comes from unmet needs, poor emotional education, and zero feedback on how to do better. Banning alone doesn't fix any of that.

Dr. K joins Amouranth to review her oldest unban requests on Twitch, and what starts as light entertainment turns into a genuine examination of why people say the things they say online — and what, if anything, changes their behavior. The conversation moves from specific appeals (incest jokes, "rapey vibes," stalker threats, body-shaming) through psychology, anatomy, parenting theory, and platform design. By the end, the question isn't just "should this person be unbanned?" but "does banning people even work?"

Why People Say Terrible Things in Chat

Most of what shows up in ban queues isn't pure malice. It usually fits one of a few patterns:

Wanting to be noticed. Someone in a 10,000-viewer chat who says something shocking and gets a response — even a ban — just beat out 9,999 other people for attention. It's the same drive as the kid in class who acts up. Crucially, that kid often doesn't know why they're acting up; they just feel the pull to do it.

Not knowing how to express feelings. Several of the appeals involve someone trying, badly, to say they find Amouranth attractive. The result is an incest joke or a sexual comment. The underlying feeling isn't necessarily predatory — it's just unprocessed and untranslated into acceptable language. Nobody taught them how to simply say "you look nice."

Negging and low-confidence targeting. The pickup artist technique of insulting someone you're attracted to works selectively — it tends to land on people with low self-confidence, which is why that community encourages high volume ("try it a hundred times"). It's not a universal technique; it's a filter for psychological vulnerability.

Projection. People who can't imagine a platonic relationship between a man and a woman often can't have one themselves. They assume everyone operates under the same internal rules they do. The person who sexualizes every woman they meet genuinely cannot process that two people could share a bed platonically.

Insecurity wearing a mask. The person spamming sexual questions about penis size and then calling people slurs when called out probably has a real insecurity. The spam is a cover; the aggression afterward is projection of that same shame. These two things — the clowning and the cruelty — can come from the same wound.

The Actual Problem with Banning

Banning is a punishment system, and punishment has one legitimate purpose: to change behavior. The issue is that a ban with no message, no context, and no feedback doesn't teach anything. It just creates a resentful person who makes a new account.

"If you ban them and don't give them any kind of substantive feedback, the concern is that it doesn't change their behavior in a positive light."

There's a second problem: what you say when you ban someone matters. Replying "not worth" to a rapey comment — satisfying as that feels — signals to that person that they should prove their worth, or just go be toxic somewhere else. It doesn't install any new understanding of why the comment was wrong.

The ban appeals that suggest unbanning aren't the ones with the best excuses. The one that stands out as genuinely deserving a second chance is the person who said, three years later: "I didn't respect the hustle back then. I don't have to like it but I can respect it." That's actual attitude change — demonstrated over time, not performed in an appeal.

By contrast, blaming alcohol ("I was incredibly not sober") is worth taking seriously as a reason to stay banned. Unbanning it teaches: blame the substance and the punishment disappears. That's the wrong lesson.

What Shame and Entitlement Actually Look Like

Two failure modes appear repeatedly in the appeals:

Entitlement to other people's bodies. The clearest example is someone who sees a man and a woman together and writes "if it was me I would f her." That's not attraction — it's the assumption that desire removes the other person's agency.

"Desire isn't wrong. I want a cupcake, but I don't get to eat it if it belongs to someone else — or even if it's my own cupcake, I don't have to give into it."

The word to watch for is entitlement, not desire. Desire is universal and neutral. Entitlement is the claim that desire creates a right.

Doubling down in the appeal. Body-shaming someone and then writing in the appeal that you were "promoting a healthy lifestyle" isn't remorse — it's escalation. That person isn't a candidate for unbanning because unbanning them teaches that the original behavior was fine as long as you reframe it.

On Educating Rather Than Punishing

When Amouranth is tempted to reply "not worth" to someone's appeal, the question worth asking is: what does that person do next? Probably: get angrier, feel more resentful, move to another streamer's chat and repeat the behavior.

A better response names the specific problem and leaves the person with something to think about:

"Your chat history gives us a bit of entitled, rapey vibes — maybe reconsider this type of wording when engaging in other chats in the future."

That's not soft. It's precise. It tells the person exactly what the problem is and implies that the behavior exists outside this one stream, which it does.

The larger argument: punishment is how we enforce rules. Positive reinforcement is how people develop an internal moral compass. These are not the same thing. Someone who learns not to be toxic because they got banned learned to avoid a consequence. Someone who learns through positive feedback — recognition, reward, seeing that their words actually matter to real people — builds something that travels with them.

The internet, like parenting 100 years ago, is very good at punishment and still figuring out encouragement. That gap is where most of the problem lives.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming bad behavior means bad intent. Several appeals come from people who probably had no idea what they did crossed a line. Treating every toxic commenter as a knowing predator misses the majority of cases, which involve ignorance, poor impulse control, and no role model for how to talk to people online.

Assuming donations equal respect. Someone offering to donate if they get unbanned isn't necessarily contrite — they may be exercising a power dynamic, buying their way out of accountability. Money and genuine attitude change are different things.

Treating "I was joking" as an explanation. Joking about stalking isn't different from stalking in terms of what it teaches the person who says it, or what it communicates to the person who receives it. The social harm of normalizing the language is real regardless of intent.

Letting the emotional drain lead to numbness. By the end of an hour of appeals, pattern recognition replaces genuine engagement. That's a real risk — both for moderators and for anyone who consumes a lot of toxic content. Numbness isn't resilience; it's a sign you need to pace the exposure.

Thinking the problem is unsolvable. It isn't. It's undersolved. There hasn't been serious, concerted effort at positive reinforcement at the platform level. The hopelessness is partly a function of how little we've tried the alternatives.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Before you ban, leave a note. Even one sentence explaining specifically what crossed the line gives the person a chance to understand something. "That comment had an entitled, threatening tone" is more useful than silence.

  2. Evaluate appeals on attitude change, not explanation quality. Ask: does this person understand what they did wrong, or are they just reframing it? A short, genuine acknowledgment beats a long, defensive justification.

  3. Don't reward substance-blaming. If someone's appeal leans on "I was drunk/high/in a bad mood," that's a reason to keep the ban, not lift it. Removing the consequence teaches that excuses work.

  4. Notice the difference between desire and entitlement in your own reactions online. Wanting something is normal. Assuming you're owed it is the problem. Practice naming which one is operating when you feel frustrated by other people's boundaries.

  5. If you carry tension in your body from consuming difficult content, take it seriously. The sun salutation postures — arms overhead, then hanging forward with straight knees and palms flat — held for 30–60 seconds each rather than flowing through quickly, can help release stored tension, especially in the hamstrings and upper back.

  6. When you see toxic behavior in a community you're part of, say something — specifically and calmly. You don't need a platform. Naming the behavior ("that comment was out of line because X") is more effective than piling on with mockery, which just escalates.

  7. Think about what positive reinforcement looks like in spaces you inhabit. Highlighting good comments, responding warmly to good-faith questions, building in recognition for people who contribute well — these aren't soft options, they're the mechanism by which people actually change.
