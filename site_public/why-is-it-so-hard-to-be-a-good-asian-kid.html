Summary

TL;DR — The Asian American mental health crisis is real, structural, and cultural. Understanding the three thought patterns keeping you from getting help is the first step to changing anything.

Being a high-achieving Asian kid has always been hard. It's getting harder. The academic and professional bar keeps rising, admissions data shows Asian applicants face objectively steeper standards than every other group, and the cultural framework most Asian families operate within actively prevents the one thing that would actually help: asking for support. The result is a quiet crisis — high rates of anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, and suicide among a demographic that, from the outside, appears to be doing fine.

This piece walks through why it's so hard, what cultural beliefs are making it worse, and how to start thinking your way out.

The World Is Actually Getting Harder (And the Numbers Confirm It)

This isn't about making excuses. It's about being honest.

Look at medical school admissions as one concrete example. To get into medical school in the U.S., Asian applicants need to score in the top 23% of MCAT test-takers. White applicants need to be in the top 30–35%. Latino applicants in the top 54%. Black applicants in the top 73%. That means an Asian pre-med student has to outperform peers from every other demographic just to get a seat at the same table.

And this isn't unique to medicine. Job markets are more competitive. Ivy League degrees no longer guarantee top-tier employment. The bar keeps moving. Objectively, the goals many Asian families set — and that many Asian kids internalize — are becoming harder to reach every year.

None of this means success is impossible. It means the difficulty is real, it's documented, and dismissing it doesn't make it go away.

The Three Cultural Beliefs That Make It Worse

The external pressure is one problem. The cultural software running in the background is another. Three specific beliefs tend to keep Asian kids stuck.

1. Everything is personal responsibility.

Struggling? That's your fault. Environment, circumstances, mental health — none of it counts. The grade you got is the grade you earned, period. This means there's no framework for recognizing when something outside of you is contributing to what's happening inside of you. You can go to Kumon. You cannot go to therapy. One is acceptable help; the other is a personal failure.

2. Saving face above all else.

If you're struggling, you don't just have to fix it yourself — you have to make sure nobody finds out. Seeing a therapist doesn't just feel unnecessary; it feels like a public indictment of your whole family. The internal logic runs something like: if you need help, it reflects on your parents. If it reflects on your parents, it's shameful. So you say nothing.

This keeps a lot of Asian kids completely isolated with their problems, carrying weight they were never meant to carry alone.

3. If you can't see it, it isn't real.

Asian parents will generally take a broken bone seriously. They'll take a B+ seriously. They cannot take depression seriously, because depression doesn't show up on a report card and doesn't require a cast. The result is that if you're struggling emotionally, you often lack even a language to describe it — and when you try, it gets dismissed as laziness, weakness, or lack of effort.

"If they can't see it, it isn't real."

Three Thought Traps to Watch For in Yourself

These cultural beliefs don't just live in your parents. They live in you. Here's a quick self-check.

Trap 1: "It's okay for other people to get help, but I should be able to do this on my own."

Notice it.

Trap 2: Jumping to comparison when someone acknowledges your difficulty.

If you hear "the world is genuinely getting harder" and your brain immediately responds with "yeah, but Sejal is still getting straight A's" — that's the comparative, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →-based framework your environment installed. The fact that someone else is managing doesn't mean your struggles aren't real. It might just mean their circumstances are different from yours.

Trap 3: "I'm not sure help would even be worth it for me."

This one cuts deepest. When you've grown up in a system that ties your value as a human being to your GPA, your ranking, your achievements — you start to believe you're not worth investing in unless you're performing at the top. The kids who deserve help are the ones with 4.0s. You, with a 3.7, feel like a questionable investment.

"When your parents say you are worth less than this kid down the street because they got a 4.0 and you got a 3.9, that does something very damaging to your self-esteemSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →."

This belief — that your human worth is tied to your output — is exactly what makes it so hard to reach out. And it's exactly why the question "do I deserve help?" is the wrong question entirely.

What You Actually Want (And Why Nobody's Asked)

Here's something that may have gone unexamined for years: what do you want?

Not what your parents want. Not the pre-approved list of instruments, sports, and majors. What do you actually want from your life?

For most Asian kids, that question has been systematically muted since childhood. The wants that didn't fit the approved track — the wrong instrument, the wrong sport, the wrong major — got knocked out early. Over time, many Asian kids stop being able to access what they want at all. The voice goes quiet. And the result is often a kind of hollow compliance: doing the engineering degree, not studying, playing video games in the dorm, scraping by, feeling nothing.

The quiet rebellion is real. But it's not freedom — it's just a muted version of the same trap.

Reconnecting with what you actually want is one of the most radical things you can do. And here's an important reframe: instead of asking whether you deserve help or need help (both questions carry shame), ask a different question entirely:

For the life you want — are you willing to accept some help getting there?

That question sidesteps the shame. It's not about worthiness. It's not about pathology. It's about whether the path you're on is the one you'd choose.

Help Doesn't Have to Mean "Something Is Broken"

One important nuance: not every Asian kid who walks into a mental health professional's office needs a diagnosis. Many don't. What a lot of them actually need is help figuring out what they want, how to talk to their parents, how to navigate a life that doesn't fit the script they were handed.

That kind of support — coaching, mentorship, honest conversation — isn't therapy. It doesn't mean you're mentally ill. It doesn't mean you failed. It's closer to having a math tutor, except the subject is your own life.

"Just like you have a math tutor, you can have a mental health tutor."

That said: if you're having thoughts of suicide, please do see a therapist. Get evaluated. Research on suicide suggests that more than half of people who experience suicidal ideation don't have a formal mental illness — but they also don't see a path forward to the life they want. That's something that can change. But it changes faster with support.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking intellectual agreement for real belief. You can nod along to "it's okay to get help" while simultaneously holding the deep conviction that you specifically don't deserve it. Watch for the gap between what you agree with in theory and how you actually behave.

  • Using someone else's success as evidence that your struggle isn't real. Another person managing doesn't invalidate your difficulty. Circumstances differ in ways that aren't always visible.

  • Waiting until things are bad enough to "qualify." A lot of Asian kids don't reach out until they're in crisis, because they've spent years telling themselves things aren't bad enough yet. You don't need to hit a floor to deserve support.

  • Confusing help with weakness or diagnosis. Getting coaching, seeing a therapist, or talking to someone doesn't mean you're broken. It means you're treating your inner life with the same seriousness you'd give a broken bone.

  • Assuming your parents' expectations are the only valid measure of your life. They came from a specific context, with specific fears. Their framework for success may genuinely not fit the world you're living in now.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the self-check honestly. Sit with each of the three thought traps above. When you hear "it's okay to get help," what does your gut actually say? Write it down. Noticing the conditioning is the first step to not being run by it.

  2. Ask yourself what you want — and write it down without filtering. Not what's realistic, not what your parents would approve of. What do you actually want? If the voice is very quiet, give it time and space. This might take more than one sitting.

  3. Reframe the help question. Instead of "do I deserve help?" or "do I need help?", ask: "For the things I want — am I willing to accept some support in getting there?" Sit with that version and see if the answer is different.

  4. If you're having any thoughts of suicide or are in significant distress, make an appointment with a therapist this week. Not when things get worse. Now. You don't need to prove you're sick enough.

  5. Consider what kind of support actually fits your situation. Therapy is appropriate for clinical mental health concerns. Coaching or mentorship might be the right fit if you're struggling with direction, motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →, or family dynamics but aren't in crisis.   6. Notice the comparison habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → in real time.
