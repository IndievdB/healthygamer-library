Summary

TL;DR — Insecurity is an internal wound that can't be healed by external reassurance. The only way out is to stop feeding it from the outside and learn to sit with it directly.

Most people who seem arrogant, attention-hungry, or combative online aren't actually confident — they're insecure. The behavior that looks like showing off is really a hunt for proof that a painful self-belief isn't true. Understanding this dynamic, and knowing how to disrupt it, can change the way you relate to yourself and other people.

The Origin of the Pattern: Yesterday's Battles

Insecurity usually starts young. You get a message — from peers, family, experience — that you're stupid, unattractive, unlovable, or worthless. That message becomes a belief you carry into adulthood, even when the original situation is long gone.

The problem is that the belief doesn't stay in the past. It keeps generating a hunger — a constant low-level pressure to prove the belief wrong. And so you spend your adult life fighting a battle that belongs to a different era, against people who aren't even in the room anymore.

"Stop fighting yesterday's battles today."

This shows up in relationships, careers, and especially online. The person who keeps needing their partner to say "I love you" every day. The person who half-asses their work because getting a B while trying feels worse than getting a C while coasting. The person who argues about theoretical physics with strangers on the internet — not because the topic matters, but because they need to be seen as intelligent.

These aren't random quirks. They're the same wound expressing itself in different costumes.

Why the Internet Amplifies Insecurity

The internet is a perfect insecurity-feeding machine. It offers an endless supply of people to flex on, impress, or defeat — and it does so with almost no friction. But notice what happens to people who use it that way: they don't flex once and feel better. They're on there every day, every year, doing the same thing.

"Egotistical people on the internet aren't egotistical one day and then they disappear. They live on the internet for their entire lives, flexing on everyone as much as they can, because they believe something about themselves that they don't want to be true."

That's the tell. Genuine confidence doesn't need constant performance. The relentless performance reveals the doubt underneath.

The things you feel the most compelled to prove to the world are almost always the things you secretly believe are true about yourself.

Why External Reassurance Makes It Worse

Here's the counterintuitive part: seeking reassurance doesn't reduce insecurity. It feeds it. And things that get fed grow.

Each time you soothe an insecurity with outside validation, you train yourself to need that validation more the next time. The hunger becomes bigger, not smaller. The reassurance that worked yesterday needs to be stronger tomorrow.

Worse, you gradually hand over the controls of your own emotional state to other people. Your mood becomes a function of how the outside world treats you that day. One good comment and you feel great. One criticism — or just silence — and you feel worthless.

"You sign away the rights to control your life to the outside world. Then you're not in control. Life is taking you wherever it wants to take you."

This is the trap. You set out to fix the insecurity, but the strategy you chose actually deepens your dependence on the very thing that can't fix it.

The Real Fix: Stop Fighting Internal Battles on the Outside

No amount of external validation can resolve something that lives inside you. This isn't pessimism — it's just accurate targeting. If the wound is internal, the healing has to be internal.

The way through is to go directly toward the insecurity instead of away from it. That means:

  1. Stop feeding it from the outside. Resist the reflex to seek reassurance, to flex, to prove yourself. Don't avoid the hunger — just stop acting on it.
  2. Bring it up deliberately and sit with it. Face the feeling directly rather than drowning it in distractions — alcohol, video games, or scrolling.
  3. Tolerate the discomfort without feeding thoughts into it. The hunger will try to generate justifications, judgments, and urges. Notice them, but don't follow them.
  4. Watch it begin to dissolve. Insecurity that is faced and not fed loses its charge over time. This is the only thing that actually works.

"It's not fighting the insecurity, it's sitting with it. And as you learn how to tolerate the bad feelings on the inside, you will start to accept them, and they'll start to dissolve — and the insecurity will dissolve with it."

You are not trying to defeat the insecurity. You are trying to stop giving it fuel.

The Mirror and Candle Practice

There's a concrete technique for this. It functions like a meditation for working directly with self-judgment.

Set up a mirror and a candle so you can look at your own reflection and then shift your focus to the flame. Here's the sequence:

  1. Look at yourself in the mirror. Let whatever thoughts and feelings arise, come up — don't suppress them.
  2. When the hunger or self-judgment becomes strong, shift your attention entirely to the candle. Put your focus on the flame.
  3. Notice that the judgments and the hunger begin to quiet as your attention moves.
  4. Look back at the mirror. Let the thoughts return.
  5. Shift back to the candle again.

Each cycle is a repetition of the same skill: feel the discomfort, don't act on it, redirect attention, let it subside. You're not fighting the insecurity. You're practicing non-reaction to it.

The same principle works with any craving or discomfort — food, a desired substance, a compulsive urge. Bring it close enough to activate the desire, then deliberately hold your attention elsewhere. The goal is to build the capacity to tolerate the feeling without acting on it.

"All you have to do is stop feeding it. Bring it up, face the hunger, and stop feeding it."

Common Pitfalls

Confusing reassurance-seeking with confidence-building. Getting compliments, winning arguments, or earning social approval feels like it's building self-worth. It isn't. It's borrowing self-worth temporarily, at interest — and you'll pay it back by needing more tomorrow.

Thinking this only applies to obviously insecure people. The insecurity that drives someone to brag about their intelligence is just as real as the insecurity that makes someone shrink and say nothing. Being "smart" or "accomplished" can be the insecurity just as much as being "ugly" or "worthless."

Misidentifying the problem as other people. If your emotional state is mostly determined by how others treat you, it's easy to conclude that the solution is to find better people or better situations. But the dependency is the problem, not the current people — and you'll recreate it wherever you go.

Expecting the feeling to go away immediately. The mirror and candle practice isn't a one-time fix. The first few times you do it, the discomfort will be strong. The point is repeated exposure and non-reaction, which gradually reduces the charge of the insecurity over time.

Avoiding yourself. Distraction — whether it's gaming, scrolling, drinking, or arguing online — delays the reckoning. Every avoidance is another meal for the hunger. Spending time simply being with yourself, without agenda, without performance, is part of the work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the mirror and candle practice. Set up a candle in front of a mirror. Look at your reflection, let judgments arise, then shift your attention fully to the candle. Alternate back and forth several times. Aim for 10–15 minutes. Notice what comes up without acting on it.

  2. Identify your specific insecurity. What's the thing you most want to prove to other people? Work backwards from that — what does the compulsion to prove it tell you about what you secretly fear is true?

  3. Name your reassurance-seeking behaviors. Do you check likes or replies compulsively? Do you need frequent affirmation from a partner? Do you argue online to be seen as smart? Just identifying the behavior is the first step to disrupting it.

  4. Practice with a craving. Choose something you find it difficult to resist — a snack, a phone check, anything. Get close enough to activate the desire, then hold your attention on something neutral instead. Watch the craving rise, peak, and fall without acting on it. Repeat.

  5. When you catch yourself fighting an old battle, pause and ask: "Whose opinion am I actually trying to change right now? Is this person even in my life?" Labeling it as "yesterday's battle" can be enough to interrupt the loop.

  6. Cut one reassurance-seeking loop. Pick one behavior where you regularly look to the outside world to feed an insecurity — and go one day without it. Sit with the resulting discomfort instead of replacing it with a different reassurance. This is hard. That difficulty is information about how much you've been relying on it.
