What Moral Injury Actually Is

Moral injury is the specific kind of harm that comes from being forced to act against your own valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition → — or watching someone you trusted do so. The American Psychological Association now recognizes it as a mental health factor, extending it into the category of spiritual or religious conflict. That matters because it separates this kind of exhaustion from regular stress or overwork.

It shows up at work in three main ways:

  1. You're made to do something that violates your valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →. A nurse in a state with abortion restrictions who is forced to watch women be denied care. A therapist who is financially incentivized to keep patients coming back indefinitely rather than helping them leave.
  2. You failed to act according to your own valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →. You didn't speak up when you should have. You went along with something you knew was wrong.
  3. Someone you trusted abandoned those valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →. A leader who championed the team for years and then carried out mass layoffs — or got fired after doing so.

The source of the injury also varies. Sometimes it's the specific job tasks. Sometimes it's the entire system (the healthcare billing structure, the education testing regime). Sometimes it's just a bad boss. Identifying which layer is causing your injury matters, because the fix looks different in each case.

The Gap Between What a Job Looks Like and What It Feels Like

Here's a trap worth naming: a job can look meaningful from the outside and feel hollow on the inside.

Working in nonprofit marketing to "help the whales and the refugees" sounds purposeful. But if your day-to-day is cold-calling donors for Giving Thursday, you're still just asking people for money. The macro mission is good; the micro reality is draining.

This distinction — macro meaning vs. micro satisfaction — is genuinely important. Some people are energized by the big picture ("I'm fighting climate change"). Others need the daily wins ("that patient actually got better"). Most people need some of both, but they don't always know which one they lean on until they're sitting in a job that has one and not the other.

Before making a career move for purpose, ask: what is my actual day-to-day going to look like? Will those specific tasks feel meaningful, or am I just buying into a shiny mission statement?

Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation — and the Amotivated Zone

Job satisfaction generally comes from one of two directions:

  • Intrinsic motivationMotivation that arises from novelty, genuine interest, and autonomy — not from rewards, emotions, or changes in biological state.Full definition →: The work itself — the achievement, the craft, the growth, the recognition for your ideas. You're driven by what the work is.
  • Extrinsic motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →: The conditions around the work — salary, flexibility, working environment, the ability to mentor others. You're driven by what the work provides.

Most people aren't purely one or the other, and crucially, the balance shifts over time. Economic pressure pushes a lot of people back toward extrinsic motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → even when they'd prefer to operate from a more intrinsic place. That's not weakness — it's just real life.

There's also a third state worth acknowledging: amotivation — a complete disconnection from both. This is where a significant chunk of people land when they've tried many paths, nothing has clicked, and the world around them feels chaotic. If that's where you are, a coaching or structured support relationship is often more useful than career advice alone, because the problem isn't information.

A useful self-check: are you a bill payer (the job funds the life you actually want), a soul feeder (you get meaning from side projects, volunteering, communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → — not necessarily from work itself), or someone who needs their core purpose to run through their daily work? All three are valid. Most people move through all three over a career. Knowing which one you are right now helps you stop chasing the wrong thing.

Navigating Systems That Frustrate You

Several of the case study questions shared a common pattern: someone who sees a broken system and wants to fight it from the inside. The instinct is understandable. The strategy is often counterproductive.

"If you are inherently anti-capitalist and you try to approach a capitalist organization, for you to label yourself as anti the whole thing, it's going to be very difficult for you to dialogue with somebody — because you need that person to be pro-you."

The moment you frame yourself as "anti-management," "anti-capitalist," or "pro-accountability of the other side," you've created two teams and positioned yourself on the losing one. That's not a moral judgment — it's a practical one. An us-versus-them dynamic in the workplace almost always results in you having to pick a side, and individuals who pick sides rarely win against institutions.

A more effective approach: - Identify what specifically is broken (scheduling chaos, poor morale, unsafe conditions) rather than indicting the whole ideology - Come in as a problem-solver: "I've noticed we're struggling with X. I have some ideas. Would you be open to hearing them?" - Build trust before asking for change — people have to be pro-you before they'll act on your suggestions

Also worth noting: employees often pick fights with the wrong person. A hospital administrator may be constrained by the school board, insurance companies, or tax law in ways that aren't visible to the people below them. That doesn't excuse bad management, but it does mean your energy is sometimes better aimed at the actual lever than the nearest face.

Timing Your Career Conversations Right

One practical piece that most people miss: companies budget slowly. If you want a raise or promotion in the first half of next year, the conversation needs to happen now — end of Q3, beginning of Q4.

The ask shouldn't be "I deserve more money" or "people in my role make X elsewhere." It should be:

"I'm looking to grow into [role] over the next 12 months. What would I need to deliver to make you confident that's the right move?"

That question does several things at once: it signals ambition without ultimatum, it invites your manager into a collaborative frame, and it gives you a clear roadmap instead of vague hope.

If what you actually want isn't a promotion but more autonomy or time, say that too — just frame it around what you're already delivering. "I think I can get everything done in a slightly shorter week. Would you be open to experimenting with that?"

Entrepreneurship: Rich or King?

If you're building your own thing as an answer to moral injury, one foundational question determines almost everything else:

Do you want to be rich, or do you want to be king?

  • Rich: Build something larger than yourself. That requires delegating, accepting others' ideas, and tolerating losing some control.
  • King: Do it your way, on your terms. More autonomy, more purpose-alignment, but unlikely to scale massively.

Most people say "both" — and most of the time, those goals actively conflict. Being honest with yourself about which one actually drives you will save enormous amounts of time and frustration. Purpose and fulfillment tend to align more naturally with the "king" path. Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming a purposeful-sounding job will feel purposeful day-to-day. Check the actual tasks, not just the mission.
  • Framing workplace conflict as moral warfare. Strong ideological identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → at work ("I'm anti-capitalist," "I'm pro-worker accountability") makes it hard to build the trust you need to actually change anything.
  • Asking for a raise or promotion at the wrong time. Budget cycles are set months in advance. A great case made in February may simply not have a home in that year's numbers.
  • Burning bridges when you leave under difficult circumstances. The macro factors behind a layoff are often invisible to the people affected. The network and resume bullets you built are real. Don't destroy them because the exit was painful.
  • Assuming more time off is the real need, then finding it's still not enough. If the quiz says you're extrinsically motivated but you feel internally driven, that gap is worth sitting with — the thing you're wishing for may not be the thing that would actually satisfy you.
  • Waiting until you need something to network. Network when you have nothing to ask.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Diagnose your moral injury layer. Is the conflict coming from the specific tasks, the broader system, or the people around you? Write it down. The fix looks different depending on where the injury lives.
  2. Check your macro/micro split. For your current or target job, list what you'd actually do on a Tuesday afternoon. Does that list feel meaningful, or are you only excited about the mission statement?
  3. Take an intrinsic/extrinsic motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → inventory. Notice where you genuinely sit right now — not where you think you should be. Ask: am I a bill payer, a soul feeder, or do I need my core purpose in the work itself?
  4. Reframe one workplace conflict using problem-solving language. Take something you're currently framing as "they're doing wrong" and rephrase it as a problem to solve: "It looks like we're struggling with X — I have some ideas." Try that version in your next relevant conversation.
  5. Have the career-growth conversation now. If you want a different role or compensation in the next year, schedule a conversation with your manager this quarter. Use the script: "What would I need to deliver over the next 12 months to make the case for [role/title/salary]?"
  6. If purpose outside work is the answer, pursue it deliberately. Volunteering isn't a consolation prize — it's a legitimate way to meet like-minded people, expand your network, and scratch the purpose itch without betting your entire income on it.
  7. If you're building something of your own, answer the question honestly: Do you want to be rich or do you want to be king? Write a paragraph on each and see which one actually excites you.
