Goals and motivation are simpler to talk about than to act on, which is why so many people set goals, feel fired up for a week, and then quietly abandon them while feeling worse about themselves than before. This piece covers the hidden architecture behind that cycle: the four types of intent driving any goal, why positive thinking about goals backfires, why raising awareness of costs paradoxically helps, and what neuroscience says about the only process that actually produces lasting behavioral change.

The Four Types of Intent — and Why Language Hides the Difference

When someone says "I have a goal," there are four distinct internal drives that could be behind it. People use vague, overlapping language — the word "should" does a lot of heavy lifting — but the underlying drives are genuinely different, and misidentifying them is like a doctor treating "sickness" without a diagnosis.

The four types map onto two dimensions: gain vs. loss (does fulfilling it give you something, or cost you something?) and internal vs. external reward (does the payoff come from inside you, or from the world's response?).

DesiresDesires are signals generated by the sense organs (the indriyas) that point toward something you currently lack — creating a gap between what you have and what you want.Full definition → — gain, external reward. These are bodily or sensory drives: hunger, craving stimulation, wanting to game. The reward is immediate and physical. Most people don't need help acting on desires; they happen automatically.

Shoulds — loss, external reward. "I should become a doctor," "I should lose weight." You give something up (your own preferences, your time, your happiness), and what you get back is social standing, prestige, or relief from judgment. The verb is "I should." It often comes from family, society, or the fear of external disapproval.

ValuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition → — gain, internal reward. Things you genuinely care about. When you do them, you get something — not prestige or pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition →, but a sense of rightness, personal satisfaction, meaning. Hiking to a summit is hard and costs effort, but the internal reward is real. The verb is closer to "I want to" or "I care about."

DutyDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition → — loss, internal reward. Something owed to another person that requires real sacrifice. Staying up all night with a sick child costs you, but it is internally satisfying because you are being the person you want to be. Unlike a "should," it is directed at someone specific, not at a social audience.

"When people use unsophisticated language they don't diagnose the problem correctly — it's like treating sickness without knowing the disease."

Most people who struggle with motivation are stuck operating almost entirely from desires and shoulds — the left side of the quadrant. That means they're living on external reward, which turns out to be structurally fragile. The shift that changes lives is moving from "should" toward values and duty — from external to internal reward. This is also what Acceptance and Commitment TherapyAn evidence-based psychotherapy, rooted in cognitive behavioral therapy but distinct from it, that pairs radical acceptance of what can't be changed with active commitment to personal values.Full definition → (ACT), an evidence-based treatment for addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, is built on: the clearer a person is about what they genuinely value, the easier it becomes to make hard changes.

"Once they understand that they're making a choice every single day — do I want to live a life that is in service to external rewards, or a life in service to what I actually care about — then they start making choices in the right direction."

The practical implication: when you're stuck, stop asking "what should I do?" and start asking "what do I actually care about here, and is there any thread of that I can connect to this goal?"

Why Positive Thinking About Goals Undermines Them

There's a counterintuitive trap almost everyone falls into: thinking about how great it's going to be when you achieve your goal.

You picture the outcome — the body, the degree, the promotion — you feel the excitement, and then you go out and take your first real steps. The gym is hard. You're exhausted and not visibly different after a week. The gap between the expectation you've built up and the reality you're experiencing tanks motivation sharply. Then you feel ashamed, identify as someone who "can't stick with things," and stop trying.

The problem isn't willpower. It's the mismatch between expectation and experience. Motivation was attached to the outcome, not to the action itself.

Raising Awareness of the Negative — Three Reasons It Works

The counterintuitive fix is to deliberately raise awareness of what a goal will actually cost — the boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition →, the discomfort, the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, the lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, the things you have to give up. This works through three distinct mechanisms.

1. It sets accurate expectations. When you know in advance that the gym will be hard, that you'll feel ashamed, that your body won't change for weeks, and then you go and it's hard but survivable, the response shifts to "it wasn't as bad as I expected." That closes the expectation gap and actually increases the likelihood you'll go back tomorrow.

2. It resolves ambivalenceAmbivalence is the state of being pulled in two directions at once — wanting something and simultaneously having a force inside you that resists it.Full definition → by making it visible. People who describe themselves as "unmotivated" are almost never actually unmotivated — they are intensely motivated to do the thing they're currently doing. Someone who games for ten hours a day despite it damaging every area of their life is paying an enormous cost to do it, which means the brain is calculating enormous benefits: emotional suppression, fun, social connection, stimulation. These benefits are almost entirely invisible to the person. Raising awareness of them — and of the costs of changing — brings the actual internal conflict into the open. Once you can see both sides of the equation, you can actually work with them. Motivational interviewingA structured, evidence-based conversational technique used by a trained practitioner to resolve a person's internal conflict and increase their motivation to change.Full definition →, another evidence-based framework, is built on exactly this: you cannot move someone past an internal conflict they cannot see.

3. Paradoxically, high perceived cost can drive commitment. Studies on wine-tasting show that expert sommeliers rating blinded wines show no preference by price — but when they know the price, the expensive wine tastes better. Studies on hazing show that people who endure humiliating initiation rituals place higher value on the group they join. The mechanism appears to be cognitive dissonance: the mind cannot easily tolerate having paid a large cost for something worthless, so it increases the perceived value of that thing. When you genuinely understand and accept that a goal will be hard, you become more committed to it — not less. And when it turns out to be survivable, that "it wasn't as bad as I thought" response builds momentum.

The Neuroscience: Why Awareness Has to Come Before Change

Knowing something and understanding it are two fundamentally different things, and only one of them changes behavior. This distinction maps onto two memory systems in the brain.

Explicit memory is information — facts, recalled events, things you've been told. It's stored in the hippocampusThe brain structure responsible for memory consolidation — taking information from short-term storage and transferring it into long-term understanding.Full definition →. You can recite it. It does not, on its own, change what you do. This is why parents can repeat the same advice a thousand times without effect, why reading about motivation doesn't change behavior, and why people who know they're in a bad habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → keep doing it anyway.

Implicit memory is understanding — it lives in other brain structures, including the cerebellum (for physical skills) and the frontal lobeThe brain region responsible for planning, executing tasks, controlling impulses, and regulating other parts of the brain — essentially the hardware of self-regulation and mature behavior.Full definition → (for behavioral and emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition →). When understanding lives in the frontal lobe, it produces actual impulse controlThe ability of the brain's frontal lobes to suppress urges before they become actions — what happens when that system works well, and what breaks down when it doesn't.Full definition →. It doesn't just pop up as a thought you can ignore; it shapes your response before you act.

The process that moves information from explicit to implicit memory is twofold: active engagement with the concepts (not passive consumption — more like "playing on the court" with the ideas, turning them over, applying them, running into their edges) and sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition →, during which the hippocampus consolidates material into long-term memory. This is why studying for two hours, sleeping, and studying for another two hours is more effective than four straight hours — and why meaningful behavioral change through coaching happens over weeks, not in a single intensive session.

"As long as it's coming from your hippocampus as explicit memory, it's not going to drive behavioral change."

The practical upshot: being told what to do, even if you agree with it, rarely works. What works is wrestling with ideas until they stop being information and become understanding — until they move from something you can recite to something that actually shapes what you do.

Common Pitfalls

Treating all "shoulds" as the same. The word "should" masks real differences between social pressure, duty to a specific person, and internalized values. Diagnosing which one is driving you matters enormously.

Focusing only on the upside of a goal. Building a vivid mental image of success is one of the most reliable ways to reduce actual motivation, because it sets expectations the early stages of work can't possibly meet.

Confusing information for understanding. Reading about motivation, watching videos about productivity, knowing what you need to do — none of this is the same as understanding it in a way that changes behavior. Accumulating more information is not the solution.

Mistaking low motivation for no motivation. If you're stuck doing something self-destructive, you are motivated — just toward that thing. The question is always what the hidden pros of the current behavior are, not how to generate motivation from nothing.

Expecting linear progress. Going 72 hours without gaming when your goal was a week is not failure — it's 72 hours. Reframing what counts as evidence of progress matters.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify the intent behind one of your current goals. Ask yourself honestly: is this a "should" (driven by external judgment), a desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → (immediate gratification), a value (something I genuinely care about), or a duty (something I owe someone)? Write down which one it actually is, not which one you wish it were.

  2. Find the value thread. If your goal is currently sitting in the "should" column, ask: is there any part of this that connects to something I genuinely care about? Even a partial connection — "I'm in this major but I care about X, so I'll pursue X as a minor" — can shift the internal experience enough to sustain effort.

  3. Map both sides of the ambivalence. For a behavior you want to change, write two lists. First: what do I gain from keeping things the way they are? (Be honest — social connection, relief from boredom, emotional numbing, whatever it actually is.) Second: what will it actually cost me to change? Getting this on paper makes the real conflict visible.

  4. Pre-brief yourself on difficulty before starting. Before attempting any goal-related action this week, spend five minutes mentally preparing for how hard it will actually be — the discomfort, the shame, the tedium. Notice whether "it wasn't that bad" shows up afterward, and what that does to your motivation for the next attempt.

  5. Prioritize sleep after learning or reflection. After a coaching conversation, a journaling session, or any period of genuine self-reflection, protect the sleep that follows. That's when the brain encodes insight into lasting understanding.

  6. Limit intensive reflection sessions to about 45 minutes. Working memory has a functional ceiling. A focused 45-minute conversation or journaling session tends to produce more usable insight than an exhausting two-hour one.
