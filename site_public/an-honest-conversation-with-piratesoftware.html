The broader lesson applies well beyond internet drama: it's about what happens when we're convinced we're being objective, and why that conviction can make things worse instead of better.

What Actually Happened (and Why It Matters Less Than You'd Think)

The inciting incident is almost comically small. During a World of Warcraft hardcore dungeon run, the shot-caller told the group to run. The streamer ran. The shot-caller then asked why he was running. Instead of saying "because you told us to," the streamer made a smug face and a sarcastic remark. Two other players' characters died. That's it.

What followed was 100+ days of death threats, swatting, pedophilia allegations, thousands of Twitch bans, and commenters celebrating the death of a rescue ferret by saying she "ran out of mana."

The gap between those two things — one snide comment in a video game and months of sustained coordinated harassment — is the real subject of the conversation. The harassment itself is not the lesson. The lesson is what lives in the space between.

The Missing Missing Reasons

There's a concept worth knowing: missing missing reasons. It comes from a forum post about estranged families, where parents describe being completely blindsided by their adult children cutting contact — and then, in the same post, reveal exactly what their children told them, but dismiss those reasons as not making sense.

The pattern looks like this: I don't know why this is happening to me. All I did was X. And then all of this happened.

When someone tells a story where their small action produced a massive reaction, and the gap can only be explained by other people being irrational, that's usually a signal that something is missing from the account. Not necessarily a lie — more like a blind spot. The person telling the story isn't being dishonest. They genuinely don't see what's in the gap. That's what makes it a blind spot rather than a cover-up.

This doesn't mean the person deserves what's happening to them. It doesn't mean the response is fair. It just means the story, as told, is incomplete — and the incompleteness is itself informative.

The Narcissistic Defense (And Why It's Not an Insult)

When people feel attacked, a specific psychological defense activates automatically. You can think of it like your abdominal muscles hardening when someone throws a punch at your gut — you don't choose it, it just happens. This is sometimes called the narcissistic defense, and it belongs to all of us.

Under this defense, the mind does a few things: - It amplifies evidence that you were right - It amplifies evidence that others were wrong - It minimizes or omits your own contribution to the problem - It frames the narrative in terms of what you did versus what they did

The result is a story structure that sounds like: I did one small thing. Everyone else did ninety-nine bad things. Even when that story is factually accurate — even when you can prove it — the emotional message people receive is: this person doesn't take responsibility.

The important nuance here is that the more you are attacked, the stronger this defense becomes. It's not a character flaw. It's a stress response. But it creates a feedback loop: the more attacked you feel, the more defended you become, the more people perceive you as not taking accountability, the angrier they get, the more attacked you feel.

Objective vs. Subjective: The Hidden Trap

One of the more subtle dynamics that emerged in this conversation is the habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of framing personal perception as objective fact.

Statements like "the response is disproportionate" or "that's not what really happened" or "these people are just not mature enough to handle proof" are all coded as objective claims. They may even be correct. But when someone consistently presents their own perspective in objective language while implicitly marking other people's responses as subjective or emotional, a pattern emerges that other people find deeply frustrating — even if they can't name exactly why.

The friction is this: if you position yourself as the objective one and others as the subjective ones, you've made your account unfalsifiable. Anyone who disagrees is just emotional. Anyone who pushes back is immature. It's a closed loop that feels like clarity from the inside and like arrogance from the outside.

"I'm being direct. They think I'm being evasive."

That line is the most useful thing that came out of this conversation. Not because it solves anything, but because it names the gap precisely. When you believe you're doing the right thing and the person across from you believes you're doing the wrong thing, and neither of you is lying, you have found the exact location of the problem.

Emotional Communication vs. Logical Communication

Humans are not primarily logical communicators. We are emotional communicators who use logic to explain ourselves after the fact. When you strip the emotional register out of communication — speaking in a flat, evidence-based, here's-what-objectively-happened mode — the words may be accurate, but the emotional signal people receive is often cold, dismissive, or evasive.

This is especially costly in conflict situations. When someone is hurt or angry, they are not primarily looking for a logical account of what happened. They are looking for a signal that you understand how it felt from their side. A complete factual account with one perfunctory acknowledgment of fault early on does not give them that signal. It can actually make things worse, because it demonstrates that you've processed the event entirely in your head and very little in your chest.

The inverse is also true. When emotion does break through — when someone drops the defensive framing and says something raw and unpolished like "I'm not going to make a fake apology video just to get people to leave me alone, that feels disgusting and dishonest" — it lands differently. It's more credible. It connects.

The lesson isn't to perform emotion or manufacture feelings for an audience. It's that the logical framing you use to protect yourself may be the thing that's keeping people away.

Mentalization: The Skill Behind the Gap

Mentalization is the ability to understand the actions of yourself and others in terms of their inner experience — their thoughts, feelings, wishes, and desiresDesires are signals generated by the sense organs (the indriyas) that point toward something you currently lack — creating a gap between what you have and what you want.Full definition →. It is distinct from empathy, though related to it. Empathy is a capacity; mentalization is a skill. You can have high empathy and still be poor at mentalization, and vice versa.

A failure of mentalization looks like: - Seeing other people as one-dimensional (they're just trolls, they're just haters, they're just immature) - Not being curious about what's going on inside someone else when their behavior seems irrational - Explaining your own actions in terms of logic while explaining others' actions in terms of their defects - Missing why you yourself did something, focusing only on what you did

A success of mentalization looks like: - Asking "what would make someone feel this way?" as a genuine question, not a rhetorical one - Recognizing that even people who hate you have information about how you come across in the world - Understanding that what you intended is not the same as what landed - Being able to see yourself the way someone else might see you, without collapsing into shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → or swinging into defensiveness

The person with the strongest grasp of this tends to be the one who can hold two things at once: I believe I acted reasonably and I understand why someone else experienced it differently.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing a logical account with an apology. Explaining what happened in great detail is not the same as acknowledging how it affected someone. People often feel more dismissed after a thorough explanation than they would have after a shorter, more emotionally honest response.

Treating the haters as the whole audience. When you're getting attacked by thousands of people, it becomes nearly impossible to engage with any individual feedback charitably. But this means the constructive signal — the feedback that could actually help you — gets filtered out along with the noise.

Believing that doing the right thing will produce the right result. If you're consistently getting the same outcome and you keep changing variables except one, the one you haven't changed is likely the problem. The person in this conversation had tried silence, tried explanation, tried reaching out — but the underlying communication pattern stayed constant.

Mistaking the narcissistic defense for narcissismA personality pattern — not an identity — rooted in low internal self-worth, where the gap between how little you feel you're worth on the inside gets filled by constant external validation.Full definition →. Being defensive under sustained attack is not a personality disorder. But it can produce the same behavioral signature, which is why people will make that accusation. Understanding the mechanism doesn't make someone a narcissist; it makes them a human being under stress.

Performing emotion instead of accessing it. The answer to "you're too logical and defensive" is not to manufacture an emotional response for optics. People can tell the difference. The goal is genuine access to what you actually feel, not a performance of what feelings look like.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Find your own missing missing reasons. Think of a conflict where you felt the other person's reaction was unfair or disproportionate. Write down the full story as you'd tell it. Then ask: what did I do or say that might have contributed to their experience, that I left out of that account? Don't defend it — just list it.

  2. Code your statements. For one week, when you're in a disagreement, mentally tag each sentence you say or write as either objective-framed ("what really happened was...", "the facts are...") or subjective-framed ("I felt...", "from my side..."). Notice the ratio. A heavy lean toward objective framing in emotional conversations is a flag.

  3. Try the narcissist exercise. When you're confident you're in the right in a conflict, spend five minutes seriously assuming you're a narcissist. What would you be missing? What would you be selectively not seeing? This isn't about self-punishment — it's about temporarily lowering your defenses enough to find the blind spot.

  4. Practice the "what would make them feel this way" question. When someone responds to you with anger or dismissal that seems out of proportion, instead of explaining yourself further, stop and ask: what is going on inside this person that makes this the response? What are they actually experiencing? This is the entry point to mentalization.

  5. Notice when logic is doing emotional work. The next time you're upset and you catch yourself making a precise, evidence-based case for why you were wronged, pause. Ask yourself what you're actually feeling, underneath the argument. You don't have to share it — just locate it. That's the first step toward communicating in a way that actually connects.

  6. Before you add to a pile-on, consider the aggregate. One critical comment, one sarcastic reply, one "just saying" in someone's chat feels like nothing. But if that person is receiving a thousand of those today, you are one of a thousand. The snowflake isn't responsible for the avalanche, but it is part of it.
