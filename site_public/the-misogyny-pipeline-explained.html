Summary

TL;DR — Trying to fix your circumstances to escape hopelessness is itself the thing keeping you stuck. The only way out is to stop running and face the hopelessness directly.

A lot of people who feel deeply hopeless about romance, career, or their own worth have genuinely tried to change things. They've gone to therapy, hit the gym, worked on themselves, put themselves out there again and again. And it hasn't worked. The standard advice — "just try harder," "put yourself out there," "work on yourself first" — doesn't just fail these people; it often makes things measurably worse.

Understanding why that happens, and what to do instead, is what this piece is about.

The Trap of Trying Harder

There's a comforting myth that effort compounds positively — that if at first you don't succeed, each new attempt gets you closer. For people whose baseline odds are decent, that's roughly true. For someone who has accumulated years of rejection and failure, the opposite is closer to reality.

Think of it like an obstacle course late in a long race. By the time you reach the hardest log to vault over, you're already exhausted. Each failed attempt doesn't reset you to neutral — it depletes you further. The sixth attempt is harder than the first, not because the obstacle changed but because you did.

The same dynamic plays out in real life. Every rejection adds a small traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →. Every failed attempt rewires the brain slightly more toward the expectation of failure. This isn't weakness or irrationality — it's the brain doing exactly what it's designed to do: conserve resources by learning not to invest in things that consistently don't pay off. The brain that stops trying to fly by flapping its arms is working correctly.

The cruel twist is that people in this state are often told their hopelessness is the problem to fix indirectly — fix the career, fix the looks, find a partner, and then you'll feel hope. But every attempt to fix those external things runs directly into the accumulated damage, fails, and feeds the hopelessness further. It's a loop that gets tighter the harder you pull.

Why External Fixes Make It Worse

The intuitive model goes: hopelessness is caused by bad circumstances → fix the circumstances → hopelessness goes away.

The problem is that trying to fix circumstances while operating from a place of deep hopelessness is a form of avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →. You're not addressing the hopelessness; you're running away from it by staying busy with external action.

Research on emotion suppression is consistent here: when you avoid or suppress an emotion rather than processing it, that emotion doesn't disappear. It sinks into your subconscious and starts running as background noise, coloring everything you perceive. It creates cognitive bias — not in a careless way, but in a systematic way where the brain filters incoming information through the lens of what it already expects. Someone deep in this state isn't misreading neutral situations randomly; they're reading situations through a lens that has been ground by repeated genuine failure.

A meta-analysis of over 1,100 studies on psychotherapy found that the single biggest predictor of improvement in therapy isn't the technique used or the therapist's approach — it's something called self-relatedness: the patient's capacity to actually know what's going on inside themselves. The people who improve most are the ones who stop being blind to their own inner experience. And the thing that most destroys self-relatedness is avoidance.

This is why the external-fix strategy backfires so consistently. It is, structurally, avoidance. It keeps you oriented outward, away from yourself.

Acceptance Is Not Resignation

This is where the counterintuitive move comes in: to break out of the loop, you have to stop trying to escape the hopelessness and start turning toward it.

That sounds terrifying to someone who has been fighting to keep the feeling at bay. The fear is that accepting the hopelessness means surrendering to it — admitting it's true, giving up, letting it win. But that's a confusion between acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → and resignation.

Acceptance means: I acknowledge that this feeling is real, that my situation is genuinely difficult, that the rejections happened, that the effort I put in didn't produce what it was supposed to. It means not pretending the problem away. Not telling someone "you're so attractive, don't even worry about it" when they don't feel that way. Honest acknowledgment.

Resignation means: This is permanent and unchangeable and there's nothing to be done.

These are not the same thing. In fact, genuine acceptance — sitting with the feeling rather than fleeing it — is what allows the feeling to move. Emotions are designed to reach homeostasis. The wedding joy doesn't last a lifetime; acute grief doesn't either. What makes an emotion persistent isn't its original intensity — it's suppression. Suppressed emotions don't discharge; they accumulate subconsciously and keep acting on you. There's even clinical evidence that administering anti-anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → medication (benzodiazepines) to people in the acute phase of trauma — specifically to prevent them from feeling the distress — actually increases their risk of developing PTSD later. Blocking the emotional experience doesn't protect you from it.

"What keeps it alive forever is your relationship with it — the way you run away from it, and it follows you."

What "Targeting the Hopelessness Directly" Actually Looks Like

The work isn't out in the world. It's internal. Concretely:

  • Acknowledge what you actually feel. Not what you think you should feel, or what you'd feel if life were fair. What you feel right now.
  • Stop the avoidance behavior — not just distraction and numbing, but also the constant project of fixing external things as a way of not sitting with the internal state.
  • Increase self-relatedness. Practice being able to name and notice what's going on inside you. MeditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → is one tool here — not as a relaxation technique, but as a way to let the suppressed material surface and be witnessed without being acted on.
  • Let the feeling exist without immediately solving it. This is the core move. Most people experience this as dangerous. In practice, emotions that are allowed to exist — rather than fought or fled — tend to equilibrate naturally.

Working with a professional can help because it gives you a container for the difficult material to come up in. But the work itself is the swimming in the difficulty, not the avoiding of it.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing acceptance with giving up. Accepting that you feel hopeless right now is not the same as agreeing that hopelessness is your permanent fate. Many people resist acceptance because they think it means admitting the worst-case interpretation is true. It doesn't.

Thinking that because you've tried therapy, you've done the work. Going through the motions of therapy while continuing to avoid the emotional content — showing up but staying on the surface — doesn't produce the self-relatedness that drives improvement. The mechanism that helps is actually engaging with what's uncomfortable, not just attending the sessions.

Treating more effort as the solution. When external attempts keep failing, the instinct is to try harder, try more, try differently — more self-improvement projects, more attempts at dating, more optimizations. But if the underlying hopelessness is the driver of the failures, adding more external attempts just adds more failures and deepens the hopelessness.

Mistaking cognitive bias for objective assessment. The brain's pessimistic predictions after repeated failure aren't random errors — they feel logical and evidence-based. Some of the reasoning may even be accurate. But a brain operating under suppressed hopelessness will systematically filter out disconfirming evidence. That doesn't mean you have to argue yourself out of it; it means the emotional substrate needs to be addressed before the cognitive picture can clear.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the feeling without trying to fix it. Once a day, sit for five minutes and articulate — out loud, in writing, or just internally — exactly what you're feeling. Not why, not what to do about it. Just what it is. "I feel hopeless about X. I feel like Y is never going to change." Let that be the whole exercise.

  2. Notice avoidance patterns. For one week, pay attention to when you launch into problem-solving, self-improvement tasks, or distraction immediately after a bad feeling surfaces. You don't have to stop — just notice that the action might be functioning as a flight response.

  3. Try a meditation session where the goal is to let things come up. Not to relax, not to clear your mind, but to sit still and allow whatever surfaces to surface. If discomfort or dark feelings arise, that's the point — let them exist for a few minutes without acting on them or pushing them away.

  4. Audit whether your self-improvement efforts are aimed outward or inward. Ask yourself honestly: am I going to the gym, pursuing this goal, or working on this because I want to grow — or because doing so lets me not feel the hopelessness for a while? Both can coexist, but it's worth knowing which is driving.

  5. Practice honest acknowledgment instead of forced positivity. When talking to yourself (or someone you trust) about a hard situation, try leading with actual acknowledgment rather than immediate reframe: "This has been really hard and a lot of my efforts haven't worked" before anything about what might change. Notice whether that acknowledgment, even briefly, shifts something.

  6. If suicidal ideation is part of the picture, engage a professional before doing this work solo. Turning toward suppressed hopelessness can surface difficult material. That's exactly what's supposed to happen — but having a trained person to hold that with you makes the process safer.
