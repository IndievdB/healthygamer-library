Summary

TL;DR — Identity, confidence, and suffering aren't abstract philosophical puzzles; they show up in the everyday texture of your life, and you can learn to recognize them.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between a psychiatrist trained in Eastern philosophy and Jason, the creator behind Daily Dose of Internet. What starts as a question about social media's long-term effects opens into an honest exploration of ADHD, self-identity, ego, the Buddhist understanding of desire, how failure shapes character, and a brief live meditation exercise designed specifically for people who "can't meditate."

The Science Can't Keep Up — And That's Worth Knowing

There's a tempting hope that research will eventually tell us exactly what social media is doing to our brains. That hope is probably misplaced, for a structural reason: science studies things at a pace suited to phenomena that stay relatively stable. Lung cancer from tobacco took decades to document, but tobacco didn't fundamentally change during that time. Social media evolves so fast that a study published today is measuring a platform that no longer quite exists. By the time data on today's TikTok or Instagram is peer-reviewed, those products will be different.

The practical implication: you can't wait for science to tell you what social media is doing to you. You already have data — your own experience. That's where to start.

Comparison, Ego, and the Distorted Mirror

Spending hours a day in feeds built on highlights and filters produces a specific kind of distortion. What looks normal on Instagram — bodies, lifestyles, possessions — isn't a representative sample of reality. Most people know this intellectually, and it barely helps.

The reason a tiny disclaimer saying "filter used" doesn't protect you is that knowing something cognitively and feeling it emotionally are different things. Your brain is still receiving a stream of idealized images and implicitly benchmarking against them. Ancient Indian philosophy had a word for this mechanism: ahankara, roughly the "I-feeling" or sense of self. The insight is that comparison degrades ahankara. The more you compare, the worse your sense of self feels — which is what the cliché "comparison is the thief of joy" is actually pointing at.

"The greater your ahankara, the more you're suffering."

The Three Noble Truths and the Cookie Problem

The Buddhist framework that keeps surfacing here:

  1. All life is suffering.
  2. The cause of suffering is desire.
  3. To remove suffering, remove desire.

The obvious objection: when you get what you want, don't you feel happy? Yes — briefly. But then:

  • The cookie is gone. You want another.
  • The quote you were desperately trying to remember: once you shared it, the relief was real, but the suffering that preceded it was caused by the desire to share it in the first place.

Desire doesn't only cause suffering when it goes unmet. It causes suffering in the wanting itself, and often a new wanting appears the moment the first one is satisfied. This isn't an argument for nihilism or passivity — it's an observation about how the mind works that you can actually use.

Ego Isn't Just Arrogance

Most people think of ego as self-importance. But ego — ahankara, the I-feeling — runs in both directions:

"I'm the best person in the world." That's ego. "I'm the worst person in the world." That's also ego.

When you say "I'm not good enough to be called a scholar," that internal voice comparing you to some imagined standard isn't humility. It's ego setting up a comparison you can't win. Genuine not-knowing — I know that I know nothing — is actually different from "I'm not as good as real scholars." The first is open; the second is still a self-judgment.

Who You Are: Neti Neti

From the Hindu tradition: neti neti, meaning "not this, not this." The idea is that any label you can attach to yourself — YouTuber, curious person, ADHD guy, bank worker — describes something you do or a role you play, not the underlying person. You existed before any of those labels, and you'd exist without them.

This isn't a reason to say "I don't know who I am" as a dodge. It's a pointer toward the part of you that's been consistent all along, underneath the roles and the confidence levels. If you changed dramatically at 23 when you first lived independently, something was still there before 23 — you just didn't have the confidence to recognize it or act on it.

"I think you've been a person all along. You just didn't have the confidence to know it."

Ahimsa vs. Fear: The Surgeon Problem

Ahimsa — non-violence, non-harm — is a genuine ethical ideal. But there's a difference between living by a principle and being controlled by a fear that looks like that principle.

A surgeon who is so afraid of a patient dying on the table that they refuse to operate isn't practicing ahimsa. They're letting fear masquerade as care, and patients who need surgery don't get it. The same logic applies to relationships, creative risk, career decisions — any domain where the fear of causing harm becomes a reason to not show up at all.

The test: is "I don't want to hurt anyone" functioning as a compass that guides how you act, or as a wall that prevents you from acting? One gives you strength; the other keeps you stuck.

A related pairing: satya (truthfulness) is a high value — but in this ethical framework, it's secondary to ahimsa. If a truth will genuinely harm someone, the principle of non-harm takes precedence. Radical honesty that's just cruelty with good branding isn't the goal.

Failure as Foundation

There's a specific advantage to having failed significantly before succeeding: you know what failure actually feels like, so it loses its power to terrorize you. People who have never really failed often can't distinguish between a setback and a catastrophe. When they hit the inevitable difficult stretch, it feels like the end, because they have no evidence that they can survive and recover.

The bank job that felt like wasted years, the monotonous performance reviews — these aren't just obstacles to route around in hindsight. They're part of what makes the success feel like something, and part of what makes it sustainable.

The Blood Mage Pattern

Content creators often run on a model where success is funded by sacrificing rest, health, and time off. The analogy: a blood mage in video game terms — instead of spending mana to cast spells, they spend HP. You get the output, but you pay out of your own lifeblood.

This is common and widely normalized in high-performance environments (investment banking runs the same way). Normal doesn't mean healthy. The absence of burnout isn't the same as genuine wellbeing. The more sustainable fuel source is a mix of genuine gratitude for where you are and a separation from the fear of going back — rather than running primarily on that fear.

Alzheimer's, Novelty, and the Brain

On the question of whether gaming protects against Alzheimer's: the variable that matters most isn't mental activity in general — it's novel mental activity. The brain appears to benefit from new experiences that demand it build new connections. If you've been playing the same game for 40 years, that's not novel. Learning to make YouTube videos at 70 — that qualifies. Heart health also matters enormously; much of the dementing process involves blood vessels in the brain getting compromised, which impairs the brain's ability to clear waste products from neurons. Physical health isn't separate from cognitive health.

Meditation for an ADHD Brain

Standard mindfulness instruction — "observe your breath, let thoughts pass" — doesn't work well for minds that are already running fast. Telling a racing mind to be still is like trying to stop a car by pushing against the front bumper.

An alternative: give the mind so many things to track simultaneously that it can't wander. The exercise demonstrated here uses standing balance with eyes closed while answering questions. With eyes open and no balance challenge, the mind drifts. Close the eyes, and suddenly proprioception is overwhelmed, the balance task demands full attention, and the chatter stops — not because you told it to, but because the mind had no spare capacity left.

This is also why advanced yoga postures exist. The increasing physical demand is there to continuously reclaim your mental attention as each posture becomes familiar and easy. Yoga is a mental practice that uses the body as its instrument.

"If meditation is hard for you, that means you're not learning it the right way."

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for the studies. Especially with technology, the research will lag too far behind to be actionable. Your own experience is your most current data.
  • Confusing intellectual knowledge with felt understanding. Knowing that Instagram is filtered doesn't immunize you from its emotional effects.
  • Treating ego as only the inflated version. Self-deprecation and imposter syndrome are ego operating in the other direction — same mechanism, different costume.
  • Mistaking fear for principle. "I don't want to hurt anyone" sounds like ahimsa. Check whether it's functioning as ethical guidance or as avoidance.
  • Assuming novelty = more. More games, more content, more mental activity isn't the same as new mental activity. The brain responds to genuine novelty, not volume.
  • Trying to meditate by slowing down an ADHD mind. This approach is almost guaranteed to fail. The path is through intensity, not stillness.
  • Normalizing the blood mage pattern. "I haven't had a day off in years but I feel fine" is a data point worth examining — not dismissing, but not accepting uncritically either.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do a personal audit instead of waiting for research. Write down two or three specific ways you've noticed social media affecting how you think about your life, body, or success. You already have this data.

  2. Catch comparison in real time. When you feel inadequate after scrolling, name what specifically you were comparing. Putting it into words interrupts the automatic process.

  3. Try the neti neti exercise. List every label you use to describe yourself (job, role, trait, diagnosis). For each one, note: "I existed before this. I am not only this." Notice what's left.

  4. Check whether a fear is actually a principle. Pick something you're avoiding because you "don't want to hurt someone" (or "don't want to fail," or "don't want to mess up"). Ask honestly: is this guiding how I act, or preventing me from acting at all?

  5. For ADHD meditation: try the balance exercise. Stand on one foot. Close your eyes. Try to also answer a question or recall a memory. When you find the level of difficulty where your mind goes quiet because it's fully occupied — stay there for 2–5 minutes. As it gets easier, increase the difficulty (harder posture, eyes closed longer, more complex questions).

  6. Introduce genuine novelty. Pick one thing you've never done before — an instrument, a language, a craft, a route home — and do it regularly for a month. The goal is to keep creating the conditions for neuroplasticity, not just to stay busy.

  7. Name your fuel source. Are you driven primarily by love of what you do, or by fear of going back to something bad? Both can produce results. Only one of them leaves room for peace.
