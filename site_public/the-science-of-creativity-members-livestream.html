Summary

TL;DR — Creativity isn't a mysterious gift you either have or don't; it's a set of learnable skills backed by neuroscience, with an optional spiritual dimension that can take it further.

Most people treat creativity as something that either strikes you or doesn't. That framing makes you passive. The better framing: creativity has identifiable components — cognitive, behavioral, and even spiritual — and you can systematically strengthen each one. This lesson covers what those components are, what the brain is actually doing during creative work, and what concrete habits produce consistently more creative output.

Two Flavors of Creativity

Before getting into how to be more creative, it helps to know there are two fundamentally different kinds:

  • Convergent thinking: working through a problem toward a single solution
  • Divergent thinking: going around a problem — finding a new angle, reframing it entirely

These aren't just different strategies; they involve different brain states. Researchers have identified four neural "modes" that produce creative output, formed by combining two axes:

Most people chase only spontaneous creativity. But deliberate creativity — showing up with a system and working through a problem — is where the bulk of consistent creative output actually comes from.

Three Prerequisites Before Any Neuroscience

There are three foundational things your brain needs before creative output is even possible.

1. A semantic apparatus — a model of your domain

Creative people don't just "think harder." They have a structured way of breaking a problem into pieces. A novelist understands character, plot, foreshadowing, and dramatic irony. A painter works with foreground/background, shadow, and light. A doctor diagnosing a patient has a framework of differential diagnoses.

Without a model, you're left with a binary like "I'm lazy" — which gives you nothing to work with. Now you have handles. Now you can move pieces around.

"Once you understand all of those components, it gives your brain a way to break apart this abstract thing into different pieces."

2. The ability to articulate your problem

This is related but distinct. Most people skip straight to hypotheses ("I can't get a date because my jaw is too small") without ever accurately diagnosing the problem. Good diagnosis precedes good treatment. Look at any internet thread where someone complains — what you almost always find is a failure to clearly define what's actually wrong. Coaches and therapists are effective largely because they help people understand their problem. Often, once the problem is clearly seen, the solution becomes nearly self-evident.

3. The right inputs

Creativity is largely the brain reassembling old ideas in new combinations. The quality of the output depends on the quality of the pieces.

"The solution to writer's block is to read more."

If you're stuck creatively, the first question to ask isn't "How do I get unstuck?" It's "What am I feeding my brain?" Playing the same video game for 10,000 hours is not the same as studying many different games. Listening to authors talk about their craft on a podcast is a different kind of input than reading their books. Diverse inputs give the subconscious more material to work with.

What the Brain Is Actually Doing: Four Frontal Lobe Functions

The frontal lobesThe brain region responsible for planning, executing tasks, controlling impulses, and regulating other parts of the brain — essentially the hardware of self-regulation and mature behavior.Full definition → are the most important brain region for creativity. They're also what most separates human cognition from other animals. Here are the four specific capacities they provide — and what you can do to strengthen each.

1. Cognitive FlexibilityThe ability to let go of a fixed belief or way of thinking and genuinely consider a different one.Full definition → (vs. Perseveration)

Perseveration is getting stuck in one mode of thinking — running the same track over and over even when it doesn't work. Creativity, by definition, is doing something differently. Cognitive flexibility is the ability to shift approaches.

This is also, notably, the cognitive variable most associated with improving at competitive games. Players stuck at a rank aren't failing because of mechanical skill — they're perseverating. The same applies to any life domain.

The simplest way to build cognitive flexibility: task switching. Leave the work. Come back to it later. Stephen King's advice to put a draft in a drawer for six months is a task-switching prescription. When you return with fresh eyes, you're not stuck in the cognitive rut you were in before.

2. Working Memory

Working memory is the RAM of the brain — how much you can hold in mind simultaneously while building something. A low working memory means you can't keep all the pieces of a creative project in view at once.

Two ways to support it:

  • External tools: JK Rowling used spreadsheets to track plot threads, character arcs, and subplots across Harry Potter. Externalizing information frees up mental bandwidth. Find whatever system works for you — outlines, index cards, whiteboards.
  • Clearing mental clutter: If you're stressed about your relationship, your finances, or a dozen unresolved problems, those occupy working memory. Your brain can't hold the pieces of your creative project when its hands are already full. Resolving background stressors directly increases creative capacity.

3. Sustained Attention

There's a gap between having a creative idea and making something with it. The bridge is sustained attention — the ability to stay with a problem even when it stops being easy or exciting.

"If you can stare at a blank page until you start writing, and if that staring involves eight hours, you will be creative."

4. Judgment of Propriety (Context-Dependent Appropriateness)

This is the underrated one. The frontal lobes give us the ability to sense what fits — what goes where, when something belongs, when it's too much or not enough. This is why the Mona Lisa's slight smile feels perfect rather than broad or absent. It's why the bass drop at exactly the right moment is satisfying and the wrong moment ruins the song.

If you want to develop this capacity, pay attention to the moments in art or music where something lands — and equally to the moments where something feels off. Your frontal lobes are doing the evaluation; you can train them by attending to it consciously.

The Subconscious as a Creative Engine

The brain does enormous amounts of processing below conscious awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →. This is the neuroscience underneath the "incubation" advice that almost every creative professional eventually discovers on their own.

Two ingredients are required for incubation: substrate (inputs — what you've fed the brain) and time.

"You need to give your subconscious time to act because it's going to put together all kinds of combinations and permutations, and then it will float things up to your consciousness."

What this looks like practically: - Sleep: Short-term memories consolidate to long-term memory during sleep. This is when connections form. Protecting your sleep is not optional for creative work. - Walks and idle time: Mind wandering during rest appears to be conducive to creativity — probably because it combines mild task-switching with reduced working memory load, giving the subconscious room to operate. - Periods of focus, then rest: The formula seems to be: focused input, then sustained attention on the work, then a real break, then back to it. Not grinding continuously, and not wandering indefinitely.

The Skill Gap: Creative Impulse vs. Creative Expression

Having an idea is not the same as making the thing. The brain needs a high level of technical skill to translate a creative impulse into reality. This is unglamorous but unavoidable.

If you want to write a novel, you need to become a better writer. If you want to make a game, you need to learn to code. The 10,000-hour figure is a rough approximation of the floor — medical residency runs about 13,000 hours, for context. The point is that creative expression requires expressive capability, and that capability must be built through deliberate practice.

"There's no way around it — and to understand why you need to grind: it's not enough to have an idea. Your brain literally needs the capacity to translate that idea into a reality."

Removing Conditioning: The Spiritual Dimension

Here the lens shifts from neuroscience to something older. The Nobel Prize-winning experiments that are worth studying share a common theme: the breakthroughs didn't come from building on existing frameworks — they came from seeing things as they actually are rather than as convention assumed them to be.

This is the yogic concept of kleshaThe mental coloring — judgments, meanings, and emotional weight — that the mind automatically adds to raw sensory experience, on top of what is simply, factually there.Full definition → (often transliterated as "glisha" in the talk) — the colorings or conditionings we overlay on raw experience. Every prior emotional experience, every assumption, every mental habit — these filter perception. They make it harder to see a situation freshly.

"The more we remove our mental conditioning, the more creative we will become."

Reducing klesha is, in cognitive science terms, the fastest way to increase cognitive flexibility — because you're approaching each situation as genuinely new rather than mapping it onto past patterns. This is also why Stephen King's "drawer" technique works: six months strips the conditioning of the original creative act from your perception.

Practices that help: - Meditation: Directly reduces habitual mental overlays - Task switching: Mechanically resets conditioning - Noticing the absurdity of situations: Humor is often the spontaneous recognition that a situation doesn't map onto your expectations — which is a form of fresh perception

Asking for Help Beyond Yourself

The most extreme version of this, and the most optional, involves something like worship or surrender — not necessarily in a religious sense, but in the psychological act of acknowledging that you can't force creative inspiration.

The ego is one of the biggest blockers of creative work. You write something, look at it, decide it's terrible, and stop. When you frame your creative project as something larger than your personal reputation — something you're doing with help, not by proving yourself — the ego loosens its grip. The outcome becomes less precious. You keep going.

"I can't do this on my own, and I need your help — can you help me create this thing?"

This reframe has a documented psychological analog: growth mindset research shows that treating ability as expandable rather than fixed leads to better performance. Worship, surrender, or simply asking "the universe" for help is one route into that frame.

The specific direction of that request (sarasvati yantra, a bodhisattva associated with creativity, a patron saint, or nothing in particular) may or may not matter. The act of surrendering the outcome, and removing ego from the equation, is where the functional benefit comes from.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing hypothesis with diagnosis. When people say "my problem is X because of Y," they've often just offered a theory — not actually examined what the problem is. Spending time genuinely diagnosing the problem almost always does more good than jumping to solutions.

Treating inputs as a single type. Doing the same creative-adjacent thing for thousands of hours (playing one game, reading one genre) is not the same as diverse inputs. The brain needs variety of substrate to form novel combinations.

Confusing exhaustion with incapacity. Feeling tired and being unable to act are different things. Behavioral activation — doing something even when you don't feel like it — is an evidence-based intervention for depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →. The question isn't "why am I exhausted?" but "why can't I act when I'm exhausted?"

Waiting for spontaneous creativity and ignoring deliberate creativity. Spontaneous insight is real but can't be scheduled. Deliberate creative work — showing up, using a model, diagnosing the problem, grinding the skill — can be. Most consistent creative output comes from the deliberate side, with spontaneous moments as a bonus.

Skipping skill development. It's tempting to focus on the mindset and habit elements. But if you can't code, your game idea stays an idea. The expressive medium requires its own investment of practice.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Build your semantic model. Pick one domain where you want to be more creative. Write down every component, dimension, or element you can think of. If you can't fill a page, your model is too thin — research, read, or find a teacher to expand it.

  2. Practice problem diagnosis before solution-seeking. The next time you're stuck on something, spend 20 minutes writing only about what the problem is — not what you think causes it or how to fix it. Try to be as precise and specific as possible.

  3. Audit and diversify your inputs. Look at your creative inputs for the last month. Are they genuinely varied? Introduce at least two new types: a book in a genre you don't usually read, a podcast where practitioners discuss their craft, a documentary, a visit to a gallery or museum.

  4. Use task switching deliberately. When you hit a wall on a creative project, stop and do something completely unrelated for at least a few hours — ideally overnight. Return to the project before doing anything stimulating the next morning.

  5. Create a dedicated creative space. Identify one physical location that you will use only for your creative work. Sit there consistently. Over time your brain will begin entering focus mode when you arrive.

  6. Protect your sleep and build in idle time. Treat sleep as creative infrastructure, not optional recovery. Add at least one daily walk or other low-stimulation activity where your mind can wander without a screen.

  7. Start an external working memory system. Use whatever tool fits you — a notebook, a spreadsheet, index cards — to externalize the moving pieces of your current creative project. The goal is to get it out of your head so your brain has room to build.

  8. Meditate daily, starting with om. Even 10–15 minutes. This builds sustained attention, reduces klesha, and supports working memory by clearing background mental noise. Start simple: chant "om" in a single breath, notice where the vibration sits in your body.

  9. Notice absurdity. Once a day, look at a frustrating or stuck situation in your life and ask: how would this look in the first 15 minutes of a comedy film? This is a lightweight practice for building the fresh perception that underlies both humor and creative insight.

  10. Try surrender. Before beginning a creative session, take 60 seconds to explicitly acknowledge that you don't know if this will be good, you're not trying to prove anything, and you're open to whatever comes. Notice whether this changes how you work.
