Summary

TL;DR — Turning a hobby into your whole personality isn't enthusiasm; it's a symptom of not yet having a secure sense of who you are. And the fix isn't finding the right hobby faster — it's doing the harder work of building identity directly.

There's a recognizable pattern: you try something new, get a spark of excitement, and suddenly you're mentally relocating to Bali to become a wellness instructor. The impulse feels like discovery, but it's actually hunger — a search for an identity that keeps misfiring because the hunger itself distorts your perception. This happens to a lot of people, but it's especially common when you've grown up with ADHD, a history of negative experiences, or both. Understanding why it happens is the only way to actually stop the cycle.

The Identity Vacuum That Starts It All

Who you are — your sense of self — is built from emotional experiences strung together into a narrative. Not big dramatic ones necessarily. Small ones count too: the 45-minute walk to the grocery store. The moment a psychiatrist ate an apple core in front of a stunned room of medical students. These moments accumulate and become the story you tell about yourself.

If you grew up with ADHD, the emotional experiences you accumulated were disproportionately negative: getting in trouble, falling behind, being left out. You didn't build a rich, positive identity — you built a deficit. The one exit ramp many kids find is becoming the class clown, trading acceptance for laughs. But that stops working in adulthood, and you're left without a reliable answer to "who am I?"

That vacuum is what makes a new hobby feel like so much more than a hobby. It isn't just an activity — it's a candidate identity. It's a possible life. And you're starving for one.

Why the Hunger Backfires

When you're desperately searching for something, that desperation becomes a cognitive bias. You get a small, genuine signal — hey, yoga felt good — and your hungry brain amplifies it into a revelation. You haven't found a new interest; you've found yourself. You start planning the move to Bali.

The problem is that the excitement isn't actually tracking reality. It's tracking the relief of thinking the search might finally be over.

"If you are looking to find the thing you are falling in love with, the chances are you won't fall in love with it."

Both times this came up personally — deciding to be celibate before meeting a spouse, deciding to cure cancer before stumbling into psychiatry — the real thing arrived sideways, not through direct searching. In hindsight, genuine self-discovery tends to feel more like being dragged somewhere uphill than smoothly clicking into place. Or, strangely, both at once: an uphill battle that somehow feels natural.

The frantic search also creates a compounding problem. Each hobby that turns out not to be the thing increases the desperation, which lowers the threshold for the next false alarm, which means you cycle faster and faster through potential personalities — never staying long enough to actually build anything.

The ADHD-Specific Mechanism

People with ADHD are more vulnerable to this cycle for a neurological reason that goes deeper than "dopamine."

The motivational center of the brain receives inputs from multiple sources simultaneously: - The frontal lobe, which does long-term planning ("we have a test Monday") - The nucleus accumbens, which tracks immediate reward ("dopamine, let's go") - The limbic system/amygdala, which processes emotion ("this is exciting and scary")

Each of these "advisers" carries a different weight in the final behavioral decision. Crucially, the weight of each adviser is determined by how often you listen to it. If your behavior has always been governed by excitement — if the limbic system wins every time — that adviser gets stronger and stronger. The long-term planning voice gets ignored and grows weaker.

This is the real reason people with ADHD start things and stop things. It's not simply a dopamine deficiency. It's that behavior has become almost entirely dependent on emotional excitement. When the excitement fades — which it always does — the behavior disappears with it. The person hasn't changed. The brain circuit governing follow-through just never got strengthened.

"The brightest flame burns out the fastest."

Going from zero to full personality adoption in 48 hours burns through the available excitement before the habit has any independent footing. The hobby never becomes part of you — it just exhausts you.

Managing Excitement Is an Underrated Skill

Most advice about ADHD focuses on managing negative emotions — anxiety, frustration, overwhelm. But the more underrated skill is managing positive emotions. Learning to modulate excitement.

This doesn't mean suppressing enthusiasm or never getting excited about new things. It means not letting excitement do all the work. When you feel that "oh my god, is THIS the thing?" surge, notice it. Name it. Understand that the intensity of the feeling is partially a product of how long you've been searching, not solely a signal about the quality of what you found.

Rein it in deliberately. Keep the hobby a hobby for now. Don't restructure your life around it on day three. This isn't pessimism — it's actually what allows the thing to last. When excitement isn't burned up all at once, it sustains longer. And the activity gets a chance to prove itself on slower, more reliable grounds.

The Identity Question You Actually Have to Answer

Here's the uncomfortable part. If your hobby isn't your identity, then what is your identity? That's not a question you solve by finding the right hobby. It's a question you solve by doing the work of answering it directly.

That means actually sitting with questions like: - Who do you think you are right now? - Where did that idea of yourself come from? - Who would you like to be? - What small things can you do today to become that person?

The answers don't arrive in a flash of inspiration tied to a new interest. They're built slowly, through accumulated small choices and small experiences — many of them uncomfortable, many of them unremarkable from the outside.

Somewhere along the way, hard became a signal to find an easier solution rather than a signal to build something. But the human body and the human brain strengthen through hardship and weaken through ease. The adviser in your brain that handles long-term follow-through only gets stronger if you actually listen to it — which means tolerating the difficulty of doing things when excitement isn't carrying you.

Common Pitfalls

"I know the pattern already." Knowing a pattern intellectually and actually understanding it are different things. Most people who think they understand their problem are missing critical elements — usually about what the behavior is solving for them, not just what it's costing them. A hobby-as-identity isn't just escapism; it's a genuine attempt to fix real problems (motivation, belonging, self-concept). Treating it as purely bad behavior misses the point.

Trying to fix the wrong thing. If you keep thinking "I just need to find the right hobby and then I'll stick with it," you're still in the cycle. The issue isn't the quality of the hobby; it's what you're asking the hobby to do for you.

Confusing excitement management with joylessness. Modulating excitement doesn't mean flattening it. It means not letting it be the only thing holding your behavior together. You can be genuinely interested in something and still not move to Bali about it.

Treating difficulty as a design flaw. When something feels hard, the instinct is to find a workaround. Sometimes that's smart. But when the hard thing is developing an internal capacity — follow-through, patience, identity — routing around it means that capacity never grows.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the excitement spike before you act on it. Next time you feel the "THIS IS MY THING" surge, write it down — what you're excited about and how intense it feels on a 1–10 scale. Don't reorganize your life yet. Give it two weeks. Then revisit.

  2. Ask what the hobby is solving, not just what it offers. Before committing to a new identity, get honest about what problem you're hoping this will fix. Motivation issues? Loneliness? A vague feeling of being behind in life? Name the actual problem — because that's the thing that needs addressing directly.

  3. Answer the identity questions in writing. Not abstractly. Literally write out: Who do I think I am right now? Where did I get that idea? Who do I want to be? What's one small thing I can do today toward that? Return to these questions monthly, not just once.

  4. Deliberately strengthen the long-term planning adviser. Pick one thing that requires follow-through without a reward payoff at the end — something with delayed returns. Do it consistently for 30 days specifically to build that neural circuit, not because you're excited about it.

  5. Keep one new interest intentionally small for 30 days. Try the thing. Enjoy the thing. But explicitly don't buy the full kit, don't tell everyone, don't plan the career pivot. Just do the thing twice a week for a month and see what's still there after the initial excitement has settled.
