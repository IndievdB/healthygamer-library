A Brief History: From Bacteria to Humans

The word "asexuality" was originally a biology term. It described microorganisms that reproduce without sexual contact — bacteria with F-plasmids that can transfer genetic material without pairing. It had nothing to do with human sexuality when first coined.

Epidemiology: How Common Is Asexuality?

Roughly 1–3% of the population appears to be asexual, based on available studies. The core feature is a lack of sexual attraction or interest — not a physiological inability, but an absence of the mental pull toward sexual activity or specific people.

Biological Markers and Variant Stability

There are several biological correlates of asexuality, suggesting it isn't simply a choice or psychological state:

  • Atypical menstruation in asexual women (higher rate of irregular cycles)
  • Delayed puberty onset
  • Non-right-handedness (more common in both asexual and homosexual populations — something in prenatal or early brain development seems connected)
  • Later birth order — children born later to a mother have a higher likelihood of being asexual or homosexual, possibly due to maternal antibodies to Y-chromosome proteins developing over successive pregnancies

One important note on stability: in one longitudinal study of adolescents who identified as asexual, only a fraction maintained that identification into adulthood. This suggests asexuality in younger people may be more fluid than it appears — which is relevant for how quickly anyone should lock in that label.
