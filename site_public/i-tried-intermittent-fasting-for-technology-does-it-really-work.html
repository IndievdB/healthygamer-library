Summary

TL;DR — Restricting all technology use to an eight-hour daily window frees up roughly 40 hours a week and forces a level of focus that makes both work and leisure dramatically more intentional.

Most attempts to build a healthier relationship with technology follow one of two paths: either cut it all out for a stretch (a dopamine detox), or try to set vague limits that collapse the moment work or social life demands the phone back. Neither solves the core problem, which is how to sustainably reintegrate technology without it bleeding into every corner of your life. Intermittent fasting for technology (IF for tech) is a structural answer: give yourself a fixed daily window — say, eight hours — and treat everything outside that window the same way a faster treats food outside their eating window. Off limits, not negotiable, with a small number of genuine exceptions.

Where Your Time Actually Goes

Before the solution makes sense, the math needs to make sense.

There are 168 hours in a week. Subtract: - 56 hours for sleep (8 hrs/night) - 40 hours for work - 10 hours for overflow work, errands, groceries - 7 hours for cooking and cleaning (~1 hr/day) - 3.5 hours for morning routines - 7 hours for exercise (1 hr/day) - 7 hours for commuting (1 hr/day round-trip)

That still leaves roughly 35–40 free hours per week. So why does no one feel like they have that time?

The average person spends four hours a day on their phone and nearly seven hours a day on screens total. That's 49–77 hours a week going into screens — more time than most people spend at work. The feeling of being perpetually out of time isn't really about capitalism or overwork. It's about where those 40 free hours are silently disappearing.

"You know how long it takes to read a book? The average book is 80,000 words. The average human reads at 300 words a minute — that means the average person can finish the average book in six hours. We spend one book's worth of time on our cell phones every single day."

Why Ordinary Limits Always Collapse

Technology platforms are engineered specifically to prevent you from leaving. Icon colors, notification tones, thumbnail selection, algorithmic recommendations — all of it has been extensively A/B tested to maximize the time you stay on the platform, because longer engagement means more revenue. This isn't incidental; it's the business model.

The problem this creates is that almost every legitimate reason to pick up your phone — checking a work calendar, looking up a recipe, texting a friend to make plans — becomes a doorway into a much longer unintended session. You opened the app for one thing. The app was designed to give you seventeen other things before you leave. And because the reason you picked it up was legitimate, it's hard to feel like you did anything wrong.

Traditional solutions (dopamine detox, screen-time limits, app blockers) work in the short term but leave the reintegration question unanswered. The moment you come back, you're in the same environment with no structural guardrail, and the same drift resumes.

How Intermittent Fasting for Technology Works

The core rule: all technology — phone, computer, TV, streaming — is restricted to an eight-hour window per day.

A practical window might be 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. Before 10, no screens. After 6, no screens.

A few clarifications worth working through upfront: - Basic appliances (microwave, car) don't count — use common sense - GPS and brief coordination texts (e.g., picking up your kids) can be allowed exceptions - Genuine emergencies — a patient call, a family crisis — are reasonable exceptions - Music and audiobooks are judgment calls; experimenting with cutting them too can reveal how much passive audio fills silence that might otherwise become thinking time

The goal isn't purity. It's creating a container small enough that you have to be intentional about what goes inside it.

What Actually Changes When You Try It

The most immediate shift is in the mornings. A typical morning without the constraint looks like: wake up, check calendar, check email, mentally rehearse the to-do list, browse Reddit, spend 60–90 minutes "preparing" for the day without doing anything. You've stressed yourself out, consumed a feed of bad news, and produced nothing.

With an eight-hour window, the hours before the fast opens become genuinely productive in a different way. You sit with paper. You think through what actually matters today before any notification tells you what to react to. You write things down, make lists, collect your thoughts.

"Usually we sit down to use a device and we don't really know why we're using it. And the more that devices can do multiple things, the easier it is to not know why you're using it when you sit down."

When the window opens and you do sit down, something shifts: you know exactly what you're there to do. Shopping takes five minutes instead of forty-five because you have a list and you're not browsing. Writing gets done faster because the thinking happened offline first. Meetings are more focused because you're not context-switching.

The flight-day analogy captures it well: when you have to leave for the airport at 1 p.m., you're ruthlessly focused from the moment you arrive at work. You do the things. You leave. The world doesn't fall apart. IF for tech makes every day feel like that.

Another surprise: the claimed "40 free hours" actually show up. In two to three weeks of implementing this, four books got read. Gardening happened. The backlog of life tasks that never get done — because there's "no time" — started getting done.

Changing Your Relationship with Boredom

There's a direct parallel between hunger in food-based intermittent fasting and boredom in technology fasting. Right now, boredom gets immediately suppressed with a dopaminergic reach for the phone. The suppression isn't even that satisfying anymore — which is why many people end up watching TV while scrolling, needing two screens simultaneously just to keep the boredom at bay.

The shift required: boredom is a sensation, not an emergency. It passes. It can even be useful — boredom is often the precondition for actual thinking.

"Just like hunger, it's a sensation, it's a concern, it's a worry. What's more important to you — gaining 40 hours a week, or being less bored? That's literally what the trade-off is."

In the early days, going for a walk with paper and a pen is one of the most effective replacements. Sitting outside. Doing physical tasks that don't require a screen. These feel small, but they recalibrate the baseline so that silence doesn't immediately feel like a problem to solve.

Common Pitfalls

Treating the planning step as optional. Just like food-based IF fails without meal planning, tech IF fails without daily planning. If you don't decide what you're doing during your window before you open it, you'll default to browsing. The planning is the intervention.

Setting the window but not the boundary. "I'll try to keep it to eight hours" is not the same as a hard stop. The structure only works if it's a genuine constraint, not a rough target.

Making too many exceptions too fast. It's tempting to immediately carve out music, audiobooks, "just a quick check," and social media as special cases. Each exception is legitimate on its own. Together, they rebuild the old pattern. Start strict; loosen only what genuinely needs loosening.

Mistaking productivity apps for non-technology. The window is for all electronics with internet access or notification systems. A calendar app inside the window is fine; compulsively checking it before the window opens is the exact behavior you're trying to change.

Expecting the first few days to feel good. They probably won't. Boredom will spike. The urge to check the phone will be frequent and feel urgent. This is expected and temporary — treat it the way someone treats hunger on day two of a fast.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the time audit. Before starting, track your actual screen time for three days using your phone's built-in screen-time tool. Include TV and computer use as best you can. Most people underestimate this number significantly.

  2. Choose your window. Pick an eight-hour block that fits your actual schedule. Account for work hours. A window like 9 a.m.–5 p.m. or 10 a.m.–6 p.m. works well for most people. Write it down.

  3. Define your exceptions in advance. Before day one, decide what counts as a genuine exception (medical emergencies, urgent family coordination). Write those down too. If something isn't on the pre-approved list, it waits until the window opens.

  4. Start each morning with paper. Before your window opens, sit down with a notebook and write out what you actually need to accomplish today — work tasks, errands, purchases, communications. This list becomes your agenda when the window opens. You're not discovering your priorities via email; you're setting them yourself.

  5. Prepare a boredom plan. Identify two or three non-screen things you'll default to when boredom hits: a walk route, a physical book, a household project, a hobby. Having these ready removes the moment of decision when restlessness peaks.

  6. Commit to two weeks minimum. The first few days involve friction. The payoff — reclaimed hours, sharper focus, actual completion of things you've been putting off — shows up in the second week. Give it enough time to actually evaluate.

  7. Log what you do with the recovered time. Each evening, write a sentence or two about what happened in the non-screen hours. This builds the evidence that boredom didn't kill you and that the time is going somewhere real.
