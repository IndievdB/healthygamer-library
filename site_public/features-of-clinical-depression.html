What "Clinical Depression" Actually Means

It's a shorthand for a depressive episode, which can be caused by several different conditions. The most common one is Major Depressive DisorderA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → (MDD) — defined as persistent low mood lasting at least two weeks and sometimes up to roughly a year.

A few stats worth knowing: - About one in three adults will experience MDD over their lifetime. - It typically starts around puberty but hits hardest between ages 18 and 29. - Women are diagnosed two to three times more often than men — but this gap is likely a measurement artifact. Common Pitfalls

Mistaking depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → for personality. When someone has lived with MDD since adolescence, the symptoms become the baseline. This is probably the single biggest reason people don't seek help: they don't know anything is wrong because they've never experienced anything else.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do an honest symptom inventory. Go through the nine criteria and honestly note which, if any, you've been experiencing for two weeks or more. Don't count, don't diagnose — just observe. Write it down if that helps.

  2. Check how long it's been going on. Duration matters. A bad week after a breakup is different from a persistent pattern over months or years. Try to trace when things started feeling this way.

  3. Ask yourself if this feels "normal" for you. If your honest answer is "yes, this is just how I've always been" — that's actually a reason to get evaluated, not a reason to skip it. Lifelong symptoms are easy to normalize.

  4. Pay attention to the three high-specificity features. AnhedoniaThe inability to experience pleasure — and, critically, the motivational collapse that follows from it.Full definition →, negative self-attitude that deflects reassurance, and early morning awakening with immediate racing thoughts are the ones worth taking most seriously. If any of these are present alongside other symptoms, weight that heavily.

  5. Get an actual evaluation. If you're on the fence — maybe you have it, maybe you don't — that uncertainty is exactly what a mental health professional is for. Use this information as a prompt to book an appointment, not as a substitute for one.
