What a Narrative Actually Does

A narrative is not just a story. It has a specific psychological structure: multiple events organized in a temporal sequence, with emotional activation tied to that sequence.

When your brain builds a narrative, it must acknowledge that certain events happened before other events. That temporal ordering is the mechanism. You cannot string together "first this, then this, then this" without implicitly placing earlier events in the past. The act of constructing the sequence is what begins moving those events from the PCC — the present — into the hippocampusThe brain's central hub for memory and learning — and, critically, the structure that consolidates short-term information into long-term understanding.Full definition → — memory.

Consider a patient who spent over a decade addicted to opiates. He eventually got clean, confronted all the underlying problems the opiates had been masking, and became a writer — channeling the raw emotional experience of hopelessness and powerlessness into dystopian fiction that made him successful. His statement that "opiates were the best thing that ever happened to me" wasn't denial or toxic positivity. His brain had genuinely reorganized the experience: this happened, it led to this, which led to this, which is who I am now. The structure of that story — a problem, steps through it, an outcome — is exactly what a narrative is.

Emotion Is Not Optional

Here's the part people often miss: telling the story in a flat, detached way doesn't work. Emotional activation is a required ingredient.

"You can talk about something, but if you're mentally neutral about it, if you're not really emotionally engaged with what you talk about, then there's really like no healing that goes on."

Something about re-experiencing the emotion while recalling the event is what allows the brain to process and file it away. This shows up in multiple therapeutic modalities:

  • In psychotherapy, moments of catharsis — where strong feelings surface during a story and then get metabolized — are understood as the actual mechanism of healing, not a side effect.
  • In EMDR, you recall a traumatic memory while simultaneously tracking a moving object (like a therapist's finger). This modulates the emotional intensity so it doesn't overwhelm you, while still keeping you emotionally present enough for processing to occur.
  • In narrative exercises like writing the story of your relationship with a substance, the power comes from the emotional rawness of speaking your truth out loud, not from intellectual analysis.

The emotion doesn't have to be tears. It can be shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition →, guilt, or anger. If someone you love was harmed, allowing yourself to feel and acknowledge the fury toward whoever caused that harm can be a genuine part of healing — not something to suppress.

Finding Your Organizing Framework

Narrative doesn't have to come from therapy or a 12-step program. Human beings have developed many systems for organizing the story of a life:

  • Religious or spiritual frameworks — the idea that events were destined, that suffering has meaning within a larger plan, that karmaKarma is the principle that all effects have causes and all causes produce effects — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition → links causes to consequences
  • Philosophical frameworks — ideas about chance, entropy, meaning-making in an indifferent universe
  • Psychological and neuroscientific frameworks — like the model described here

None of these is declared right or wrong. What matters is whether they give you a set of organizing principles that lets you connect events into a coherent sequence and engage emotionally with what happened. Different people need different tools. The goal is the same: take disparate, overwhelming experiences and weave them into a story with a before, a during, and an after — one where you are the person who lived through it, rather than someone still trapped inside it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write the story of your relationship with the difficult thing. Don't start with "what went wrong." Start from the very beginning — when did this thing (a substance, a relationship, a period of your life) first enter your life, and what did it do for you at the time? Follow the story forward chronologically, including the positive chapters before the painful ones.

  2. Notice where you're still using present-tense language about past events. Pay attention to when you say "this is ruining my life" versus "this ruined a chapter of my life." The language reflects which brain region is running the show. Gently practicing past-tense framing — not as denial, but as accuracy — can support the shift.

  3. Don't skip the anger. When writing or talking through your story, actively check whether you're suppressing anger, guilt, or shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → as "inappropriate."   4. Find your organizing framework. Ask yourself: what system of understanding — religious, spiritual, philosophical, psychological — actually resonates with you? You don't need to borrow someone else's. But you do need something that lets you make sense of why things happen and how events connect. Spend time with that question.

  5. If solo narrative work stalls, seek a structured context.
