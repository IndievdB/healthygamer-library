Summary

TL;DR — When someone is completely convinced nothing can help them, the first job isn't to fix them; it's to create just enough confusion that they stop being certain.

This is a live demonstration of what coaching a "pre-contemplative" client actually looks like — someone who has all the answers, knows they're broken, and is daring you to prove them wrong. The session doubles as a breakdown of why that mindset is so sticky, and what a skilled coach does (and doesn't do) to loosen it.

The Pre-Contemplative Trap: Being Right Feels Better Than Getting Better

The client archetype here is someone who:

  • Knows exactly what they should be doing but can't do it
  • Has a comprehensive theory of why they're the way they are ("I was just born undisciplined")
  • Has consumed enormous amounts of self-help content without it changing anything
  • Frames their situation as fundamentally hopeless — but with certainty

That last part is the key. There's something ego-gratifying about being certain, even when what you're certain about is your own hopelessness. If you can prove nothing can help you, then there's nothing to be done — and that's oddly comfortable. You're not failing to change; the world is just confirming what you already knew.

"Weirdly enough, that's kind of ego-gratifying. It makes them feel better because they're like, 'Well, I'm right, no one can help me, so there's nothing to be done.'"

The client in this session demonstrates this perfectly. He's watched hours of content about psychology. He knows the vocabulary. He can even predict what the coach is going to say ("you're going to say it's all in my head") — and uses that prediction as a shield. If he can preemptively name the intervention, it loses its power.

The Trap-Laying: How a "Stuck" Person Tries to Stay Stuck

Throughout the session, the client throws out questions that are designed to be unanswerable without confirming his worldview:

  • "Do you think I'm attractive?"
  • "You must think I'm an idiot."
  • "You're going to say this is all in my head, right?"

These aren't genuine questions. They're binary traps. If the coach says yes, you're attractive, that's obviously his job to say — just like a mom would. If the coach says no, the client was right all along. Either answer closes the loop and puts the client back in familiar, unchangeable territory.

The same dynamic plays out around the "self-fulfilling prophecy" moment. The client almost gets there himself — he starts saying "maybe it's all in my head" — and then immediately pivots to you're going to say that as a way of defusing it before it lands.

"I have all these patterns in my mind that are going to snap to judgments and close off free thinking."

The rigid belief system isn't just intellectual — it's a document that's readable but not editable. The client can tell you everything about himself. He just can't update any of it.

What the Coach Actually Does: Tai Chi, Not Debate

The coach's job here is not to convince, correct, or reassure. Every one of those moves would have backfired. Instead, the approach is closer to tai chi: don't resist the push, don't push back — just redirect the energy and let the person move themselves.

Concretely, that means:

  1. Don't take the bait on binary traps. When the client asks "do you think I'm attractive?", the right move isn't yes or no — it's "what would it mean to you either way?" This forces the client to notice that his mind has already decided in advance, regardless of the answer.

  2. Reflect the pattern back without judgment. "It sounds like no matter which answer I give you, you've already decided what it means." Said neutrally, not accusatorially, this is the first moment the client actually sees his own thinking from the outside.

  3. Don't agree with the client's framing, even when he invites you to. When the client said "maybe it's all in my head — that's what you're going to say," agreeing would have handed him a reason to get defensive and retreat. Instead, not confirming it forces him to sit with the half-formed thought a little longer.

  4. Keep returning to feeling when the client keeps retreating to logic. The client was asked "how do you feel?" multiple times and kept answering with analysis. Eventually, when pressed for a physical response, something cracked — tightness in the chest, needing to take a breath. That's real information.

"Confusion is actually the first step of moving from pre-contemplative to contemplative."

The goal of the session isn't insight. It isn't a breakthrough. It's just to create enough genuine uncertainty that the rigid document becomes, slightly, editable.

Why Information and Rules Don't Work Here

The client has read the books. He's watched the content. He knows Jordan Peterson's 12 Rules. He's researched "signals" women give when they're attracted to someone. He's not lacking information.

This is important: more rules and frameworks will not help a pre-contemplative person. They'll absorb the new information into the existing belief system. "I know what I need to do, I just can't do it" is itself a piece of content that came from somewhere. Adding more content to that pile doesn't change anything.

What the client actually needs isn't a new rule — it's a crack in the certainty that the rules even apply to him. That crack only comes from genuine confusion, which only comes from a conversation that doesn't go the way he's already scripted it.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to reassure or compliment the client. Telling someone who's convinced they're unattractive that they're actually fine is the fastest way to lose credibility and confirm their suspicion that you're just being polite.

Agreeing with the client's half-formed insights too quickly. When the client edges toward "maybe it's all in my head," jumping in with "yes, exactly!" feels helpful but actually triggers defensiveness. Let the realization breathe.

Answering the binary traps. Yes or no, you've confirmed something that wasn't open to question. The only move is to redirect: What would it mean to you if I said yes? What would it mean if I said no?

Confusing pre-contemplative with unintelligent. This client is smart, self-aware, and well-read. That's actually what makes him harder to help through conventional means. Intelligence can be used to construct a more airtight version of a false belief.

Treating "I know what I need to do" as the starting point. It feels like the problem is execution. But the more foundational issue is that the client has foreclosed the possibility of being wrong — about himself, about what's possible, about what other people think of him. That's what needs to move first.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice when you're setting binary traps for yourself. If you find yourself thinking "if X happens, it proves I was right" and "if Y happens, it also proves I was right" — that's the pattern. Write it out. Both outcomes can't logically confirm the same conclusion.

  2. Sit with confusion instead of resolving it immediately. The next time you feel confused or unsettled by a conversation or piece of feedback, resist the urge to immediately file it under a pre-existing belief. Just stay with the discomfort for a few minutes. You don't have to decide what it means right away.

  3. Ask yourself: what would change my mind? Pick one belief you hold about yourself — about your discipline, your attractiveness, your intelligence, your potential. Then ask: what evidence would actually update this belief? If you can't name anything, the belief isn't a conclusion; it's a wall.

  4. When trying to help someone who's stuck, practice not answering their traps. If a friend or person you're supporting asks a question that has "yes or no but both confirm what I already believe" as its structure, try redirecting: "What would it mean to you if I said yes?" You don't have to have coaching training for this to be useful.

  5. Check what's happening in your body, not just your head. The client in this session couldn't access feelings through direct questions, but did notice tightness in his chest when pushed. If you're spinning on something intellectually, try asking yourself: Where am I feeling this right now? The body often has cleaner information than the reasoning mind.
