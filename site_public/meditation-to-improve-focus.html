Summary

TL;DR — Motivation is the ability to hold a thought in your mind, and like any ability, you can train it directly. A specific visual meditation called trataka (fixed-point gazing) is one of the best tools for that training.

Struggling to follow through on goals isn't a character flaw — it's a skill gap. The mind naturally lets thoughts dissolve. When you notice you want to go to the gym, that thought doesn't just stay there waiting for you to act on it. It slips. Understanding why it slips, and then training your capacity to hold focus, is the practical path forward.

Motivation Is a Holding Skill, Not a Feeling

Most people think of motivation as something that either shows up or doesn't — a mood, an emotion, a spark. A more useful framing: motivation is the capacity to hold one thought in mind long enough to act on it.

Watch what actually happens when you have a goal. A thought arises — I should go to the gym — and almost immediately it starts to dissolve. If you try to grip it harder, you stop thinking about the gym and start thinking about the effort of holding the thought: Oh god, I'm trying so hard to hold it. I can't hold it. I'm so lazy. Now you're not thinking about the gym at all, and you've added a layer of self-criticism on top.

The first meditation is simply to observe this process without fighting it. Don't try to hold the thought yet. Just watch what happens to it. This is the beginning of understanding your own mental mechanics.

"Pay attention to yourself and notice what happens when you have a goal and you're looking for motivation — notice the thought that would get you to go, and then notice what happens to it."

Why Thinking About Quitting Something Doesn't Work

This same logic explains something counterintuitive about bad habits: you cannot quit something by thinking about not doing it. If you're thinking I must not play this game, you are thinking about the game. The thought is active.

The things you've actually quit in your life — games you played obsessively as a kid, foods you used to crave — you didn't quit them by willpower. You stopped thinking about them, and they faded. Attention is fuel. Remove the attention, and the habit starves.

This is also exactly where OCD goes wrong in the other direction: obsessive attempts to not think about something keep the thought continuously alive.

Trataka: The Core Practice

Trataka (sometimes written tratika or thrataka) is a yogic technique of fixed-point gazing. It is a form of dharana — concentration — which is distinct from mindfulness. Where mindfulness cultivates a witnessing, observing attitude toward whatever arises, dharana is about forcing the mind into one place. These are opposites, and both are valid tools for different purposes.

Trataka is essentially resistance training for attention.

How to do it:

  1. Sit up straight. Take a few slow deep breaths to settle.
  2. Open your eyes and fix your gaze on a single point — traditionally a candle flame, or a geometric image called a yantra. (A common starting point is the Sri Yantra with a red dot at the center.)
  3. Do not blink. Keep your gaze on that fixed point. Your eyes will water — that's fine; it's lubricating them.
  4. While your eyes stay fixed, let your attention move. Eyes on the center dot, but attention moves outward to the surrounding triangles, then further outward to the next layer, then the next. You are decoupling where your eyes point from where your attention goes.
  5. After 30–90 seconds (or as long as you can manage without pain), gently close your eyes.
  6. An afterimage of the yantra will appear on the inside of your eyelids. Now practice holding that image in focus. If you grip it too tightly, it shakes and blurs. If you let your attention go completely slack, it fades. The goal is a relaxed focus — finding the sweet spot between straining and drifting.
  7. Rub your palms together, cup them over your closed eyes, breathe, then slowly open.

"Optimal flow state is not a state of stress. It's a state of relaxed focus."

Trataka, especially the eyes-closed phase with the afterimage, teaches this distinction in your body. The physiological reason the afterimage appears has to do with photoreceptor pigments (rhodopsin) exhausting in your eyes; the longer you can hold your gaze open, the stronger and clearer the afterimage becomes when you close them.

Relaxed Focus vs. Effortful Concentration

The afterimage phase is where the deeper lesson lives. Most people assume focus requires effort and strain. Trataka demonstrates otherwise. If you tense up trying to keep the afterimage stable, it destabilizes. If you relax completely, it dissolves. The only way to hold it is to find a calm, non-grasping attention — present and steady without clenching.

This is directly applicable to motivation. Effortful, strained focus on a goal ("I have to do this, I must hold this thought") tends to collapse into self-criticism and distraction. The trained capacity for relaxed focus is what allows a goal to stay present in your mind long enough to move you toward action.

Why This Isn't "Mindfulness"

Mindfulness — in the scientific sense — is one branch of a much larger tree. Scientists studying meditation across many traditions extracted what seemed like the common ingredient and labeled it mindfulness. That's useful for research, but it misses a lot.

Trataka is not mindfulness. It is dharana: concentrated, directed attention, not open observation. For some situations — particularly trauma work — mindfulness is actually contraindicated, because opening awareness to difficult material without containment can backfire. What actually helps in those cases is grounding, which is a focusing technique — the opposite of mindfulness — even if it gets called mindfulness in clinical settings.

Different conditions call for different tools.

Common Pitfalls

  • Straining to not blink to the point of real discomfort. If your eyes are hurting, blink. The goal is practice, not pain.
  • Confusing effort with focus. More tension does not mean better concentration. The afterimage phase will make this obvious — straining makes it worse.
  • Trying to quit habits by thinking about not doing them. Keeping the thought active (even in negation) keeps the habit fueled.
  • Expecting results immediately. The afterimage becomes clearer and more stable the longer you can keep your eyes open before closing them. This improves with practice over days and weeks.
  • Treating motivation as a mood to wait for rather than a capacity to build. Waiting to feel motivated is like waiting to feel strong before lifting weights.
  • Only doing this once. A single session teaches you the idea. Regular practice builds the underlying capacity.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Observation exercise (start today): The next time you notice a goal-related thought — exercise, studying, a task you're avoiding — don't try to hold it or act on it immediately. Just watch what happens to it over the next 30 seconds. Notice whether it dissolves, whether you start thinking about trying to hold it instead, or whether self-criticism appears. This is research, not failure.

  2. Start a trataka practice: Find or print a Sri Yantra image (freely available on Wikipedia) with a clear center point. Begin with 30–60 seconds of open-eyed gazing at the center dot without blinking, then close your eyes for at least the same duration and work with the afterimage. Aim for a total session of 5 minutes to start.

  3. Practice decoupling eyes from attention: While gazing at the center point, consciously move your attention to the outer rings of the image without moving your eyes. This builds the specific attentional control that carries over into daily focus.

  4. Work on the afterimage with relaxed attention: When the image appears behind closed eyes, experiment with the tension in your effort. Notice what happens when you grip harder versus when you back off slightly. Find the level of engagement where the image stays clearest.

  5. Gradually extend your sessions: Work toward 20 minutes of total practice daily (open-eyed and closed-eyed combined). Experienced practitioners can sustain this for 20–30 minutes. Frequency: every day if possible, every other day at minimum.

  6. Apply the quitting principle: If there's something you want to stop doing, identify what keeps putting the thought back in your head (ads, people around you, environmental cues) and reduce exposure to those triggers rather than fighting the thought directly.
