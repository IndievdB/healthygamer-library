Summary

TL;DR — When you feel unloved, the instinct is to try harder. But trying harder is exactly what keeps you stuck, exhausted, and cycling through the same painful patterns.

This is a conversation with someone who has been battling depression, sex addiction, and codependency for years — a person who is, paradoxically, remarkably resilient, adaptive, and morally driven. The core insight that emerges isn't about fixing addictive behaviors directly. It's about recognizing that multiple addictions (sex, weed, compulsive effort itself) are symptoms of one underlying wound: a terror of being unlovable, and the exhausting, futile project of trying to make other people love you to make that terror go away.

The Mountain You Keep Pushing

There's a particular kind of exhaustion that doesn't come from weakness. It comes from pushing against something immovable for years.

Imagine waking up every day, walking outside, and finding a mountain blocking your path. You push against it all day. It doesn't move. You go to bed. You wake up. You push again. After years of this, you're not failing because you're weak — you're failing because the mountain literally cannot be moved that way. The problem isn't effort. The problem is what you've chosen to push against.

This is what compulsive trying looks like from the inside. You've been dealing with real, difficult circumstances — a dying cat, a tiny apartment, a career change, lost friendships, a brutal relationship — and your mind files all of it under "everything is wrong." And when everything is wrong, your mind can't fix anything, because "everything" is not a solvable problem. So you cope. You avoid. You use whatever provides temporary relief.

The insight here is that addiction, in many cases, is a symptom of avoidance — not a disease in itself. Whether it's sex, weed, video games, or endless scrolling, these behaviors sit on a spectrum from healthy coping to full addiction. What they share is the same function: making the unbearable feeling go away for a while.

The Control Illusion at the Heart of Codependency

Here's the part that cuts deepest: when you're desperately trying to make someone love you, who holds the power in that dynamic?

Your instinct is probably to say they do — they're the one withholding love, they're the one who could give it or not. But look more carefully. You're the one exerting effort. You're the one treating love as something that can be manufactured through enough work, enough accommodation, enough self-sacrifice.

"You presume that you have the control to make someone love you, otherwise you wouldn't do it. You actually have the control — they don't have the control. What you're terrified of is giving them the capacity to not love you."

This is the trap. You take total ownership of the relationship — all the effort, all the emotional labor, all the rope-pulling — because surrendering that ownership means accepting that they might not choose you. And that possibility feels unbearable.

So you invest 100%. You let them off the hook entirely. And then you wonder why the relationship collapsed, or why you ended up in a six-month stretch where she was sleeping with other people while you were finishing nursing school in the same apartment.

The cruelty of the pattern is that your maximum effort produces the worst outcomes. The harder you try, the more powerless and overwhelmed you feel. The more overwhelmed you feel, the more you cope. The more you cope, the more you become the thing you hate — someone who isn't present for the people around him.

You're Not Unlucky — You're Setting Yourself Up

This pattern isn't random. When you see someone consistently end up in relationships where they give everything and receive nothing, in friendships where they're abandoned, in living situations where they're exploited — at some point the common variable isn't bad luck. It's the person choosing to keep pushing against unmovable walls.

You're not creating these situations consciously. But you are creating them. The mechanism is this: you feel unloved (or sense the threat of it), and instead of acknowledging that feeling and sitting with it, you respond by investing more. More love, more effort, more tolerance for behavior that's genuinely unacceptable. You think you're fighting for the relationship. What you're actually doing is removing any incentive for the other person to meet you halfway.

There's also something subtler happening in how you talk about yourself in real time — constantly adding disclaimers like "I know this sounds weird" or "I don't want to be cathartic." You're managing other people's perceptions of you even while talking about your own pain. That's the same pattern: trying to control how you're received, rather than letting people see you and react honestly.

"Let us see you for who you are and be free from the possibility that we think you're an awful person. Twitch chat thinks you're a dumbass — so what? Let go of trying to control other people's perceptions."

The Samskara You Keep Missing

When you try to rationalize the feeling of being unloved — "they're just busy, people have their own lives, I'm not the center of the universe" — you're doing something genuinely useful for the first few steps. You're catching cognitive distortions early. That's real progress.

But watch what happens at the end of that thought process. "Who am I to them?" That sentence isn't logic. That's the wound talking. That's the deep, pre-verbal belief that you're not worth someone's time — a belief that was probably formed long before you could examine it, likely in the relationship with a father who left.

This kind of scar — called a samskara in the Ayurvedic framework — is subtle. It hides inside otherwise rational thoughts. You'll do nine reasonable things and then, disguised as humility or perspective-taking, the old belief sneaks in and confirms itself. You have to learn to catch it there, in that tenth thought, where rationalization slides into self-erasure.

What "Letting Go" Actually Looks Like

The solution sounds passive but is one of the hardest things to do: stop trying.

Not stop caring. Not stop showing up. Stop trying to make outcomes happen that depend on another person's free choice. Stop treating love as a problem you can solve through sufficient effort.

This means accepting, genuinely, that: - You cannot make someone love you - You might not be loved by the people you want love from - You need love — that's not weakness, that's being human - You cannot guarantee that need will be met

That last one is the terrifying part. The reason you push so hard is because you cannot tolerate leaving that need unguaranteed. But the pushing is what creates the futility.

"When you accept that people may not love you and that you can't make them love you — when you let go of that control and let go of that paranoia — you're also going to stop tolerating the abusive behavior."

When you're not desperately trying to make someone love you, you can actually see clearly whether they do. And if they don't, you can leave without doubling down first.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing insight with change. Recognizing the pattern — "I try too hard, I'm codependent, I have a fear of being unlovable" — feels like progress. It isn't, on its own. The diagnosis is only useful if it leads to something different happening in the next moment someone doesn't text back.

Turning "let go" into a new project. The moment you decide to try to be independent, or try to stop caring what people think, you've already looped back into the same engine. You're now pushing against a new wall. The goal isn't to achieve independence through effort — it's to accept dependence and impermanence without scrambling to fix them.

Wanting to fail gracefully. Even the desire to give up with dignity is the pattern wearing a different outfit. It's caring what people think of your surrender. Failing like a dumbass — ungracefully, visibly, without a good story about it — is actually the rep you need.

Rationalizing without feeling. Catching the cognitive distortion is good. But if you talk yourself out of the feeling before you've actually felt it, you haven't processed anything. The feeling of not being someone's priority needs to be sat with, not argued away.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify who you're currently trying to win over. It might be a romantic interest, a friend, a parent, a coworker, or even an abstract audience. Name them specifically. Notice what behaviors you're engaged in that are designed to make them love or approve of you.

  2. Catch the feeling of being unloved in real time. When it happens — a slow text reply, a canceled plan, a cold response — notice the specific sensation before you do anything about it. Don't act. Don't rationalize. Don't fix. Just notice it.

  3. Sit in the feeling without moving. Like putting your hand in the garbage disposal — the first three seconds are the worst. Sit with the feeling of rejection or invisibility without texting back, without doubling down on investment, without withdrawing dramatically. Just sit.

  4. Practice giving up small things. You teach judo on a sprained ankle. You push through. Start practicing the opposite: sit out one class when you're injured. Leave a task half-done on purpose. Order food you don't like. Cancel something you feel obligated to do. These are the repetitions that train a different kind of muscle.

  5. Sneak-attack your breath. Throughout the day, try to catch yourself mid-breath and observe it without changing it. Don't slow it down, don't deepen it — just notice. This is harder than it sounds, and that difficulty is the point. It builds the habit of noticing internal states without immediately trying to control them. Apply the same sneak-attack awareness to the feeling of unlovedness when it arises.

  6. See a therapist and talk about your father. Not as a last resort — as the most direct path to the root. The specific wound, the specific story of someone who left, shapes everything downstream. Many universities offer low-cost sessions. Use them.
