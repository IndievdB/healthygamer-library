Summary

TL;DR — Karma is simply cause and effect applied to an individual life, and the part of it that actually shapes your future is almost entirely mental — meaning you have far more control over it than you think.

Most people think karma is about cosmic fate, past-life debt, and things happening to you. That's a small slice of the picture. The vast majority of your karma is created moment-to-moment in your own mind — through the way you interpret events, the expectations you attach to actions, and the mental patterns you reinforce by giving into (or resisting) impulses. Understanding this distinction turns karma from a mystical abstraction into a practical operating system for daily life.

Karma Is Just Cause and Effect — All the Way Down

The yogis who developed the concept of karma weren't doing anything mystical. They were doing the same thing physicists, chemists, and psychologists do: tracing causes to effects. Physics explains molecules. Biochemistry explains proteins. Neuroscience explains the brain. Psychology explains behavior. Karma is the name for this same chain of causation applied to a human life — including the parts that science hasn't caught up to yet.

What makes the yogic framing unique is the scope. Western science draws an arbitrary line: we study cause and effect in controlled laboratories across populations. The yogis said there is no line — cause and effect applies everywhere, including to individual experience, including across time, including to things that happened before you were born. Your height, your ZIP code, your instinctive fear of snakes — all of these are karmic consequences in the technical sense. They are effects of prior causes that you didn't choose.

The yogic approach is essentially "N of 1" science. The advantage: everything translates directly to your life. The disadvantage: it can't make population-level predictions. Both traditions are useful; they just answer different questions.

The Three Types of Karma — and Why Only One Matters

The tradition divides karma into three categories:

Sanchita karma is the totality of all accumulated karmic debt across all past lives — the entire ledger.

Prarabdha karma is the portion of sanchita karma that ripens and manifests in this specific life. This is the hand of cards you were dealt: your parents, your birthplace, your genetic conditions, the random events that hit you before you had any say. If you're born with a degenerative neurological condition, or your ZIP code predicts your income, that's prarabdha. It's the RNG of your existence. You cannot control it.

Agami karma is the karma you create in this life through your actions, reactions, and mental patterns. This is where the game is actually played.

Here's the critical insight: the vast majority of people spend their mental energy worrying about prarabdha — fate, past lives, "why did this happen to me?" — when prarabdha is, by definition, the part you can't touch. Agami karma, the part you can shape, is what actually determines most of how your life unfolds.

"I would say that 90% of your life is determined by agami karma."

The Real Karma Is Mental

This is the part that surprises most people: the majority of your agami karma isn't even your actions — it's your mental reactions.

The video game example makes this vivid. Imagine you're playing a team-based game and a teammate plays poorly. That's prarabdha — you didn't choose your teammates. But then you tilt. You destroy your items, flame your team, throw the game. Now you queue again, still tilted. You lose again. You post on Reddit blaming forced 50% win rate, the trench, bad teammates. You have fully absolved yourself of responsibility for outcomes that your own mental state created.

This exact pattern — where a person's interpretation of a situation generates consequences that then get attributed to the situation itself — has support in Western psychology too. Research on the stress generation framework shows that low perceived control doesn't just result from bad interpersonal situations; it causes them. The direction of causation is backward from what we assume. Your sense of powerlessness generates the very relationship problems you then point to as proof of your powerlessness.

"It is not the interpersonal stress that creates the sensation of low control — it is literally the other way around."

This is aami karma in action. The abusive relationship, the dating frustration, the stuck rank — all of it has its roots in the mental patterns you bring to each new situation.

Expectations Are the Engine of Karmic Entanglement

Here is the mechanism by which karma accumulates:

You go on a date. You have fun. You text afterward. So far, neutral. But the moment you craft the perfect text designed to elicit a response, you've added the dimension of future time to a present experience. Now you're not done. You're waiting. You're checking your phone. Your peace is disturbed. If they don't respond quickly, you escalate. You send an angry follow-up. The interaction blows up. You carry resentment into the next date. You develop rules ("I text twice and then never again"). You conclude that everyone in dating is terrible. You've built an entire negative architecture out of one unreturned text — not because of the text, but because of the expectation you attached to it.

The distinction that breaks the cycle:

  • Intention/expectation/attachment → propagates karma, extends the chain
  • Full experience without attachment → resolves karma, ends the chain

You are allowed to hope. You are allowed to act. What binds you is the attachment to a specific outcome, the mental continuation of the event beyond its natural end.

"I can send the text and I can be done. I can just say, in this moment, I went on a date with this person, I enjoyed it — I'm finished."

How to Break Free: Experience as Resolution

The way to discharge karma is to experience things fully and completely, without adding anything to them before or after.

When Buddha was spit on by a student who left without reaching enlightenment, he let it go completely. His disciples expected retaliation. He said: that was just his karma — it happened, it's done. Reacting with ego ("how dare you spit on me, I'll show you") doesn't resolve the situation; it opens a new karmic chain.

Experiencing something fully — including pain, including difficult emotions — is actually karmic resolution. This aligns with what psychotherapy calls catharsis: an emotion fully experienced tends to discharge. It's the suppressed, unprocessed, attached-to emotion that keeps surfacing in dreams, compulsions, and relationship patterns.

The problem with pleasure is the same as the problem with pain: we rarely experience either fully and then let go. We finish a video game and immediately need another one — not because the game was bad, but because we never fully arrived at done. We eat a piece of junk food and immediately layer guilt on top of it, which means we're now carrying the junk food and the shame forward into the next decision.

"It is the attachment that you add to it. There's no such thing as good karma or bad karma — there's just karma. There's cause and effect."

Your Life Is a Deck-Building Game

A useful reframe: every action you take today adds a card to the deck you'll draw from tomorrow.

  • Taking your phone to bed adds: minus 5 energy tomorrow, add one "take phone to bed" card to deck
  • Getting high adds: plus 10 enjoyment, end turn immediately, minus 5 energy tomorrow, add one "get high" to deck
  • Completing a task you've been avoiding adds: remove one task from deck, add one energy tomorrow
  • Finishing a game with full acceptance adds: nothing — the card resolves cleanly

This isn't about moral judgment. Force equals mass times acceleration — it's not good or bad, it just moves things. The question is only: what am I building? What does tomorrow look like given what I do right now?

The compulsions that hijack this process — the urge to queue again, to text-rant, to binge one more episode — are themselves karmic products. Every time you give in to a compulsion, the compulsion grows. Every time you observe it without acting on it, you weaken its pull. The goal isn't to eliminate desire through willpower; it's to become aware of what each mental impulse is signing you up for.

"Notice all of your mental compulsions and ask yourself: if I give into this, what am I signing up for in the future?"

Taking Over Someone Else's Chess Game

There is a clean image for how to live without karmic accumulation: imagine you sit down at a chess game that's already in progress. You didn't start it, you have no ego invested in the score, you have no attachment to winning or losing. You just look at the board and make the best moves available.

This is how to approach life. You play to the fullest capacity of your skill. You don't hold back. But there's no ego architecture around it — no "I can't lose to this person," no "I need to prove I'm better than this rank." Just the board as it is, and the best move available now.

A doctor can't control life and death. That doesn't mean they should stop practicing medicine. It means every patient who walks in gets the totality of that doctor's skills and attention, without attachment to outcome. The outcome isn't the point. The quality of engagement is.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing prarabdha with agami. People spend enormous energy trying to understand, justify, or reverse the circumstances they were born into — while ignoring the mental patterns actively making things worse right now. The hand you were dealt is real. The way you play it matters more.

Thinking karma is about actions, not mind. You can do the "right" action and still accumulate karma through the story you tell yourself about it. Doing charity while privately feeling superior ("look at what a good person I am") creates ego, which creates future karmic entanglement. The action isn't the problem; the attachment is.

Mistaking low perceived control for an accurate read of the situation. The research is clear: low perceived control tends to generate the interpersonal problems we then cite as proof we have no control. This is the tilt-and-blame loop. Naming it doesn't fix it immediately, but catching it is the first step out.

Treating "experiencing without attachment" as passivity. This is not "don't act." It's "act fully and completely, then be done." The chess player takes the board seriously. The doctor works hard. You text the person you like. You just don't need the response to complete the experience.

Wanting to have zero negative karma — and turning that into an attachment. Craving karmic cleanliness is itself a karmic attachment. The point isn't to win the game of life without getting hit; it's to play it as fully as possible.

Blaming circumstances for what is actually mental Karma. "I'm stuck because of my situation" almost always contains a hidden contribution from the way you're interpreting the situation. Responsibility and power are inseparable — giving up one gives up the other.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Observe the mind's activity for one day. Don't try to change anything yet. Simply notice: what thoughts arise, what do they reinforce, and what actions or further thoughts do they lead to? This is the foundation of everything else.

  2. Map your attachments. Pick one recurring frustration in your life (dating, work, gaming rank, finances). Trace back the moment where you added expectation or future-time to an experience. Where did the simple event become a chain? Write it out.

  3. Practice experience without attachment on a low-stakes activity. Play a game, watch a show, or eat a meal — and when it ends, let it be over. Notice the pull to continue, to queue again, to check for a response. Don't act on it. Just observe it and sit with the discomfort.

  4. Use the deck-building question before any compulsive impulse. Before giving into an urge (scrolling, queuing, texting in anger, procrastinating), pause and ask: if I play this card, what does it add to my deck? What does tomorrow look like? You don't have to resist — just ask the question first.

  5. Complete one task today that you've been putting off — not the whole backlog, just one. Notice that the task itself is rarely as painful as the mental weight of having it undone. Notice whether finishing it creates a different feeling in the evening.

  6. Practice eating without preference once. Order something randomly, skip the sugar in your coffee, eat what's in front of you without modifying it. This small act builds the muscle of experiencing what comes rather than engineering the outcome.

  7. When something goes wrong, resist the first attribution. Before concluding "this always happens to me" or "everyone is like this," ask whether any part of how you responded contributed to the outcome. This isn't self-blame — it's recovering your agency. Accountability and power arrive together.
