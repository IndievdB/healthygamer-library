Stuckness Is a Fear Response, Not a Real Barrier

Nobody on this call is literally chained to a chair. What feels like being stuck is almost always a mind caught in binary thinking — the mental state where a thousand options collapse down to two, and both seem bad.

"I either date her or I don't date her, divorce her or don't divorce her. I either buy the car or I don't buy the car."

Binary thinking is a reliable signal that fear is running the show. When you're not afraid, you naturally see nuance: different ways to be in a relationship, different cars to consider, different ways to handle a hard conversation. Fear narrows the aperture to two terrible choices, and then you feel trapped between them.

The second cognitive move fear makes is constructing a false end — a worst-case scenario (fired, broke, homeless, rejected) that the mind treats as a final destination with nothing beyond it. The Stoic, Samurai, and Tibetan Buddhist traditions all addressed this directly: they trained practitioners to visualize past the feared outcome, including death itself, moving slowly through the full sequence of what happens next. The fear loses its grip once you realize there is a "next."

So when you feel stuck, two diagnostic questions are worth asking: - Am I only seeing two options? (If yes, fear is narrowing your thinking.) - Am I treating a bad outcome as a final destination? (If yes, mentally walk past it and ask: then what?)

The Emotional Root: Repressed Anger That Turned Inward

Intellectual stuckness is relatively easy to address. The emotional layer runs deeper.

When anger can't be expressed safely — because a parent escalated, because love disappeared every time you had a tantrum, because you watched someone lose control and swore you never would — it doesn't disappear. It gets redirected inward. That inward-directed anger becomes the relentless internal critic: you're lazy, you said you'd stop and you didn't, what's wrong with you.

That self-attack creates emotional stagnation. The system is now caught between two options: go do the numbing thing (scroll, play games, avoid) or feel the full weight of that self-attack. The longer you avoid feeling it, the more catastrophic feeling it seems. But the resistance to the emotion is far larger than the emotion itself.

Signs of repressed anger include low-level depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, chronic procrastination, passive-aggressive behavior, subtly sabotaging yourself or others, and telling people you'll do things and then not doing them. You don't need to excavate the original childhood incident to start moving it. Simply getting anger moving through the system — expressing it in a way that harms nobody — begins to unblock things. The emotional logic tends to follow: memories and insights surface after the emotion moves, not before.

The binary thinking trap applies here too. Many people believe the only options are "suppress the anger completely" or "explode and destroy something valuable." There's a third option: express it privately, in a way that harms no one. Screaming alone in a car is a real technique, not a joke.

The Nervous System Layer: When the Body Won't Stand Down

The deepest layer of stuckness is in the nervous system — the part of the brain that acts before thinking or feeling can even register. If you've been in a chronic environment of verbal attack (from others or from yourself), the nervous system goes into a constant low-grade threat scan. There's no escape because the source of threat is internal.

To work with this layer directly:

  • Slow breathing: A simple 5-seconds-in, 5-seconds-out cycle is particularly effective.
  • External sensation focus: Not internal body scanning (which can increase anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →), but external sensations — rubbing your hands together, feeling fabric on your legs, prayer beads. The attention on external physical sensation reliably calms nervous system arousal.
  • Downward attention: Placing all attention in the soles of your feet, moving slowly. The lower in the body and the slower the movement, the more the nervous system tends to settle.

For the emotional layer: trying to escape fear increases it. The counter-intuitive move is to get curious about it — where does it sit in your body, what color is it, how does it move, what is its texture? That relationship of curiosity and wonder toward the fear is what allows it to shift.

What the Coaching Revealed: Shame Lives Behind Every "Should"

The live sessions made one pattern unmistakable across all four participants: behind every "should" is a shame.

Lucas had been telling himself for ten years that he should work out. The moment the framing shifted from "should" to "want" — as in, I want to be healthy and not suffer — something lit up. There was a smile. There was energy. The "should" flattened him; the want was alive.

He'd been so busy dodging rejection from the outside world that he hadn't noticed he'd been rejecting himself for a decade.

The practical reframe: every external rejection from a sports club or fitness group is actually smaller than what he already endures internally. He's already a professional at handling it. Rejection from a group simply tells you that group isn't yours — it's directional information, not a verdict.

The resolution is simple to say and hard to do: put loving attention on the shame. Don't try to fix it, analyze it, or make it a new should. Just notice it and move toward it with curiosity.

Self-Esteem Built on Problem-Solving Has a Hidden Dependency

OC's situation looked like a self-esteem crisis triggered by an unsolvable family problem. His parents' relationship was in trouble; one parent asked him to help decide whether to leave; he couldn't find a solution and felt his self-esteem collapse.

The coaching reframe: the self-esteem was never actually built on solving problems. It was built on a particular relationship to the people around him — specifically, a sense of dutyDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition → to manage their wellbeing. The intellectual work he'd done (school, career, personal development) stood on its own. But the emotional layer had a rule running underneath it: if someone I love is struggling and I could help, I must.

The key question that unlocked it: When is a struggle yours to solve and when do you let someone wrestle with it themselves? The butterfly analogy is apt — a butterfly that gets helped out of its cocoon can't fly, because the struggle is what pumps blood to the wings.

More precisely, the parent hadn't actually asked OC to solve the problem. They'd asked for permission to leave. They handed him their fear and framed it as a question about his welfare. He internalized it as his responsibility to fix — and then couldn't, and called it a failure of his own capability.

"It wasn't your job. It wasn't right for them to ask that. They were scared. And they just handed you their fear."

Authenticity Is the Fastest Route Out of Perception-Management

Matthias's coaching session started as a question about why it's easy to pitch clients but hard to recruit colleagues in his financial consulting business. The real issue was subtler: when asking for something that might benefit him personally, shame activated and created blind spots.

He couldn't see that he was actually the higher-risk party in the transaction (a bad hire costs him more than it costs the recruit). He couldn't see that recruits would learn something valuable regardless of outcome. He couldn't see that full transparency — saying out loud, "I've never done this pitch before, so I might say it wrong" — was an option at all.

The algorithm that emerged: when you're scared of asking for something that may benefit you, be honest about what's underneath the fear. Say the scared thing. Don't manage how you're perceived; manage how authentic you're being. The moment you stop hiding, there's nothing to protect, and the fear dissolves. He'd already discovered this instinctively with clients — he just hadn't applied it to recruiting.

"The more you're authentic, the more you're not rejecting yourself."

The Perfectionism Trap: Doing It Right vs. Being Yourself

Scott wanted to become a personal trainer but kept avoiding studying the textbook. The surface problem looked like procrastination or a learning style issue. The real pattern: whenever he got close to something that genuinely mattered — a job, a woman, a goal — he manufactured a hurdle that said you're not good enough yet.

The insight that cut through: he'd found happiness in the coaching conversation because he had nothing to hide. The moment he was asked to identify what would make him happy right now, he answered immediately — and then noticed that trying to savor the happiness "correctly" immediately interrupted it. The perfectionism that was supposed to produce happiness was the thing actively preventing it.

The practice offered for the inner critic — the voice that says you did it wrong, you should have known that, you weren't good enough:

"Every time it says something to you that you would not say to Newton [the person you're coaching], just say 'ouch.'"

Out loud. Not to fix the voice, not to argue with it, not to replace it with positive self-talk. Just "ouch." Common Pitfalls

Treating intellectual insight as a substitute for emotional work. Knowing you shouldn't be afraid of your boss doesn't make you less afraid. The intellectual and emotional brains run on different operating systems. Insight can occasionally help, but it mostly needs to be followed by the emotional work, not replace it.

Trying to escape fear instead of entering it. Trying to get out of fear is itself a fear response. The curiosity-and-wonder approach — treating the fear as an interesting sensation to explore — is counter-intuitive but far more effective.

Turning self-work into a new "should." "I should address my shame" is just another should. It flattens the energy the same way every other should does. The question isn't whether to address it, but how to do it without it becoming another form of self-punishment.

Assuming the textbook (or the "right" method) is mandatory. Multiple coaching participants tried to follow a prescribed path — study the textbook, do it the right way — and got paralyzed. Asking people who are actually doing the thing, learning by trial and error, using audio or video — these aren't shortcuts. They're often just how humans actually learn.

Confusing "useful" with "arbitrary." The framework of useful vs. true isn't a license to believe whatever is convenient. It's specifically for understanding yourself — a domain where your observation changes your reality. It doesn't replace engineering or medicine.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Spot your binary thinking. When you feel stuck, write down the two options you believe you have. Then deliberately generate at least five more. This won't dissolve the fear, but it interrupts the cognitive narrowing enough to remind you there are more paths.

  2. Walk past the false end. Take the outcome you're most afraid of and visualize what happens after it. Then after that. Keep going for five steps. Move slowly. Let yourself feel whatever comes up at each stage.

  3. Convert your shoulds to wants (or drop them). Take any "should" that's been living in your head for more than a few months. Rephrase it as "I want to" and notice what happens in your body. If there's no genuine want underneath, the should probably isn't yours — it came from somewhere else.

  4. Try the 5-5 breath. Five seconds in, five seconds out. Use it whenever you notice binary thinking or a spike of fear before a difficult action. Do it for two to three minutes before attempting the thing you're avoiding.

  5. Put loving attention on one specific shame. Identify one recurring instance where you feel inadequate, excluded, or "not the kind of person who does X." Don't try to fix it. Just notice it's there, and for a few minutes, treat it the way you'd treat a friend who told you the same thing about themselves.

  6. Run five experiments before committing. For any area of life where you know you "should" be doing something but aren't, design five low-commitment experiments to find what you'd actually enjoy. Ask to try one session before paying for a full term. Sample before buying in.

  7. Practice the "ouch" exercise. Set a timer for 10-20 minutes. Close your eyes and listen to your inner critic. Every time it says something you would never say to someone you care about, say "ouch" out loud. Don't argue, don't fix — just mark it. Do this daily for a week and notice what shifts.

  8. When asking for something that benefits you, lead with the fear. Before a pitch, a request, or a difficult ask, say out loud what you're nervous about. "I've never done this before so I might get it wrong." "I want to be upfront — there's something in this for me too." Transparency removes the thing you're protecting, and with it, the shame.
