Summary

TL;DR — Eastern and Western medicine aren't really opposites — they're two different starting points trying to reach the same destination: the best outcome for the individual patient. Understanding where they diverge, and why, reveals something important about the limits and possibilities of both.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between two physicians — one steeped in Western evidence-based psychiatry, the other skeptical of anything that hasn't been rigorously tested — about what Ayurvedic medicine actually is, what it gets right, what it gets badly wrong, and what modern medicine might be quietly borrowing from it without saying so. Along the way, the discussion touches on the philosophy of the randomized controlled trial, the "art of medicine," cultural appropriation of spiritual practices, and why some techniques work even when we can't explain the mechanism.

The Two Starting Points: Population vs. Individual

The deepest disagreement in this conversation isn't really about Ayurveda — it's about the fundamental unit of medicine.

Western medicine starts with populations. The randomized controlled trial (RCT) is its gold standard precisely because it strips away individuality. You take 10,000 people, remove the noise of their personal variation, and ask: does this intervention work? That abstraction is also its greatest strength — it's what allows allopathic medicine to be reliable at scale. Two physicians from different hospitals, different training backgrounds, different personalities, will both give you roughly the same care for a heart attack on an airplane because they're both working from the same population-derived evidence base.

Ayurvedic medicine starts with individuals. Its foundational premise is that there is no disease process independent of the person who has it. Treatment, therefore, cannot be standardized — it must be derived from understanding this particular person, their constitution, their digestion, their cognitive fingerprint.

"In the Ayurvedic system of medicine, they think that randomized controlled trials are the antithesis of practicing medicine."

This isn't a failure to understand science. It's a genuinely different ontological claim: that individualizing medicine isn't the art you apply after the science — it is the science.

Where Western medicine has leveled up to 100 on population-based medicine and maybe 50 on individualized medicine, Ayurveda has done the reverse. Neither system has solved the problem. They've each solved half of it from opposite ends.

Why the RCT Can't Fully Evaluate Ayurveda — and Why That's a Problem

This isn't a defense of avoiding evidence. It's a genuine methodological puzzle.

If Ayurveda's core claim is that treatment must be individualized, then running an RCT on it is like trying to evaluate bespoke tailoring by measuring whether 10,000 random people fit into the same suit. The study design is structurally incompatible with what's being tested.

A more appropriate study design would be a cohort comparison: take a group of people receiving full, properly individualized Ayurvedic treatment and compare their outcomes to a group receiving standard allopathic care. That's not an RCT, but it's still high-quality evidence. The problem is that this research largely hasn't been done — and Ayurvedic practitioners, caught in a defensive crouch against Western medicine, haven't been pushing for it either.

"The biggest problem with Ayurvedic medicine is they are so hung up on getting widespread acceptanceAcceptance is the clear-eyed acknowledgment of your present circumstances — not a surrender to them, but the necessary starting point for doing anything about them.Full definition →. That unwillingness to let treatments fail is a critical weakness. What makes Western medicine so dominant — more than any specific discovery — is its willingness to be wrong. Thalidomide worked, then it was found to cause severe birth defects, and it was pulled. The system has a mechanism for self-correction. Ayurveda, as currently practiced, largely doesn't.

What Ayurveda Gets Right That Western Medicine Misses

Despite the criticisms, there are areas where Ayurveda has made genuinely useful observations that Western medicine is only now catching up to.

Cognitive fingerprinting. Ayurveda uses constitutional types (doshas) to describe individuals' physical and psychological tendencies. The five-factor personality model and other Western psychometric tools exist, but they're calibrated at the population level — the translation to individual clinical practice is thin. Ayurvedic personality frameworks, whatever their mechanistic shortcomings, appear to have more direct clinical utility for understanding how a specific patient will respond.

The Survivorship Bias Problem

An important caveat runs underneath all of this: what you see of Ayurveda today is not a representative sample of Ayurveda.

"It's absolutely a possibility of survivorship bias. The stuff from Ayurvedic medicine — ashwagandha, brahmi, meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition → — these are the things that we now associate with it, because we remove things like heavy metals in the usage of medicine."

Ayurveda also includes Rasashaastra — a branch using substances like arsenic and mercury as treatments. There's some evidence these are actively harmful. That branch doesn't get discussed much, because it didn't survive the natural selection of "things that seem to help." The things that did survive are now held up as evidence that the whole system works. That's a selection bias, not a validation.

Similarly, some of the apparent sophistication — like the observation that diabetics have sweet urine that attracts ants — may reflect astute correlation-detection rather than any deep mechanistic understanding. They noticed a pattern. They may have been wrong about why it happened. The pattern was still useful.

What Gets Lost When Western Science Absorbs Eastern Practices

There's a more subtle issue worth taking seriously. When a practice from Eastern tradition gets picked up by Western research, it typically gets denuded of its theoretical framework before publication. The NEJM paper on Tai Chi and osteoarthritis has a brief line about its origins; it has nothing about what is happening to chi during practice.

This seems reasonable — why include an unverifiable theoretical claim? But there's a hidden cost. The theoretical framework of a practice is often what generated the practice in the first place. Removing it can mean losing the map that would lead to further discoveries.

The antibiotic analogy is useful here: if you give someone penicillin but strip away all the microbiology — just tell them "this pill works for infection" — they'll use it correctly until they don't. Without the underlying theory, they have no way to anticipate antibiotic resistance, no way to know when not to use it, no framework for developing the next treatment. The RCT proved penicillin works. Understanding microbiology is what prevented it from becoming useless.

The Reliability Problem: Ayurveda's Achilles Heel

The most serious practical objection to Ayurveda isn't philosophical — it's that you cannot assess practitioner quality.

Western medicine is far from perfect, but if you see a board-certified family physician anywhere in the United States, there are minimum competence standards enforced by licensing boards, training requirements, and malpractice liability. You can assess the credential. When something goes wrong, there is a mechanism for accountability.

Ayurveda, especially as currently practiced, has no comparable mechanism. Historically, quality control came through parampara — lineage, the idea that a teacher would only certify a student they had personally trained and vouched for. That system has largely been replaced by university degrees and certificates that carry no equivalent guarantee. The result is a field wide open to charismatic practitioners engaging placebo effects in high-socioeconomic-status patient populations, with no way to distinguish genuine skill from performance.

This is why Dr. K — despite being genuinely interested in Ayurvedic principles — uses evidence-based techniques with the majority of his patients. Not because he thinks Ayurveda is worthless, but because reliability matters when you're responsible for outcomes.

Common Pitfalls

Conflating the best of Ayurveda with Ayurveda as a whole. When people point to meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition →, ashwagandha, or yoga as evidence that Eastern medicine "works," they're citing the survivors. The same tradition includes arsenic-based treatments. You don't get to take credit for the winners and disown the losers.

Treating skepticism about a system as closed-mindedness. Dr. Mike's frustration with Ayurveda comes partly from watching health gurus exploit people's trust using its language and frameworks. That's a legitimate concern. But it can shade into a bias where the whole system is dismissed because bad actors have weaponized parts of it.

Assuming Western medicine already does everything Ayurveda does, just better. The "art of medicine" isn't a solved problem with a few rough edges — it's a largely unarticulated skill that varies enormously between clinicians and that no textbook adequately describes. Calling it "art" is partly an admission that we haven't made it scientific.

Assuming that because something lacks a proven mechanism, it has no mechanism. Placebo effects are real biological effects whose mechanisms we don't fully understand. "We can't explain it yet" is not the same as "it doesn't work."

Believing that individualizing treatment is the same as bro science. The Ayurvedic approach is not "this worked for me, so it'll work for you." It's an attempt to develop a system for identifying what will work for each person — a system, not anecdote.
