Summary

TL;DR — Failure isn't a dead end; it's a clearing. The burden of "potential" is what makes failure feel catastrophic, and the path forward starts with accepting the wreckage, not immediately rebuilding.

This is a coaching conversation with Zack, a former PhD student in biomedical engineering who dropped out after a cascade of research failures, a hostile advisor, an escalating mental health crisis, and a moment on a sidewalk where he considered stepping into traffic. Three and a half months later he's living in his parents' basement, unemployed, and spending most of his waking hours on RuneScape and anime. The conversation traces the real psychological mechanics underneath the surface story — the role of expectations, the neuroscience of failure, cognitive bias, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → vs. fear vs. anger, and what it actually means to "move forward."

The House of Cards Called Potential

Zack kept returning to one sentence: "I've always been able to get things done faster than everyone else." That belief wasn't just confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → — it was the foundation of an entire identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →. And like any foundation built from a single idea, it was catastrophically fragile.

Potential is a curse disguised as a gift. When you have it, there are exactly two outcomes available:

  • You meet expectations → nobody is surprised, because that's what was expected
  • You fall short → everyone is disappointed, and some simply stop talking about it entirely

"When you sign up for potential, you sign up to be average at best."

Zack noticed this himself: his friends celebrated him as a PhD student. When he dropped out, no one brought it up again. The praise was always conditional on the performance. That's the trap. You can't win with potential because you're always playing against a ceiling that rises with every success. Each achievement just adds another floor to the house of cards.

When the house finally collapses — and it will, because it's built on expectation, not reality — you feel like a failure not just at the thing, but at being a person. That confusion between failing at a task and being a failure is where most of the damage lives.

Why Failure Is Actually a Beginning

The collapse of potential, as brutal as it is, has one massive hidden benefit: it burns down the expectations along with it.

"You've burned down the idea of 'I will always be able to do things faster than everyone else.' All of that expectation and potential — you're actually free of it. It's a burden."

The logic is simple: you can't fail if there's no expectation attached. The failure has already happened. The house is already down. That means there's nothing left to protect, no precarious stack to keep upright. That's not defeat — it's a strange kind of freedom.

This isn't toxic positivity. It's not "failure makes you stronger." It's more specific: the particular psychological structure that made failure so devastating — the comparison, the ego, the expectation of ease — has been dismantled. You don't have to carry it anymore. A smaller, more honest identity can be built on what you actually care about and what you're actually like, not on a projected ceiling.

The Equilibrium Problem

There's a structural reason why high achievers end up feeling like impostors: you rise until you reach your level.

Think of an object placed in a density gradient of fluids — it sinks or floats to wherever the surrounding density matches its own. Smart people keep getting promoted, accepted, selected — until they reach an environment where they're average. That's what PhD programs do. Everyone there was exceptional somewhere. In that room, exceptional is the floor.

This is why impostor syndrome hits hardest at exactly the moments of greatest achievement. If you were selected into a competitive program, you were going to encounter people who make your résumé look thin. That's not evidence you don't belong — it's evidence the selection worked.

The problem is when you've been told your whole life that you're faster than everyone else, suddenly being average in a room of high performers feels indistinguishable from incompetence.

Reading the Data Honestly

Zack's brain did something every stressed brain does: it discarded evidence.

Twelve years of academic success — second grade through a college degree. All of it dismissed as "not counting" or "too easy." One bad year of PhD research: cited as definitive proof of failure. That's not analysis. That's confirmation bias doing what confirmation bias does.

The analogy is a rigged scientific experiment: you grow twelve plants on water and one on beer, the water plants thrive, the beer plant struggles — and then you throw out the twelve healthy plants and announce that beer is better than water.

"Your mind wants to discard good data. And it's doing it all the time."

Pointing this out rationally helps a little. But the brain doesn't fully let go of it, because the belief isn't coming from logic — it's coming from emotion. Emotions aren't rational and shouldn't be expected to be. You can see the bias clearly and still feel it. Both things are true at once.

Shame, Fear, and Anger Are Not the Same as "Overwhelmed"

Words like stressed and overwhelmed describe quantity — how much is happening — not quality. They don't tell you what you're actually dealing with. Once you break "overwhelmed" into its actual components, something useful becomes possible.

For Zack: - Shame — from feeling exposed in front of peers, from living in his parents' basement, from the gap between who he was supposed to be and where he is - Anger — at a PI who ignored his input, changed five variables at once against his judgment, and publicly humiliated him; at a system that exploited his labor - Fear — of trying something outside science and failing there too; of repeating the same crash; of never breaking the current cycle

Each of these needs different handling. You can't "deal with" an undifferentiated blob of overwhelm. You can start to work with specific fears and specific anger once you've named them.

The Observer and the Feelings

There's a practice that keeps appearing in Buddhist traditions, stoic philosophy, and modern therapy under different names: the capacity to notice what you're feeling without being consumed by it.

The difference is something like being inside a cage with a lion versus standing outside the cage. The lion hasn't changed. What changes is your position relative to it.

Zack had glimpsed this in therapy — his therapist had told him to "not be in the storm but be aware of the storm." He could only pull it off a handful of times. That's normal. The skill starts in calm waters (breath meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →) and gets applied progressively to harder emotional material. It's not taught widely enough, and people usually only encounter it when they're already drowning.

The observer creates distance. That distance is what allows you to articulate what you're feeling. When fear fully takes over, you can't name it — you just react. When you have even a little distance, you can say: I'm afraid that whatever I try will fail. I'm afraid I'll end up back here.

Naming it doesn't make it go away. But it changes the relationship to it.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to move forward before you've healed. This is the most common mistake after a major setback. If you try to rebuild while your instrument is miscalibrated, every stumble confirms the belief that you're a failure. More data points get added to the wrong column. The case against yourself gets stronger. Give the legs time to heal before you try to walk.

Fighting the part of you that feels like a failure. Suppression doesn't work. When you push something into the unconscious, it exerts influence through the back door — through avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →, through self-sabotage, through picking the wrong situations. AcceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → is what actually loosens its grip. Carry the failure with you. Let that part of you have its opinion. Don't let it make the decisions.

Mistaking "overwhelmed" for a complete diagnosis. If you only know you're overwhelmed, you have nothing to work with. The actual work starts when you identify what specifically is hurting — the shame, the specific fears, the anger at specific injustices.

Conflating failing at something with being a failure. These are categorically different. Failing at a PhD under a poorly supervised, adversarial research environment after doing what you were instructed to do is not evidence that you are broken as a person.

Making major decisions with a broken instrument. A scale that isn't calibrated gives bad measurements. Major career and life decisions made from inside a depressive episode after traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → will be skewed by that context. The decision can wait. The healing can't.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your specific fears. Don't stop at "I'm overwhelmed" or "I'm stressed." Sit with the question: What exactly am I afraid of? Write down at least three specific fears (e.g., "I'm afraid that if I try something outside my field, I'll fail there too"). Named fears can be worked with. Unnamed fear just runs the show.

  2. Start a basic meditation practice — even five minutes a day. Begin with the breath only. The goal is not to stop thinking; it's to notice that you are not your thoughts. This is the observer skill in its simplest form. Build from there.

  3. Practice triphasic breathing when you notice anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → spiking. Breathe in three phases: first expand the belly, then expand the chest, then raise the shoulders slightly. Exhale fully. Do three slow rounds. This activates the parasympathetic system and creates a moment of observer distance from whatever you're in.

  4. Get back into mental health treatment if it's accessible. Not because you're "too broken to function," but because unprocessed trauma colors every decision you make going forward. Deal with it directly rather than carrying it unexamined into the next thing.

  5. Have the conversation with your parents. Not about job applications or plans — just ask them what they think about the last year. Let them talk. The conversation you're avoiding is keeping a weight on you that's heavier than whatever they might actually say.

  6. Read Meditations by Marcus Aurelius. It's in the public domain (free online or very cheap). It won't fix anything, but it's a practical companion for exactly the kind of thinking described here — observing one's mind, accepting what can't be controlled, acting anyway.

  7. Stop trying to make the big decisions right now. The question of whether to go back to school, switch careers, move out — those can wait until September or later. Your instrument is miscalibrated. Calibrate first. The big questions will be clearer when you're not asking them from inside a crisis.
