The "Just Work Harder" Scam

There's a pervasive cultural idea that effort is the universal cure. Bad grades? Study more. Not enough money? Work overtime. Stuck in life? Build more discipline, more willpower, more motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →.

This is incomplete at best and wrong at worst.

The key problem with it is that effort costs are not equal across people or across circumstances. Someone with ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by a disorder of attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition → can appear completely functional in a conversation — engaged, articulate, participating — and still be expending three times the energy a neurotypical person would burn doing the same thing. Someone with depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → moves through a day that costs them enormously just to begin. Comparing output without accounting for the cost of producing that output leads to a completely distorted picture of what "not working hard enough" actually means.

There's also the trap of sheer misdirection. Think about a lid on a board game box: if the lid is tilted slightly off-axis, you can push with enormous force and it will never close. Tilt it correctly and it drops into place with almost no effort. The problem was never the quantity of force.

"If you are working really hard and things are not working for you, what you need is better understanding. You need to shift that box just a little bit."

This is why people who grind for years — who force themselves into promotions, who optimize and hustle and never stop — can end up exhausted and trapped high on a mountain they don't actually want to be on. They were working very hard in a direction nobody examined closely.

Why More Effort Creates the Effort-Burnout Pendulum

When sustained effort doesn't produce results, the instinctive response is to add more effort. More self-control. More willpower. More discipline. This produces a predictable cycle:

  1. Work incredibly hard, ignore all internal signals of strain
  2. Burn out completely
  3. Swing to the opposite extreme — wanting to do nothing, be a "parasite," check out entirely
  4. Feel guilty, try to work harder again

The person in the opening post described this exactly: years of telling themselves I should try harder, I can do this, I must keep going — followed by collapse into I am a walking corpse, I just want to be a parasite.

The reason internal signals get ignored is a specific learned pattern. If you listen to your body when exhausted, it tells you to do nothing. Doing nothing doesn't work either. So you conclude that your internal signals are unreliable and train yourself to override them entirely. Now you have no compass.

The alternative isn't hustle culture or a four-hour workweek. The alternative is sustainable output that stays above zero without dipping into debt. Yogis in the Himalayas don't take weekends off — but they also never sprint until collapse. Every day is the same moderate engagement. The moment you draw on reserves to get something done, you are signing up for a debt of exhaustion that has to be repaid.

"Being able to engage in sustained effort is about not going into the negative. The moment that you dip into reserves to get something done, you are signing up for a debt of exhaustion."

This is a long-term architectural problem, not a willpower problem. The practical goal is to gradually craft your circumstances so that the demands on you shrink over time — not to endure indefinitely.

What Actually Precedes Change: Diagnosis Before Treatment

Good treatment requires good diagnosis. If a patient is misdiagnosed, increasing medication doses does nothing useful. The same logic applies to every stuck life pattern.

Most people skip the diagnosis step entirely. They seek solutions — and both human advisors and AI are trained to supply them. A paper discussed in the talk illustrated this with AI: when presented with three very different patients all reporting two weeks of insomnia, it offered essentially the same advice to all three. The diagnostic approach involves genuinely looking inward and paying attention to what leads to contentment versus regret. Contentment here is defined precisely: it's not pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition → in the moment, it's the absence of regret after an action is completed. If you rest when you're exhausted and feel at peace, that was the right call. If you play video games for eight hours when you meant to do something meaningful and wake up feeling hollow, that signal is real data.

Two specific internal patterns to watch for:

  • The mind turning a win into a loss — you move in the right direction and instead of peace, your mind immediately produces not enough, too late, should have started sooner. This is the mind punishing you for doing the right thing.
  • Ego blocking effective paths — you resist getting help, working with a coach, seeing a therapist, or trying approaches that feel beneath your self-image, because you want to be the kind of person who does it alone. The hateful patient literature describes a related phenomenon: people who seek help and then systematically reject every suggested intervention, appearing almost smugly satisfied each time something fails.

The Gray Zone: Why Sobriety Isn't Enough

One of the more striking clinical observations in the talk: sobriety does not fix addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →. People who stop using substances, walk away from gambling, quit gaming, leave a bad relationship — they often find themselves technically clean and emotionally empty. Nothing feels interesting. Music sounds like white noise. Things they used to love feel flat and gray.

To explain why, the talk draws on Freud's Mourning and Melancholia and the concept of libidinal energy — meaning not sexuality specifically, but emotional investment, the energy of attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → and engagement. When you're deeply into something, your libidinal energy flows outward toward it. The drug, the game, the relationship, the former glory — these become the objects that hold your investment.

When that thing disappears (you get sober, the relationship ends, the career is over), your emotional investment doesn't automatically return to you. It stays pointed at the thing that's gone. You're not really investing in anything else because on some level you're still hunting for a replacement that fits the same shape.

"People never willingly abandon a libidinal position, not even indeed when a substitute is already beckoning them."

This is why nothing in sobriety quite scratches the itch. It's not that the dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → system hasn't recalibrated (though that's also real). It's that the person is still trying to find something that fits the exact contour of what they lost. They want the feeling back, not a new feeling.

The solution Freud describes is decathexis — withdrawing that emotional investment back into yourself — but this is neither simple nor single-step. It happens in slices, because the attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → to a thing is actually a thousand different attachments: to specific experiences, rituals, feelings, contexts. Giving up alcohol means giving up barbecues, happy hours, cruise ships, Napa Valley, feeling frisky, going to Vegas with your friends. These are mourned one at a time, over months and years.

"Mourning is a thousand different cuts."

The brutal part of this process: you have to pass through the gray zone willingly. Withdrawing libidinal investment leaves you temporarily empty — and that emptiness is so uncomfortable that most people reflexively reach back for the old attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → rather than sit with it. They keep the pipeline open because closing it means facing genuine absence.

The key insight is that the emptiness is not permanent. Libidinal energy has a natural tendency to form new attachments — but only once the old ones are truly relinquished. The joy from a new relationship, a new creative pursuit, a new way of life, cannot be properly invested in while you're still comparing everything to what came before.

"You can't fall in love with somebody else until you've truly given up on your ex."

Sobriety is necessary but not sufficient. The psychological step — genuine internal severance — has to happen as well, and it can even precede behavioral change. Some patients psychologically separate from the substance first and the behavior follows.
