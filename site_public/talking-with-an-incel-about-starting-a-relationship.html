Summary

TL;DR — The reason many people can't find a relationship has nothing to do with their clothes, their weight, or their social skills. It has to do with a deep, protective hopelessness that other people can sense almost immediately.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Paul, a 35-year-old man in London who has never been in a relationship and can't figure out why. What unfolds is a gradual uncovering of something that Paul had been treating as a logical conclusion about his life — that he simply cannot be helped, cannot find a partner, and is probably meant to be alone. By the end, it becomes clear that this "hopelessness" isn't a personality trait or a rational assessment. It's a psychological defense mechanism, built up over years of rejection, that actively sabotages every new attempt at connection.

The Wrong Diagnosis, Treated for Years

Paul had tried everything the conventional wisdom suggests: going to clubs, speed dating, losing weight, buying new clothes. None of it worked. The natural conclusion — the one his mind kept arriving at — was that he was the problem. Too overweight. Too bald. Too awkward. Too much himself.

But here's the diagnostic error: if the treatment doesn't work, you may have the wrong diagnosis.

Paul had been treating external factors for years. If external factors were actually the problem, at least some of those attempts should have worked. They didn't. Which means something else was going on — something internal.

"The reason you can't find a girlfriend has nothing to do with any of that, because you tried all that and it didn't work. Ergo, you're treating the wrong disease."

Lots of overweight, balding men in their 30s are in relationships. Physical appearance alone doesn't explain a total absence of romantic success across a decade of trying. The real obstacle was upstream of all the external fixes.

Hopelessness as a Shield

The core insight is counterintuitive: hopelessness isn't just a feeling — it's a protection.

Every time Paul let himself hope, he tried something, and life "smacked him across the face." The natural response to repeated pain is to stop exposing yourself to it. If you decide in advance that you're hopeless and that this will never work out, you don't have to feel the sting of rejection anymore. The hopelessness becomes comfortable, even therapeutic.

"If I tell myself I cannot be helped, I don't have to be hurt anymore. I can live with loneliness. What I can't live with is hope and trying again."

This is why Paul kept "relapsing" — losing weight, buying new clothes, signing up for speed dating — only to conclude again that it was pointless. The cycle wasn't random. Loneliness would build until it outweighed the comfort of hopelessness, he'd let himself hope, get hurt, and retreat back into resignation. Each cycle added another data point to the evidence pile that he was fundamentally unlovable.

The tragedy is that this protective mechanism broadcasts. When you're carrying that kind of burden into a four-minute speed dating conversation, people feel it. You don't get to show them who you actually are — they're picking up on the weight you're carrying before you've said anything meaningful.

The Cognitive Bias That Protects the Belief

Throughout the conversation, a specific mental pattern kept appearing. Whenever an alternative explanation was offered — maybe three months of clubbing just isn't enough time to meet someone, maybe the issue isn't appearance — Paul's mind would quickly produce new evidence to reinforce the original conclusion.

This isn't lying, and it isn't stupidity. It's a well-documented feature of how minds work: instead of using new data to update a belief, the mind uses the existing belief to filter and interpret new data.

"Your mind is doing something where instead of using the data to change your interpretation, you use your interpretation to change the data."

What makes this particularly hard to overcome when you're smart is that you're better at constructing arguments. The logical case for hopelessness becomes more sophisticated, more bulletproof, more convincing. Intelligence amplifies the bias rather than dissolving it.

The first step out of this loop isn't to argue with the conclusion. It's to notice the process — to catch the moment when your mind is reaching for more evidence to defend a position it was already committed to.

Awareness Is Not a Consolation Prize

Paul kept asking when the "fixing" part would begin. The whole conversation felt like a digression to him. But noticing is the intervention.

The analogy used was dental anesthesia: when part of your mouth is numb, you lose control of it and can accidentally bite yourself. As sensation returns, control returns. Awareness precedes control. You can't change something you can't feel.

The same logic applies to emotional patterns. If you're not aware that hopelessness is running in the background when you walk into a room, you can't do anything about it. It just operates automatically, shaping how you present yourself to everyone you meet.

What "noticing" means here is specific — it's not just intellectually acknowledging that hopelessness exists. It's creating distance between yourself and the thought. There's a meaningful difference between:

  • "I will be alone for the rest of my life."
  • "My mind believes I will be alone for the rest of my life."

The first treats the thought as a fact about reality. The second recognizes it as something the mind is doing — a habit, a scar, a pattern — not a prophecy. Thoughts come from a fallible mind. They are not the same as truth.

The Moment Something Shifted

About an hour into the conversation, Paul was asked how he felt about the upcoming speed dating event. He said, flatly, "I feel hopeless, doctor" — and then laughed. Not a performance. Something just broke open.

It was brief. He immediately started questioning whether it would actually work, whether he could do it again, whether it was real. But it had happened. And the observation was simple:

If you've done something once, you can do it again. There's an enormous difference between "I have never done this" and "I did it once." Once is proof of possibility.

That moment — catching the hopelessness and laughing at it instead of living inside it — is what unburdened presence actually looks like. It's not a technique. It's what happens when you see the thought for what it is rather than mistaking it for reality.

Common Pitfalls

Treating the symptom instead of the cause. Changing your wardrobe, losing weight, going to more social events — none of these are wrong, but they won't work if the underlying hopelessness is still broadcasting to everyone you meet. External fixes applied to an internal problem produce no lasting change.

Mistaking intelligence for insight. Being smart makes it harder to challenge cognitive bias, not easier. The smarter you are, the better you are at building an airtight case for whatever you already believe. Logical arguments for hopelessness are still just arguments.

Waiting for the transformative moment. The "pearl of wisdom on the wall" fantasy — that one insight will flip a switch and change everything — is appealing and false. Growth here is gradual, iterative, and often invisible in the moment.

Believing that noticing is doing nothing. Awareness feels passive. It isn't. It's the prerequisite for every other change. Without it, everything else operates in the dark.

Confusing the belief for the evidence. The fact that hopelessness feels evidence-based doesn't make it true. The mind is selective about what evidence it retrieves. It will always find more data to support what it already believes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Before any social interaction, pause and check for hopelessness. Literally, before you walk into the room — speed dating, a bar, anywhere — notice whether that sense of "this won't work" is present. Name it out loud if you need to. The goal isn't to eliminate it immediately; the goal is to see it.

  2. Practice the distancing reframe. When you catch yourself making an absolute statement about your future ("I'll always be alone," "this will never work"), rephrase it deliberately: "My mind is telling me I'll always be alone." Do this slowly and notice whether it changes anything about how the thought feels.

  3. Watch the evidence-gathering pattern. When someone offers you an alternative explanation for why something didn't work out, notice whether your mind immediately produces counterevidence. You don't have to agree with the alternative. Just notice the reflex.

  4. Try meditation, even skeptically. You don't have to believe it will work. Meditation is practice at stepping back from your thoughts — observing them rather than being inside them. That's the same skill as noticing hopelessness with distance. Alternate nostril breathing is a low-stakes starting point.

  5. Focus on the action, not the outcome. The goal isn't "find a girlfriend." That outcome is not fully in your control and measuring yourself against it will feed the hopelessness cycle. The goal is: go to speed dating as an unburied version of yourself. Do the work of noticing. Show up. The outcome is not your department today.

  6. Watch the alexithymia videos (available on the Healthy Gamer YouTube channel) if you find it hard to identify or remember your own emotional states. Difficulty with emotions isn't a character flaw — it's a learnable skill, and naming it helps.
