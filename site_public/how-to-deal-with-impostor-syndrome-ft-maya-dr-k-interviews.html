Summary

TL;DR — Impostor syndrome isn't about thinking too much; it's about which "thought machine" is running the show. Understanding where that machine came from — and letting go of the value judgment attached to it — is the path forward.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Maya, a Twitch streamer in her early twenties who does wildlife conservation work. She raises tens of thousands of dollars for conservation, runs a weekly science podcast, and still regularly feels like a fraud. The session moves from surface symptoms (overthinking, difficulty internalizing success) all the way back to a third-grader writing a desperate letter to her math teacher. What emerges is a clear model for how impostor syndrome actually works — and what you can do about it.

The "Thought Machine" Model

Your mind doesn't produce one unified stream of thoughts. Think of it as having several distinct thought machines — each one generates its own kind of thinking, and on any given day one machine is louder than the others.

Maya had two main machines running in parallel:

  • Machine 1 (Real Maya): I educated 30 kids about conservation today and that mattered. I'm doing something I genuinely care about.
  • Machine 2 (Behind-the-Curve Maya): My numbers are inflated. I wouldn't be here without external help. I'm not as smart as the people around me.

The problem isn't that she thinks too much. The problem is that some days she's thinking from the wrong machine. Calling this "overthinking" actually misdiagnoses what's happening — it implies the volume needs to come down, when really the source needs to change.

"It's not like your thinking is too high or too low — there are different kinds of thinking, and one source of your thinking is kind of problematic."

Where the "Behind the Curve" Machine Comes From

Maya grew up in Silicon Valley, in a hyper-competitive school district with one of the highest youth suicide rates in the country. Her older sister — 18 months ahead of her — made varsity soccer, got into an Ivy League school, and excelled in advanced academics. Every milestone her sister hit became a bar Maya felt she was supposed to clear.

She didn't make the advanced math class. She didn't make varsity as a freshman. And somewhere around third grade, she wrote a handwritten letter to her teacher asking to be placed in the advanced math class anyway — not because she loved math, but because she felt she needed to do something extra just to be normal.

That letter is the whole thing in miniature. It shows a child who already believes:

  • I start behind other people by default
  • I am not good enough on my own merits
  • I need to do something extraordinary just to catch up to ordinary

By high school, this belief stopped being painful. It just became fact. That's actually worse — not because the belief became accurate, but because it became invisible. When something hurts, you notice it. When it's just true, you stop questioning it.

"It's that point it becomes chronic — and what I'm saying is that you think this is just the way that you are."

Fraud vs. Judgment: A Crucial Distinction

Here's a useful test. When Maya imagined people "finding out" she was a fraud, what exactly was she afraid of?

Not the discovery itself. The judgment that follows.

"Is it the fraud that hurts, or the judgment that hurts? Absolutely the judgment."

This is why hearing the same words from someone who clearly doesn't judge you negatively barely stings at all. The fear isn't about facts — it's about how other people will evaluate you once they know the facts. The impostor feeling is a social threat disguised as a self-assessment.

This matters because it means the goal isn't to convince yourself you're secretly brilliant. The goal is to stop treating the "facts" about yourself as evidence of a verdict.

The Kestrel vs. Red-Tailed Hawk Problem

A kestrel is a small falcon. A red-tailed hawk is much larger. Neither is objectively better — they're adapted for different things.

Maya grew up in an environment that treated the red-tailed hawk as the only valid bird. Engineering. Ivy League. Advanced math. High SAT scores. Everyone around her implicitly agreed this was the standard, and she measured herself against it her whole life.

The issue was never that she wasn't a red-tailed hawk. The issue was that she accepted the value judgment that came with not being one.

"The problem is not that factually you aren't as smart as other people — the problem is that you don't think about what it depends on what you want to do."

She chose agricultural communications, conservation education, and Twitch. She raises money for wildlife organizations and teaches thousands of people about species they'd never otherwise care about. These are kestrel skills — precision, communication, passion, connection. Applied to the right prey, they're extraordinary.

The kids in her high school who died by suicide were, in many cases, kestrels who couldn't stop trying to be red-tailed hawks. She survived partly because she eventually let herself be what she actually was.

Why Your Success Is Actually Yours

Maya felt her Twitch following was artificially inflated — that a bump from her boyfriend's audience made her "look like a better streamer than she was."

But consider how Twitch actually works: someone might find your channel through a clip or a raid, but they keep watching because they want to. No one stays for a stream they don't enjoy. The bump gets you in the door; you're the reason they stay.

The same logic applies everywhere the "I don't deserve this" machine runs. The makeup didn't make her prettier — that was her own face. The networking didn't build her podcast — that was her own work, her own guests, her own $50,000+ raised for conservation. External factors can open doors. They can't make people care about what's behind them.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "overthinking" with "thinking from the wrong place." Labeling yourself an overthinker implies you need to think less. You don't. You need to recognize which machine is generating the thoughts and whether those thoughts are worth following.

Treating adaptation as proof of acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition →. When painful beliefs stop hurting, it feels like you've dealt with them. Usually it means you've just baked them in. The absence of pain isn't resolution.

Trying to talk yourself out of the facts. The goal isn't to convince yourself that you are as smart as your sister or that your numbers are legitimately earned. Some of the underlying "facts" might even be roughly accurate. What you can let go of is the judgment — the assumption that those facts mean you're worth less.

Locating the thought machine in the wrong place. Like finding a horror movie that made you anxious and assuming that's the origin — when the movie just activated something older. The surface trigger is rarely the source.

Waiting for the thought machine to turn off before acting. Recognizing the machine is running doesn't mean the thoughts stop. It means you don't have to follow them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your thought machines. Write down two or three recurring thought patterns you notice — not just the content, but the flavor (e.g., "behind the curve," "I'll be abandoned," "I'm a fraud"). Giving each one a label creates just enough distance to notice when it's active versus when you're just thinking clearly.

  2. Trace the machine to its origin. When a thought machine fires up, ask: When is the earliest I can remember feeling this way? If you feel like a fraud now, look for the version of you at 8, 10, or 13 who first had that feeling. What was happening? What did that kid conclude about themselves?

  3. Separate the fact from the judgment. Take any belief you hold about yourself that feels bad (e.g., "I'm not as technically skilled as others in my field"). Write it down. Then ask: What's the factual part, and what's the value judgment I've attached to it? Practice holding the fact without the verdict.

  4. Ask "which machine is talking right now?" When you catch yourself spiraling — about an email you sent, an achievement that feels unearned, a decision you're second-guessing — pause and ask which thought machine is active. You don't have to shut it down. Just label it: "That's the behind-the-curve machine. Noted."

  5. Find your kestrel skills. List three to five things you do well that would never show up on a conventional résumé or leaderboard. Ask yourself what those skills are actually adapted for — not whether they're impressive by someone else's metric.

  6. If the thought machine feels deep-rooted, consider EMDR or somatic therapy. Talk therapy is useful for insight. For thought machines that are rooted in early experiences or traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →, approaches that work at a more embodied level (like EMDR) can reduce the machine's power in a way that pure reflection sometimes can't. The goal isn't to excavate the trauma endlessly — it's to deprive the machine of its energy source.
