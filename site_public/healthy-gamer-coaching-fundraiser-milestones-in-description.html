The One Genuine Insight: Sitting With Sadness on Purpose

Buried in the fundraiser chatter is a small but honest admission worth pausing on. The "shower crying playlist" isn't a joke about being depressed — it's a deliberate practice of making space for a feeling that usually gets avoided.

The logic goes like this: if you are fundamentally okay — if you have a stable foundation of positivity and health — then sadness doesn't have to be a threat. It can just be a feeling you visit. Listening to music that makes you cry isn't wallowing; it's giving a real part of your inner life some airtime.

"Deep down, I have this very deep sadness. Which I like."

That sentence sounds strange at first. But it points at something real. A lot of people who struggle emotionally aren't struggling because they feel too much sadness — they're struggling because they've spent years running from it. The sadness didn't go away. It just went underground, where it quietly influences everything without ever getting processed.

The retreat context matters here too. A group of people who brought their gaming laptops to a cabin in Yosemite ended up, almost accidentally, setting the hardware aside — not because anyone banned screens, but because there were genuinely engaging things happening. Conversation, ghost stories, stargazing. And in that space, the question "what kind of music do you like?" opened a door to something more honest: what do you actually feel, and are you willing to feel it?

The point isn't that sad music is the tool. The point is that intentionally making contact with difficult emotions — in a safe, controlled context, for a few minutes — is different from being overwhelmed by them. You're choosing to feel it. That choice matters.

The Coaching Program: What Was Actually Being Funded

The fundraiser existed to support a sliding-scale coaching program. A few details worth knowing:

  • The coaches are people personally trained by Dr. K, not random hires
  • A beta program had already been completed and generated data the team felt good about
  • The explicit goal was to keep costs low enough that people without much money could still access it
  • The fundraiser donations were meant to subsidize sessions for people who couldn't afford even a reduced rate

The underlying philosophy: mental health support shouldn't be gated entirely behind the ability to pay full therapy rates. Coaching isn't therapy, but for a lot of people dealing with things like gaming disorder, social isolation, or motivational struggles, structured coaching from someone who understands the subculture is a meaningful step.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking this for a teaching stream. It isn't. If you came here expecting a lecture on psychology, this particular session won't deliver one. The value is narrow and specific.
  • Dismissing the sadness observation as a bit. The shower playlist framing is self-deprecating and funny, but the underlying point — that a psychologically healthy person can deliberately sit with sadness and find it valuable — is genuine and worth taking seriously.
  • Assuming "feeling your feelings" means feeling them constantly or intensely. The practice described is bounded: a few minutes in the shower, a playlist, a chosen moment. It's not about marinating in misery. It's about not permanently locking the door.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice what emotions you actively avoid. For one week, pay attention to moments when you reach for your phone, a game, food, or anything else the instant you feel a difficult feeling. You don't have to change anything yet — just notice the pattern.

  2. Try a controlled dose of a difficult emotion. Pick a song, a piece of writing, or a film scene that reliably makes you feel something uncomfortable — grief, longing, nostalgia. Give yourself five minutes with it, on purpose, without immediately doing something to shake the feeling off. See what happens.

  3. Distinguish between visiting sadness and being trapped by it. After the five minutes, check in: are you still functional? Can you return to your day? If yes, that's what a healthy relationship with a difficult emotion looks like. If you find yourself unable to come back up, that's worth paying attention to and possibly worth discussing with a professional.

  4. If you're interested in the coaching program, look into Healthy Gamer GG's current offerings — the program described here was in early 2020, and the structure has evolved since then.
