Summary

TL;DR — You can't make the "right" decision because right and wrong decisions don't exist the way you think they do, and your paralysis is partly a logical problem and partly an emotional wound left over from childhood.

Indecision isn't a character flaw or a lack of willpower. It has two distinct roots: a fundamental misunderstanding of how planning works, and a conditioned terror of making mistakes that society has drilled into you since elementary school. Understanding both roots — rather than just trying to "be more decisive" — is what actually loosens the grip.

The Trap Hidden Inside "Making the Right Decision"

Before reaching for solutions, it's worth sitting with a genuinely hard question: how would you even know if you made the right decision?

Consider someone who skipped an important work meeting because their father fainted. They spent the day angry, imagining their rival getting the promotion, their career derailed. That meeting was on the morning of September 11, 2001. If they'd gone, they wouldn't have survived.

Or consider failing Spanish freshman year of college. In the moment, obvious wrong decision. Years later, the struggles and imperfections that came from it are precisely what makes someone relatable to people who are also struggling — which becomes the entire foundation of a career built on helping others.

The point isn't that everything works out in the end. It's that you do not have access to enough information to classify a decision as right or wrong at the moment you make it. Even logically, the standard you're using is shaky. If you drive drunk and don't crash, was it the right decision? If so, does that mean the rightness of a choice is determined entirely by its consequences? Most people would say no — and that inconsistency reveals that the concept of a "right decision" is far less solid than it feels.

"There's no such thing as a right decision or a wrong decision. There's only the best decision you can make today."

The Planning Fallacy: Why Early Plans Are the Worst Plans

This is the cognitive piece. Here's the core paradox:

  • You make plans at the beginning, when you have the least information
  • You would make the best plan at the end, when you have the most information
  • Therefore, by definition, early plans are the worst plans you could possibly make

When an 18-year-old picks a college major, they have almost no idea what that career actually feels like from the inside. The "right" answer to what you should major in is only knowable after you've lived 40 years of consequences — but you have to choose now, before you know anything.

This isn't a failure of intelligence. It's a feature of how information and time work. The correct response to the planning fallacy isn't to plan harder or longer. It's to iterate: make the best call you can today, accept that it might be wrong, gather new information, and adjust. Repeat.

The poker analogy captures this well. You get two cards. You don't know the flop, the turn, or the river. You make a probabilistic bet based on what you can see. More cards appear. You adjust. You can never see the future, yet some people consistently play better poker than others — not because they make perfect decisions, but because they've learned to make reasonable bets under uncertainty and respond well to new information.

Think about how grades work. A student who got zeros on three tests but aced the final — demonstrating mastery of all the material — still fails the class. Common Pitfalls

Thinking that accepting uncertainty means outcomes don't matter. The point isn't that decisions are random or that nothing is better than anything else. It's that you can't optimize perfectly from day one with limited information. Probabilistic bets still matter; some bets are better than others.

Using "there's no right decision" as an excuse not to decide at all. This is the opposite of the intended takeaway. The whole point of accepting uncertainty is to free you to decide — to make the best call available today rather than waiting for a certainty that will never arrive.

Trying to logic your way out of a samskarA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from past experiences that gets stored in the mind and automatically activates in present situations, distorting your perception of what's actually happening.Full definition →. If the feeling of being trapped is old, body-level, and pre-rational, pure intellectual understanding won't dissolve it. Recognizing that the two problems are different — cognitive vs. emotional — helps you not waste years applying the wrong tool.

Measuring your progress by the race metric you're trying to escape. It's easy to accept the iterative framing and then immediately worry that you're iterating too slowly compared to others. Notice when you're doing this.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try to define "right decision" and "wrong decision" precisely. Sit with it seriously. What metric are you actually using? Consequences? Intentions? Feelings? Push until you find the contradiction in your own definition. This is the core Jnana Yoga practice from the talk — contemplating the nature of the problem until you see through it.

  2. Name the race you think you're in. Write down the specific timeline you feel behind on. ("I'm 24 and haven't started my career yet, so I'm behind.") Then ask: behind whose schedule, and why does that schedule apply to you? Where did that benchmark come from?

  3. Make one small iterative decision this week. Choose something you've been sitting on — even something low-stakes — make the best call you can today, and explicitly label it: "This is my best guess with current information. I will reassess." Practice the framing before you need it for bigger decisions.

  4. Trace the feeling of being trapped. The next time indecision brings that physical sensation of walls closing in, don't immediately push through or suppress it. Sit with it briefly. Ask: when did I first feel this? What does being trapped mean to me? You don't need a clear answer immediately — just beginning to look at the root is different from running from it.

  5. Practice "I am that." When struggling with a decision or a conflict, say to yourself quietly: I am that. Try to find the part of yourself in the thing you're struggling with — the choice, the uncertainty, the other person. It's an advanced technique and may feel strange at first; that's expected. The goal is to start dissolving the hard boundary between "me" and "the difficult thing out there," which reduces the threat-level of uncertainty over time.
