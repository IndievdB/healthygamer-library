Why Appreciation for Life Gets Shut Down

When some part of your body or mind becomes a source of suffering, your brain's natural response is to numb it. That numbing is protective — but it isn't selective. You can't turn down the volume on pain without also turning it down on pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition →.

  • Physical numbing through substances, compulsive behaviors, or even extreme exercise can sever you from bodily experience.
  • Emotional numbing cuts you off from the very fuel that makes experiences feel meaningful — excitement, love, joy, connection.

"Once we numb something, we can't selectively numb the bad stuff. We numb the good stuff and the bad stuff."

The path back to appreciation for life starts by reversing that numbing process. Developing emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → skills makes it safer to let emotions back in. Gentle body-based practices like yoga or Tai Chi offer a low-stakes way to reconnect with physical sensation — not by forcing positive feeling, but by allowing contrast. The relief of resting after an uncomfortable yoga pose, for example, is a small, genuine experience of the body feeling good. That's where appreciation gets rebuilt, one moment at a time.

Why the Future Feels Inaccessible After Trauma

Untreated traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → doesn't just leave a scar — it pulls the past into the present. Flashbacks are the clearest example: an event that happened years ago is functionally occupying your mind right now, leaving no psychological space for anything forward-looking.

"As long as we are stuck in the past, it is very difficult to consider the future."

The counterintuitive solution is that you can't simply decide to think about the future. The past has to be processed and placed in the past first. Once unresolved memories stop dominating your present experience, your attention naturally begins to open toward possibility. The future doesn't become accessible through willpower — it becomes accessible through working through what happened.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming these five qualities are prerequisites, not outcomes. It's tempting to read the list and think, "Of course people with strong relationships and a sense of self don't get PTSDTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → — they were fine to begin with." The point is that these are also products of doing the work. You don't need to arrive with them; you develop them through the process.

Treating numbness as neutrality. Many people who have emotionally shut down don't realize they're numb — they just think they're "not emotional" or "not the type to enjoy things." Recognizing the numbing for what it is, a protective strategy that costs you access to positive experience, is essential before anything else can shift.

Trying to force future-thinking before processing the past. Journaling about goals or doing visualization exercises won't work if unresolved traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → is still pulling your attention backward. The sequence matters: past first, then present, then future.

Mistaking surface relationships for connection. Having people in your life doesn't mean you have the emotional bonds that support growth. If relationships feel functional but hollow, that's a signal — not a personality quirk.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your numbing strategies. For one week, track the things you do when you feel bad — substances, excessive exercise, binge-watching, scrolling. You're not trying to stop them yet; you're just mapping the pattern. AwarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → of how you numb is the prerequisite for changing it.

  2. Start a gentle somatic practice. Commit to a short yoga or Tai Chi session two to three times per week. Pay specific attention to the moment of rest at the end. That small window of physical relief is a real experience of appreciation — let yourself notice it.

  3. Practice tolerating one uncomfortable emotion without numbing it. When a negative emotion arises, try staying with it for two minutes before doing anything to make it go away. This builds the emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → capacity that allows you to eventually feel positive emotions more fully too.

  4. Create a shared experience with someone. Reach out to one person and do something that generates a mild shared emotional state — watch an intense film together, take on a physical challenge, work toward a common goal. Notice whether the conversation feels more real afterward.

  5. Write a brief narrative of what happened. In a private journal, describe a difficult experience in the past tense, as a story with a beginning, middle, and end. The act of giving it narrative structure helps move it from the present into the past, where it belongs.

  6. Examine one conclusion traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → taught you about yourself. Write it down explicitly (e.g., "I am weak" or "I can't handle hard things"). Then write two or three specific examples from your life — however small — that contradict it. You're not trying to overwrite the belief in one sitting; you're introducing friction.
