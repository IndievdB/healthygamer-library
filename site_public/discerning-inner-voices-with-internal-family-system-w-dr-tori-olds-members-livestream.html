Summary

TL;DR — You are not as crazy as you think. Every feeling, impulse, and behavior you have — even the ones that seem self-defeating or contradictory — makes perfect sense once you understand the hidden learning underneath it. Internal Family Systems (IFS) gives you a practical framework for meeting those inner forces with curiosity instead of judgment.

This talk by psychologist Dr. Tori Olds covers the psychological foundations of why humans feel internally conflicted, introduces the IFS model of "parts work," and walks through how to actually use mindfulnessNon-judgmental, present-moment awareness — specifically, the capacity to create a gap between the self and the mind, observing thoughts and sensations without being swept up in them.Full definition → not just to calm down but to explore and understand your inner world. Nothing You Do Is Random: The Principle of Coherence

The most foundational idea in this entire talk — and arguably in all of psychology — is coherence: every single thing your brain produces, whether a thought, feeling, urge, or behavior, makes total sense based on prior learning.

Your brain is not capable of doing something it genuinely believes is wrong. The neocortex (your thinking, reasoning brain) might recognize that a behavior is counterproductive. But the neocortex is not the final decision-maker. The deeper, subcortical (emotional) brain carries far more weight, and it runs on a different kind of evidence: not logic or ideas, but lived experience. What you actually saw, felt, and survived as a child.

Think of it this way. Your brain has been doing probabilistic, statistical mapping since the day you were born — learning what is dangerous, what leads to connection, what leads to pain, what keeps you safe. That mapping is held implicitly, meaning you are not conscious of it. You just feel its output: sudden anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →, a compulsion, an inexplicable shutdown.

"Even responses that seem crazy are simply because of something we learned early that actually wasn't crazy at the time — but in a new context isn't a fit anymore."

This is why the classic therapeutic insight "I know logically I shouldn't do this, but I keep doing it" never changes anything on its own. Your neocortex knows the logical answer. Your subcortical brain is operating from a completely different dataset — one built from actual experience — and it trusts that dataset far more than any argument you can make to yourself.

The vocabulary for this underlying learned map varies: schemas (schema therapy), mental models (attachmentA framework for understanding how early caregiving experiences shape the way people form bonds, relate to others, and navigate the world throughout life.Full definition → research), predictions (neuroscience), emotional learnings, unconscious beliefs. Pick whichever word resonates with you. What matters is recognizing that these maps exist, that they were built on a skewed sample (your specific family and environment), and that they have two components: a problem the brain learned to fear, and a solution (defense) it learned to deploy.

The reason you feel stuck is not that your brain is broken. It's that you grew up in one context and now live in another — and the brain hasn't updated the map.

You Are More Than One Person: The Science of Multiplicity

The brain is a parallel processor. Different neural networks specialize in different contexts, different types of people, different emotional situations. This means you don't have one unified personality — you have multiple sub-personalities, or what IFS calls parts.

This is not pathology. It is not multiple personality disorder. It is how human brains are built. You probably already notice this: you are one person with your boss, another with your closest friend, another when you haven't slept, another when you're with your family of origin. These are not performances — they are genuinely different self-states coming online based on which neural network gets activated.

Aaron Beck (the founder of CBTA structured, protocol-driven form of psychotherapy developed by Aaron Beck that targets the interplay between your thoughts, emotions, and behaviors — treating each as something you can identify, break apart, and…Full definition →) described what he called modes: "suborganizations within the personality to deal with specific demands, integrated by a web of cognitive, emotional, motivational, and behavioral components." This is nearly identical to the IFS concept of parts. When a part activates, you don't just get a thought, or just a feeling. You get a whole package — a particular way of thinking, feeling, perceiving, and behaving that belongs together.

The Inside Out films actually illustrate this, though imperfectly: Joy behaves not like a raw emotion but like a part with a learned rule ("you must always be happy") and a whole set of behaviors designed to enforce that rule.

The Two Kinds of Parts in IFS

IFS organizes parts into two broad categories:

Protector parts are your defenses. They learned that something is dangerous — a feeling, a type of situation, a kind of person — and they developed strategies to prevent you from encountering that danger again. There are two subtypes:

  • Managers are proactive and preemptive. They plan ahead to prevent pain. Perfectionism, people-pleasing, intellectualizing, maintaining rigid control, over-achieving — these are classic manager strategies. They tend to be socially acceptable, even rewarded.
  • Firefighters are reactive. When a painful emotional state breaks through anyway, firefighters intervene with whatever works fastest, regardless of collateral damage: drinking, raging, risky sex, self-harm, gambling, binge eating. The goal is to put out the fire now. Firefighters don't weigh long-term consequences — they're in crisis mode.

Exile parts are the underlying wounds. They are the stored memories of times you were hurt, humiliated, abandoned, or overwhelmed — often in childhood. They get stored implicitly, as a present-tense map of reality. When triggered, they produce what might be called an emotional flashback: you suddenly feel five years old again, even though nothing that dangerous is actually happening right now.

"The mantra of our protector parts is: never again."

The inner critic, which looks like it hates you, is actually a protector. It shames you preemptively so that someone else can't humiliate you first. Narcissistic defenses are protectors. The part that keeps you from being emotionally vulnerable is a protector — because vulnerability once led to pain.

There are no bad parts. This is a core IFS principle, arrived at not through philosophy but through direct clinical experience. Dick Schwartz, who developed IFS, discovered it when a client who was self-harming kept returning to the behavior no matter how many logical arguments were made against it. When he finally stopped trying to convince the part it was wrong and instead asked it to explain itself, the part said: "I just want to be understood." Every part, no matter how destructive-looking, has a protective intention at its core.

Self-Energy: Your Intelligence Was Never Broken

Here is the hopeful part: having all these parts, all these learned schemas, all this complexity — none of it damages your underlying intelligence.

IFS calls the experience of your undamaged core self self-energy. Other frameworks call it wise mind (DBT), neural integration (interpersonal neurobiology), or simply a mindful state. The eight C's associated with it in IFS: calmness, curiosity, clarity, compassion, confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition →, courage, creativity, and connectedness.

"No matter how traumatized we are, if we can learn to step back from all of these multiple parts, we do have a core self — and it can't be broken by traumaTrauma is what happens when an experience generates more emotion than you can process in a single moment — the overwhelm itself is the defining feature, not just the event.Full definition →."

Self-energy is not a permanent state you achieve. It's more like a question of degree: how much of your core self is currently present and online? When a part takes over completely — when you're fully identified with it — your self-energy is crowded out. When you can observe what's happening with some curiosity and compassion, self-energy is present.

The IFS term for moving from identification to observation is unblending. You are blended with a part when you think "I am an anxious person." You are unblended when you think "a part of me is feeling anxious right now." The shift sounds small. It's actually enormous, because it repositions you from being the problem to being the one who can work with the problem.

The most useful unblending question: How am I feeling toward this part? If the answer is judgment, irritation, or contempt, that's just another part getting in the way. You work to unblend from that part too, until what arises is curiosity — or at least the willingness to be curious.

The relational metaphor matters here. The brain is highly evolved for conversation and relationship. When you adopt a relational stance toward a part — treating it as someone worth listening to rather than a malfunction to fix — information flows much more easily. The implicit becomes speakable.

Working with Conflicting Inner Voices

When two inner voices seem to be directly contradicting each other — "leave the relationship" vs. "stay and honor your commitment," or "it's his fault" vs. "no, it's all my fault" — you are experiencing what IFS calls polarization.

Each part holds one concern and holds it absolutely, because neural networks are not the same as your full intelligence. They're specialized. They don't weigh trade-offs. They just protect against the one thing they learned to protect against.

The way through is not to pick a side. It's to: 1. Name both parts without judgment 2. Let each one speak fully, without interruption from the other 3. Hear each one's underlying concerns all the way to the bottom 4. Then, from self-energy, let your actual intelligence assess the situation — informed by both but controlled by neither

"Our self, our mind actually just kind of freshly looking at things and using our intelligence and sorting it out — is ultimately the one that makes decisions for our life. But it does that through the input of our parts."

Common Pitfalls

Treating parts as the enemy. The single most common mistake is coming at a difficult part with the goal of eliminating it, arguing it out of existence, or shaming it into stopping. This never works. The part will either dig in harder, or a new part will pop up to manage the conflict. You have to genuinely become curious before anything shifts.

Mistaking the neocortex for the whole self. Knowing something is bad for you does not make you stop doing it, because knowing is a neocortical function and the behavior is driven subcortically. Trying to logic your way out of an emotional pattern is like trying to convince someone else's nervous system with a PowerPoint presentation.

Jumping to exiles too fast. Exiles carry unprocessed pain. When you go directly to exile work without the support of a therapist, and without the permission of your protector parts, you risk flooding — re-activating old traumaTrauma is what happens when an experience generates more emotion than you can process in a single moment — the overwhelm itself is the defining feature, not just the event.Full definition → without sufficient resources to integrate it. Start with protector parts. They're the ones most likely to show up first anyway.

Overreifying the parts metaphor. Parts are a useful lens, not literal beings. The map is not the territory. Some people get so into parts language that it becomes its own kind of avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → — talking about their parts rather than actually working with what's happening in their experience.

Using self-exploration as another form of management. Going into inner work with the agenda "I'm going to fix this part" is still managing. The exploratory stance requires genuinely setting down the fix-it goal, at least temporarily. A teenager won't open up to a parent who walks in already knowing what the teenager needs to change.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick your vocabulary. Choose one word for the unconscious learning patterns that drive your behavior — schema, mental model, emotional learning, unconscious belief, prediction. Use it consistently so your reflection has a stable anchor.

  2. Practice the unblending question. The next time you notice a strong reaction, pause and ask: How am I feeling toward this reaction right now? If the answer is irritation or judgment, notice that as another part. Keep asking until you can approach the original reaction with at least mild curiosity.

  3. Name the part, not yourself. When you catch yourself saying "I am anxious" or "I am lazy" or "I am an angry person," practice replacing it with "a part of me is feeling anxious right now." Do this out loud if possible. Notice whether the feeling of being stuck in it changes even slightly.

  4. Slow down a specific moment. Think of a recurring behavior or reaction you find genuinely puzzling — something you do that you don't understand. Find a recent specific example. Replay it like a slow-motion film. Ask: What happened right before the behavior? What was I thinking, feeling, anticipating in that exact moment? Stay curious. Don't try to fix it yet.

  5. Try the golden question. Once you've identified a protector behavior (a defense, a pattern, something you do to manage discomfort), ask yourself: What am I afraid would happen if I didn't do this thing? Let the answer be specific. Follow it further: And if that happened, then what? Keep going until you hit the original fear.

  6. Work with one polarized conflict. If you're aware of two competing inner voices on a question in your life, write each one down separately. Give each a few sentences to make its full case without interruption. Then ask each one: What are you most afraid of? Notice whether understanding both fears changes how the conflict feels.

  7. If you're new to all of this, start with protector parts only. Exiles — the wounded, scared, younger parts — carry more activation and are better approached with professional support. Protector parts are where the accessible work is: the inner critic, the perfectionist, the part that numbs out, the part that rages. These are the ones most available for conversation.
