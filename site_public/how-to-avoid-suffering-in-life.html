Pain and Suffering Are Not the Same Thing Either

Pain is an emotion in the mind. Suffering is something else.

Consider a few examples: - A massage can technically involve physical pressure that borders on pain, yet feels wonderful. - A flu shot hurts, but it doesn't cause suffering. - Being on call for 24 hours without sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition → is genuinely painful, but it can feel fulfilling.

The difference is in your relationship to the experience, not the experience itself.

Grief is a useful illustration. You can lose someone you love, feel real sadness and loss, and still arrive at a place of peace — because you understood it was their time, because you feel gratitude for what you had, because you're not still clinging to what's gone. The sadness is real. The suffering is optional. Suffering would require holding on to something that isn't there anymore: the wish that things had turned out differently, the feeling that life owes you a different outcome.

"There's a certain amount of peace that you can get — sure, of course I want my dad to be alive, but he lived a life, he was an awesome dad, and I'm grateful for the person that he was."

Detachment Is Not Apathy

This is the most commonly misunderstood point.

DetachmentThe Sanskrit term for non-attachment — specifically, detaching from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → does not mean not caring. It does not mean becoming numb, indifferent, or emotionally flatlined. You can: - Find someone attractive and want to ask them out - Work hard toward a promotion - Study seriously for an exam - Deeply love your family

...and still be unattached to specific outcomes.

The difference lives entirely inside your head, in what you're telling yourself while you do the work:

Attached framing Detached framing
"I really need her to say yes" "I'd love to go out with her"
"If I don't get this promotion, my life is shattered" "I want this promotion and I'm going to work hard for it"
"I have to get into Harvard" "Harvard would be great; let me do my best"

Both people may study the same hours. Both may put in the same effort. The difference is what happens when the outcome doesn't go their way — and whether they're even able to enjoy the journey.

DetachmentThe Sanskrit term for non-attachment — specifically, detaching from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → tends to produce more fulfillment over time, not less, precisely because you're no longer spending emotional energy fighting against what actually happened.

The Game of Thrones Test

Here's a useful thought experiment. If you go back and rewatch Season 8 of Game of Thrones knowing how it ends — knowing it won't deliver the satisfying conclusion you wanted — you'll probably enjoy it more than the first time. Why? Because the first time you were attached to a specific outcome. Now you have no expectations. You can just watch, notice the gaps and inconsistencies, and laugh at them.

"If you can make Season 8 of Game of Thrones an enjoyable experience — that's what enlightenment is. Living your life and being able to enjoy Season 8 of Game of Thrones."

It sounds like a joke, but it's not. The capacity to show up to something without needing it to go a particular way — and find real enjoyment in it anyway — is what a reduced-suffering life actually looks like in practice.
