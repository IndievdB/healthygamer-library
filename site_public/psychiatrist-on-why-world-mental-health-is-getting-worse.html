Summary

TL;DR — Suicide is not a single illness with a single cause, and the common belief that you're "safe" because you're not actively planning anything is one of the most dangerous myths around it.

Mental health is the one area where modern medicine is losing ground. Deaths from cancer, heart disease, and stroke have dropped significantly over recent decades. Suicide rates have gone up — by roughly 33% over the last twenty years. Understanding why is the first step toward changing that, whether for yourself or someone you care about.

This piece covers four questions: who is most at risk, why people reach that point, how suicidality actually works in the mind, and what you can do.

Who Is Most at Risk

About 80% of completed suicides are men. More women attempt suicide, but men are more likely to die from an attempt. One contributing factor is method — women more commonly attempt overdose, which leaves a longer window for medical intervention, while men more commonly use firearms, where that window is nearly zero. But the gap is probably more complex than method alone.

Suicidality is also rising among younger people, and there's a specific warning worth knowing: adolescents starting antidepressant medications (SSRIs) face a temporarily elevated suicide risk in the first few weeks. The leading theory is that motivation and energy return before the suicidal thoughts lift — so the drive to act can briefly outpace the healing. If you or someone you know is starting an SSRI, watch the first two weeks carefully.

Why People Want to Die (It's Not Always What You Think)

Suicidality is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Just as fever can come from a cold, lupus, or lymphoma, suicidal thoughts can arise from very different underlying causes — and those causes require different responses.

The main categories, drawn from clinical and research literature (including work by Malzberger, Ronningstam, and Weinberg):

1. Psychopathology — brain malfunction Conditions like major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, and addiction can generate suicidal thoughts that don't match the person's actual circumstances. Someone with a loving family, stable job, and good relationships can still be suicidal here — the brain is simply not working the way it's supposed to. This is the bucket most likely to respond to medication.

2. Broken life dream — shattered hope Some people's lives genuinely fall apart. A false accusation ends a career. A relationship collapses. A lifelong goal becomes impossible. The suicidality here isn't a neurochemical error — it's a perception that life has become irreparable. These people may receive a depression diagnosis, but they often don't respond to medication because the root problem is psychological, not biological. The emotion driving this bucket is hopelessness.

3. Anger turned against the self Rooted in Freud's early work on depression: rather than directing anger outward at circumstances or other people, some people turn it inward. This can escalate into something darker — a belief that they deserve to be punished, that they are so fundamentally bad that death is appropriate. The emotion here is self-hatred and shame. These people don't think "life isn't worth living" — they think "I don't deserve to live."

4. Sudden defense breakdown Most people with chronic suicidal thoughts maintain a kind of equilibrium — reasons to live balance reasons not to. Sometimes something shatters that balance acutely. A common example: alcohol or other disinhibiting substances. You don't have to be addicted. A single instance of drinking can erode the mental defenses that were keeping the suicidality in check, leading to an attempt that wouldn't have happened otherwise.

5. Shame, humiliation, and envy A distinct and often underestimated driver. Some people become suicidal when they feel profoundly humiliated, or when they fixate on the gap between their life and someone else's. This is one of the hardest to treat because people hold on to envy tightly and it requires deep self-reflection to shift.

"These are very different buckets in terms of what goes on in the mind of the person."

The practical implication: prescribing SSRIs as a universal answer to suicidality misses most of these causes. The rise in SSRI prescriptions over recent decades has not produced a drop in suicide rates. Something important is being overlooked at a societal level.

The Most Dangerous Myth: "I'd Know When It Was Getting Serious"

There is a widespread assumption that suicide is a slow, deliberate process — that someone puts their affairs in order over days or weeks, and there are clear signs at every stage. The research does not support this.

In studies where suicide attempt survivors were asked when they made the decision to attempt:

  • The most common answer: within 60 seconds
  • The vast majority decided within two hours
  • In at least one study, nobody had been planning for more than three days

Suicidal ideation also fluctuates wildly — someone can score very high on a suicidality measure and then, just four to eight hours later, score near zero. This is not a steady escalation toward a threshold. It's chaotic and impulsive.

"Past performance is not an indicator of future gains."

The fact that you've had suicidal thoughts for years without acting is not evidence that you're safe. It's not a reliable signal. The practical implication: if you are anywhere on the spectrum — from passive thoughts about not waking up, to active intent and planning — go see a professional. Not when it gets worse. Now.

The other key clinical variable is means of access. If someone can make a decision and execute it within 60 seconds, having a firearm at home dramatically increases risk. Reducing access to means buys time — and time is often what saves a life, because suicidal intensity fluctuates.

Common Pitfalls

"I haven't done anything yet, so I have it under control." The research directly contradicts this. Most attempts happen within minutes of the decision. Stability so far tells you nothing about what happens when the right stressor meets the wrong moment.

"Everything is mental illness and medication will fix it." Not all suicidality stems from a biological brain malfunction. Hopelessness from a shattered life, self-loathing, envy, and defense breakdown are distinct causes that require different approaches. Treating all suicidality with SSRIs leaves most of the problem untreated.

"I've tried to figure it out myself and failed, so no one can help me." This is a symptom masquerading as logic. The mind generating suicidal thoughts is the same mind telling you there's no point seeking help. That thought is not trustworthy. A trained professional has tools and frameworks you do not.

"It's my job as a friend to fix this." You're not trained to treat suicidality. Trying to be your friend's doctor will stress you out and probably won't work. Your job is to be their friend — which includes helping them access professional help, not replacing it.

"Talking about it will make it worse or give them ideas." Not addressed explicitly in this talk, but the entire framing here is that open education and conversation is the starting point for reducing suicide, not a risk factor.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you are having suicidal thoughts at any level — get a professional evaluation. Not when it escalates. Community mental health centers exist in most cities and many offer free or sliding-scale services. Organizations like Rise Above the Disorder (RAD) provide financial support specifically for gamers.

  2. If you are starting an SSRI (especially if you're an adolescent), actively monitor yourself for the first two weeks. Tell someone you trust. Check in with your prescriber more frequently during this window.

  3. If you have access to a firearm at home and you're struggling, create distance between yourself and that means. Give it to someone to hold, store it off-premises, or ask someone to secure it. This is not a permanent statement — it's buying yourself time when your judgment may be compromised.

  4. If a friend is struggling, help them with the practical steps to get into care. Offer to make phone calls with them. Offer to search for providers together. One declined appointment can feel like confirmation that help is unavailable — your presence through that process matters.

  5. If someone you care about won't seek help, don't try to convince them — try to understand them. Ask why they're resistant. Fear of hospitalization, parental judgment, or shame about needing help are common blockers. Understanding the specific fear often opens a path forward that argument never would.

  6. If someone is at immediate risk — call emergency services. This is not a betrayal. Roughly 90% of people who are hospitalized after a crisis are grateful they were when they come out the other side.

  7. If you catch yourself thinking "there's no point in getting help" or "I don't deserve it" — treat that thought as a signal, not a conclusion. Those thoughts are part of the problem. The appropriate response to "I don't deserve help" is to get help.
