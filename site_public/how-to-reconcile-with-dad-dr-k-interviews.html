Summary

TL;DR — Your relationship with your parents, especially unresolved hurt with your father, can quietly drain the mental energy that powers everything else in your life: motivation, focus, creativity, and peace.

Miguel is a 19-year-old university student who had a genuinely good routine at school — exercise, class, meditation, practice — and lost it when COVID sent him home. The conversation starts looking like a productivity problem, but it turns into something more honest: the real drain on Miguel's life isn't his habits or willpower. It's the unresolved fear, hurt, and resentment toward his father that he's been carrying since childhood — and that living at home makes impossible to ignore.

The Environment You're In Changes Who You Are

Before blaming yourself for being less motivated, less focused, or less disciplined, notice what changed in your circumstances — not just your character.

Miguel is the same person at school and at home. The difference is the environment. At school his mental resources were mostly free. At home, a significant portion of his cognitive and emotional energy is being consumed by unresolved family tension — running constantly in the background like a process eating up RAM.

This isn't a character flaw. It's a resource problem. When you're spending mental energy keeping emotional walls up, checking whether you're being heard, managing resentment, and suppressing old fear, there's simply less left over to pursue what you actually want.

"At school your mana bar is like max. At home the debuff you've got is your mana is cut by 50%."

You can stack all the habits and routines you want on top of that — meditation, exercise, alarms, atomic habits — and they'll help at the margins. But if you don't address the root, you're managing symptoms.

The Barricade You Built for Good Reason

When you grow up in a home where a parent is unpredictable or frightening, you learn to protect yourself. For Miguel, that was literal — barricading his bedroom door at age 10. Later it became emotional: going monotone, minimizing interaction, caring about nothing his father does or says.

These aren't failures. They're adaptations. A 10-year-old who is genuinely afraid does what he needs to survive. The problem is that the same protection mechanism that kept the child safe follows the adult around and makes genuine connection impossible.

"The werewolf doesn't need to really protect 19-year-old Miguel so much as it needs to protect 10-year-old Miguel. And I think both of those Miguels exist within you."

Anger is part of this protection. It's not the root feeling — it grows out of hurt. When someone who matters to you causes you pain, the mind converts that pain into anger because anger feels safer and stronger than vulnerability. Recognizing that your anger is protecting a hurt underneath it doesn't mean the anger was wrong. It means you can eventually start to work with what's underneath.

What "Not Caring" Actually Costs You

One of the more subtle traps in this conversation: Miguel says he doesn't care whether his father loves him or not. That sounds like freedom. It isn't.

"Sometimes we choose not to care about something because if I don't care about it then it can't hurt me. But the problem is that if I don't care about it, I can't feel joy from it either."

Choosing not to care is another form of the barricade. It protects you from the pain of not being loved — but it also blocks you from feeling loved, even when love is being offered. The words land and do nothing. The connection never forms.

This non-caring can harden over time into resentment, which has its own weight and cost. Numbness isn't neutral. It's expensive.

Reinterpreting What You Were Told

Miguel's father once said he never wanted to say "I love you" to him. Miguel heard: he doesn't love me.

But consider the actual words more carefully. "I wanted to tell you I love you" is not the same as "I don't love you." It says something got in the way. Fear. Awkwardness. Not knowing how. A man who grew up without his own father, who probably never had the language modeled for him.

"He doesn't say 'I don't love you.' He says 'I wanted to say something to you but I didn't know how, or it was too hard.'"

This reinterpretation isn't about excusing harm. It's about not permanently locking in the most painful possible reading of an ambiguous statement. Your father's emotional limitations have a history. That history doesn't undo what it felt like to grow up inside it — but it can change what it means going forward.

The Mind Loves Complexity as a Form of Avoidance

Near the end of the conversation, something worth sitting with: when you can't figure out why you're stuck, notice how many reasons your mind generates.

One reason or zero reasons: probably not the whole story. Five or six reasons? That's often the mind building a maze to avoid looking at the one real thing.

"The more variables you introduce, the more opportunities the mind has to avoid looking at what it doesn't want to look at."

The same logic applies in reverse. Your compass for finding what's actually holding you back often points opposite to where your mind naturally goes. If your mind is telling you the problem is your sleep schedule, your discipline, your lack of structure — it might be worth heading south instead of following that north.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating root problems as background noise. It's tempting to think you can optimize around an unresolved relationship — build better habits, work harder, find the right system. You can make partial progress, but the underlying drain doesn't stop.

  • Confusing the protection mechanism with the problem. The emotional walls, the anger, the "not caring" — these were smart responses to a real situation. The mistake is assuming they're permanent features of who you are, or that lowering them means the danger was never real.

  • Assuming "I'll deal with it later" is a safe option. This is framed gently but clearly: you may not have as much time as you think. And even if you do, the point of repairing something isn't just to fix it and check a box. It's to have time to live inside the repaired relationship.

  • Interpreting ambiguous statements in the most painful direction. Not every "I wanted to tell you I love you" means what your worst fear says it means. Being willing to hold more than one interpretation is not naivety. It's a prerequisite for opening the door.

  • Waiting until you feel "ready." Part of you will always find a reason to wait. That part is the protector, and it's not wrong — but it's also not running the calculation correctly anymore.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your "should" statements. Any time you catch yourself saying or thinking "I should have," pause. That's self-judgment, and self-judgment quietly kills motivation. Try replacing it with curiosity: what made it hard to do that?

  2. Start the conversation sideways. If a direct conversation about your relationship with your father feels impossible, start somewhere less threatening: ask him about his own childhood. What was it like growing up for him? You're not solving the relationship yet — you're finding a door that doesn't require the werewolf to wake up.

  3. Practice alternate nostril breathing (Nadi Shodhana). Sit upright. Fold your index finger down. Use your thumb to close your right nostril and inhale through the left. Switch — close the left with your ring finger, exhale through the right. Inhale right. Switch. Exhale left. Continue at your own pace. This is a tool to return to when you feel mentally scattered or emotionally flooded.

  4. When you feel stuck, count your reasons. If you're explaining to yourself why you didn't do something and you come up with five interlocking reasons, that's a signal. Ask: what's the one thing I'm not looking at?

  5. Don't wait until you're fully ready. Identify the smallest possible first step toward a real conversation with the person you've been avoiding — not the whole conversation, not the "raid boss." Just clearing a few trash mobs first. Then take that step.
