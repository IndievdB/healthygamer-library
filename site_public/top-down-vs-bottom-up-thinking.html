How does our brain direct our attention? When I want to focus on a particular task - let's say studying - what does my brain do? What happens in the brain when I get "distracted?" In this video, we'll explore how attentional control happens in the brain, and the two competing systems of Top Down Processing and Bottom Up Processing. Once we understand how these systems work, it reveals very important things about how to stay focused: why studying is hard in a messy / noisy environment, and how technology is evolving to take advantage of our distractibility. Equipped with this understanding, we'll lay the foundation to shape our lives so that we can focus more easily.

Two Ways Your Brain Directs Attention

Top-down processing is the brain's command-and-control system. The cerebral cortex — the seat of abstract thought, logic, and planning — sends orders downward through the rest of the brain. When top-down control is working well, you decide to study, and your brain falls in line: it suppresses hunger signals, ignores the urge to check your phone, and keeps you on task. It's the general giving orders to the troops.

Most people who struggle with motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → are really struggling with top-down control. They want to tell their mind to do something and have it actually listen. That's the dream, and it's achievable — but it takes some understanding first.

Bottom-up processing is the opposite. Instead of your goals directing your attention, incoming sensory information hijacks it. Imagine walking with a friend, deep in conversation about philosophy. A snake slithers across the path. You stop mid-sentence. You didn't decide to look at the snake — your visual system detected it and yanked your attention there automatically. That's bottom-up processing.

Even though your top-down direction is to discuss philosophy, suddenly there's a visual stimulus which interrupts your attention and says, "Hey, we saw a snake."

This kind of interruption is not a flaw — it's a survival feature. The brain is constantly scanning everything in your environment, quietly suppressing most of it below the level of conscious awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →. The only way it can catch a snake is if it was watching in the first place. That background scanning is always running.

Why a Clean Room Actually Makes You Smarter

Here's something that seems trivial but has real neuroscience behind it: it's genuinely easier to focus in a clean space than a messy one.

Even when you're not consciously looking at the pile of dishes, the open tabs of browser windows, or the clutter on your desk, your brain is still processing all of it. That background scanning system — the one that catches snakes — never turns off. Every piece of visual clutter is received and then actively suppressed so it doesn't reach your conscious awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

That suppression is not free. It costs energy. Your brain is doing constant subconscious work that it can no longer devote to the thing you actually want to focus on. Researchers who had people focus in messy versus clean spaces found a statistically significant difference in performance — the clean room wins.

This reframes tidying up. It's not a productivity cliché. It's directly reducing the load on your bottom-up processing system, freeing up cognitive resources for top-down work.

Training Both Systems

The top-down and bottom-up systems are not fixed. They can both be modified.

Bottom-up sensitivity changes with experience. New parents are a striking example. Before having children, a quiet whimper from across the house wouldn't break anyone's concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition →. After becoming a parent, the same signal cuts through everything — instantly and involuntarily. The brain has recalibrated which stimuli matter. This isn't a decision; it's a biological adaptation. And it generalizes: parents at a playground find themselves noticing when other people's children do something dangerous, even when they're focused on their own kids.

This shows that bottom-up sensitivity is plastic. It changes based on what your life has taught your brain to care about. Technology companies understand this, which is why their products are designed to create habits that keep recalibrating your brain toward their content.

Top-down control can be strengthened deliberately. That cycle, repeated, is literally training your brain's ability to hold attention where you want it and resist bottom-up interruptions.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Clean your workspace before you start. Before a study or work session, spend five minutes removing visual clutter from your immediate environment. This isn't procrastination — it directly reduces the subconscious processing load your brain carries while you try to focus.

  2. Put your phone out of sight, not just on silent. Out of sight means it no longer generates visual bottom-up load. On silent but visible still registers in that background scanning system. Physical distance matters.

  3. Start a basic meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → practice focused on returning attention. The core skill is simple: choose a focus (breath, a word, a sensation), notice when your attention has wandered, and bring it back. Do this for even five to ten minutes daily. The returning is the training — that's what builds top-down strength.

  4. Notice when you're experiencing bottom-up capture. When you find yourself on your phone or distracted, pause and name it: "That was a bottom-up interruption."   5. Audit one technology habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → this week. Pick one app or notification type and ask: is this serving my goals, or is it capturing my bottom-up attention against my intentions? Then make one concrete change — turn off that notification, delete the app from your home screen, or set a usage window.

  6. Design for focus before you need it. Rather than relying on willpower in the moment, set up your environment in advance. Close unused browser tabs, put your phone in another room, and create a physical space that signals "work" to your brain. The cleaner the environment, the more cognitive fuel stays available for what you actually want to do.
