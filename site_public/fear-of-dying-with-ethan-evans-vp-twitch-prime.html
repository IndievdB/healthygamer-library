Summary

TL;DR — FOMO and over-scheduling are often fear of death in disguise. Feeding that hunger doesn't satisfy it; it grows the hunger.

This is a deep conversation about what drives compulsive over-scheduling, pleasure-seeking, and the fear of missing out — tracing those patterns back to their root in mortality anxiety. The discussion moves from practical barriers to meditation all the way into Buddhist metaphysics, ego death, and a specific meditative practice for people who have "won" the material game and are still miserable.

FOMO Is a Symptom, Not the Problem

When someone schedules nine weekend activities, tries to layer multiple "value propositions" into a single outing, and feels like a failure when they can't attend two ski trips simultaneously, it's tempting to call that a productivity habit or even an admirable zeal for life. But that framing misses what's underneath.

The root emotion isn't enthusiasm. It's desperation.

If you trace FOMO back far enough — past the excitement, past the optimization instinct — you often hit terror. Specifically, terror about death. The logic is simple once you see it: if death is nothingness, then doing seven things at once is proof that you are intensely, undeniably alive. Every packed weekend is a small argument against the void.

"Every weekend is packed with desperation."

The imagery that captures it best is a toddler who has been left at daycare — crying, hungry, desperate for the parent. That same hunger for life, for experience, for presence gets carried forward into adulthood and re-expressed as an overfull Google Calendar.

The Hungry Ghost Problem

There's a concept from Buddhist cosmology called the realm of hungry ghosts — a state that isn't quite hell but isn't far off, where souls eat and eat and never feel full. That's the trap of feeding the beast.

Here's the perverse logic: the more you satisfy a desire born from emptiness, the larger that desire grows. Every successful weekend, every friendship, every new experience doesn't fill the hole — it trains the hole to expect more. What satisfied you five years ago no longer registers. The appetite ratchets up.

This isn't a character flaw. It's just how the mechanism works. You can watch it play out over a lifetime: the kid who just wanted one friend to say yes on a Friday night grows into an adult who has two groups of friends who both want to ski with him, and still feels like a failure because he can't be in both places at once.

"The more you feed it, the more it grows."

The intellectual version of this insight — "the way to have enough is to want less, not to get more" — is easy to agree with. Actually living it is something else entirely, especially if you've spent decades building pride and skill around doing the opposite.

Why Willpower Won't Fix It

The obvious prescription — plan less, meditate more, just stop — doesn't work. This isn't a behavioral problem at its core. It's an emotional one.

The inner structure looks like this: there's a two-year-old who is terrified and hungry, and an adult who has learned to bully that child into silence. The adult tells the child to suck it up, stop being embarrassing, and act mature. The problem is that the child — the emotion, the elephant — always wins in the end. Suppressed emotion doesn't disappear; it goes underground and exerts its influence covertly, cloaked in positivity. "I just love life!" "I hate wasting time!" "I want to make every moment count!" These are socially acceptable costumes the desperation wears.

The rider on the elephant (the intellect) can pull the reins for a while, but the elephant always outlasts the rider. The only path that actually works is aligning what the child wants with what the adult wants — which means addressing the root fear, not managing the symptoms.

Why High Achievers Are Especially Stuck Here

There's a painful irony in succeeding at the material game. Most people sustain themselves on the belief that if I get the job, the family, the money, the friends, then I'll finally be happy. That belief — even if it's wrong — functions as a motivating force. It pulls people forward.

Once you've gotten all those things and the hunger is still there, that illusion is gone. You can't even tell yourself "I just need a little more." You've already had more, and it didn't work. At that point, there's nothing left to pull you toward contentment through external means.

"Spirituality is not born of positivity. It's born of abject desperation and terror."

This is why the timing matters. Spiritual practice isn't most useful as a lifestyle accessory for people who have everything going well. It becomes genuinely necessary — urgent, even — when material success has been achieved and found insufficient. That's the moment you're actually ready for it, because nothing else is left to try.

Learning to Be Comfortable with the Void

The specific practice introduced here is called trataka (fixed-point gazing), used as a precursor to shunya meditation — "shunya" being the Sanskrit word for void or zero.

The connection to mortality anxiety is direct: if you fear death as nothingness, as the end of experience, then the antidote is an experience of nothingness that doesn't kill you. Shunya meditation offers an experience of ego dissolution — a state where the sense of "I" temporarily falls away — and many people find that state not terrifying but profoundly restful. The void turns out to feel like relief rather than annihilation.

Trataka is the on-ramp: - Gaze at a fixed point (ideally a candle flame) without blinking - Start at the base of the candle and slowly move up to the flame over about 30 seconds - Hold your gaze on the flame for another 30–60 seconds - Close your eyes and focus on the afterimage that appears on the "screen" of your mind - Hold your concentration on that afterimage as long as you can

What this practice does is drain you rather than energize you — the opposite of most breath-focused meditation. That fatigue is intentional. It's pulling your mental activity down rather than building it up.

The moment during the practice when you're focused only on the afterimage — not thinking about what you're getting from it, not feeding any hunger — that absence of grasping is a small taste of shunya. Nothing is being pursued. Nothing is being consumed. And that's the state worth practicing.

"For a moment your mind is not trying to feed anything. There's no FOMO, there's no feeding, there's no hunger. There's just you. And what are you in that moment? You're not anything."

Intense physical exercise can reach the same place through a different door — when the demand of the activity is high enough that thinking and worrying become physically impossible, you've achieved a similar state of consciousness. Both paths lead to the same destination: mental function quieted while awareness remains.

Common Pitfalls

Intellectualizing the diagnosis. You can arrive at a psychologically beautiful formulation — "I grew up isolated, so I developed a compensatory hunger for experience" — and feel like you've solved something. You haven't. Understanding the origin story has no emotional teeth unless the underlying feeling is also felt, not just named.

Treating FOMO as the root problem. FOMO is legible and addressable-sounding. Mortality terror is neither. It's easier to try to schedule better, say no more often, or practice mindfulness than to sit with the actual fear underneath. But managing FOMO without touching the fear of death is trimming branches while the root is still intact.

Feeding the beast in more acceptable forms. The hunger doesn't only appear as social scheduling. It shows up as any compulsive pursuit of experience — travel, food, achievement, relationships. Recognizing it only in its most obvious form lets it continue operating under cover everywhere else.

Assuming more success will eventually be enough. It won't. The adaptation mechanism ensures that the next level of achievement simply becomes the new baseline. This isn't pessimism; it's what the evidence of one's own life tends to show, if you look at it honestly.

Pursuing spirituality from a position of comfort rather than necessity. Spiritual practices undertaken as lifestyle enhancement tend to stay shallow. The conditions that make deep change possible are usually desperation and the collapse of external solutions — not peace and stability.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Trace your next FOMO moment to its root. The next time you feel stressed about missing something or over-schedule your weekend, pause and ask: what emotion is underneath this? Don't stop at "excitement" or "FOMO." Keep asking until you hit something that feels heavier — desperation, sadness, anger, fear.

  2. Notice the tantrum. When you can't do everything you want, there's often a layer of anger — a two-year-old throwing toys. Most high-functioning people skip straight to managing the feeling rather than acknowledging it. Let yourself notice the anger before you move on.

  3. Practice trataka with a candle. Get a tall candle. In a dim room, spend about 30 seconds slowly moving your gaze from the base of the candle up to the flame, then hold your gaze on the flame without blinking for 30–60 seconds. Close your eyes and concentrate on the afterimage. Hold it as long as you can. Do this daily. Expect to feel tired afterward rather than refreshed — that's normal for this practice.

  4. Find your existing behavioral anchor for meditation. Identify something you already do every day (a walk, a commute, a morning coffee) and attach 5 minutes of practice to it. Don't try to carve out new time from nothing; attach it to what already exists.

  5. Ask honestly: what have I actually missed out on? Not philosophically — specifically, concretely. Where did the hunger actually begin? What were you genuinely deprived of? Let that have emotional weight rather than treating it as a clinical case study about yourself.

  6. Watch the hunger when you feed it. For one week, notice what happens to a desire after you satisfy it. Does it go away, or does it come back larger? Track this honestly. The lived observation of your own ratcheting appetite is more convincing than any argument.
