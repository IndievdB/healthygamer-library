Growing Up Between Two Worlds

Being biracial doesn't mean you get the best of both cultures. Often it means fitting cleanly into neither. The feeling is captured in a lyric that comes up in the conversation: "Too black for the white kids, too white for the black." In any given group, part of your identity fits and part of it doesn't.

When one part of your identity is more visibly marked by discrimination, you tend to lean into that part — which can inadvertently make the parent from the other background feel unseen. At the same time, that parent genuinely cannot share certain everyday experiences with you: noticing the security guard at a store trailing you, or registering the weight behind a comment like "you're pretty for a black girl." These aren't dramatic confrontations. They're ambient, constant, and largely invisible to people who haven't lived them.

Microaggressions work like this: they carry an insulting premise inside what sounds like a neutral or even complimentary surface. "You're so pretty for a black girl" translates to "I don't normally find black women attractive, but you're an exception." The cough-syrup quality — artificially sweetened but still bad — means the harm can take years to consciously register.

The deeper damage isn't a single incident. It's growing up in an environment where the beauty standard, the pop culture heroes, and the celebrated celebrities consistently look nothing like you. You don't need to be bullied to absorb the message. You just need to notice who gets called the prettiest, who plays the heroine, and whose category only gets celebrated in a separate genre — never in the mainstream.

The Box Inside the Box

One of the more quietly striking moments: joining a Black Student Union and finding community, pride, and belonging — but still not being able to voice a different kind of shame. Having spent years feeling ashamed of being Black, there was now a new shame available: being a Black person who isn't proud enough.

"I had two opposite shames, but both are bad."

The "strong, independent Black woman" framing is well-intentioned, but it operates as a box too. It assigns a fixed emotional posture — strong, not vulnerable; independent, not in need of support — that makes it hard to exist outside those parameters. And when a box gets attached to an identity, stepping outside it can feel like failing that identity entirely.

The pattern here isn't specific to race. Groups that have had to fight hard for pride or dignity often create internal norms about how members are supposed to feel. Expressing doubt or shame inside those norms becomes a second-order transgression. So the shame goes underground.

The distinction matters. You have genuine autonomy over your choices — what you do, how you show up, whether you hit the go-live button. You have very little control over what the world gives back. Who shows up to your stream, whether they subscribe, whether the algorithm rewards you — these aren't in your hands.

When confidence is built on outcomes, it becomes fragile, because outcomes are only partially yours. When it's built on choices, it becomes durable, because choices are fully yours.

This reframe also dissolves some of the impostor feeling. How Impostor Syndrome Actually Works

Impostor syndrome isn't a personality flaw or a sign of low self-esteem. It has a specific structure: your external value (how the world treats you, your follower count, your title, your platform) rises faster than your internal sense of value has had time to develop.

Internal identity is slow to change — it evolves over years, not weeks. Social media and content creation can short-circuit that gradual calibration. One year you're a nursing student; the next year, thousands of people have an emote of your face. Your sense of who you are hasn't caught up.

"Impostor syndrome is the gap between what you feel you are and the way the world treats you."

Impostor syndrome is also more common at high-prestige institutions and in high-visibility roles — not because those people are less capable, but because the external signal is louder. The bigger the gap, the stronger the impostor feeling.

Two things help close it: 1. Let your internal growth catch up naturally over time, especially as external growth becomes more gradual. 2. Detach your self-worth from outcomes and external signals. The gap shrinks when you stop treating your follower count as evidence about who you are.

Emotional Unavailability Is a Solution, Not a Problem

When it comes up that approaching romantic interest requires near-certainty of acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → — waiting for that 99% signal before making a move — the psychology underneath is worth understanding.

The fear isn't really rejection itself. It's the unanswered question. If someone rejects you with a specific reason, you can actually handle it: introspect, decide whether it's something you want to change, adjust or not. But a rejection with no explanation leaves the mind to fill in the blank — and it fills it with its own fears. Not-good-enough, too-hot-headed, not emotionally available, boring.

None of those answers actually come from the other person. They come from you.

"What your mind fills in is coming from you."

Stacking the odds in your favor — making sure a yes is nearly guaranteed before risking a no — feels protective but creates a different problem. If someone says yes after you've performed your most polished version of yourself, you can't actually trust the yes. Did they choose you, or the performance? The validation soothes the insecurity for a moment, then the insecurity returns the next time there's an unanswered text.

Emotional unavailability is a rational protective response to watching vulnerability lead to pain. It makes sense given what you learned. But the solution is not to be convinced it's safe — it's to process the original experiences that made the protection necessary, and to practice small moments of genuine exposure.

A practical workaround: go meta. If you can't express what you feel in the moment, say exactly that. "I'm having trouble saying what I feel right now." That itself is vulnerability, and it's often received as such.

The "My Fear" Shift

Near the end of the conversation, something small but significant happens. Up to that point, the insecurities — I'm not emotionally available, I'll be boring — were being spoken as facts or possibilities. Then, when the framing gets pushed ("it sounds like you really can't afford to be boring"), the response is: "That's my fear."

Possession matters here. "I am boring" is a truth claim about identity. "That's my fear" is an acknowledgment that a thought is something you're carrying, not something that defines you. Moving from I am to it's my fear is a real cognitive shift — you've externalized the thought enough to observe it rather than inhabit it.

Insecurities are not hidden truths about yourself. They're fears. The problem is that we treat them like evidence.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking validation for acceptance. When friends say "you totally deserve this" in response to impostor feelings, they're combating you, not accepting you. Reassurance temporarily quiets the insecurity without resolving it.
  • Confusing the box for the community. Pride-based communities can inadvertently enforce their own conformity. Feeling you have to perform pride — rather than sometimes feeling shame, confusion, or ambivalenceAmbivalence is the state of being pulled in two directions at once — wanting something and simultaneously having a force inside you that resists it.Full definition → — is still a box.
  • Building confidence on outcomes rather than choices. Success that depends on factors outside your control (audience size, external recognition) can't actually build a stable internal sense of value. Only ownership of your choices can.
  • Treating emotional unavailability as a character flaw. It developed for a reason. Trying to eliminate it through willpower without understanding the original function usually doesn't work. Understanding it as protection — then processing what it's protecting against — is more effective.
  • Waiting for the gap to close on its own. Impostor syndrome won't resolve just by accumulating more success. Uncoupling self-worth from external metrics is the actual work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Track which fears you're treating as facts. When a negative thought about yourself arises, practice rephrasing it: "I'm not good enough" becomes "I have a fear that I'm not good enough." Notice the difference in how it sits.

  2. Own your choices, not your outcomes. When evaluating how you did on something, focus on what was within your control — the effort, the decision, the showing up — and consciously separate that from the result. Celebrate the choice to hit go live. Let the numbers be a separate thing.

  3. Practice going meta in vulnerable moments. If you find it hard to express a feeling, say so explicitly to the person you're with: "I want to say something but I'm having trouble getting it out." That statement is itself vulnerability, and it's a starting point.

  6. Notice whose box you're living in. When you feel pressure to perform a particular identity — strong, successful, proud, always-on — ask who assigned that as the standard. Then ask whether you actually endorse it, or whether you just haven't examined it yet.
