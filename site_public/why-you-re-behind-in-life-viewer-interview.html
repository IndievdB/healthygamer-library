Summary

TL;DR — The feeling of falling behind is a mental construction that gets worse the more progress you make — and no external achievement will fix it until you deal with what's driving it from the inside.

This is a one-on-one conversation with JT, a 20-year-old from Singapore who feels desperately behind his peers in education and life. He's working his way up through a tiered school system, pulling a 3.79 GPA, and has a clear ambition to build a mental health organization in Singapore. By most measures he's doing well. His mind refuses to accept that. The conversation unpacks why, and the answer turns out to be something almost everyone who's "fallen behind" shares.

Why Progress Makes the Feeling Worse, Not Better

Here's the paradox at the center of this talk: the further JT has climbed, the worse he feels about how far he has left to go.

When he was in the lowest-tier school, barely trying, he felt behind — but not desperately so. Once he transferred into polytechnic and started genuinely competing, the anxiety spiked. That seems backwards. It isn't.

Think of it as standing at the base of a 10,000-foot mountain. If you're not climbing, you don't have to think about how high it is. You're just hanging out at the bottom. The moment you start climbing and reach 1,000 feet, you look up and suddenly realize: oh no, I still have 9,000 feet to go. The climb itself makes the mountain feel bigger.

The same thing happens when you enter the game everyone else is playing. Before JT was seriously trying, the comparisons weren't live. Once he started seriously trying, he could finally see exactly how far ahead everyone else was — and the gap felt crushing, even as he was actively closing it.

"As you start to catch up, you begin to realize… once you climb 1,000 feet, then you look up and say, 'Oh, I have 9,000 feet to go.'"

This is why telling yourself (or someone else) "you're doing great, you're on track" doesn't land. The feeling of falling behind isn't a measurement error that logic can correct. It's an emotional pattern with its own momentum.

The Feeling Is an Entity, Not a Reading

The feeling of falling behind behaves like a living thing with one job: to survive. It:

  • Discredits any evidence that you're okay. JT is ahead of the schedule his own mentor kept, by two years. His mind immediately found a reason that didn't count.
  • Latches onto any evidence that you're not. One friend has a higher GPA and more volunteer hours? The mind locks onto that and won't let go.
  • Feeds on comparison. Comparison is the mechanism by which the feeling sustains itself. And the feeling will always find a new comparison. Get into university, and you'll compare yourself to people further along in university. Get your master's, and there will be someone with a doctorate. The goalposts never stop moving because the feeling, not reality, is what's setting them.

"The feeling of falling behind is its own entity that will push aside any data that makes it feel weak and will latch on to any data that makes it feel validated."

This is why investment bankers who get promoted still feel behind. This is why high achievers in any field are often quietly miserable. The external metric is never actually the problem.

Goal-Focused vs. Present-Focused Improvement

There are two very different ways to try to improve yourself, and they produce opposite results emotionally.

Goal-focused improvement means your eyes are fixed on the finish line — the degree, the job title, the version of yourself that will finally be "fixed." Every step you take gets measured against that distant endpoint, and since the endpoint is far, every step feels inadequate. The emotion reinforced with each step is: not enough.

Present-focused improvement means your eyes are on the next step. Not the top of the mountain — just the next foothold. You're a 3.79 GPA and you need a 3.9, so you work toward that. Not because it closes the gap between you and some ideal future self, but because it's simply what comes next. The emotion reinforced here is: I'm moving.

The distinction sounds minor. It isn't. Every time you take a step forward in goal-focused mode, you build the feeling of not-enough-ness. You are simultaneously moving forward and telling yourself you haven't moved. Over years, that becomes the dominant mental habit.

"The more you move forward, the more behind you feel. Because what is the emotion that you're reinforcing within yourself? Not enough. It's never enough."

"Fixed" Is Not a Thing

One of the most important moments in this conversation is when JT says he wants to get his problems sorted out so that he can then go help other people — that he needs to be in good shape before he can be of use.

This is a very understandable instinct and it's completely wrong.

There is no version of you that arrives at a finished, healed, fully-capable state and then starts living your actual life. That endpoint doesn't exist. The work and the living happen simultaneously, in the same messy process. The people best equipped to help others who have struggled are not the ones who solved everything cleanly — they're the ones who have actually been through it.

"You have this artificial construction of like one day I'm going to be this thing and I'm going to be fixed. And once I'm fixed then I'm going to go out and I'm going to help the world. Like it's not how it works, man. Like you're never fixed."

This also applies to the meritocratic idea that there is a formula — do A, then B, then C, and you will arrive at success. The world does require you to play the game to some extent. But over-indexing on any fixed map of what success looks like causes you to miss the actual opportunities that show up, which are rarely the ones you planned for.

How to Actually Respond to That Voice

The instinct when an anxious, self-critical voice shows up is to argue with it, suppress it, or try to reason it away. None of those work reliably.

A more effective response is something closer to acknowledgment without capitulation. You're not agreeing that you're a failure. You're not denying that you're behind. You're saying: yes, and — now what?

"Yeah, you're right. Like I can never go back in time and be 15 and learn how to study well. And therefore, I will always be behind my colleagues. I'm going to be behind for the rest of my life. Now what?"

The voice tends to go quiet at "now what." It's built for catastrophizing, not for problem-solving. When you refuse to fight it and instead ask it to offer a constructive suggestion, it often has nothing.

The goal is not to eliminate this voice — it has provided real motivation — but to stop letting it be in control. Treat it the way you'd treat a younger sibling who's scared: acknowledge the fear honestly, don't dismiss it, but also don't let it make the decisions.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking external achievement will resolve the internal feeling. It won't. Getting into a better school, hitting a GPA target, earning a credential — these things don't touch the underlying pattern. You'll find a new comparison almost immediately.

Using awareness alone as the fix. Noticing the feeling is genuinely useful, but if every time you notice it you also take a step forward and tell yourself it's not enough, you're shrinking the samskara with one hand and feeding it with the other. Progress is slower than it should be.

Choosing a sankalpa or affirmation that reinforces the problem. "I am capable" sounds positive. But capability is about potential not yet realized — it keeps your eyes on a future self you haven't become yet, which is exactly the wrong orientation. The wording of the inner story matters more than it seems.

Confusing playing the game with being the game. You do need credentials, you do need to study, you do need to compete in the world as it is. But those are tools, not identity. The moment the metric becomes the point, you've lost the thread.

Waiting to be fixed before starting. If your plan involves first solving yourself and then helping others, the help never comes. The work and the living are the same thing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. When the "not enough" voice appears, don't argue — ask it "now what?" Acknowledge the truth in it ("yes, I am behind") and then ask what it thinks you should actually do. It rarely has an answer, and that breaks the loop.

  2. Redirect your comparison axis. Instead of measuring where you are against where peers are or where you think you should be, measure where you are against where you were six months ago. JT went from gaming all day to studying 2–4 hours daily and pulling near a 3.9 GPA — that gap is real and belongs to him alone.

  3. Identify whether your improvement efforts are present-focused or goal-focused. Ask: "Am I doing this because it's the next step, or because I'm trying to close a gap?" The first is sustainable. The second feeds the samskara.

  4. Rewrite any affirmation or sankalpa you're using. Check whether it orients you toward a future state you haven't reached ("I am capable," "I will succeed"). Replace it with something grounded in what you already are — something like I am what I am or what I am is enough to take the next step.

  5. Practice the role reversal. When the inner critic gets loud, imagine a younger person coming to you with the exact same complaint. What would you actually tell them? Then say that to yourself — honest about the difficulty, but not cruel, and not dishonest about the reality of the path ahead.

  6. Notice where the feeling doesn't apply in your life. JT handles loneliness the way he should handle professional anxiety — he feels it, acknowledges it, doesn't catastrophize it, and lets it pass. Find an area where you already do this well, and use it as a reference point for what the healthier relationship with difficulty feels like.

  7. If you use meditation practice, try japa with Soham. Sit up straight, hold your mala without using the index or pinky finger, and on each bead try to hear the "so" in the inhale and the "hum" in the exhale rather than just repeating the word mentally. The meaning — I am that which I am — is the orientation you're practicing, not a goal you're chasing.
