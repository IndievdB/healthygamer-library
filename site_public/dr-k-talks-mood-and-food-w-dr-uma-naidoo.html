Artificial Sweeteners: Why "Zero Calories" Isn't Neutral

The common assumption — no calories, no consequences — is wrong in a specific and interesting way.

Your body has sweetness receptors not just on your tongue but in your gut, liver, and elsewhere. When an artificial sweetener activates those receptors, your body interprets the signal as sugar incoming and begins clearing glucose from the bloodstream in preparation for a new influx of calories. The calories never arrive. The result is a mild blood sugar dip, which can trigger cravings and prompt you to eat more.

"Your body starts to remove sugar out of the blood because it perceives more food is coming — and then you have a sugar crash."

This is one reason people who regularly drink diet soda with high-calorie meals often don't see the weight-loss benefit they expect. The physiology is working against them.

Artificial sweeteners also disrupt the gut microbiome — they don't provide the fiber and whole-food nutrition gut bacteria need, and they feed a dysbiotic environment that promotes inflammation. Inflammation, in turn, is increasingly linked in research to depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, brain fog, and impaired attention and memory.

Newer sweeteners like allulose appear to behave differently and may not trigger the same insulin response — but the safest heuristic is still moderation, not substitution at scale.

Fermented Foods, Prebiotics, and Feeding Your Microbiome

Every culture has fermented foods: kimchi, kefir, kombucha, yogurt, miso. These contain live active cultures that populate your gut. You don't need to know which bacterial strain does what to benefit — eating a diverse diet heavy in plant foods and including fermented foods regularly will give you a broad range of microbes.

Prebiotics are what feed those microbes — and they come from food, not just supplements:

  • Garlic, leeks, onions, bananas, jicama, Jerusalem artichokes
  • Vegetables generally, via their fiber content

The simple principle: fiber feeds your microbiome. Most Americans are chronically fiber-deficient. Meat and seafood contain no fiber. If your diet is heavy on animal protein, your gut bacteria are being starved of their preferred fuel.

The psychobiotics field — using pre- and probiotics intentionally to improve psychiatric symptoms — is emerging as a potential way to reduce reliance on prescription medication for some individuals. It's not a replacement for treatment; it's an additional tool.

Why Eating Healthy Is Biologically Hard (And Dieting Makes It Worse)

This is the part that most nutrition advice skips entirely, and it explains why you've probably failed at changing your diet before.

Your current gut bacteria crave what they eat. If you've spent years eating processed food, you've selected for bacteria that thrive on processed food. When you switch to broccoli and cut the french fries, those bacteria start to die. As they die, they release chemical signals — including specific neurotransmitters that cross the blood-brain barrier — that induce cravings for the foods that kept them alive. They also trigger whole-body inflammation, which fatigues your frontal lobesThe brain region responsible for planning, executing tasks, controlling impulses, and regulating other parts of the brain — essentially the hardware of self-regulation and mature behavior.Full definition → and depletes willpower.

This is why dieting is hardest at the one-week mark, not the first two days. The first two days your bacteria have reserves. By day seven, they're dying, and they're taking your willpower with them.

"Your body doesn't want to eat healthy. For millions of years, the struggle for life was a struggle for calories."

Caloric density was survival. Your body still runs that old code. High-fat, high-sugar food isn't tempting because you're weak — it's tempting because your biology correctly identified it as survival-relevant and wired that preference in deep.

The obesity set point makes crash dieting self-defeating. Your body maintains a weight it considers "safe." When you crash diet, your body interprets this as famine. When the diet ends and food returns, it raises the set point to prepare for the next famine. Repeat this cycle enough times and each rebound lands higher than the last. The diet industry depends on this mechanism — it keeps customers coming back.

What actually works: Permanent changes small enough that they don't require willpower to sustain. The goal is to find substitutions you genuinely don't mind making — removing one slice of bread, switching soda for sparkling water, choosing plain yogurt with frozen blueberries over the pre-sweetened kind. The standard isn't perfection; it's slightly better than yesterday, indefinitely.

Meeting People Where They Are: The Practical Psychiatry of Food

The most useful framing from the clinical side: start with what's bothering you most, not with what you "should" eat.

There is no single correct diet. Carnivore dieters miss phytonutrients and fiber. Strict vegans may miss omega-3 fatty acids and vitamin B12. Neither direction is automatically wrong — both have gaps to address. A nutritional psychiatrist's job isn't to convert anyone to a particular eating ideology; it's to work within what someone already eats and find targeted improvements.

Food and lifestyle changes aren't alternatives to medication; for most people, they're the multiplier.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating healthy eating as a temporary state. "I'm on a diet" implies there's an end date. An end date means your body eventually interprets the deprivation as famine and adjusts upward. Changes need to be permanent enough that your body never registers a famine.
  • Trying to optimize before establishing the basics. Worrying about digestive enzyme efficiency, optimal macronutrient ratios, or specific bacterial strains while still eating primarily processed food is the nutritional equivalent of studying pro-level game mechanics when you're still in bronze. Get the foundations first.
  • Drinking diet soda expecting a free pass. The zero-calorie assumption ignores the receptor signaling cascade, the gut microbiome disruption, and the downstream cravings.
  • Smoothies as a complete substitute for eating whole foods. They're better than nothing — especially if they're getting you fiber you'd otherwise skip — but combining multiple macronutrients into one drink dilutes digestive efficiency. A simple test: chew a plain piece of bread for 30 seconds and notice when it starts to taste sweet. That's amylase doing its job at full efficiency. Add cheese to the same bite and the sweetness doesn't develop the same way.
  • Cracking during the first week of a dietary change and interpreting it as proof you can't do it. The first week is when your bacteria are dying and screaming. If you eat the thing they're demanding at day five, you reinforce the craving circuit and make the next attempt harder.
  • Conflating "supplement" with "fix." Supplements are not substitutes for a bad diet; they're partial compensations. The supplement industry's business model depends on daily indefinite consumption, not on solving the underlying problem.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your one-week willpower cliff. Before changing anything, set a realistic expectation: the hardest moment of any dietary change will be around days five to seven. Knowing this in advance lets you prepare support rather than being blindsided.

  2. Make one permanent substitution, not a diet. Pick something small — swap your daily soda for sparkling water, switch from pre-sweetened yogurt to plain yogurt with frozen blueberries and cinnamon, or try an open-face sandwich instead of a full one. Choose something you can actually live with indefinitely. That constraint matters more than the quality of the swap.

  3. Add one fermented food to your regular rotation. Kefir, plain yogurt with live cultures, kimchi, kombucha — whatever your palate tolerates. Consistency over the weeks is what matters, not the specific product.

  4. Eat prebiotic foods regularly. Garlic, onions, leeks, and bananas are common and inexpensive. These feed the bacteria you're trying to cultivate.

  5. Get 10 minutes of outdoor light daily, before sunscreen. This isn't a metaphor for "go outside more." It's a specific behavioral sequence: step outside, no sunscreen, 10 minutes, then apply protection if you're staying longer. Do this before noon when UVB is more available.
