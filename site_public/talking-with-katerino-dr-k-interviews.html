Depersonalization Is a Survival Skill, Not a Defect

Depersonalization — feeling like a passenger in your own body, watching your life from a few steps back — sounds like a malfunction. It isn't. It's a protective response that the brain learned under unbearable conditions.

When you go through enough traumaTrauma is what happens when an experience generates more emotion than you can process in a single moment — the overwhelm itself is the defining feature, not just the event.Full definition → young enough, your nervous system figures out a shortcut: if you don't fully inhabit your experience, it can't fully hurt you. The problem is that skill doesn't turn off when the danger passes. It becomes your default.

Kate describes it as "telling all my thoughts and feelings to f*** off." She wasn't choosing numbness — her brain had just gotten very, very good at it after years of practice. Every major loss added more points to that skill:

  • Sexual abuse ages four to six
  • Grandfather's death at age eleven
  • Father's death at seventeen
  • A long relationship she felt stuck in

"You're not bad at processing emotions. Being resistant to your emotions has been a survival skill for you."

The reframe matters. "My brain is broken" leads nowhere. "My brain learned something that protected me, and now that protection is costing me" opens a door.

On Self-Forgiveness: What It Actually Requires

A lot of people want to forgive themselves but can't figure out how. The reason is usually that they're making excuses — and you cannot forgive what you've explained away.

"If there's an excuse, there isn't a sin. And if there isn't a sin, there's no capacity for forgiveness."

Kate's path through this was to accept the labels without softening them. She cheated. She lied. She hurt people she cared about. She didn't frame it as a mental health episode, or blame the relationship dynamics, or make herself a victim. She owned it — and that is what made forgiveness possible.

This is a genuinely hard tightrope to walk. The goal isn't self-flagellation ("I'm an awful person") — that's not mourning, it's just ranting. The goal is to let shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → have a seat at the table without letting it run the meeting.

You Don't Deserve Anyone Else's Forgiveness — But You Deserve Your Own Understanding

One of the more clarifying moments in the conversation is the distinction between forgiving yourself and expecting others to forgive you.

Nobody owes you forgiveness. It isn't something you're entitled to, and expecting it puts you in a powerless position — your healing depends on what someone else decides. What you can do is extend to yourself the same understanding you'd give a friend.

"I deserve understanding just as much as everyone else does. The understanding I give to my friends when they make mistakes — I deserve to give myself that too."

This isn't letting yourself off the hook. It's recognizing that self-understanding and accountability aren't opposites. You can fully own what you did and try to understand the person who did it.

Common Pitfalls

"I depersonalized, so I wasn't fully responsible." Depersonalization and responsibility can coexist. Explaining the mechanism behind your behavior is not the same as being absolved of it.

Making excuses instead of seeking understanding. These feel similar from the inside but work oppositely. Understanding opens the door to change; excusing closes it.

Confusing emotional numbness with emotional processing. If you rarely cry, rarely get angry, and mostly deflect with humor — that's not equanimity. That's suppression wearing equanimity's clothes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the skill, not the flaw. If you've been telling yourself "I'm bad at emotions," reframe it: My emotional resistance developed for a reason. Ask yourself when you first learned to check out — what was happening in your life that made numbness useful?

  2. Use a sensory anchor when feelings get overwhelming. Pick an object with visual detail — the grain of a wooden cup, the texture of a fabric, a candle flame. When emotions flood in, focus on the object. This narrows the overwhelm down to something examinable rather than something to escape.

  3. Identify the emotion before analyzing it. When something happens, resist jumping straight to "I think what's going on is..." First, ask: what did I want to do in that moment? (Run away. Stay. Shout. Disappear.) That impulse is the raw feeling. The analysis can come after.

  4. Find the unfinished goodbyes. Think about losses where you didn't fully show up — where you made a joke instead of grieving, or walked out of the hospital room, or stayed busy so you wouldn't have to feel it. You don't have to do anything about them today. Just name them.

  5. Practice accepting a label without excusing it. For something you've done that you feel bad about, try saying — out loud or in writing — "I did X. I hurt Y. That was me." No qualifiers. Notice whether that feels like self-attack or something else.

  6. Check the physical symptoms. If you have a symptom — chronic pain, sexual dysfunction, GI issues, skin problems — that you've been quietly embarrassed about and assuming is just you, talk to a doctor. A meaningful number of things people assume are permanent character defects are treatable medical conditions.

  7. Separate self-forgiveness from others' forgiveness. Make a list of who you feel you need forgiveness from. Circle your own name. Work on that one independently of the rest — it doesn't require anyone else's participation, and it isn't contingent on what they decide.
