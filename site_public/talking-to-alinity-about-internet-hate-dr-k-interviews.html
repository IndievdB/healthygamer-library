Summary

TL;DR — Receiving relentless internet hate is a form of ongoing abuse that erodes a person's sense of self, and the path through it isn't about controlling what others think — it's about returning to who you actually are on the inside.

This is a candid, emotionally raw conversation with a streamer named Natalia (Alinity) who has spent years absorbing massive, organized hatred online — daily messages telling her to kill herself, coordinated harassment campaigns, and drama channels that monetize her suffering. What unfolds is less a clinical session and more an honest exploration of what it costs a human being to absorb that much hatred over that much time, and what — if anything — can be done about it.

What Sustained Hatred Actually Does to a Person

Most people get to make mistakes, learn from them, and move on. When you have years of footage online and an audience dedicated to surfacing it, you don't get that. Every mistake gets replayed, recontextualized, and weaponized — sometimes indefinitely.

The psychological effect is strikingly similar to what happens in abusive relationships or childhood abuse: when you hear the same accusations loudly enough and for long enough, you start to believe them. The internal voice that knows better gets quieter. You begin to treat the crowd's verdict as evidence.

What makes Natalia's situation unusual is that the crowd never moves on. A new streamer mentions her, and a fresh wave arrives. An unrelated scandal trends, and somehow she's implicated. The accusations compound — copyright abuse, immigration fraud, animal cruelty — each one built partly on real mistakes, partly on distortion, and partly on pure fabrication. Official investigations cleared her on most counts. Nobody noticed.

"I just I hit a point where like I don't want to have to deal with it anymore."

The cost isn't just emotional exhaustion. It reshapes how you move through the world. Natalia describes calculating every word before she streams — obsessively anticipating how it might be clipped, framed, misread. That constant internal audit is the very opposite of who she used to be: carefree, spontaneous, funny.

The Control Problem Underneath It All

The most important insight in the conversation doesn't come from the interviewer — it comes from Natalia herself, on a bathroom break.

She returns with a realization: her lifelong pattern has been about control. Her eating disorder as a teenager — controlling food when everything else felt chaotic. Reorganizing her closet color by color when stress peaks. And now: the anguish of being unable to control what thousands of people think and say about her.

This matters because it reframes the problem. The internet hate isn't just painful — it's painful in a specific way, one that maps onto her deepest vulnerability. Growing up with a father in prison, parents separating, cartel violence, rapid upheaval — she learned young that the world is unpredictable and that the only safe harbor is what you can personally manage.

The internet takes that harbor away completely.

"I can't control what people think about me — that upsets me. I can't control what people say about me — that upsets me."

Naming this doesn't solve it, but it transforms it from "the world is against me" into something that has a shape and a history. That's the beginning of being able to work with it.

Why They Hate — and Why It Isn't Really About You

Natalia's own theory: hurt people hurt. People who send daily harassment messages probably don't have many sources of joy or belonging, so they find it in the collective ritual of hating a shared target. She became a meme — which means she became useful to people who needed something to organize against.

Drama channels amplify this by monetizing it. Every video about her gets views. Every wave of hate brings engagement. Her suffering is a content format.

The important implication: the intensity of hatred directed at someone says almost nothing about what that person actually did. The mathematics of internet outrage — confirmation bias, influencer amplification, the dopamine of collective punishment — can sustain massive hatred against someone who did something minor, or even something they didn't do at all.

"There is no way that you have done anything that would justify random people on the internet who you have never met — and who have never met you — hating you that much. That has nothing to do with you."

This isn't the same as saying she did nothing wrong. She acknowledges real mistakes: off-color jokes, drinking heavily on stream, the cat incident. But the scale of the response is not proportional to the offense. When the scale is that disproportionate, the hatred is about the haters, not the target.

The Trap of Performing Strength

For two years, Natalia kept her composure publicly. Didn't let them see it hurt. Stayed strong.

This backfired in two ways. First, it made the haters angrier — they want a reaction, and not getting one escalates the campaign. Second, it forced her to maintain a performance that slowly drained her, while preventing her from accessing the support she actually needed.

She hadn't told her psychiatrist she'd been having suicidal thoughts because it felt too hard to explain. She'd been managing it through her friends simply not leaving her alone — effective, but not the same as getting real help.

"I appear really strong, but it's so much work."

When people tell you that you're strong, they're also — without meaning to — telling you that you need to keep being strong. That you can't stop. That breaking down would disappoint them. It's its own kind of trap.

What Actually Helps (And What Doesn't)

A few things that genuinely provided some relief:

Separating yourself from the idea they hate. Natalia's main coping mechanism was reminding herself that the person being hated online is a character — a narrative constructed from clips, rumors, and confirmation bias — not actually her. This works, but requires constant conscious effort, which is exhausting.

Understanding why the hatred isn't about you. Not as a feel-good mantra, but as a genuine structural insight. When you understand the mechanics of internet pile-ons, the hatred stops feeling like a verdict.

Limiting exposure. Simple, unsexy, real. Not reading the comments, not doom-scrolling through mentions. She already knows this. The challenge is that her job requires her to be online.

Meditation and breath work. Not as an escape from circumstances, but as training for creating a quiet space inside your own mind — something the Twitter DMs can't follow you into. The specific technique introduced is triphasic breathing: belly first, then chest expansion, then a slight lift of the shoulders to fill the top of the lungs. Three distinct phases. The goal is learning to be present inside your own body rather than inside the noise.

Accepting what you can't control. You control your actions. You do not control how people receive them. Holding that distinction clearly — not as resignation but as accurate perception — removes a source of enormous ongoing suffering.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming all of it is a mental health symptom. When someone has a diagnosis like bipolar disorder, it's easy to attribute impulsivity and emotional intensity entirely to the condition. But meanness, impulsivity, and emotional reactivity are things all humans experience and can work on — a diagnosis may lower the threshold, but it doesn't eliminate agency or the possibility of growth.

Trying to appease the haters. There's no action she can take that will satisfy people whose relationship with her is primarily about belonging to an outrage community. Trying to find the magic apology or the right behavior that will finally make it stop is a trap — it assumes a rationality and good faith that isn't there.

Performing invulnerability. Staying strong publicly for two years made things worse in both directions: it escalated the harassment and cut her off from real support. Showing that something hurts isn't the same as crumbling — and crumbling isn't the same as losing.

Taking "I can't control the narrative" as a license to stop caring about actions. The insight that you can't control reception is not an argument for doing whatever you want. You're still responsible for what you do — you're just not responsible for the irrational responses to it.

Isolating from mental health providers because you don't think they'll understand. The psychiatrist might not get Twitch. Explain it anyway. They've probably seen more of this than you'd expect, and even a partial understanding is better than them not knowing you're struggling at all.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Contact your mental health provider this week if you're experiencing suicidal thoughts, even passively. You don't need a perfectly articulated explanation. "I've been having a really hard time and I want you to know" is enough to start.

  2. Practice triphasic breathing once daily. Sit upright. Breathe into the belly first, then expand the chest, then lift the shoulders slightly to fill the top of the lungs. Exhale slowly. Repeat three to five times. The goal isn't immediate peace — it's building a mental space that you own, that external noise can't enter.

  3. Do a one-week audit of your exposure. Note how much time you spend reading comments, mentions, and DMs. Identify one category you can reduce or delay (e.g., don't read Instagram comments until noon, or only check once per day). You won't eliminate the problem, but you may reduce the dose.

  4. Write down what you know to be true about yourself — not general human goodness, but specific things. What do the people who actually know you say? What have you changed? What are you actively trying to do differently? Keep this somewhere you can read it when the outside noise gets loud.

  5. Separate "what I did" from "how people responded to it." When you catch yourself blaming yourself for the scale of the hatred, try writing two columns: what you actually did, and what the response was. Notice where they're disproportionate. The gap is not your fault.

  6. Protect your carefree self, even in small doses. Whether it's a gaming session with close friends, time outside, or anything that feels like the pre-harassment version of you — schedule it deliberately. The erosion of that self is one of the most serious long-term costs of sustained harassment.

  7. If you're the one sending hate to someone online: pause and ask what you're actually angry about, and whether this person is actually the source of it.
