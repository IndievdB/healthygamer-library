Summary

TL;DR — Panic attacks aren't just "anxious thoughts." They're a self-reinforcing loop between your mind and body, and breaking that loop requires working on both ends simultaneously.

Feeling perpetually on edge, or getting hit by full panic attacks, isn't a character flaw or a sign you're broken. It's a physiological cascade that, once you understand the mechanics, becomes something you can actually intervene on. There are four levels of treatment, from the most evidence-backed to the more variable, and they work best in combination.

The Panic Loop: Why Your Mind and Body Trap Each Other

A panic attack isn't just a thought spiraling out of control. It's a two-way feedback loop between your cognition and your nervous system, and once it starts, each side makes the other worse.

Here's the chain of events:

  1. A thought triggers the sympathetic nervous systemThe body's fight-or-flight activation system — one half of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes you for intense physical action.Full definition → — the fight-or-flight system.
  2. Signals travel down the sympathetic chain ganglia (a series of nerve clusters along your spine) to the adrenal glands, releasing cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition → and adrenaline.
  3. These are hormones, meaning they travel through your entire bloodstream — including back to your brain.
  4. Adrenaline in the brain does something particularly sinister: it makes potential dangers feel like real dangers. It also speeds up your thinking and ramps up emotional reactivity.
  5. More thoughts → more sympathetic activation → more adrenaline → more thoughts. That's the panic loop.

This is why telling yourself "just calm down" rarely works mid-panic. By that point, the loop is running on physiology, not just psychology.

Why Breathing Into a Paper Bag Actually Works

When your sympathetic nervous system fires, your breathing speeds up. Hyperventilation is the result — and the real problem isn't too little oxygen, it's that you're blowing off too much CO2.

Low CO2 causes lightheadedness and intensifies the feeling that something is terribly wrong. Breathing into a paper bag recycles CO2 back into the air you're inhaling, restoring that balance. But there's a more important mechanism: as the oxygen concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → inside the bag drops, you're forced to breathe more deeply to get enough of it.

Deep, slow breathing — especially long exhalations — directly activates the parasympathetic nervous systemThe branch of the autonomic nervous system responsible for "rest and digest" — the body's recovery and regeneration mode, as opposed to the fight-or-flight activation of the sympathetic system.Full definition →, the "rest and digest" counterpart to fight-or-flight. The speed of your mind is closely tied to the length of your exhale. Slow the exhale, slow the mind, interrupt the loop.

This is why meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → techniques that emphasize long exhalations work. They're not just relaxing — they're doing something measurable to your nervous system.

The Two Places You Can Break the Cycle

You can interrupt a panic attack at the cognitive level (changing the thought) or at the physiological level (changing the body's state). Doing both is more powerful than either alone.

Physiology-first tools like breathwork, meditation, and grounding techniques don't require you to think your way out of panic — which is useful, because thinking clearly while panicking is extremely hard. They buy you enough calm to then address the thoughts.

Why Medication and Skill-Building Work Better Together

This is one of the most useful reframes for anyone uncertain about medication: medicine doesn't replace skill-building, it makes skill-building possible.

Imagine your panic attack is level 100 intensity, but your coping skills are only level 50. Your skills are simply overwhelmed. You can't practice your way out of a flood.

Medication reduces the intensity — say, down to level 50. Now your skills are sufficient. You successfully use them, and each successful use levels the skill up. Over time, even if you come off the medication, your skills have grown enough to handle the full-intensity panic on their own.

Your Baseline State Matters — and It Can Be Changed

Most of the conversation about panic focuses on the acute attack. But there's a more fundamental issue: where your nervous system lives day-to-day.

People with anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → and panic disorders tend to have a baseline sympathetic bias — their nervous system is already running hot before anything stressful happens. This is measurable through heart rate variability (HRV). People with anxiety often have lower HRV and resting heart rates closer to 90 bpm. High HRV — seen in athletes and people with very active parasympathetic systems — corresponds to resting heart rates as low as 40 bpm, and a much greater capacity to absorb stress before tipping over.

The good news: this baseline is not fixed. Things that shift it:

  • Diet — gut bacteria have a surprising role in anxiety. A specific bacterium called Ruminococcus (sometimes called ruminobacteria) is found in higher levels in people with anxiety. Supporting healthy gut serotoninA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition → production through diet is a legitimate lever.
  • Mind-body practices — yoga, tai chi, and meditation are particularly effective at shifting the sympathetic/parasympathetic balance over time, more so than exercise alone (though exercise helps too).

This is where the separation of mind and body in mainstream mental health has caused real harm. Physiologic anxiety — where panic arises from a body that's perpetually primed, not from a specific thought — gets missed when clinicians are trained to look only at cognition.

The Deep Work: Psychoanalytic and Unconscious Approaches

The fourth bucket of treatment is the least predictable, but sometimes the most transformative. The idea is that some anxiety has a root in an unresolved psychological complex or emotional knot — something buried in the unconscious that keeps firing the alarm system without your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

The evidence here is real but messy. Duration of treatment is unpredictable. It works for some people and not others, and it's hard to know why. But the moments when it does work tend to be the most profound in clinical practice.

"Sometimes in therapy you get breakthroughs where you find the origin of your panic and you release that emotional knot of energy and then you feel way better."

The practical framing: this approach — sometimes called "scar work" or depth psychotherapy — is worth knowing about, but it shouldn't be the first or only tool you reach for. Think of it as potentially addressing the root when the other approaches have helped but haven't fully resolved things.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to think your way out of a panic attack in real time. Once the physiological loop is running, cognition alone is usually insufficient. You need to intervene at the body level first.
  • Treating reassurance-seeking as a solution. Reassurance gives temporary relief but doesn't address the underlying wiring. Over time it can reinforce the anxiety cycle rather than dissolve it.
  • Stopping medication and expecting nothing to change. If you stop medication without having built skills during the window it provided, the panic is likely to return at full intensity. The medication buys you the window — you still have to use it.
  • Assuming anxiety is purely cognitive. Many people have physiologic anxiety — their body is the trigger, not a specific thought. Treating only the thought patterns while ignoring the nervous system's baseline state leaves a major lever untouched.
  • Expecting deep psychoanalytic work to be quick. It can produce dramatic breakthroughs, but it can also take years with uncertain outcomes. It's a valuable tool, not a reliable fast track.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice long-exhale breathing daily. Try a 4-count inhale and 6–8 count exhale for five minutes. Do this before you're anxious, so the skill is available when you need it. The goal is to make parasympathetic activation a trained reflex, not a desperate measure.

  2. During a panic attack, go physiological first. Before trying to challenge your thoughts, slow your breath. Take the deepest breath you can and extend the exhale as long as possible. Repeat until you feel the loop beginning to slow before addressing what you were thinking.

  3. Track your resting heart rate over a few weeks. If you have a wearable or can take a morning pulse, watch the trend. As you add in the practices below, a gradual downward drift is a concrete sign your baseline is shifting.

  4. Add a mind-body practice to your weekly routine. Yoga or tai chi three or more times a week has a measurable effect on your sympathetic/parasympathetic balance over time. This isn't about acute relief — it's about changing where your nervous system rests.

  5. If you're considering medication, reframe the goal. Use the period when medication is reducing symptom intensity as an active training window. Practice your coping skills as much as possible during this time. The goal is to level the skill high enough that the medication becomes optional.

  6. Look into your gut health as a factor. This doesn't require dramatic dietary changes — start by increasing fiber-rich and fermented foods that support healthy gut microbiome diversity, and notice over weeks whether baseline anxiety shifts.

  7. If you've worked through the first three approaches and still feel stuck, consider depth-oriented therapy. Ask a therapist explicitly about working with the unconscious origins of anxiety, rather than only cognitive symptom management. Go in with realistic expectations about timeline.
