The Fantasy Trap

In this video, we'll explore the fantasy trap. The fantasy trap is one reason that we get stuck. Our mind actually uses negative emotion to motivate us to change. However, our mind has a coping mechanism - fantasy - that protects us from negative emotion. Unfortunately, getting caught up in fantasy can lead us to be trapped in a false world - where we dream and dream, and never make a change.

Summary

TL;DR — Fantasy feels like coping, but it secretly keeps you stuck by draining the negative emotion that would otherwise push you to change.

Many people who describe themselves as depressed aren't just sad about the present — they're caught in a loop of regret about the past. Their minds are obsessed with the "shoulda, woulda, coulda": if I hadn't made that mistake, my life would be completely different by now. What follows is a mental escape into a rewritten history, a fantasy where everything worked out. It feels like relief. It isn't. This piece breaks down exactly why that trap works, why it's so hard to escape, and what to do instead.

How Fantasy Actually Works in the Mind

Fantasy isn't random — it has a specific psychological function. It takes a negative emotion and transforms it into a positive one.

Consider being bullied as a kid. You're sitting there, ashamed, holding back tears. Then your mind kicks in: I'm going to learn martial arts, and one day I'll come back and teach that bully a lesson, and everyone will cheer. Suddenly you feel triumphant — even though nothing has changed. You just got beaten up.

That's the mechanism. The negative emotion (shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, humiliation, regret) triggers a mental script. The script rewrites a painful reality into a satisfying alternative. The negative feeling dissolves and a positive one takes its place.

This is why fantasy is so seductive. It isn't laziness or stupidity. It's your brain doing something it's genuinely good at: emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition →. The problem isn't that it feels good. The problem is what it costs you.

Why the Relief Is Actually the Problem

The discomfort of a situation is what pushes you to change it. This isn't just a pop-psychology idea — the brain's emotional centers (particularly the amygdalaThe brain's negative emotional center — the region that generates fear, anxiety, and survival responses.Full definition →) sit directly next to the learning and memory centers (the hippocampusThe brain structure responsible for memory consolidation — taking information from short-term storage and transferring it into long-term understanding.Full definition →). The wiring between "this feels bad" and "I need to do something differently" is tight and strong.

Think about food poisoning. Ten great meals at a restaurant mean nothing after one time getting sick there. Negative experience drives behavioral change far more powerfully than positive experience does.

Fantasy short-circuits that process entirely. When you feel bad about your life and immediately escape into a mental rewrite of the past, you bleed off the very emotional pressure that would have pushed you to act. You pull the plug on your own fuel supply.

"Instead of using that negativity to actually change their behavior and make a change in their life — when they engage in the fantasy trap, they sort of pull the plug on that negative emotion. It kind of drains away. And then they end up doing nothing to fix their life."

This same dynamic shows up with video games, substances, and other escapes. The common thread is that they all shut down the emotional discomfort before it can do its job.

The Two Ways Fantasy Keeps You Stuck

There are two distinct costs, and it's worth separating them.

First: it drains your emotional fuel. As described above, negative emotion is the engine of change. Fantasy metabolizes that emotion before it can drive action. You end up feeling temporarily better and permanently stationary.

Second: it spends your cognitive energy on a dead end. Your mind has a limited budget of problem-solving energy. When you fantasize about how the past could have gone differently, you are doing real, effortful cognitive work — tracing counterfactuals, building alternate timelines, imagining ripple effects. If I had done X, then Y would have happened, and then Z would be different today.

That mental effort produces something called a false present — a vivid, emotionally satisfying picture of a life you are not actually living. And here's the trap: you cannot take any action from a false present. It is not real. There is nowhere to go from there.

"When your mind rewrites history, it creates a false present. And you spent all of your energy doing this. You can't actually move anywhere from a false present."

You've burned your cognitive budget building a room you can never actually stand in.

Why "Just Live in the Present" Is Harder Than It Sounds

The standard advice — be present, practice mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition →, stay in the now — is correct but usually delivered without honesty about why it's so difficult. Here's the honest version: the present is painful.

When you stop rewriting the past and actually look at where you are, you have to face the full weight of your current situation. The mistakes weren't undone. The time wasn't recovered. The gap between where you are and where you want to be is real and in front of you.

Fantasy is appealing precisely because it makes the present bearable without requiring you to do anything about it. Living in the present, by contrast, means acknowledging that things are hard and that moving forward from here is going to take effort. That's genuinely uncomfortable. But it is the only place from which any action is possible.

"The only time action can be taken is from the present. The problem with the fantasy trap is that it lets us get out of the present. It absolves us of dealing with the present. And if it absolves us of dealing with the present, we can't actually move forward."

It's worth noting that forward-looking fantasy has the same problem. Imagining a glorious future if you start working hard today is still a fantasy. It still relocates your mental energy away from the actual present moment. The question isn't where will I end up — it's what do I do right now.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the emotional relief of fantasy for progress. Feeling better after a mental rewrite feels like you've processed something. You haven't. You've deferred it. The regret and negative emotion will come back, and you'll need to fantasize again to suppress it again — a cycle that never moves.

Thinking the solution is to fantasize about the future instead. Swapping past-directed fantasy for future-directed fantasy doesn't escape the trap. Both versions relocate your energy away from the present moment. Both produce a reality you can't take action from.

Believing your regret is uniquely justified. Some people feel their past mistakes were so definitive, so final, that they genuinely have nothing to work with now. This feeling is real — but it's also what the trap produces. It's worth questioning whether the hopelessness is an accurate assessment of reality or itself a product of the cycle.

Confusing tolerating negative emotion with wallowing in it. Sitting with discomfort doesn't mean ruminating indefinitely. The goal is to let the feeling exist long enough to ask a productive question, not to marinate in self-criticism.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the script as it starts. When you notice your mind beginning to rewrite the past — if only I had, if I could have, if I would have — name it out loud or in writing: "I'm doing the fantasy trap right now." AwarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → of the pattern is the first interruption of it.

  2. Sit with the negative emotion before escaping it. When you feel the pull toward fantasy, deliberately pause. Don't immediately redirect. Let the discomfort be there for a moment. Notice what it actually feels like in your body. This is uncomfortable, and that's the point — you're letting the emotional information exist instead of draining it away.

  3. Swap one question for another. The mind naturally asks what should I have done? Practice replacing it with: "What am I going to do now?" Write the question down if it helps. Keep redirecting back to it every time the old question resurfaces.

  4. Ask for the next step, not the destination. Don't ask yourself where this path leads or what your life will look like in five years. Ask: What is the first step I can take today? What is the next step from where I actually am right now? Keep the scope small and concrete.

  5. Notice when other escapes serve the same function. Pay attention to whether gaming, scrolling, substances, or other activities are being used the same way fantasy is — to bleed off discomfort before it motivates change. The mechanism is identical even when the behavior looks different.
