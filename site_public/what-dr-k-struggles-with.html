The Counterintuitive Fix: Work With Your Temperament, Not Against It

The insight that changed everything wasn't "try harder to be consistent." It was the opposite:

"You're destined for failure if you just do one thing. You're going to get bored. You need three or four different things where you can switch from one to the next. When you get bored, the next thing is ready for you — and by the time you get to thing three, you can get excited about thing one again."

Understanding as the Engine of Not-Struggling

There's a pattern here that goes beyond temperament. The root cause of most struggle is a lack of understanding — of yourself, of the situation, of why something is happening.

Consider learning a video game. When you don't understand the mechanics, you struggle constantly and often blame other players. Then you realize you were the problem — you simply didn't understand what was happening. That understanding doesn't feel like failure; it feels like relief. Now you can actually improve.

The same thing happens with emotions. If you start feeling anxious when something hits close to home, the instinct is to shut it down or move away from it. But that instinct is the problem:

"Don't try to make it go away. Try to understand where it's coming from. That's the biggest problem in our society today — we're so hell-bent on making things go away instead of understanding where they come from."

Suppressing the signal doesn't resolve what's generating it. Understanding does.

Environment Is a Variable You Can Change

One of the most practical takeaways here is that you don't have to fix your temperament — you can fix your environment instead.

The question to ask yourself isn't "why can't I do this the normal way?" It's "what conditions would let me do this in my way?"

What Happens to Creative People When They Succeed

There's a secondary insight worth pulling out: success can become creatively paralyzing. When you're making your first thing, you create from the inside — you're just putting out what's in your heart with no audience expectation to manage. When that thing succeeds, the audience forms an idea of what they want from you. And the more you care about delivering that, the more your mind moves outward — toward what people want — and away from the internal source that made the first thing good.

"The more you start caring about the people, the worse your art becomes."

This isn't a moral failing. It's a structural trap. The work that reached people was authentic; the attempt to reproduce that authenticity by reverse-engineering what the audience liked is almost guaranteed to miss. As a fan, demanding more of the same thing you loved, or punishing deviation, contributes to this trap.

  1. Identify your rough temperament type. You probably already know. Take it seriously as data about how you work.

  2. If you're a vataOne of the three Ayurvedic doshas, Vata is governed by the elements of wind and ether — making it cold, dry, light, and intensely mobile in nature.Full definition → struggling with focus: add structure through variety, not subtraction. Instead of cutting everything down to one thing, keep two to four projects or interests running in parallel. When you hit a wall on one, switch to another intentionally rather than abandoning it.

  3. Audit your current environment against your temperament. Where does your daily life demand you operate like a different temperament type? What's one adjustment — even small — that would reduce that friction?

  4. The next time you feel anxious, resist the urge to distract or suppress. Sit with it for a moment and ask: What specifically triggered this? What does this feeling tell me about what I care about or fear? You don't need to resolve it — just understand it a little better.

  5. When you catch yourself calling someone (or yourself) lazy, stupid, or broken — stop and ask "what am I not understanding here?" Swap the judgment for curiosity and see what you find.

  6. If you're a creator or doing any kind of meaningful work: periodically check whether you're creating from the inside or optimizing for what people want. The test is simple — does this feel like something you genuinely want to make, or does it feel like you're trying to recreate something that already worked?
