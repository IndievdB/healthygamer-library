Summary

TL;DR — A man who nearly carried out a school shooting in middle school describes, in his own words, how he got there and what pulled him back. The conversation reveals that mass violence isn't born from a single broken mind but from a cascade of failed safeguards — and that it can be redirected by surprisingly small acts of human decency.

JC grew up with an emotionally unavailable father, a sibling whose bipolar disorder consumed family attention, overprotective parents who couldn't give him the social exposure he needed, and — when he hit middle school — relentless bullying that authority figures either ignored or actively punished him for enduring. By the time he had one disciplinary strike left before expulsion (for getting punched in the back of the head while sitting quietly watching a movie), he had already drawn up a list, tested his plan in a dry run, and was waiting for the next trigger to follow through. What stopped him was a 13-year-old cheerleader who walked up and announced she was going to be his friend — and then absorbed the social cost of doing so for a full month before he even believed her intentions were genuine.

The interview unpacks how someone builds toward violence, what the inside of that mindset looks and feels like, and what it actually takes to pull someone back from the edge.

How the Path to Violence Actually Builds

Violence doesn't erupt randomly. It accumulates through a layered failure of the systems that are supposed to protect a young person.

In JC's case, every tier of support broke down in sequence: - Home: emotionally absent father, a sibling crisis that consumed parental bandwidth, a child placed in the role of "don't add to dad's stress" - School: bullying that was visible to teachers and ignored; a zero-tolerance policy that punished the victim equally with the aggressor; administrators who read JC's lack of remorse (for something he didn't do) as evidence of guilt - Family response to the first crisis: "Do you know how bad this makes me look?" — the parent's first words when JC was suspended

Each failure doesn't just wound — it teaches a lesson. The lesson JC absorbed was that no one in authority is looking out for you, the system doesn't provide fairness or justice, and therefore you have to provide it yourself.

"I was just like okay, the administrators aren't doing anything to protect us — they're not going to do anything to protect them."

Once a young person reaches that conclusion, building a list stops feeling like a crime and starts feeling like the only logical response.

The Worldview That Makes Violence Feel Justified

JC describes his mindset at the time as naturalistic/materialistic — a Richard Dawkins–style reductionism where people are just "dancing to their DNA," nobody fundamentally means anything, and life has no moral weight. He even notes the irony: a true naturalist worldview has no room for revenge, which briefly gave him pause. But the worldview also fed a deeper operating assumption: all humans are selfish and looking out for themselves.

What's especially important is the cognitive architecture violence-prone thinking builds on top of that assumption:

  1. An "anti-worldview": The foundation isn't for a set of principles — it's against a group of people. The Nazis weren't built on values; they were built on anti-Semitism. JC's version was: these specific people are bad and must face justice.

  2. Situational truths universalized: Something that is true in one case gets treated as universally true across all cases. The bullies really were bullying him. That's real. But from that, he extrapolated that all people operate this way.

  3. Conclusions shaping information, not the other way around: Once the conclusion is fixed ("everyone is against me"), every piece of incoming data gets interpreted to confirm it. A genuine act of kindness looks like a setup. This is why just telling someone "you're wrong" doesn't work — they're not processing facts neutrally anymore.

  4. Disassociation: JC describes splitting into two internal voices — a hyper-intellectual one and an emotional/angry one — each trying to win the other over. For a period, the angry one was winning, and the intellectual one started helping it plan more effectively.

What Actually Turns Things Around

Here is the part that most people miss: recovery didn't start with therapy. The counselor JC was already seeing went in one ear and out the other. He experienced the counselor as just another authority figure who didn't understand — and who was telling him to accept responsibility for a situation that wasn't his fault.

What actually moved the needle was a peer — a 13-year-old girl with no clinical training who simply chose to befriend him and then bore the social consequences of doing so without backing down.

"She didn't have the PhD she needed to help me deal with all my problems, but she provided community and enough of a challenge to the way I was viewing the world to make me pause and think about what I was about to do."

The mechanism wasn't advice or therapeutic technique. It was external evidence that challenged the worldview. His worldview said all people are selfish. She then behaved in a way his worldview could not explain. That created enough cognitive instability that he started actually questioning, rather than just receiving more information that confirmed what he already believed.

Once the worldview began to shift, two more things helped compound it: - An AP Government teacher who attacked every belief his students held — not to replace it with his own, but to prove that any belief that can't survive a question won't survive life - Years of reading across religions and philosophies, trying to construct a worldview consistent with observed reality, including the hard question: why do some people do genuinely good things for no biological reason?

The key insight: therapy and peer community are not substitutes for each other — they're complementary. The peer gave JC enough hope and enough evidence against his worldview to make him willing to engage with what the counselor was actually saying. Without one, the other doesn't work as well.

The Shared Responsibility Frame

One of the most useful ideas in this conversation is the sliding scale of individual vs. corporate (societal) responsibility.

On one end: it's entirely the person's fault; they should have just handled it better. On the other: society created this person; they bear no individual responsibility. Both are wrong in isolation.

A more accurate framing is that there are risk factors — conditions that make a harmful outcome more or less likely — at the individual level and at the societal level. Absent fathers, lack of mentorship, no community, authority figures who punish victims, zero-tolerance policies that remove incentives not to fight back: these are societal risk factors. They don't remove individual responsibility, but if we pretend they don't exist, we're guaranteeing more of the same outcomes.

JC's point: what we're often missing is mentorship — specifically, one adult who shows up with no agenda and checks in consistently. He cites a nonprofit that pairs mentors with youth in juvenile detention; since it launched, the reincarceration rate for juveniles dropped 98%. Whether that specific figure is verified or not, the direction of the effect aligns with everything else in this conversation.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming therapy alone is enough. If someone already sees authority figures as unreliable, a therapist is just another authority figure. The therapeutic content won't land without some trust already in place, which often has to come from peer connection first.

Labeling someone and then expecting them to behave better. If you call someone a stalker, a loser, or a potential shooter — even accurately — and offer nothing else, you've now given them a social category to live up to. As JC puts it: "If I'm already being called a rapist and a murderer and they're right about being gay, why wait on the other three?" Harsh, accurate labeling without compassion can accelerate the very behavior you're trying to prevent.

Treating violence risk as a mental illness problem. The data doesn't support the idea that mental illness is the primary driver of mass violence. Most people with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder are not violent. Most mass violence comes from people who are not formally mentally ill — they're people whose worldview has been shaped by compounding failures and isolation.

Waiting for a "big fix." The word eventually is more dangerous than it sounds. "I'll feel better eventually." "Life will turn around eventually." That framing keeps you grinding toward a finish line that resets every time you reach it. Velocity can be turned by a small acceleration in the opposite direction — one person, one genuine connection, one conversation that challenges the worldview even slightly.

Responding to brewing violence only with punishment and judgment. Every punitive response that lacks any accompanying dialogue or compassion confirms what the person already believes: that the system is against them, that no one is actually listening, that they have to handle this themselves.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you're in a dark place and having thoughts of hurting others or yourself: Contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988 in the US). They handle crises beyond just suicide — including thoughts of violence toward others. Don't wait until it escalates.

  2. Identify one person you can reach out to this week — not necessarily a therapist, just someone who isn't already inside your exact worldview bubble. The goal isn't agreement; it's contact with a different perspective.

  3. Audit your worldview for over-generalizations: When you catch yourself thinking in terms of all people, or everyone always, stop and ask: is this situationally true or universally true? Write down the original observation (what actually happened) separately from the conclusion you drew from it.

  4. Notice the word "eventually" in your internal monologue. Any time you catch yourself thinking "I'll deal with this eventually" or "things will work out eventually," flag it. Ask instead: what's one small thing I can do today — not to fix everything, but to move slightly in a better direction?

  5. If you're not in crisis but know someone who seems isolated and angry: You don't need a PhD. You need about a month of consistent, low-pressure presence. Show up. Don't set an agenda. Be willing to absorb some social cost for it. That's what the 13-year-old did, and it was enough.

  6. Seek diverse input deliberately. If every podcast, community, and friend you have confirms one worldview, you're building an echo chamber. Find at least one source — a book, a creator, a person — who holds a meaningfully different view and engage with it seriously rather than defensively.

  7. If you're a therapist, teacher, coach, or parent: When someone seems like they're headed toward trouble, ask yourself whether your first response will feel, to them, like another authority figure writing them off. Lead with curiosity before you lead with correction.
