What Medication Is Actually Trying to Do

Medication targets both the symptoms of depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → and the underlying neurochemical processes driving it. The goal is the same as overall treatment: reduce frequency, duration, and severity of episodes.

Because people respond differently, choosing the right medication is still largely trial and error. Genetic testing services that promise to match you to the right drug sound promising, but studies haven't shown they reliably improve outcomes yet. That said, prescribers do have general guidelines — they know which medications work for the average person — so it's not completely blind guessing.

Two principles guide smart prescribing:

  • Double-dipping: choosing a medication that has indications for more than one condition you have. If you have both depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → and chronic pain, for example, one medication might treat both.
  • Strategic side effect selection: rather than just minimizing side effects, a thoughtful prescriber looks for side effects that might actually help you. The Three Branches of Psychotherapy

There are many types of therapy, but they cluster into three broad families with meaningfully different approaches.

Psychodynamic Therapy

This is the older, more traditional talk therapy that traces back to Freud. A therapist listens more than speaks, explores your past and upbringing, and works through deep psychological patterns. It's harder to study because individual therapists vary so much. The payoff tends to be slow — it takes time to get traction — but the changes it produces may be more permanent. Think of it as excavation: slow going, but what you find can last.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

It's highly structured, often involves homework, and is extremely well-studied because the protocol is consistent enough to run controlled trials on. Complementary and Alternative Medicine That Actually Works

CAM covers everything outside conventional medication and psychotherapy. The evidence here is genuinely mixed, so it's worth knowing what has solid support versus what doesn't.

What has strong evidence:

  • MeditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → sits at the top. Research shows it reduces cancer pain, improves skin conditions like eczema, and is effective for essentially every major mental health condition. The volume of evidence here is substantial and keeps growing.
  • Mind-body practices — yoga, tai chi — have been shown in studies to outperform exercise alone on clinical outcomes. That's a notable finding worth taking seriously.
  • Long-standing medicinal traditions like Ayurveda and traditional Chinese medicine have survived centuries and accumulate a growing body of supportive research.

What has inconsistent evidence:

Energy healing, most broad nutritional supplements, essential oils, crystal healing, and high-cost "new age" interventions like certain infusions or magnetic treatments don't show consistent, measurable benefit across systematic studies. That doesn't mean they never help anyone — individual practitioners in these areas can be remarkable — but there's no quality control, and the average outcome is unreliable. You can't easily tell a skilled practitioner from an ineffective one, and the price tags can be significant.

Common Pitfalls

  • Quitting medication too early. The side-effect timeline and the benefit timeline are out of sync. Feeling worse in week one doesn't mean the medication isn't working — it means the medication hasn't started working yet.
  • Assuming one therapy type is objectively "best." Psychodynamic work may produce deeper or more lasting change even with less published evidence.
  • Cascading medications. Starting a second drug to handle the side effects of the first, then a third for the second, is a real trap. The goal is the fewest medications that accomplish the most — adding complexity should be deliberate and justified.
  • Treating CAM as all-or-nothing. Dismissing everything outside conventional medicine misses tools with real evidence (meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →, yoga). Embracing everything because it's "natural" ignores the genuine inconsistency in unregulated modalities.
