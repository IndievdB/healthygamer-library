The Two Versions of You (And Why You Can't Just Kill the Bad One)

Most people who struggle with self-criticism want to get rid of that inner voice. The instinct is completely understandable, but it's the wrong move.

Think of it this way: there are two versions of you running at different times. One is grounded, capable of gratitude, able to feel good about what she's built. The other wakes up at inconvenient moments — after a bad comment, a late night, a quiet moment — and runs a different program entirely: you're not good enough, you got here by accident, they're going to figure it out.

The second version isn't evil. She's scared. Her primary emotion, underneath all the self-criticism, is dread — specifically, dread of disappointment. Not failure in the abstract, but the specific experience of letting someone down, of being seen as not enough.

You can't fix this by telling her to shut up. When you try to suppress that voice through distraction, drinking, or just pushing through, you're not resolving anything — you're just postponing it. What that scared version of you actually needs is the same thing you'd offer a friend who voiced those fears out loud: acknowledgment, not elimination.

When you catch yourself in that mode, the goal isn't to argue yourself out of it or perform positivity over it. It's to meet it with the kind of steadiness you'd offer someone you love.

Where the Inner Critic Comes From

That critical voice didn't appear out of nowhere. It was installed.

If you grew up in an environment where love felt conditional on performance — where a parent's approval depended on grades, on being a certain kind of person, on not being a burden — you learned a very specific lesson: I am a disappointment. Not as a one-time assessment, but as a recurring truth that got reinforced until it became background noise.

The cruel irony is that the voice designed to protect you from disappointment is the thing making you miserable. If you never let yourself believe you deserve success, you can't be crushed when it's taken away. If you never fully commit to a relationship, you can't be devastated by betrayal. The armor works. It just costs everything it was supposed to protect.

This is also why self-deprecating humor can become a trap rather than just a personality trait. It's genuinely funny, and there's nothing wrong with it as a mode of expression — but when it becomes a defense mechanism, you're essentially beating yourself up preemptively so no one else gets the chance. There's a real difference between joking about yourself and using self-deprecation as a shield against hope.

Grief Doesn't Work Like Leveling Up

After a major loss — a relationship ending through betrayal, the collapse of a future you'd planned — there's a temptation to track your progress toward being "over it." But grief isn't linear, and it doesn't respect the schedule you set for yourself.

There are two different kinds of emotional release after a loss: - Cathartic processing — where crying or sitting with the feeling actually moves something through you, and over time you cry about it less - Re-experiencing without resolution — where you feel the pain again without it going anywhere, and the total weight doesn't diminish

The goal isn't to stop feeling it faster. It's to distinguish between the two and create conditions where processing can actually happen — rather than distraction, which just delays the reckoning.

Alcohol, scrolling, gaming, keeping busy: these are not grief. They're ways of postponing it. The feeling will be there when you stop.

Why Online Hatred Has Nothing to Do With You

It's worth thinking carefully about what it actually takes to write a seven-page letter by hand, find someone's address, buy a stamp, and mail it to a stranger. That is an enormous expenditure of energy directed at someone you've never met.

That effort doesn't come from you. It comes entirely from them.

The most intense hatred is usually reserved for people who represent something the hater can't allow to be true. If someone has organized their life around the belief that their depression, or their circumstances, or their bad luck is the reason they haven't built anything — and then you appear, also depressed, also knocked down by your circumstances, also imperfect, and somehow still building something — that is genuinely threatening to them. Your existence challenges their story.

"Nothing inspires hate as much as seeing the person that you could be."

This doesn't mean all criticism is projectionProjection is when something inside you — a feeling, impulse, or trait you can't consciously accept — gets attributed to someone else instead.Full definition →. Some feedback is fair, delivered badly. But there's a reliable signal in the intensity and the irrationality of the attack. A seven-page letter in multiple colors from a stranger is not a critique. It's someone's pain looking for a target.

The other thing to understand: anger almost always originates in hurt. The person sending venom is not doing so from a position of security and contentment. They're doing it from a position of pain that has nowhere else to go.

None of this makes it not hurt. You can understand where hatred comes from and still feel it land. Especially if you're someone who absorbs other people's emotional states easily — understanding the source doesn't create distance from the impact.

On Being an Emotional Sponge

Some people move through difficult situations with relatively little residue. They compartmentalize easily, feel the feeling, and move on. Others absorb emotional experience deeply — their own and other people's. They feel things more intensely, hold onto them longer, and are affected by interactions in ways that less empathic people simply aren't.

If you're the second type, you cannot take care of yourself the way the first type does. Strategies that work for people with lower emotional permeability will not work for you. "Just don't let it get to you" is useless advice if your nervous system isn't built that way.

What you need instead is a conscious practice of what you might think of as wringing out the sponge: actively processing and releasing what you've absorbed throughout the day, rather than hoping it dissipates on its own. This isn't distraction. Distraction is setting the full sponge aside. Wringing it out means actually moving the emotion through you — feeling it, acknowledging it, and letting it pass.

This is why the inability to sleep is so common for people with high emotional sensitivity. The unprocessed residue of the day accumulates, and at 2am with nothing left to distract yourself, it surfaces all at once.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing self-criticism with self-improvement. Telling yourself you're not good enough is not the same as working to be better. It's just suffering, and it's often counterproductive.
  • Treating distraction as processing. Keeping busy after a loss feels like moving forward, but if you haven't actually felt the feelings, they're still there.
  • Apologizing for your emotional state on behalf of other people. Deciding in advance that your sadness is too much for viewers (or anyone else) and suppressing it to manage their experience is a form of self-erasure that helps no one.
  • Taking the intensity of hatred as evidence of its accuracy. A loud, emotional accusation is not more true than a quiet one. Often the reverse.
  • Trying to delete the self-critical voice rather than understand it. You cannot argue, suppress, or distract yourself free of a pattern that formed over years. You have to approach it differently — with curiosity and compassion, not warfare.
  • Applying other people's coping strategies to your own nervous system. What works for someone who feels things less intensely may actively fail for you. Your emotional architecture requires different maintenance.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your inner critic.   2. Just name the emotion and move to the moment before. Expect to miss a lot in your first run. Do it again. The goal is to surface and acknowledge the emotional residue you've accumulated, so it stops circulating at 2am. Practice five times per week for eight weeks before evaluating whether it's working.

  3. Track your emotions in real time during the day. This makes the reverse-day meditation dramatically more effective. Simply note, as things happen, how you're feeling — not in a journal necessarily, just a moment of awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.   4. When you receive hatred, practice the sequence: feel it (don't suppress), identify what you're feeling (absorbed anger, hurt, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, whatever it is), then ask yourself what this person's pain might look like. Not to excuse them, but to create some psychological distance between the attack and your sense of self-worth.

  5. Try Om chanting for acute anxiety. Sit with your eyes closed, take a deep breath, and produce a sustained "Aum" sound (three syllables: ah, oo, mm). Focus on where you feel the vibration in your body rather than on how you sound. Do five to seven rounds. The initial embarrassment and resistance is part of the point — you're practicing moving through discomfort rather than away from it.

  6. Write a letter to your younger self at the hardest moment. Think of the age when the self-critical voice was being formed most intensely. Write to that person from where you are now — not to fix the past, but to offer the steadiness and perspective that wasn't available then. This is the orientation that actually helps the inner critic settle: not elimination, but compassion from the stronger version of you.
