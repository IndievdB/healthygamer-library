Common Pitfalls in Goal Setting

The way we set a goal can sometimes set us up for failure. There are three very common pitfalls people slip into when setting a goal: setting a compensatory goal, which is when we set a goal to "make up" for something in the past; setting a fantasy based goal, which is when we set a goal that is so far away that it is hard to experience positive reinforcement along the way; and setting a goal based on the wrong intentions, like living up to expectations of others. Understanding how we set goals in the wrong way can help us start setting inspiring and achievable goals.

Summary

TL;DR — Half the reason most people fail at their goals isn't a lack of willpower; it's that they set bad goals in the first place. Three specific goal-setting traps — compensatory goals, fantasy-based goals, and moving goalposts — quietly guarantee failure before you even begin.

Most advice about goals focuses on execution: work harder, stay disciplined, build better habits. But if the goal itself is structurally broken, no amount of effort will save you. Understanding these three pitfalls lets you design goals that work with your brain rather than against it.

Pitfall #1: The Compensatory Goal

A compensatory goal is what happens when past failure gets baked into a new goal — and turns it into two goals at once.

The classic example: you fail a class. When you sign up to retake it, you don't just want to pass. You tell yourself you have to get an A, because anything less won't undo the shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → of the F. Passing the class was already hard — you failed it the first time, after all. But now you've stacked a second, emotionally-driven goal on top: you need a spectacular victory to balance out the failure.

"The only way I can recover my GPA from an F is to get an A+, and then it'll kind of balance out."

Here's the problem. That framing turns the goal all-or-nothing. If you're studying and it looks like you're heading toward a B or a C, your brain says that's not good enough — and you quit. The compensatory goal doesn't just make success harder; it actively encourages you to stop trying the moment you sense you won't hit the top.

Real progress doesn't work like that. You don't jump from F to A. You go from F → D → C → B → eventually A. A compensatory goal skips every realistic stepping stone and replaces them with a single, near-impossible leap.

The deeper issue is that the goal is being driven by an emotion — shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition →, resentment, the need for triumph — rather than a clear-eyed assessment of what you can actually achieve. Emotionally-born goals set you up to fail in a very specific way: you can only "win" in the most spectacular way imaginable, so anything short of perfection reads as defeat.

The issue is neurological. When a goal is extremely far away, forward progress produces no visible feedback. Imagine you're trying to climb Mount Everest. You take ten steps. You take a hundred steps. You hike for four hours. Does the peak look any closer? No. What does your brain actually register? That you've spent four hours and gotten nothing for it.

That's the exact opposite of what motivates behavior. Video game designers figured this out and exploited it brilliantly. Games subdivide every journey into tiny, constantly rewarded steps: experience points per battle, loot from each encounter, level-up after level-up. A fantasy-based goal does the opposite. It strips away every intermediate reward and leaves you with nothing but a distant horizon that never seems to get closer. The same psychological machinery that game developers weaponize against you is the machinery you're voluntarily disabling when you set a goal that's too big and too vague.

Pitfall #3: Moving Goalposts

This one is sneaky because it's triggered by success, not failure.

You set a goal to go to the gym once a week. Tuesday comes around and you've already done it. You feel great. So you tell yourself: well, it's only Tuesday — let's go again tomorrow, and Thursday, and Friday. Let's make it every day. The initial success creates excitement, and that excitement pushes the goalposts further out.

You do it twice. You do it three times. You set a new expectation. Eventually the expectation rises past what you can actually sustain — and you fail. And then you stop entirely.

"As you get excited from succeeding in your initial goal, you actually move the goalposts for yourself. As you move the goalposts for yourself, you actually set yourself up to fail."

The real problem with most goal-pursuit isn't starting — it's follow-through and consistency. Moving goalposts is the mechanism that destroys consistency. You picked "once a week" for a reason: that's what you're actually capable of sustaining right now. The right move after hitting that goal isn't to immediately raise the bar. It's to ask: can I do this once a week for two weeks in a row? Consistency should be the goal, not escalation.

Every time you move the goalposts on yourself, you trade a sustainable win for an exciting-but-fragile stretch target. Do it enough times and you'll inevitably set a standard you can't meet — and interpret the failure as proof that you're the kind of person who gives up.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing ambition with good goal design. "Dream big" is culturally celebrated, but big dreams need to be broken into proximate, reinforceable steps before they become practical goals. Keeping a dream as a goal doesn't make you more ambitious — it makes you less likely to make real progress.

  • Treating compensatory goals as motivating. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your current goals for compensatory roots. Ask yourself: Is this goal trying to make up for something in the past? If the answer is yes, rewrite the goal as a purely forward-looking one. Instead of "I need to ace this class to make up for last semester," try "I want to pass this class with a grade that shows genuine understanding." Remove the emotional debt from the goal statement.

  2. Break any fantasy goal into the nearest next step. Take one big goal and write down what the next smallest, visible unit of progress would look like. If the dream is to run a marathon, the proximate goal is "run for 10 minutes without stopping." That next step should be achievable enough that completing it actually registers as progress in your mind.

  3. Design reinforcement into your goals the way game developers do. Identify three or four checkpoints between where you are now and your larger goal. Treat each checkpoint as a win worth acknowledging — not just a waypoint to rush through.

  4. Commit to holding goalposts still after a win. When you hit a goal, deliberately resist the urge to immediately escalate. If your goal was to go to the gym once this week and you did it, your next goal is to go to the gym once next week too — not to go five times. Consistency across time beats frequency in a single week.

  5. Replace all-or-nothing framing with incremental framing. If you catch yourself thinking "if I can't do it perfectly, it's not worth doing," that's a compensatory or fantasy goal talking. Write out the realistic progression (F → D → C → B → A) and set your current target at one step forward, not the final destination.
