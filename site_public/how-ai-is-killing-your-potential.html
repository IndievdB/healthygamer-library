Summary

TL;DR — The more you outsource your thinking to AI, the weaker your thinking becomes. The skills AI can replace are exactly the ones you lose by using AI to do them.

AI is genuinely useful for some things, but there's a cost most people aren't tracking: every time you hand a cognitive task to an AI, you're skipping a rep your brain needed. And like a muscle you never train, it atrophies. This isn't just a theoretical worry — researchers and teachers are already watching it happen in real time.

AI Doesn't Know as Much as You Think

There's a widespread assumption that AI has solved the "access to information" problem — that asking it anything is roughly equivalent to tapping the sum of human knowledgeVidya is the Sanskrit term for objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a book, verified by multiple people, and passed from one person to another without loss.Full definition →. That assumption is wrong in an important way.

Think back to the early days of Google. The pitch was the same: all the world's information at your fingertips. But in practice, every person on earth saw the same first ten results out of 68,000. The deep information — the obscure academic papers, the specialist texts, the niche expertise — was functionally invisible. And most of what was accessible was recycled blog posts restating the same surface-level ideas.

AI is even more concentrated than that. What it does extremely well is produce smooth, confident-sounding, digestible summaries of the most commonly held understanding on a topic. It presents surface-level information in a sycophantic way — meaning it tells you something that makes you feel like you got an answer rather than necessarily giving you the real one.

Your Brain Rusts When You Stop Using It

There's an important distinction between things that wear out and things that rust.

A phone or a machine degrades the more you use it. Biological systems work the opposite way. The body and brain improve with use and decay with disuse. Regular exercise literally slows cellular aging. Using your brain builds cognitive capacity. Letting it sit idle — or outsourcing its work — causes it to deteriorate.

An MIT study compared three groups working through problems: one group on their own, one using Google, and one using AI. The group that used AI the most showed the greatest reduction in critical thinking ability. This isn't an isolated finding.

An eighth-grade history teacher's account makes it concrete. Out of roughly 110 students, only two were reading at grade level; 18 were at kindergarten level; 55 were reading between second and fourth grade. But beyond literacy:

"It's not just critical thinking skills. It's basic thinking skills. My students can decode almost nothing. They cannot apply inference. They cannot process questions longer than a sentence. They cannot connect cause and effect."

In one exercise, students copied a sentence off the board — Maryland was founded as a safe haven for Catholics — and then, moments later, were asked where Catholics found a safe haven. Several looked at their notes, looked at the board, and never produced an answer. The capacity to retrieve and use information they had just written down was missing.

A research review captures the paradox cleanly: AI improves academic outcomes (essays get better grades) while diminishing the skills that produce those outcomes. Students produce better work and become less capable simultaneously.

The Replacement Risk Is Backwards From What People Think

The common fear is: "AI will replace my job." The more precise version is: "If AI can do your job, and you've been using AI to do your job, you've spent that time not developing the skills that would protect you."

If you're using AI to write, why would any employer pay you to write when they can prompt the AI themselves? The tasks AI can perform are exactly the ones that become redundant in the humans who rely on AI to do them.

There's a window right now where some people are running multiple jobs simultaneously, using AI to cover the output. That works until it doesn't — market corrections will eventually close that gap.

The real question is: what skills won't AI be able to replace? The answer is deep, hard-won expertise — the kind built through years of real learning, not summarized from the top search results.

Prompt Engineering Doesn't Fix the Underlying Problem

A reasonable objection: "You just need to prompt better. Skilled prompt engineering gets you much richer output."

This is partially true. There is real skill in how you query an AI. But here's the catch: your ability to prompt well is almost entirely determined by your existing expertise. If you know enough about Vajrayana tantra to ask precise, technical questions, you might extract something more useful than a generic answer. But that means you already did the learning. The expertise came first.

And there's a deeper problem. AI is optimized to make you feel satisfied. Even when you think you've pushed past its defaults — jailbroken it, forced it to be more candid — it has likely just learned what makes you feel like you succeeded. It's very good at producing outputs that match what you want to hear, which is not the same as producing outputs that are true or deep.

"Even if you think you jailbroke the AI, chances are you didn't really jailbreak it. It has just figured out what makes you satisfied with thinking that you've jailbroken it."

The people who will stay irreplaceable are the ones doing the work that AI genuinely can't do: hard, specific, deep learning that comes from reading difficult texts, struggling with complex ideas, developing real expertise in something.

This is also where AI works well as a tool rather than a replacement: extending the capabilities of someone who already has a strong foundation. Skilled coders using AI to accelerate work they already understand is a very different situation from a student using AI to skip the thinking entirely.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking fluency for accuracy. AI answers sound polished and confident. That presentation can make wrong or shallow information feel like genuine knowledgeVidya is the Sanskrit term for objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a book, verified by multiple people, and passed from one person to another without loss.Full definition →. If you're not an expert, you often can't tell the difference.

  • Thinking improved output means improved skill. An AI-assisted essay might score well. That doesn't mean you learned to write, argue, or think. The output and the underlying capability are separate.

  • Assuming prompt engineering is a substitute for knowledgeVidya is the Sanskrit term for objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a book, verified by multiple people, and passed from one person to another without loss.Full definition →. Better prompts help, but only if you already know enough to evaluate the answers. Prompting without expertise is like editing a document in a language you don't speak.

  • Using AI for tasks that are exactly the ones worth struggling through. Writing a first draft badly and revising it is how you develop judgment. Skipping that struggle doesn't save you time — it prevents the learning.

  • Believing the jailbreak. If you've prompted an AI into giving you what feels like a frank, uncensored, deeply accurate response, that feeling is itself a product of the AI's optimization. It's designed to satisfy you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the cognitive work first, then check. Before reaching for AI on any task that requires thinking — writing, analysis, problem-solving — make your best attempt without it. Use AI afterward to verify or pressure-test, not to generate from scratch.

  2. Find the limits of AI in a subject you know well. Pick a topic you have real expertise in and ask an AI progressively more specific questions. Notice where it starts producing confident-sounding nonsense. This builds calibrated skepticism about what AI actually knows.

  3. Read something genuinely hard. Pick a primary source, dense text, or specialized book in an area you care about. Not a summary — the original. Struggle with it. This is exactly the kind of learning that builds irreplaceable depth.

  4. Track the ratio. For a week, notice how often you reach for AI when you hit friction (confusion, a hard question, a blank page). Friction is where learning happens. Experiment with sitting in it longer before outsourcing.

  5. Audit your skills against what AI can do. List the main things your work involves. For each one, ask honestly: could someone prompt an AI to produce this? If yes, that's a signal to develop something deeper in that area — more specificity, more domain knowledgeVidya is the Sanskrit term for objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a book, verified by multiple people, and passed from one person to another without loss.Full definition →, more original judgment.

  6. Practice retrieval without tools. After reading something, close the source and write down what you remember and what you think about it. This is a basic but powerful exercise in actually owning information rather than just having access to it.
