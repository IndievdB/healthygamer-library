TL;DR — Stoicism isn't numbness, your reactions in a video game lobby are the same as your reactions everywhere else in life, and the "inner guide" you already talk to probably knows more than you think.

This is a wide-ranging conversation between two people clearly enjoying themselves — touching on fear of death, trauma responses in gaming, what stoicism actually means versus what TikTok says it means, the inner structure of the self, confidence, and why smart kids so often underperform. The ideas are scattered but consistently sharp.

Awareness Is Enough to Start Healing

Two years before this conversation, Ludwig had a fear of death. He didn't resolve it through therapy or medication. He just started allowing himself to think about it — exploring the feeling rather than running from it — and the fear lost its grip.

This matches something from yogic and meditative traditions: you don't necessarily need to fix a problem. Awareness of the problem can be sufficient. There's even scientific backing here from psychotherapy research suggesting that articulating a problem — putting it into language — can produce some of the same neurological relief as solving it. This is why talking to a partner who just listens, without offering solutions, can feel genuinely therapeutic. It's not a workaround for fixing things. For a huge class of emotional problems, it is the fix.

Trauma Is a Feature, Not a Bug

Trauma is not your brain malfunctioning. It's an adaptive survival mechanism that once worked — and in many cases worked very well — but is now running in a context where it no longer fits.

"Back then it worked. But it no longer works anymore. It's like keeping a prayer defense spell on in RuneScape, but you're no longer in battle and you're just wasting your prayer points."

The sniping strategy that wins at low MMR gets you demolished at high MMR. The temper tantrum that got you what you wanted at age four gets you fired at thirty. Nothing is broken. The strategy just hasn't updated.

This reframe matters because it removes shame. You're not defective. You learned something that worked. The task now is to notice when those old patterns are firing and ask whether this is actually still the right context for them.

Your Gaming Lobby Is a Relationship

When someone insta-locks Duelist and you insta-lock Duelist in response, that is not a gaming decision. It is a trauma response — the same one that leads an addict to relapse when their family treats them like an addict, the same one that leads an abused child to become a bully.

"I'm going to control this thing a tiny amount. I'm not going to be the victim here. I'm going to be the predator."

The ship doesn't need to burn. But once you're in reactive mode, you're not choosing — you're just reciprocating. Whatever people throw at you, you throw back.

The alternative is communication, specifically a style built on questions rather than commands. Instead of "stop feeding," try "I notice you've been ganked twice — my bad for not having wards, do you want me to fix that or move to a different part of the map?" The second version gives the other person agency. You're also taking ownership, which disarms defensiveness. And if you want to humanize strangers quickly: introduce yourself. "Hi, my name is Ludwig" triggers social norms that make the person across from you feel like a person.

What Stoicism Actually Means

The TikTok version of stoicism — "nothing affects me," total emotional numbness, impervious to everything — is a misreading. It sounds like stoicism but is functionally its opposite.

Real stoicism is not about avoiding emotion. It's about not being controlled by emotion. Things still hurt. Marcus Aurelius felt the full range of human emotions. The goal is tranquility in the face of those feelings, not the absence of them.

"Tranquility is not about being numb. It's about staying grounded when there are emotions in front of you."

There's a Buddhist concept that maps onto this called the two arrows: life shoots the first arrow and it hurts. The second arrow is what you shoot at yourself for being hit. Stoicism is about not firing that second arrow — not about becoming immune to the first one.

The numbing and avoidance strategy that passes for stoicism online actually produces the opposite of what it promises. Instead of gaining control over your emotional life, you build a life organized around avoiding anything that might trigger you. The true stoic can walk into any room. The false stoic is quietly shrinking the list of rooms they can enter.

Feeling things fully — letting the tears come instead of pushing them away — is what leads to tranquility. The acceptance of death leads to peace, not the avoidance of it.

The Inner Guide

There's a part of you that you talk to when you ask yourself hard questions. It's not quite "you" — it seems to know more, it doesn't get rattled by emotion the way you do, and when you ignore it and call your mom, she often just confirms what it already said.

That guide sits deeper than the surface layer of thoughts and emotions. Thoughts and emotions are the most visible layer of mental life — reactive, time-sensitive, emotionally colored. Below that is personality: the collection of algorithms built up through experience that shapes how you perceive and react to the world. And below that is something closer to the self — the constant, the part of you that has always been you through every version of you that has ever existed.

The inner guide lives closer to that layer. It doesn't experience emotion the way the surface mind does, which is partly why it can be a little cold — but also why it isn't confused by fear or insecurity when you ask it something real.

You don't talk to it. You listen to it. The more you stop generating inner noise and simply tune in, the less it feels like a dialogue and the more it feels like just... knowing.

Smart Kids and the Ego That Stops Learning

Being told you're smart is, paradoxically, one of the most reliable ways to prevent someone from developing competence. If your identity is "I am smart," then trying hard and still failing means you're no longer smart. So you stop trying hard. You self-sabotage. You brute-force things you could brute-force, and you retreat from anything that requires genuine effort.

"The more one-dimensional my personality became, the more I had to preserve it."

Over time the list of things you're willing to attempt shrinks because each new challenge risks collapsing the only identity you have.

The fix isn't to tell these kids they're dumb. It's to ask them a question almost no one asks: what do you actually want? Not what you should want, not what you have potential for, not what would impress people — what do you want? Effort that comes from your own desire is qualitatively different from effort spent living up to expectations.

Confidence, Goals, and Letting Things Work Out

Having no wrong decisions isn't recklessness — it's a precondition for confidence. People who believe there is a single correct choice for every fork in the road are paralyzed, because the stakes of choosing wrong become existential. People who believe any path can be made to work stay in motion.

Goals can be useful as targets but can slip easily into outcome orientation — measuring your worth by whether you hit the number. The alternative is direction and intention: what are you moving toward, and are you taking genuine steps? You can have a clear desired outcome (make playoffs, become a full-time streamer) without your self-worth being contingent on it.

The "it'll all work out" philosophy isn't magical thinking. It's the recognition that the Earth keeps spinning, no decision is irreversible in every way that matters, and the variable you can actually control is your own actions — not the results those actions produce.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking numbness for stoicism. If you're organizing your life around avoiding things that might hurt, you have moved in exactly the wrong direction.
  • Thinking your inner guide is just another part of "you." It's tempting to fold it back into normal self-talk. It isn't the same layer. Treating it that way makes it harder to actually listen.
  • Treating "smart" as an identity rather than a starting point. The skill gap between high-IQ people and top earners is enormous. Stats don't win games; skill levels do.
  • Using fantasy as procrastination without realizing it. Imagining the achievement can produce enough of the reward feeling to kill the motivation to actually pursue it — especially if you're not genuinely committed to the goal.
  • Assuming high-risk = high-reward. Most high-risk things are just risky. The cost of something is not reliable evidence of its value.
  • Canceling the only people validating men's suffering without offering an alternative. If nobody else is acknowledging that a group's experience is genuinely hard, don't be surprised when the only voices doing so — however flawed — keep drawing crowds.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. When something is bothering you, name it out loud or in writing before trying to fix it. Give articulation a genuine shot before problem-solving. Notice whether the charge around the issue decreases.

  2. In your next conflict or competitive situation (online or in person), ask a question instead of issuing a correction. "I noticed X — what do you need from me?" Try it once and observe the response.

  3. Introduce yourself by name at the start of any new group interaction where you'd normally stay anonymous. This applies to gaming lobbies, online calls, anywhere you're tempted to treat people as non-real.

  4. The next time you feel a strong reactive impulse, pause and name it before acting. You don't have to suppress it. Just say internally: "I want to lock Duelist right now because I'm angry." That gap between impulse and action is where stoicism actually lives.

  5. Ask yourself, once: what do I actually want? Not what you should want or what you have potential for — what you genuinely want. Write one sentence. Let yourself be honest even if the answer seems small or embarrassing.

  6. Try listening to your inner guide rather than debating it. The next time you face a hard decision, notice what the quiet answer already is before you start reasoning aloud. Then check whether your reasoning is finding truth or manufacturing justification for avoiding that answer.

  7. Give yourself a defined quit date on anything you're pursuing. Set a deadline, take genuine action before it arrives, and then make a real decision — continue or close the chapter. The ability to quit cleanly is what keeps you from getting stuck.
