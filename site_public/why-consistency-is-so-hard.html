Summary

TL;DR — Consistency is hard not because you lack willpower, but because you don't fully recognize that you're a different person each day. The fix is to stop trying to be consistent across time and start being a good custodian of the life you're temporarily inhabiting.

Most people approach consistency by trying to build motivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition →, discipline, or better habits. These feel like the obvious levers to pull. But the root problem is something older and stranger: you don't actually know who you are. In practical terms, it means you're not really living a single continuous life. You're waking up each day as a slightly (or dramatically) different person, inheriting a mess from whoever was in charge yesterday.

Once you see this clearly, the whole project of "becoming consistent" changes shape.

You Are Not the Same Person Every Day

Think about the last time you had a genuinely great week — you woke up early, got things done, felt like yourself. You probably look back on that version of you with longing, wishing you could just be that person again. The fact that you're wishing for it from the outside is the clue. That person is gone. Today you're someone else.

This isn't just about moods or motivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition → fluctuating on the surface. You can wake up on January 1st, order a guitar, feel completely genuine about learning to play it, and then on January 3rd, when it arrives, feel absolutely nothing for it. The person who wanted the guitar and the person unpacking the box are not operating from the same place.

The usual response to this is to blame yourself for being weak or undisciplined. But that framing misses what's actually happening.

The Body-Swap Problem (And Why You Screw Over Tomorrow-You)

Here's the pattern playing out constantly: you're living today, and there are things that need doing — laundry, applications, emails, practice. You tell yourself you'll do them later. But "later" isn't you. It's some other person who has to wake up and deal with the consequences.

You love screwing over tomorrow-you. Not maliciously — it's just that your brain is wired this way. There's a structure in the brain called the nucleus accumbensThe brain region at the center of your dopaminergic circuitry — the part that generates motivation and drives behavior through dopamine signaling.Full definition → that drives future discounting: the tendency to value an immediate reward more than a larger reward in the future. A dollar today feels worth more than two dollars a year from now. Fun today feels worth more than the same fun tomorrow. This isn't a character flaw. It's how human brains evolved.

The result is that your life runs like a bad body-swap comedy. Some new consciousness wakes up in your body, sees $10,000 in the bank, and thinks: I'm going to spend whatever I want — the next person can deal with the fallout. Then the next person wakes up furious at whoever came before, and the cycle continues.

"You spend your life enjoying today and then you wake up tomorrow and you get mad at the dude that came before you — why didn't that guy do the crap that needed to be done?"

Public toilets are disgusting for this exact reason. No one who makes the mess ever has to use it again. No sense of stewardship.

Playing the Game as a Five-Minute Custodian

This is where the gaming frame becomes genuinely useful. Imagine a MOBA where players rotate in and out, each playing for only five minutes before handing the game off. When you sit down for your five minutes — even knowing you won't be there for the victory — what do you do? You play the best game you can. You don't int down mid just because the early game went badly. You try to leave the game in better shape than you found it, for whoever comes next.

"I am a custodian of this game for the next five minutes. How can I play this game to improve things for the person who comes next?"

This is exactly the frame to apply to your actual life. You don't get to live the rest of your life. You get today. The question isn't "how do I stay consistent for the next five years?" The question is: how do I want to set things up for the person who wakes up tomorrow?

There's a useful analogy from competitive cooking: teams of chefs working in five-minute relay shifts, each one inheriting what the last person started, each one trying to move the dish forward without knowing exactly where it's headed. The dish gets made — not because one chef had a perfect plan from start to finish, but because each person did their best with what they were handed.

When games (and lives) go wrong, it's when someone takes a rough start as a reason to actively make things worse. Tilting, inting, punting — these are all the same mistake: conflating a bad outcome down the road with permission to stop caring about the present five minutes.

Why Self-Compassion Is the Hidden Gear

There's an important pitfall here. Some people hear "just live for today" and think: fine, I'll be completely hedonistic. If you only exist in the present, why sacrifice anything for tomorrow-you?

The answer is self-compassionTreating yourself with the same basic understanding you'd extend to another person who made a mistake — instead of turning anger inward and assigning blame.Full definition → — and not the greeting-card version. The real version: do you actually care about the person who's going to inherit your life tomorrow?

We make sacrifices for things we love. You'll eat well and go to the gym if the person you're doing it for feels worth the effort. If you genuinely don't like yourself — if there's real self-contempt underneath the surface — then making sacrifices for future-you feels pointless, because that person doesn't seem worth it.

This is why working on self-compassionTreating yourself with the same basic understanding you'd extend to another person who made a mistake — instead of turning anger inward and assigning blame.Full definition → isn't soft or optional. It's load-bearing. Without it, the custodian frame collapses. You end up caring for the game the way someone cares for a public toilet — not at all, because why bother.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to build consistency through willpower and habits alone. These tools aren't useless, but they're downstream of the real problem.   • Believing tomorrow-you will follow through on today's promises. You've tested this. It doesn't work. The person who makes the promise and the person who has to keep it are operating under completely different conditions.

  • Using "I'll make up for it later" as a genuine plan. This is how things get punted indefinitely. Each new "you" arrives with their own priorities. The debt never gets paid.

  • Mistaking the custodian frame for an excuse to be nihilistic. "I only exist today" can sound like a reason to do nothing. It's actually the opposite — it's a reason to take today seriously, because that's all you actually have.

  • Skipping self-compassionTreating yourself with the same basic understanding you'd extend to another person who made a mistake — instead of turning anger inward and assigning blame.Full definition →. If you try to apply this framework while running on self-contempt, you won't care about the person inheriting tomorrow. The whole frame only works if you've decided that person is worth taking care of.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do a "custodian audit" tonight. Not as self-judgment — just as information. Notice what you're handing off.

  2. Practice the five-minute framing during a gaming session first. The next time you're tilted or your game looks lost, consciously ask: "I'm a custodian for the next five minutes — what's the best I can do right now?" Build the muscle in a low-stakes context before applying it to life.

  3. When you catch yourself saying "I'll do it tomorrow," reframe it explicitly. Say out loud or in writing: "I'm leaving this for the person who wakes up in my body tomorrow." Notice how that feels different from "I'll do it later."

  4. Spend five minutes writing a short letter to tomorrow-you. Not a to-do list — a genuine note from one custodian to the next. What are you handing off? What would make their morning easier? This makes the continuity concrete rather than abstract.

  5. If you notice you don't care about tomorrow-you, take that seriously. Don't push past it. That's the self-compassionTreating yourself with the same basic understanding you'd extend to another person who made a mistake — instead of turning anger inward and assigning blame.Full definition → gap. Spend some time — journaling, talking to someone, or working through resources on self-worth — on why future-you doesn't feel worth the effort. That's the actual bottleneck.

  6. Stop trying to architect a consistent future self and focus entirely on the quality of today's handoff. You don't need a five-year plan. You need to play the next five minutes well, then let the next person take over.
