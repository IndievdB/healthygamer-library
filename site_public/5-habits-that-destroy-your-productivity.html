Summary

TL;DR — Most popular productivity advice is either wrong or misleading. The habits that feel productive — reading more, multitasking, grinding longer hours — often actively work against you.

There's a whole industry built around productivity tips: read more books, hustle harder, juggle more projects, put yourself first. The problem is that a lot of this advice runs directly against what neuroscience and performance research actually show. Here are five common "productivity habits" that are really traps — and what to do instead.

Pitfall 1: Reading Books as a Substitute for Doing

Books feel productive. You pick up The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People and imagine that by the end you'll be a fundamentally different person. The logic seems airtight: books contain knowledge, knowledge leads to improvement, therefore more books equals more improvement.

But passive reading doesn't reliably produce behavioral change. What actually produces change is practical application of theoretical concepts. Medical school is a good example: the first two years are heavy on reading, but doctors don't really learn medicine until the final two years in the hospital — and then continue learning through three to seven years of residency. The doing is where the learning lives.

There's also a psychological trap at work. When you struggle to finish a book, you unconsciously assume that if you had finished it, you'd have gained everything it promised. Then you see someone else who's read dozens of books and project all those imagined gains onto them. This causes you to systematically overestimate what books actually deliver.

"Books without practical application are actually not that useful. The real value of books is to trigger introspection — and it's the introspection where the real yield comes from."

The implication: you don't even need the book to start that introspective process. Reading is a useful input, not a destination. If you do read, treat it as a prompt to reflect and then act — not as an accomplishment in itself.

Pitfall 2: Valuing Multitasking Over Deep Focus

Multitasking has become a status symbol. People wear it as a badge of honor: I can juggle a dozen things at once. Managing parallel workflows at a macro level — knowing what projects are in motion — is genuinely useful. But that's different from trying to do two things simultaneously in the same moment.

The brain can only truly focus on one thing at a time. When you split your attention, both tasks suffer. ER physicians who mentally juggle all fifteen of their patients at once — rather than giving each one full attention when they're in the room — make more errors. A neurosurgeon spending five hours focused on a single patient isn't less productive than an ER physician; the nature of the work just demands different modes.

You've probably felt this watching a show while scrolling your phone. You enjoy neither the show nor whatever you're doing on your phone. Distraction doesn't create parallel value — it degrades both streams.

The cultural bias toward multitasking over single-tasking is backwards. Protecting focused, uninterrupted attention is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

Pitfall 3: The Side Hustle as a Long-Term Strategy

The side hustle is a staple of productivity culture: keep your main job, but build something on the side to generate extra income and diversify your value. The problem is that this ignores how income and expertise actually grow.

Income tends to scale with depth, not breadth. Moving from floor 5 to floor 7 in your existing career — through an advanced degree, a certification, a subspecialty — typically yields far more than starting a brand-new income stream from scratch. Society rewards deep expertise and clear niches. A programmer who learns additional languages or earns a specialized certification is likely to see a much better return on 20 hours per week than that same programmer starting a real estate side hustle from zero.

The caveat matters here: side hustles aren't inherently bad. They make sense when you're transitioning — when you can't just quit one career and leap to another, so you build the new thing gradually until it can replace the old one. The key is intent. A side hustle as a permanent supplement is usually a drain. A side hustle as a bridge toward a deliberate transition is often the right move.

"Moving from the ninth floor to the tenth floor is worth more than starting from the first floor to the second floor over here."

Pitfall 4: Hustle Culture and the More-Hours Myth

The hustle culture gospel is simple: the more hours you put in, the more successful you'll be. Work 80, 90, 100 hours a week. Sleep less. Rest is for the weak.

The science says the opposite. The concept of eustress — the optimal level of stress for performance — is key here. Performance follows an inverted-U curve:

  • Too little stress → boredom, disengagement, underperformance
  • The eustress zone → mentally activated, focused, high performance
  • Too much stress (distress) → overwhelmed, exhausted, diminishing returns

Grinding 100-hour weeks with four hours of sleep pushes you deep into distress. The output per hour collapses, even if the total hours look impressive on paper. A line cook working 90 hours a week has dramatically less to show for it than someone working 40 well-structured hours in a higher-leverage role — hours alone don't determine outcomes.

In medical school, studying ten hours a day produced exhaustion and poor retention. Two focused hours first thing in the morning, with a fresh mind, produced far better recall. The research on this is consistent: what correlates with top academic performance isn't total hours studied — it's organization and protecting dedicated, bounded periods of focused work.

High turnover at investment banks illustrates the organizational cost of this myth. Overworked high performers leave for places that treat them better. Training a replacement is expensive. The math on burning people out doesn't work, which is why smart organizations bring in consultants to fix their culture — not to push people harder, but to create conditions where people are appropriately challenged and well-rested.

Pitfall 5: "Put Yourself First" Becomes a Self-Fulfilling Trap

The dog-eat-dog worldview goes: no one is going to look out for you, so you'd better look out for yourself. Put yourself first in every situation.

The problem is that this creates exactly the outcome it predicts. When you make it obvious — through behavior, not just words — that you're only looking out for yourself, the people around you respond in kind. They stop looking out for you. They stop passing opportunities your way. They stop investing in your success. And then you look around and confirm: See? Nobody helps me. I was right.

It's a self-fulfilling prophecy. The environment you experience is partly one you created.

The alternative isn't being a doormat. It's going into interactions — especially networking situations — with a genuine orientation toward helping others. What does this person need? What opportunities do I know about that might fit them? When you operate this way, people notice. They want to reciprocate. Opportunities flow back toward you, not because you engineered it, but because trust and goodwill compound over time.

"If I make it very clear that I don't give a [damn] about other people, why on Earth would anyone look out for me?"

You should absolutely take good opportunities when they come, advocate for yourself when it matters, and protect your own interests. But defaulting to always putting yourself first is a productivity killer disguised as a competitive edge.

Common Pitfalls

  • Conflating reading with learning. Finishing a book feels like an accomplishment, but if you close it and change nothing, nothing changed. The book is a prompt, not a result.
  • Thinking "managing parallel workflows" = multitasking. Knowing that multiple projects exist is fine. Trying to mentally work on two at once degrades both.
  • Using a side hustle to feel like you're doing more rather than as a deliberate bridge to a new career. If it's permanent and it's pulling attention from your primary work, it's probably costing you more than it returns.
  • Mistaking busyness for productivity. Long hours feel like effort, and effort feels like progress. But if you're in distress rather than eustress, your output per hour is low.
  • Assuming that self-interest is purely rational. The people who insist "look out for yourself because no one else will" often don't notice that they're the reason nobody is looking out for them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. After your next book chapter, stop and write for ten minutes. Don't summarize what you read — ask yourself: what does this change about how I'm going to behave this week? No action item = no point reading further until you've tested the last one.

  2. Run a focus audit for one workday. Every time you switch tasks or check something unrelated to what you're doing, mark it down. Most people are shocked by the frequency. Use this as your baseline, then try one day of working in 25–50 minute single-task blocks.

  3. Map your career tower. Write down what you're already building professionally. Then list what a move "one floor up" would look like — a certification, a specialization, a skill adjacent to your current role. Compare the likely return on that investment versus starting something new from zero.

  4. Track your actual productive hours, not total hours. For one week, note when your thinking is sharp versus when you're just physically present. Most people find their real window is three to five hours, not eight to twelve. Protect those hours fiercely.

  5. Try the networking inversion. Before your next professional conversation, event, or email, ask: what does this person need, and do I know anything that could help them? Lead with that. Notice over weeks whether the reciprocity changes.

  6. Experiment with a short, high-quality work session. Pick something cognitively demanding. Work on it for 90 minutes first thing in the morning, fully rested, with no phone. Compare what you produce in that window to what you produce in a tired, distracted three-hour block. Let the data shape your schedule.
