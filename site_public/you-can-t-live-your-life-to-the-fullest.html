The "Make-Up Test" Trap

When you've fallen behind, the stakes on every action quietly inflate. Think of it like a transcript with a failing grade. You can't afford a B — you need an A, ideally a guaranteed A, to make up for lost ground. Your mind won't settle for less.

Applied to real life: if you've missed two or three years of socializing, the next party can't just be fine. It has to be the best night of your life. The gym session can't be a tentative 15 minutes — it has to signal a transformation. A conversation with an old friend can't be awkward — you need to come out of it looking like someone who has their life together.

But a guaranteed A doesn't exist. No party, gym session, or reconnection comes with that promise. So your mind — hunting for a certainty it will never find — keeps you home instead.

"Readiness means knowing that you will live life to the fullest. But that's never going to happen. So then you get stuck."

The painful irony: it's because you want to live life to the fullest that you end up living almost none of it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Separate being ready from feeling ready. Before you avoid something, ask: Am I actually unready, or do I just feel unready? Write down what you would literally need to do the thing. Be concrete. If the list is short (a water bottle, clean clothes, enough bus fare), you're probably ready and just don't feel it.

  2. Take the B or the C deliberately. Choose one thing you've been avoiding because it couldn't be perfect. Do a half-version of it this week: 15 minutes at the gym, a short message to an old friend, one drawing you don't try to make portfolio-worthy. Practice the idea that an imperfect attempt still improves your average.

  3. Rehearse honest answers about your past. Write out a one-to-two sentence version of your real story — something like: "Honestly, the last few years were really hard — depressionA state of persistently low mood that goes beyond a normal emotional response to difficulty — it becomes clinical when symptoms last at least two weeks, impair functioning, and include at least five of nine recognized…Full definition → and anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → hit me badly, and it took a long time to get through. I'm putting things back together now." Say it out loud a few times. Notice what you feel. Then notice that the sentence is not actually shameful — it's just true.

  4. Meditate on the totality of life. During a quiet sitting, turn your attention outward and observe: what does life actually contain? Not what you want it to contain — what is it? Include the uncomfortable parts. Let them be there without trying to resolve them. This is the contemplative version of accepting that pain isn't a sign something has gone wrong.

  5. Catch the make-up mentality in real time. When you notice you're refusing to act because the outcome "has to be" great, ask yourself: Am I inflating the stakes because I feel I have something to make up for? Name it. The inflation is the mind's trick — call it out rather than comply with it.

  6. Start living, then aim for fullness — not the other way around. Pick the smallest available version of something you want to do and do it without the condition that it goes well. The goal isn't to arrive at "the fullest." The goal is to be doing the thing today, with whatever the thing has to offer.
