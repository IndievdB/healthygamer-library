Summary

TL;DR — Chanting Om (AUM) is a vibration-based meditation practice that works by loosening what's stuck inside you emotionally, so it can surface and be released rather than staying trapped.

This is a guided Om chanting session paired with a short teaching on what the practice is actually doing and why. The core idea is simple: vibration creates movement, and movement allows release. Whatever is held inside — anxiety, shame, sadness, grief — needs a way out. Chanting is one of the oldest tools for opening that door.

What Om Chanting Actually Is

Om is not just a sound. It is three distinct syllables — A, U, M — each produced at a different point in the mouth, moving from completely open to completely closed. That physical progression is intentional.

When you chant Om:

  • You take a full, deep breath before each round
  • You start with the mouth wide open on the "Ah" sound
  • You transition through "Oo"
  • You close into "Mm", lips sealed, feeling the vibration in the skull and chest
  • Then silence

Seven rounds are done in sequence, followed by a period of stillness — however long naturally feels right. The instruction to sit with a straight back isn't incidental; posture affects how much of the vibration you can actually feel move through the body.

The critical reframe: chanting is not about sound, it is about energy and vibration. The point isn't to make a beautiful noise. The point is to feel what the vibration does inside you.

Why Vibration Works: The Physics of Getting Unstuck

"If I've got something stuck, I shake it — it starts moving again."

That's the whole mechanism in one line. Emotional material — anxiety, shame, grief, unexpressed feeling — doesn't dissolve on its own just because time passes. It stays lodged. Vibration is a physical force that acts on what is stuck and gets it moving again.

This is why the instruction is to be loud. A quiet, polite hum won't do much. The goal is to maximize vibration, to let it move through the chest, throat, and head. The louder and fuller the breath, the more the vibration reaches into the body, and the more of what is held there begins to loosen.

With practice, the breaths get bigger, the vibration gets stronger, and more material surfaces. It is a skill that compounds.

What "Letting It Out" Actually Means

During the silence after chanting, the instruction is to simply notice what is there and allow it to move through rather than push it back down.

"If you just want to cry, then cry. Let it out. The tears are there — if you hold on to them they'll stay. If you let them out, they'll go."

This applies to everything that might surface:

  • Anxiety
  • Sadness
  • Shame
  • Stress
  • Even joy or energy

None of it is wrong to feel. The problem is not the feeling itself — the problem is keeping it inside. Anxiety held in stays as anxiety. Tears held in stay as tears. The practice creates a container where it is safe to let things move.

Watching what surfaces with awareness — not reacting to it, not judging it, just observing it as it comes up and passes — is the meditative skill being practiced here. Breathe it in, breathe it out. Seven breaths, nine breaths. Let the process do its work.

The Deeper Point: What Enlightenment Is

This isn't framed as a self-help technique. The practice points toward something larger.

"As you chant, as you meditate, what you think you are continues to come out — 'I'm ashamed, I deserve to cry, I'm anxious, I'm worried' — that comes out. You let it go, and you let it go, and you let it go. Whatever is there, let it up and out."

The Sanskrit word shunya means empty or zero. Moksha means liberation or enlightenment. The framing here is that what most people identify as their "self" — the shame, the anxiety, the sense of being a failure — is not the self. It is accumulated content. Emotional material that has built up and been held in.

The practice of chanting and meditation is, over time, a process of allowing that content to surface and leave. Not suppressing it, not analyzing it, not identifying with it — just letting it come up and out. What remains when the accumulated content is gone is the actual self, or at minimum, a much quieter and cleaner experience of being alive.

This reframes shame and the feeling of failure entirely. Those feelings are not who you are. They are things that are inside you, and things that are inside you can come out.

Common Pitfalls

Treating it as a performance. The instinct is to chant quietly and self-consciously, especially in a group setting. That kills the practice. The vibration needs to be full and loud to do anything useful. Politeness works against you here.

Focusing on the sound instead of the sensation. If you are listening to how you sound, you are missing the point. The instruction is explicit: pay attention to what you feel, not what you hear.

Pushing feelings back down during the silence. The silence after chanting is where the work happens. If something surfaces and you immediately tighten up and suppress it, you have just undone the benefit. The practice requires willingness to let what comes up actually move through.

Expecting instant results and stopping too soon. Bigger, deeper breaths come with practice. More material surfaces with practice. One session is a start, not a cure.

Intellectualizing rather than experiencing. This kind of practice resists analysis during the doing of it. Understanding the mechanism is useful beforehand. During the chant, the job is to feel, not to think.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try one session of seven-round Om chanting. Sit with a straight back. Take a full, deep breath before each round. Chant loudly — open mouth to closed lips, A to U to M. Do seven rounds.

  2. After the final round, sit in silence for at least 60–90 seconds. Don't rush to open your eyes or check your phone. Notice what is present — any emotion, sensation, or feeling that has surfaced.

  3. Practice the "let it out" instruction literally. If tears come, let them. If you feel anxious, breathe into it rather than suppressing it. If nothing comes, that's fine too — just sit with the stillness.

  4. Work on breath capacity deliberately. Each session, try to take a slightly bigger, deeper breath than the last. The fuller the breath, the stronger the vibration, the more effective the practice.

  5. Return to the reframe when shame or failure thoughts arise outside of meditation. Remind yourself: these feelings are inside you, which means they are not permanently you. They are content that can move.

  6. Build a regular practice. One session is useful. Repeated sessions over days and weeks are where the cumulative effect — progressively emptying out what has been held in — actually shows up.
