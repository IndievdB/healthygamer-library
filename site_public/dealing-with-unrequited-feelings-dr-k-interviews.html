Summary

TL;DR — Believing you're unlovable isn't a fact about the world; it's a belief you formed somewhere, and beliefs can be dismantled. This is a conversation with Hayden, a 21-year-old who identifies as an incel, working through what that label actually means for him — and uncovering that the real obstacle isn't women, race, looks, or bad luck. It's a core conviction that his "pie" isn't good enough to offer anyone.

What "Incel" Actually Looks Like Up Close

Hayden walks in calling himself an incel and a misogynist. But as the conversation unfolds, a more complicated picture emerges. He goes clubbing. He has friends. He makes people laugh. He fell in love. He treated the girl he liked "like a human." None of that matches the archetype he handed himself.

The label "incel" (involuntarily celibate) is supposed to mean someone else is responsible for your aloneness. But when pressed, Hayden admits he never told the girl how he felt. He decided she didn't like him before she ever had a chance to say otherwise. He made her choice for her.

"You're the one who's actually giving up your power. And the simplest thing is that you have no idea how she feels, but you make assumptions based on what you observe."

The misogyny, when it finally gets examined, turns out to be less about hating women and more about being hurt by one specific situation — and then using a general theory about women to protect himself from having to be hurt again.

The Real Story: Unrequited Feelings and What We Do With Them

Hayden met a girl clubbing. She made out with someone else that night. He later added her on Discord, played games with her for months, and quietly fell in love while she messaged him about her guy problems. He never said a word about how he felt. Eventually she forgot about plans they'd made, and he quietly stopped reaching out.

What emerges from this story is a pattern, not a verdict on women:

  • He felt powerless (she chose someone better-looking) and blamed himself (if he weren't so awkward, it would've gone differently)
  • He was putting far more emotional weight on the connection than she was
  • He dismissed the good moments — making her laugh, being the person she trusted — instead of building on them
  • He interpreted her treating him like a friend as proof she couldn't possibly like him romantically

"You forget nothing about her. She forgot about the plans. It feels like you're an afterthought to her."

This is painful and real. But the leap from "this one person didn't prioritize me" to "women as a class are at fault" is where the thinking goes wrong. The pain is legitimate. The explanation is a defense mechanism.

The Pie Analogy: Why You Never Give Anyone a Chance

The core of what went wrong — and goes wrong repeatedly for people in this pattern — is captured in a simple analogy. Imagine you bake a pie. Every time a guest comes over, you pull it out of the oven, decide it's not good enough, and throw it in the trash before they can taste it. Then you lie awake thinking, "No one likes my pies."

The guest never got a piece. You made the choice for them.

This is exactly what happens when you don't share your feelings because you've already decided the answer is no. You are not protecting yourself from rejection. You are guaranteeing the outcome you fear. And each time you do this, the belief that you're not good enough gets a little stronger — not because it was tested and confirmed, but because it was never tested at all.

"You have a fear that you're unlovable. And you will never risk that being true. And by not ever testing that hypothesis, you reinforce it with each girl you meet. It's a hypothesis that's never put to the test, but becomes more and more fact."

The belief gets confused with truth. But it isn't truth — it's just a belief that hasn't been challenged.

What Emotional Availability Actually Is

The conversation ends up in unexpected territory: emotional availability. Not as a soft therapeutic buzzword, but as the practical thing Hayden was missing.

Emotional availability isn't confessing your undying love. It's being willing to hold and communicate mixed feelings. An example of what this might actually sound like:

"Hey, I've always thought you were gorgeous and I valued our friendship. I also realized over time I had some romantic feelings for you. And there was that time you canceled our plans — I felt like I wasn't important to you, and that hurt. I'm glad we're talking again. I just wanted to share that."

That's not a confession. It's not a demand. It acknowledges attraction, friendship, hurt, and uncertainty — all at once. It gives the other person room to respond as a full human being rather than an NPC whose answer you've already scripted.

The incel and red-pill framework, as described here, treats women as objects to be decoded and won rather than people with their own inner lives. The irony is that Hayden clearly does see her as a person — which is why he was hurt. But his framework didn't give him the tools to act on that.

Where the Belief Comes From

Near the end, something important surfaces. The conviction that she couldn't possibly like him didn't come from her behavior. It came from somewhere earlier — childhood, people telling him he wasn't enough, accumulated experiences that hardened into a worldview.

"If it comes from people telling you that you're a piece of shit when you were growing up, that doesn't actually mean you're a piece of shit. It just means you've been convinced that you're a piece of shit."

A belief that was installed by other people, reinforced by a pattern of self-protective behavior, is not the same as a fact about your worth. The distinction matters enormously. If it's a belief, it can change. It takes work — probably therapy, probably uncomfortable conversations — but the mechanism is available.

The Meditation Interruption: Doing Things Anyway

Toward the end of the session, there's a brief meditation exercise — breathing, posture, sitting up straight. And the whole time, the conversation keeps needling Hayden: you're sitting like a beta, now you look like a Chad, you feel like an idiot, don't you?

The point isn't the breathing technique. It's that Hayden did the exercise anyway. He sat in front of thousands of strangers, aired things he'd never said out loud, felt anxious and embarrassed the whole time — and kept going.

When asked how he managed to do that, he said: "I just did. I don't know."

That's the answer to his own question about how to offer the pie. You don't wait until you're not scared. You don't wait until the pie is perfect. You feel the anxiety, the embarrassment, the certainty that you're going to look dumb — and you do it anyway. Not because the feelings go away, but because you discover, incrementally, that they don't actually stop you unless you let them.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking inaction for protection. Not telling someone how you feel doesn't shield you from rejection — it guarantees a particular kind of loss while adding deniability. The rejection happens either way; you just don't get the information.

  • Treating your worst interpretation of a situation as the only logical one. Hayden hid his feelings from her. He assumes she couldn't have hidden anything from him. Both can't be true.

  • Confusing a confession of love with sharing mixed feelings. These are not the same thing. One is a demand for a binary answer; the other opens a conversation.

  • Setting an impossible standard and then blaming the standard-setter. Hayden wants a guarantee that she'll like the pie before he'll offer a slice. That guarantee doesn't exist. Waiting for it is a choice to stay stuck.

  • Expanding one person's behavior into a theory about an entire gender. The misogyny here functions as a way to turn a painful, specific experience into a stable worldview that feels rational and lets you off the hook for trying again.

  • Thinking you need to be fixed before you're allowed to participate. "I don't want a girlfriend until..." The word "until" is the problem. You learn by doing, not by waiting to be ready.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Locate the belief, not just the feeling. When you feel like you're not good enough, ask: where did I first learn to think this? Trace it back to a specific time, person, or experience. The belief has a source. Finding the source weakens its authority.

  2. Stop deciding for other people. The next time you assume someone won't like you, catch the moment you're making their choice for them. You don't know. That's the point.

  3. Practice sharing mixed feelings, not declaring emotions. Instead of either saying nothing or making a grand confession, try: "I've had some complicated feelings about this and I wanted to talk through them with you." Mixed feelings are honest. They give the other person room.

  4. Run the smallest possible experiment first. If approaching someone feels impossible, start earlier in the chain. Smile at someone at the grocery store. Smile at a parent. Say something small to a stranger. You are building emotional resistance the same way you build any other skill — incrementally, against resistance.

  5. Fix your posture. This sounds trivial. It isn't. How you physically carry yourself affects how you feel and how you're perceived. Sit up straight. Take up the space you actually occupy.

  6. Do the thing while the anxiety is present. Don't wait for the anxiety to go away before acting. It probably won't. Notice it, breathe into it, and act anyway. Each time you do, you're proving to yourself that the feeling doesn't have veto power.

  7. Expand your criteria beyond one dimension. If you're evaluating potential partners only on physical attractiveness, you're using an impoverished model. Ask: does this person make me feel like I matter? That question will get you further than a rating scale.

  8. Consider talking to a therapist or coach — not to be told what to do, but to help excavate where the original belief about your worth came from and start dismantling it systematically.
