Technology Habits

As technology is becoming more sophisticated, it gets better at hijacking our attention. Remember that technology platforms and apps make money off of the amount of time you spend on the app - more time = more ad revenue. Apps and companies are fighting a war for your attention. The challenge is that even as they strive to take your attention, what loses out are the things that you need to do: studying, cleaning, etc. In this video, we'll explore the nature of how technology affects your attention, and key tips to help you develop healthy technology habits.

How Technology Is Reshaping Your Attention

Your brain is an efficiency machine. It constantly trims whatever it doesn't need. When the content you consume gets shorter and the reward comes faster, your brain draws a reasonable conclusion: there's no point maintaining the machinery for long-form focus. It's the same as letting a language atrophy — stop practicing and the brain quietly reassigns those resources.

Consider the shift over the past few decades: - Long-form blog posts → tweets - Facebook albums and status updates → Instagram - Long video essays → TikToks - Reading books → skimming headlines

Each step in that progression shortens the gap between stimulus and payoff. And as the payoff gets quicker and sweeter, sitting with discomfort — with a slow chapter, a complicated argument, a dull meeting — becomes harder. Your brain has been trained to expect the next hit immediately.

This isn't a moral failing. It's adaptation. But adaptation can work against you, and you can consciously push back on it.

Your Phone Is the Central Problem

The phone is uniquely dangerous because it erases the boundary between everything. Work and play, rest and productivity, presence and distraction — the phone collapses all of it into one glowing rectangle you carry everywhere.

Two consequences follow from this:

  1. Distraction is frictionless. The moment your attention drifts — which it will, because that's what minds do — your phone is right there to reward the drift with a dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → hit. Every time that cycle completes, you've trained your brain that wandering attention gets a prize.

  2. You never fully rest. Answering a work email at 9:35 PM isn't just one small concession. It signals to your coworkers that you're available at 9:35 PM, so they keep sending things at 9:35 PM, so you feel like you can never fully stop. The boundary erodes completely, and burnout follows.

Creating Friction Where It Matters

The key insight for managing technology isn't willpower — it's friction. Apps and platforms are designed to make engagement effortless. Your job is to make distraction slightly harder than staying on task.

A useful analogy: studying at a library with a physical textbook. You still got distracted every few minutes. But to act on that distraction, you'd have to pack up, walk down six flights of stairs, and leave the building. That barrier meant most distractions dissolved on their own. You lost 30 seconds but stayed on task for the rest of the hour.

That's what you're recreating with these habits.

Leave your phone somewhere you can't reflexively grab it — near the door, in a drawer, with your keys. If you're worried about missing emergencies, honestly audit: how many genuine emergencies has your phone delivered in the past three to six months? For most people, the answer is very few.

Separate Work from Play at the Device Level

Don't rely on self-discipline alone to keep work and recreation separate. Build the separation into the setup itself:

  • Use different logins on a shared computer for work and personal use
  • Use different devices entirely if possible (a work phone and a personal phone)
  • Adjust your home screen so high-distraction apps aren't one tap away

The goal is to increase the number of steps between you and a distraction. You don't need the barrier to be massive — you just need it to be big enough that a 30-second impulse doesn't turn into a four-hour detour.

Turn Off Notifications Ruthlessly

Every notification is a company fighting for your attention. When one lands, ask yourself: what does this do to the next five minutes? The next hour? Your focus for the rest of the day?

For anything non-essential, turn notifications off entirely. Be especially skeptical of apps that make it hard to disable notifications — that resistance is intentional. They're making it difficult because interrupting you is how they win.

The Alarm Trick for Breaking Autopilot

One of the most underrated tools is the humble alarm. Before you open YouTube, start a game, or begin scrolling TikToks, set a timer — 10 or 20 minutes.

Here's why this works: platforms are specifically designed to keep you on autopilot. One video ends and three more are already queued. You're not really choosing to keep watching — you're just not stopping. The alarm interrupts that loop and forces a moment of actual choice.

When the alarm goes off, ask yourself: - Am I still enjoying this? - Do I actually want to keep going, or am I just on autopilot? - Is there something I'd rather be doing?

You might decide to keep going — and that's fine. The point isn't mandatory stopping. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do a distraction audit. List the specific apps or content types that most reliably steal your time. For each one, estimate: when you lose a 30-second battle to it, how many hours do you actually end up spending? This maps your highest-risk entry points.

  2. Pick one place to leave your phone. Designate a spot — near your front door, in a drawer — and make a habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → of leaving it there when you're eating, working, or spending time with people. Start with one context and expand from there.

  3. Move your phone out of arm's reach at bedtime. Tonight. Leave it on a dresser, on a counter, or in the bathroom. Keep it accessible for true emergencies but not graspable by reflex.

  4. Audit your notifications. Go through your phone's notification settings and turn off everything that isn't genuinely time-sensitive. For anything that makes disabling hard, treat that resistance as a red flag about the app.

  5. Set an alarm before you start any open-ended tech use. Before you scroll, watch, or play — set a 10- or 20-minute timer. When it goes off, pause and consciously decide whether to continue. Track whether hour three feels as good as the first 10 minutes.

  6. Create at least one structural separation between work and play. Different logins, different devices, or different browser profiles. Make switching between modes require a deliberate step rather than a single click.

  7. Track your "emergency" calls for one month. If the fear of missing something urgent keeps your phone glued to you, keep a simple tally of how many genuine emergencies actually come through. Let the real number inform how you manage access.
