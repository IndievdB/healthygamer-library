Summary

TL;DR — When you blame yourself for everything bad that happens to you, anger has nowhere to go but inward, and that internalized anger is what depression actually is. This conversation traces how that pattern forms, why it persists, and what it takes to begin dismantling it.

The interview is a long, winding, remarkably honest conversation with a professional esports figure (LS) who has lived with depression and anxiety for seventeen years. What emerges over the course of the talk is less a clinical picture and more a portrait of someone who was never allowed to simply be who he was — and who learned, very early, to make every bad thing that happened to him his own fault.

The Self-Blame Pattern and How It Forms

There's a concept in developmental psychology called theory of mind — the ability to understand that other people exist as separate beings with their own intentions. Very young children don't have it yet. When something painful happens to a small child who hasn't developed theory of mind, they can't yet think "an adult did this to me." The only available explanation is: I caused this. Blame lands on the self by default, because the self is the only thing that exists in that child's world.

This is why early experiences of not being accepted, of being treated as a problem to be fixed rather than a person to be known, leave such a deep mark. The lesson gets encoded before there's any framework for questioning it. Long after the child grows up and develops the intellectual capacity to construct perfectly accurate theories about why things happened the way they did, that original encoding stays intact — not in the thinking mind, but somewhere beneath it.

LS can explain, articulately and correctly, the environmental factors that shaped him. He can extend enormous generosity to people who have genuinely wronged him — including someone who stole money from him, whom he frames primarily as someone who must have been in a desperate situation. He can empathize with his estranged mother by imagining what it would be like to parent an eleven-year-old. What he cannot do, yet, is let any of that understanding actually reduce how much he blames himself.

"You're able to construct a theory of why you're the way that you are, but I don't think you let that theory absolve you of responsibility."

The intellectual understanding and the emotional reality are operating in completely separate rooms.

Depression as Anger Turned Inward

There's a psychoanalytic idea worth sitting with: depression is anger directed against the self. When something genuinely bad happens to you — something unfair, something you didn't deserve — you're supposed to feel anger. That anger is information. It's the signal that something wrong occurred and that it was not your fault.

But if you have been trained, from very early on, to locate blame inside yourself, then anger at other people or circumstances has nowhere to land. It doesn't disappear. It turns around and comes back at you. The result looks like depression: a diffuse, heavy, purposeless misery that seems to have no object, because its real object — the legitimate anger at real wrongs — has been rerouted.

"Bad shit happens to you and happens to you and happens to you, and you just blame yourself — the blame just sits within you."

The cesspool metaphor is blunt but accurate. Emotions don't evaporate just because you decline to feel them. They accumulate.

The Protective Function of the Pattern

Here's the piece that makes this genuinely hard to treat: the self-blame pattern isn't irrational. At some point, it was adaptive. If everything bad is your fault, then you have agency. You have a road forward. You can do something differently next time. The alternative — accepting that bad things happen to you through no fault of your own — is, paradoxically, more terrifying, because it means you're not fully in control.

Fear operates the same way for LS. He identifies fear as his primary motivator. Fear of returning to poverty, homelessness, and helplessness is what gets him out of bed and keeps him moving. He knows, intellectually, that this is not an ideal way to live. But he also correctly recognizes that, for now, it's working. Taking that fear away before something replaces it would leave nothing.

"The dangerous thing is that there's probably a very large part of you that doesn't want the fear to leave."

The self-protective system — call it the inner critic, the survival mechanism, the monster, whatever language fits — is not evil. It was built out of necessity. It kept someone alive through genuinely difficult circumstances. The goal is not to destroy it but to help it calm down, because it no longer needs to run on high alert all the time. Getting a guard dog to relax requires a different approach than trying to eliminate the dog.

Why Intellectualizing Keeps You Stuck

LS is, by his own account and observable behavior, extremely intelligent. He thinks in systems, patterns, and cycles. He has mapped his own psychology with considerable accuracy. He can deduce the origin of a belief, trace its consequences, and anticipate the next ten moves of a conversation.

This is genuinely impressive. It is also, in this specific context, part of what maintains the problem.

The analytical mind is fast. The moment you begin to feel something uncomfortable — anger, grief, the raw sensation of having been genuinely wronged — the mind jumps in with an explanation. The explanation is often even correct. But the act of explaining is also the act of leaving the feeling. You step out of the emotional experience and into the cognitive one, and the emotion never gets processed. It gets understood instead of felt, which is not the same thing.

"The less your machine is active, the more you can just be in the moment — the more you will start to feel better."

During a brief exercise in the conversation, LS is asked to close his eyes, notice what he wants to say, and then describe what that impulse feels like physically. For a few seconds, the analytical narration drops away and something underneath becomes briefly visible — frustration, incompleteness, and then, unexpectedly, anger at specific people from his childhood. That's the real material. It surfaced because the analytical mind was briefly paused, not because it was argued with or explained to.

What Three Good Therapists Actually Did

Over seventeen years and many clinicians, only three were genuinely helpful. All three were women. What made them different was not technique or approach. They went over their allotted session times. They met in coffee shops and paid out of pocket for nicer places. They went to bat for LS in ways that could have cost them professionally — including confronting a school board and a parent directly.

"None of you are asking why — what's causing him to act like this."

That question is the thing. Not the diagnosis, not the treatment plan, not the CBT worksheets. Someone finally asked why instead of just cataloguing symptoms and trying to fix them. Someone extended to LS the same benefit of the doubt he extends, automatically and generously, to everyone else. The experience of being cared for — not managed — is what made those relationships different.

The bitter irony: LS does not believe that people genuinely care about him without an ulterior motive. The very thing that helped him most is the thing he can't fully receive.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking intellectual understanding for emotional processing. You can have a perfectly accurate theory of why you are the way you are and still be completely stuck. The theory needs to land somewhere below the neck.

Treating the self-protective pattern as the enemy. If you try to eliminate fear, self-blame, or the inner critic by force of will, you're fighting a mechanism that believes it's keeping you alive. It will fight back. The approach has to be different — slower, more like coaxing than attacking.

Forgiving others as a substitute for grieving. Genuine forgiveness requires first acknowledging that someone hurt you. If you route straight to "I understand why they did it" or "it was partly my fault anyway," you skip the step where you actually feel the injury. That step is not optional.

Confusing analytical insight with progress. Every time the mind produces a beautiful, accurate explanation of a feeling, it is also leaving that feeling behind. Insight is useful. It is not the same as healing.

Pathologizing the self based on others' definitions. Being treated as broken, sick, or in need of fixing from a young age teaches you to see yourself that way. The diagnoses and the treatment and the referrals can reinforce a story: there is something fundamentally wrong with me. That story is worth questioning.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Learn to meditate — seriously, not casually. The specific value here is that meditation is one of the few practices that can interrupt the analytical loop without engaging it. You are not trying to think better thoughts. You are practicing not thinking at all, briefly. Start with five minutes of focused attention on breath. When the mind narrates, label it "thinking" and return to the breath. Do this daily.

  2. Practice feeling anger at the right target. When something bad happens to you, before you reach for an explanation or ask what you could have done differently, pause. Ask: whose fault is this, really? Not as an intellectual exercise — stay with the question long enough to notice if there's a physical sensation of frustration or anger that wants to point somewhere other than inward.

  3. Notice the moment you leave a feeling for an explanation. You don't have to stop doing it immediately. Just notice it. "I was starting to feel something and then I started explaining it." That gap between feeling and explaining is where the work lives.

  4. Do your part in relationships — not more. In any relationship where you're trying to fix things, identify what your actual responsibility is and do exactly that. Not more. You cannot do another person's portion of the relationship, and attempting to do so is not generosity — it's a way of avoiding the helplessness of acknowledging that you can't control their choices.

  5. When something bad happens that you didn't cause, say so out loud. Not to anyone in particular. Just: "This happened. I didn't deserve it. It wasn't my fault." This will feel wrong, probably for a long time. Do it anyway. The goal is not to believe it immediately but to introduce the possibility that the automatic self-blame reflex is a reflex — not a verdict.

  6. Notice what your mind does with hope. If you find yourself immediately analyzing whether a positive development applies to you, or finding reasons it probably won't work in your specific case — notice that. The protective system co-opts hope and turns it into evidence for hopelessness. Catching it doing that is not nothing.
