Summary

TL;DR — The capacity for darkness isn't what makes someone a bad person; choosing what to do with it is what makes them a good one.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and Kitboga, a scam-baiter who has spent years on the phone with fraudsters posing as elderly victims. What starts as a casual chat about desensitization turns into a genuinely searching exploration of empathy, the psychology of sociopathy, why good people struggle with "dark" impulses, and what it actually means to have a moral compass.

What Callousness Actually Does to Empathy

After three years of talking to scammers, Kitboga describes becoming callous — a word he reaches for carefully. Where he once felt visceral shock hearing someone threaten an 87-year-old grandmother over a gift card, that shock has faded. He still goes through the motions of feeling bad for the victim he's pretending to be, but it costs him something.

There's a useful distinction being drawn here between two kinds of empathy:

  • Emotional empathy — actually feeling what another person feels, the way you feel a gut-punch when someone on TV takes a hit somewhere painful
  • Cognitive empathy — intellectually recognizing that someone is suffering without that recognition landing in your body

Kitboga's empathy has shifted toward the cognitive end, at least for the scammers. He can think "this is just another person who got here somehow" without it landing as a felt experience. He notices this himself and is uneasy about it.

This isn't unique to scam-baiters. Psychiatrists and physicians have to develop something similar, deliberately. A therapist seeing ten severely depressed patients in a day cannot have the emotional faucet fully open or they'll be destroyed by it. That controlled emotional distance is actually adaptive — and it places on a continuum with what is clinically described as sociopathy. That doesn't make the person a sociopath. It means sociopathy isn't a binary category you either have or don't have. It's a dimension, and most people — especially those doing emotionally demanding work — develop a slice of it as a protective mechanism.

"Callousness and essentially sociopathy is a function of the mind that can be grown and adaptive in certain circumstances."

The Leaky Valve Problem

Here's a curious side effect of building walls in one emotional domain: the feeling doesn't disappear — it accumulates and leaks out elsewhere.

Kitboga is emotionally closed off to scammers. But when one of his older children casually shoved the younger one away from something on a table, he cried. Not because the injury was serious, but because on his youngest child's face he saw something he recognized: the specific look of unexpected betrayal. Why would someone who's supposed to be safe hurt me?

That reaction wasn't disproportionate — it was the stored emotional weight of a compartmentalized life finding an exit. People who work in high-callousness environments (emergency medicine, crisis counseling, fraud investigation) often report this: numbness at work, sudden overwhelming feeling at home, usually triggered by something that seems objectively minor.

The feelings don't disappear. They just move.

The Predator Question — and Why It's Uncomfortable

Kitboga is not just wasting scammers' time. He lures them in with a fake persona, strings them along for hours, codes a fake Google Play redemption page, and lets them think they've won before pulling the floor out. That is, functionally, hunting. He knows how they think, knows the script they're running, knows exactly which lever to pull at each stage. He describes moments where he can "play them like a fiddle."

When this gets named directly — you're a predator — he gets uncomfortable. And then he immediately starts explaining why what he does is good: he educates people, he wastes the scammer's time, the ends justify the means.

That discomfort and the rapid rationalization that follows it are worth paying attention to. The discomfort comes from something true: there is a part of him that enjoys the hunt, that gets satisfaction from control, that likes the precision of setting a trap and watching it spring. That feeling gets associated in his mind with villains — "it feels like something a bad guy in a movie would say."

But those feelings don't make someone a bad person. Having them is not the problem.

"Goodness or badness doesn't come from having those thoughts... thinking negative thoughts doesn't make us bad people."

The same architecture that makes Kitboga good at what he does — the ability to model another person's thinking, anticipate their moves, guide them without their awareness — is morally neutral. It's a capability. A scammer uses it to drain retirement accounts. He uses it to waste a scammer's afternoon and teach his audience how the con works. The capacity is the same. The choice is different.

Why Good People Struggle to Accept Their "Dark" Side

There's a reason Kitboga reflexively justifies himself when asked whether the work is fun, rather than just saying "yes, I enjoy outwitting someone." Every positive description of the work — I'm educating people, I support my family, I believe everyone has value — is accurate. But it's also functioning as counterweight to something he's less comfortable naming.

The discomfort isn't hypocrisy. It's actually the signature of a functioning moral agent. Someone who felt no internal friction, who said yeah I just love controlling people and this is a good outlet for that, would be concerning. The struggle itself is the evidence of good faith.

This is the common misunderstanding about what makes someone a good person. Most people assume good people simply don't have the dark impulses — the hunter instinct, the satisfaction in another person's suffering (schadenfreude), the enjoyment of control. So when they notice those feelings in themselves, they feel like hypocrites or secretly bad people.

But the internal experience of a genuinely good person is not the absence of those impulses. It's the ongoing negotiation with them.

"A good person is not someone who lacks negative feelings. A good person is one who strives to balance."

What the Scammers' Responses Reveal

Over three years of asking scammers "why are you doing this — do your parents know?" Kitboga has noticed a rough distribution in how they respond:

  • ~50% hang up immediately
  • ~25% become defensive and boast: I make hundreds of millions, my life is perfect
  • ~25% show a crack: this is the only way I can feed my family
  • ~2% get genuinely honest about being trapped and not knowing how else to survive

That 2% is the one he's fishing for. And the fact that he still fishes for it — even though it irritates some of his audience, even though it happens rarely, even though it's become harder over time — is the real signal. The audience wants the pounce. He keeps offering the outstretched hand. Not because it usually works. Because he can't fully stop believing it might.

Common Pitfalls

Conflating empathy with sympathy. Sympathy is feeling bad for someone from a distance. Empathy is feeling bad with them — their experience lands in you. People use these interchangeably and end up confused about what they actually feel and why.

Treating sociopathy as a binary. Either you're a sociopath or you're not. In reality, the relevant traits exist on a spectrum, and developing some degree of emotional detachment in high-stress work is normal and adaptive — not a sign of pathology.

Believing good people don't have dark impulses. This is probably the most damaging misconception in the whole talk. When you feel satisfaction at someone else's failure, or enjoy having control over a situation, or find yourself thinking something that embarrasses you, you're not revealing a secretly bad character. You're having a normal human experience. The question is what you do next.

Using rationalization as a warning sign rather than a tool. Kitboga rationalizes — the ends justify the means, I'm educating people — and that's not a failure of honesty. It's how a person with genuine values integrates behavior that doesn't fit neatly into a clean self-image. The problem would be if the rationalization were covering up actual harm.

Mistaking callousness for permanent damage. Becoming emotionally closed off in one domain doesn't mean you're broken. It often means the feeling is building up somewhere and will find another exit. Noticing where it comes out (usually at home, usually over something small) is more useful than worrying about the numbness at work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Distinguish your two empathy channels. Next time you say "I feel bad for someone," ask: did I feel it — did it land in my body — or did I think it? Neither is wrong, but knowing which is happening gives you better information about your actual emotional state.

  2. Name your dark impulses without immediately explaining them away. When you notice a feeling you'd normally rush to justify — enjoyment of control, satisfaction at someone else's failure, the fun of knowing more than the other person — sit with it for ten seconds before reaching for the rationalization. You're not endorsing it. You're just looking at it.

  3. Track where suppressed feeling leaks out. If you're in a demanding job that requires emotional blunting, pay attention to what makes you unexpectedly emotional at home or in low-stakes situations. That's not random. It's telling you something about where the pressure is actually going.

  4. Reframe goodness as a practice, not a trait. Instead of asking "am I a good person?" (which invites defensiveness and justification), ask "what choice am I making today with what I'm capable of?" The capability itself is neutral. The daily choice is where character lives.

  5. If you do emotionally demanding work, monitor the "reaching out" impulse. Kitboga used to try to have real conversations with scammers at the end of every call. Over time, he stopped — not because it was the wrong thing to do, but because he stopped believing it would work. Notice if you're losing that impulse in your own work. It's often an early sign of burnout, not just callousness.
