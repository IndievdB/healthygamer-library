Summary

TL;DR — This is a year-end recap stream where Dr. K reflects on 2024's highlights: a book launch, government meetings about the mental health crisis, the state of video game addiction, and a handful of raw coaching moments with community members.

2024 was a sprawling year. The clips and conversations woven through this recap touch on parenting and tech relationships, the nature of desire and character, how to think about regret, the loneliness crisis reaching the policy level, and the quiet courage of keeping going when you don't know how things will turn out. What follows pulls out the ideas worth sitting with.

Why a Book for Parents Instead of for You

One of the more counterintuitive moves of 2024 was writing a book aimed at parents rather than at the young adults who make up most of this community. The reasoning is worth understanding.

You can try to help people work through the damage that's already been done — the anxiety, the avoidance, the screen dependency, the difficulty connecting. That's valuable. But if you can reach the generation of parents raising kids right now, you address the conditions that create those problems in the first place. It's upstream intervention.

The book's subtitle frames it around screen habits and parent-child power struggles, but the deeper subject is all relationships — with technology, with other people, with ourselves. The framing "end power struggles" is less about winning arguments and more about understanding why those struggles happen at all.

"The biggest mistake that we make is we stop trying. That's the only time that you're guaranteed to lose — when you stop trying."

Video Game Addiction Is Bigger Than Most People Realize

When presenting to a national addiction conference and to government officials, one statistic landed hard: alcohol addiction is one of the most common addictions in the general population, and video game addiction rates run roughly twice as high — and are still climbing.

  • In 2010, the rate was around 6%
  • It's now closer to 10–12%
  • That trajectory is moving rapidly upward

Most public conversation about gaming still treats addiction as a niche or fringe concern. The data says otherwise. This matters for policy, for parents, and for anyone who finds themselves unable to stop even when they want to.

The good news from the government meetings: high-level officials are aware. The conversations included the Science and Technology Adviser to the President, and topics ranged from gaming and loneliness to the psychological impact of deepfakes. Awareness at that level doesn't guarantee good policy, but it's a necessary precondition.

Desire Doesn't Make You a Bad Person — What You Do Does

One of the more clarifying moments in the recap comes from a coaching conversation about character. There's a confusion most people carry without realizing it: conflating having a desire with acting on it, or worse, with being the kind of person who would act on it.

"Being a good person is not about what you want. We all have selfish desires. Being a good person is about the way that you act in relation to them."

Wanting something — even something you'd be ashamed to admit — doesn't make you a bad person. The want and the action are separate. The problem is that people blur the line, which leads to one of two failure modes:

  1. Suppression and self-deception — you pretend you don't want what you want, which means you can't think clearly about it or make a real choice
  2. Capitulation dressed as honesty — you tell yourself that wanting something means you should have it, or that resisting it is somehow fake

The more honest path is uncomfortable: acknowledge what you actually want, and then decide how much to act on it. You don't control where your desires come from. You do have some say over what you do next.

The Problem with "Living Without Regrets"

"Live a life of no regrets" is everywhere. It's on graduation speeches, tattoos, motivational posters. As a life strategy, it doesn't hold up.

The better frame is to understand the nature of regret rather than to avoid it entirely. There's a meaningful difference between:

  • Regret born of ignorance — you made a choice without really understanding what you were doing or what it would cost
  • Regret from eyes-open risk — you understood the stakes, made the best call you could, and it didn't work out

The first kind is worth trying to reduce. The second is just part of being alive and making choices under uncertainty. Trying to avoid all regret usually means avoiding all meaningful decisions — which creates its own, larger regret.

"The best strategy is to understand the nature of regret, accept that you are going to have some regrets in your life."

The honest position is something like: I'm going to keep going until the instrument breaks. When enough is enough, then we're done. That's not recklessness. It's clarity about the present moment without false promises about forever.

Emotional Avoidance Disguised as Self-Sufficiency

One coaching exchange surfaces something subtle. A person describes not wanting to share their emotional life with others — framing it as not wanting to "give" people something, preferring to keep it for themselves. On the surface it sounds like healthy independence.

The reframe: this is avoidance. When you're so cut off from your own emotions that they don't come out, and when you rationalize that distance as "I just don't think people deserve it," you're not being self-sufficient — you're protecting yourself from vulnerability by building a story that makes the avoidance look principled.

The tell is the logic: I keep it for myself so I can be happier. But if the emotions aren't actually being processed or felt, they're not being "kept" — they're being buried. That's not the same thing.

The Loneliness Crisis Is Now a Policy Issue

The White House visit was a genuine milestone. Mental health, loneliness, and addiction reached the level of active policy conversation with senior government officials in 2024. That's not a small thing.

The loneliness crisis in particular is worth naming plainly: it isn't just individuals feeling isolated. It's a structural condition with documented consequences for physical health, mental health, and social trust. The fact that it's now being discussed alongside addiction and technology policy suggests the scale is impossible to ignore.

For anyone doing this work — as a clinician, a coach, or just a person trying to help people around them — being taken seriously at that level changes what's possible.

A Small Moment Worth Keeping

Buried in the recap is a story about a seven-year-old who kept asking her father what his favorite color was. When he finally asked why, she said: "I don't really know what to say, but I want to talk to you, so I'm just asking you this question."

It's a social skill problem — she hadn't yet learned how to initiate connection directly. But it's also just human. The desire to be close to someone without quite knowing how to say so is not something we fully grow out of. We just develop more sophisticated versions of asking about favorite colors.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking desire = character. You don't choose what you want. Judging yourself harshly for wanting something (rather than for what you do about it) creates shame without producing better behavior.
  • Treating "no regrets" as a workable goal. It isn't. It leads either to paralysis or to rationalizing every impulsive choice as boldness.
  • Mistaking emotional closure for strength. Not sharing emotions, not needing people, keeping everything "for yourself" — these can be genuine boundaries, but they're often avoidance in a flattering disguise.
  • Underestimating gaming addiction because it doesn't look like other addictions. No substance is involved, so people dismiss it. The data doesn't support that dismissal.
  • Thinking upstream problems can only be fixed downstream. If the conditions that create a problem can be addressed at the source, that's often more powerful than treating symptoms after the fact — even if it feels less direct.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit one desire you've been judging yourself for. Write it down. Ask: Have I been conflating having this want with being a bad person? Then separately ask: What do I actually want to do about it? Keep those two questions distinct.

  2. Rethink one decision you regret. Was it ignorance — you genuinely didn't understand the stakes? Or was it an informed risk that didn't work out? The response to each is different. Ignorance calls for learning; informed risk calls for self-compassion.

  3. Notice your avoidance patterns around emotion. When you pull back from sharing something real, ask yourself what story you're using to justify it. "They don't deserve it" or "I don't need that" may be true — or may be a cover.

  4. If you have kids (or work with them), pay attention to indirect bids for connection. The child asking about favorite colors isn't annoying — she's reaching out and doesn't have the language yet. Adults do this too, just differently.

  5. Take video game addiction seriously as a category, whether or not it applies to you. If someone in your life is gaming in a way that has consequences — to relationships, school, work, health — treat it with the same seriousness you'd bring to any other behavioral addiction.

  6. Do 30 minutes of nothing. Sit down. No phone, no content, no task. Notice what comes up. It won't be comfortable. That discomfort is information.
