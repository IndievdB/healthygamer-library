Summary

TL;DR — A "mixed" résumé isn't a weakness if you pitch it correctly, and the jobs you think you can't get are sometimes the ones that actually hire you.

Breaking into a field after graduation is genuinely hard. Entry-level jobs demand two years of experience, and the jobs you feel genuinely excited about seem out of reach. This talk walks through both sides of that trap — and how to escape it — using a real conversation with a recent graduate who spent six months failing to land work in her field, then got hired by a tabletop RPG startup she almost didn't apply to because she assumed she wasn't qualified.

The Entry-Level Experience Paradox

The frustration is real and structural: companies post entry-level roles that require two years of professional experience. For anyone who just graduated, that's a wall with no door in it. You can't get experience without a job, and you can't get the job without experience.

The mistake most people make here is treating this wall as the only wall that exists. It isn't. There are also jobs you'd genuinely love, posted by companies you'd never send a résumé to because on paper you don't match their requirements. Those two walls are very different, and conflating them keeps you stuck.

"If I don't apply to the job, I don't get this job. If I apply and they say no, I don't get the job either."

That logic — equal downside either way, but one path has upside — is the mental shift worth internalizing. Rejection from a stretch application costs you nothing you didn't already not have.

Why Startups and Smaller Companies Play by Different Rules

The reason the startup in this story hired someone who didn't match the job description comes down to how they evaluate candidates. Larger, more established companies often screen for demonstrated, narrowly defined expertise. Startups — especially early-stage ones — frequently care more about willingness to learn than prior mastery, because they're still figuring out what they need.

The job turned out to be 3D print support work — placing structural supports on miniature models so they don't fail during printing. The software took about a day and a half to learn. It's not a role requiring a specialized degree. What it required was someone curious enough to apply and honest enough to say "I want to learn this."

The compensation wasn't high. But the company treated her as someone worth investing in — and that matters, especially early in a career when experience is the thing you're actually trying to accumulate.

How to Frame a "Scattered" Résumé

The second concern raised in this talk is more universally relatable: what do you do when your work history looks all over the place? Graphic design, environmental design, 3D printing for miniatures — on paper that reads as shallow or directionless.

The framing shift: breadth is a genuine advantage for specific kinds of roles. It's not just spin.

There are three legitimate ways to pitch a varied background:

  1. Cross-domain understanding. You see how different parts of a field connect. Someone who knows environmental design and graphic design and the structural constraints of 3D printing understands design problems from multiple angles simultaneously. That's valuable.

  2. Demonstrated learning speed. You've entered completely different fields, picked them up, and produced results. That's evidence of adaptability — a skill that's extremely hard to fake and genuinely useful to companies that are growing or changing. Framing: "My background is varied because I learn quickly and adapt to what's needed."

  3. Management-track positioning. Breadth is the natural background for project managers and creative directors who need to understand the work their team does without necessarily executing it themselves. This isn't for everyone — if you're creative and want to keep making things, this path might not appeal — but it's worth knowing the option exists.

The honest caveat: if a job requires deep specialization, a generalist résumé is less competitive. The goal isn't to pretend otherwise — it's to stop applying only to those jobs and start finding the roles where what you've actually built is the thing they need.

Rethinking the "Lifetime at One Company" Model

There's a cultural script in many families and countries: find a good company, stay there, build your career in one place. That script produces a specific anxiety when your résumé reflects movement — it feels like you're failing at stability.

That model is shifting. Staying at one company for a career-long tenure is still viable and can be deeply rewarding — but it works best when you've found the right company, and the right company is rarely the first one. Moving every few years early in a career isn't instability; it's the process of finding where you actually fit and, practically, it's often how people maximize earnings over time.

"The long-term company may not be your first company."

The early years are for learning, building experience, and developing a clearer sense of what you actually want. Staying at an okay job for five years because you're hoping it becomes the forever job is often the slower path, not the safer one.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating entry-level experience requirements as literal. Many are aspirational or auto-populated by HR systems. Apply anyway, especially if something about the role genuinely excites you.

  • Assuming a varied background is automatically a liability. It's only a liability if you're applying for roles that require narrow specialization. For many roles, it's the opposite.

  • Pitching breadth as breadth. "I've done a lot of different things" isn't compelling. The pitch needs to show what that variety enables — faster learning, cross-domain insight, flexibility.

  • Selling yourself into a role you won't enjoy. Breadth can credibly position you for management or project coordination. But if you actually want to do creative work, taking a management job because it's easier to sell is a mismatch worth avoiding.

  • Self-selecting out before applying. The psychological cost of not applying is invisible, so it feels like zero. It isn't — it's a guaranteed no that you handed yourself.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Apply to one job this week that you think you're underqualified for. The criteria: you find the work genuinely interesting, and the gap isn't absurd (don't apply to be CFO of a Fortune 500 company). Practice treating rejection as neutral information rather than confirmation of inadequacy.

  2. Rewrite your résumé summary to lead with what your breadth enables, not just what you've done. Draft one or two sentences that explain the through-line: what you can do because you've touched multiple areas that a pure specialist couldn't.

  3. Identify which type of employer fits your current background. Make a short list: which roles genuinely benefit from someone who knows multiple things? Project management, cross-functional teams, startups, generalist design roles. Apply there intentionally rather than exclusively targeting roles built for specialists.

  4. Separate "long-term fit" anxiety from "current job" decisions. If you're at an early-career job that's teaching you something and treating you decently, it doesn't need to be your forever job. Give yourself explicit permission to treat it as a phase rather than a verdict.

  5. If networking feels inaccessible, start with genuine interest. The startup in this story was found because the applicant was already interested in tabletop RPGs. Communities built around things you care about — online or in person — are a more natural networking environment than formal professional events.
