Summary

TL;DR — Not enjoying something isn't automatically a reason to quit. The more important question is whether it's worth it.

A lot of high-achieving people hit a wall where they're doing objectively impressive things but feel no joy in them — and the confusion that follows can be paralyzing. This talk walks through that specific situation: what's really going on, why happiness isn't always the right metric to optimize for, and how to think clearly about whether to push through or change course.

The Trap of Doing Things for Other People's Pride

The person in this scenario is accomplished by almost any measure — published papers, academic textbook, symposium presentations. But a pattern runs quietly underneath all of it: they're doing things to make people proud. Mentors, family. The pride of others is what generates their happiness.

That's not automatically a problem. Wanting to make people proud is a real and legitimate source of motivation, and there's nothing wrong with it. But it becomes a problem when you start doing things you genuinely dislike — not because they serve your goals, but because you're afraid of what happens if you stop performing for the people watching you.

There's also a telling detail here: this person doesn't want to talk to their mentors about how they're feeling, because they don't want to seem "not good enough for their mentoring." They even acknowledge that their mentors are chill and wouldn't actually judge them that way. The fear is real even though it's not grounded in reality.

That gap — between what you know logically and what you feel — is important to notice. It's often a sign that the relationship you have with external approval is running deeper than you've admitted to yourself.

Why "Am I Happy?" Is the Wrong Question Here

The instinct when you're miserable is to chase happiness — to drop the painful thing and go find something better. That instinct isn't always wrong, but for people with high potential and serious long-term goals, it can lead you astray.

Here's the reframe: instead of asking am I happy doing this?, ask is this worth it?

Those are very different questions. One is about your present emotional state. The other is about whether what you're enduring is connected to something that genuinely matters to you.

This is where dharma becomes relevant. Dharma isn't just about finding your passion or your purpose in some abstract sense. It's what allows you to sacrifice happiness, tolerate real suffering, and still feel at peace — because you understand why you're doing it. When you have a clear sense of what you're trying to build or become in the world, temporary suffering stops being meaningless. It becomes the cost of something you've chosen.

Without that clarity, the same suffering just feels pointless. And pointless suffering is much harder to bear.

The Real Questions to Ask About the MBA

Rather than asking "should I stay or should I quit," break the situation into smaller, more answerable questions:

  1. Why did you get into this program in the first place? Was it because it genuinely fits where you're trying to go — or was it because it seemed like the right thing to do, or because people expected it of you?

  2. What specifically do you dislike about it? Not just "I don't enjoy it" — that's too vague. Is it the subject matter? The culture? The pace? The direction it's pulling you in? Getting specific helps you figure out whether what you're feeling is temporary discomfort or a genuine mismatch.

  3. Are the skills you're developing actually useful for what you want to accomplish? If the answer is yes — if an MBA genuinely equips you for the next phase of what you're trying to build — then suffering through two years might be exactly the right move, even if it's unpleasant. If the answer is no, or you're not sure, that's a different conversation.

  4. Is this sacrifice worth it? This is the core question. Two years of not enjoying yourself is a real cost. But it's a cost that can make sense if it's connected to something larger. The goal isn't to eliminate all discomfort from your path — it's to make sure the discomfort you're accepting is paying for something you actually want.

"Remember that dharma is what allows you to sacrifice happiness and tolerate suffering — and still find peace."

No one enjoys 24-hour hospital calls. Working weekends. Missing time with family. But when those sacrifices are in service of something you've consciously chosen and understood, they feel different than suffering that has no purpose. That distinction matters enormously.

Reach Out to Your Mentors

One of the most practical pieces of advice here is also the one being avoided: talk to your mentors.

The fear of seeming "not good enough" is understandable but almost certainly unfounded. People who mentor graduate students and young professionals have seen this exact feeling dozens of times. Feeling like you don't want to be in the program anymore is not a sign of failure — it's extremely common. And the people who have been through it themselves are often the most valuable resource available to you.

"You'd be surprised what your mentors may say."

There's also something worth noticing about why it's hard to be vulnerable with them. If the relationship is built entirely on performing for their approval, then any crack in the performance feels threatening. Working through that reluctance — actually having the honest conversation — is part of breaking the pattern of living for other people's pride.

Common Pitfalls

Treating unhappiness as automatic evidence that something is wrong. Discomfort and misery during difficult programs, transitions, or skill-building phases is normal. It doesn't mean you're in the wrong place.

Treating unhappiness as something to push through no matter what. The flip side: if something genuinely doesn't connect to your goals and you're only doing it to perform for others, suffering through it indefinitely isn't noble — it's avoidance of a harder decision.

Staying vague about what you dislike. "I just don't enjoy it" isn't enough information to act on. Pinning down what specifically is making you miserable gives you something to actually work with.

Isolating from the people who could help most. Hiding struggle from mentors to protect an image of competence tends to make the situation worse and lonelier. The image costs more than the honest conversation would.

Confusing external validation with genuine motivation. Making people proud can be part of what drives you, but if it's the only driver, you'll eventually find yourself doing things you actively dislike with no real understanding of why.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down specifically what you dislike. Not just "the MBA" — list the actual things. Coursework? Culture? The direction it's taking you? Being specific is the first step toward knowing whether the discomfort is fixable or fundamental.

  2. Revisit why you got into the program. Write it out honestly. Was it your goal, or someone else's? Does the reason still hold up?

  3. Connect the program to your actual goals — or find out that it doesn't. Ask yourself: will the skills I'm developing here help me do what I actually want to do in the world? If you can answer that clearly, the "stay or quit" question becomes much easier.

  4. Ask yourself the worth-it question directly. Write it out: "Is it worth it to me to do this for [X] more months/years in order to get [specific outcome]?" Answer it honestly. Don't let discomfort make the decision for you, but don't dismiss your own answer either.

  5. Have the conversation with a mentor. Pick one. Tell them honestly that you're struggling with the program and not sure you're enjoying it. You don't have to have it all figured out before you speak. Just open the door.

  6. Notice when you're performing vs. pursuing. Over the next week, pay attention to moments when you're working hard to make someone proud versus moments when you're working toward something you've chosen. That distinction, practiced over time, is how you start to understand your own motivation more clearly.
