Summary

TL;DR — Stop trying to fix or reassure people when they're upset. Reflect their feelings back to them instead, and watch them feel genuinely supported.

Most people respond to someone else's pain by trying to make it go away — offering reassurance, silver linings, or solutions. This instinct feels kind, but it almost always backfires. There's a better approach: reflective listening, which validates what someone is feeling rather than arguing with it. This talk also touches on why that kind of emotional engagement is energizing rather than draining — and what that reveals about purpose and low energy more broadly.

Why Reassurance Quietly Backfires

When someone comes to you hurting — say, they just got dumped — the natural impulse is to say something like "there are other fish in the sea" or "you'll find someone else, I know it." That feels supportive. It isn't.

Here's why: when you tell someone "it's going to be okay," you're implicitly telling them that what they currently feel is wrong. The subtext lands something like: "You're being irrational. Obviously everything will be fine. Why are you even upset?" That's invalidation — and it's incredibly isolating, even when it comes from a place of love.

The person didn't come to you for a verdict on whether their feelings are accurate. They came to you because something hurts. Reassurance skips right past the hurt and tries to delete it. But feelings don't work that way. You can't logic someone out of an emotion.

"It's actually incredibly invalidating. It's kind of telling them — hey, you're stupid, your feelings are wrong."

What Reflective Listening Actually Looks Like

Reflective listening means mirroring back what the other person is expressing — their feelings, their fears, their experience — without adding your interpretation, your fix, or your optimism.

Someone says: "I'm scared I'll never find anyone who loves me again."

You don't say: "That's not true, you're amazing, you'll definitely find someone."

You say: "It sounds like you're afraid you're not going to find someone to love you again."

Then you stop. You go silent. You let them respond.

They say something else. You reflect that back too:

"Yeah, it sounds like you're going through a really rough time. It sounds like you're not feeling very confident in yourself right now."

That's it. No sunshine. No solutions. No timeline on when they'll feel better.

A few principles that make this work:

  • Match, don't correct. You're not there to tell them what's true about the future. You're there to confirm what's true about right now, for them.
  • The period matters. After you reflect something back, stop talking. The silence is where the other person processes and goes deeper.
  • You never have to say "it'll be okay." In fact, a conversation can stay entirely in the dark — exploring everything that's hard and painful — and the person will still feel supported at the end of it. Not because the problems got solved, but because they felt heard.

"A lot of what I did was actually reflecting back what she said. I didn't tell her anything was going to be okay. I just sort of repeated back what she was saying — and that's what helps people come out of it. That's what helps the emotions come out."

This is counterintuitive. It feels like you're not doing anything. You're not offering wisdom or comfort or a path forward. But the act of accurately reflecting someone's inner state is the comfort. It tells them: I see exactly where you are, and I'm not trying to move you out of it.

The Connection Between Authenticity and Emotional Stamina

Being this kind of listener for hours at a time raises an obvious question: doesn't it wear you out?

The answer is more interesting than a simple yes or no. What's actually exhausting isn't feeling emotions alongside someone — it's being inauthentic. Performing care you don't feel, wearing a mask, going through motions that don't mean anything to you: that's what drains people. It feels like working a job you hate for ten hours. No matter how physically easy it is, it's spiritually hollow.

Genuine engagement — actually caring, actually being present — is tiring in the way a good workout is tiring. You're physically spent at the end, but you feel something like expanded rather than depleted.

"What fatigues me is being inauthentic. What's tiring is putting on an act."

This matters beyond therapy or difficult conversations. It points to something about energy in general. Energy doesn't primarily come from rest or from doing less. It comes from living in alignment with something you care about. When something matters to you — when a friend genuinely needs you — you can stay up until 4 a.m. supporting them and still function the next day. That's not willpower. That's purpose providing fuel.

The inverse is also true. Chronic low energy, difficulty getting out of bed, trouble engaging with life — these often aren't signs that someone is doing too much. They're signs that what they're doing doesn't mean enough. When there's no sense of purpose animating the day, no feeling that what you're doing matters, the energy simply isn't there.

Common Pitfalls

Reflecting sounds like agreeing with false beliefs. It doesn't. When you say "it sounds like you're afraid you'll never find love again," you're not confirming that they won't find love. You're confirming that the fear is real and present. Those are completely different things. You can validate a feeling without endorsing a prediction.

Going silent feels awkward, so people fill the space. The urge to immediately follow up a reflection with more words — reassurance, a question, advice — is strong. Resist it. The silence is doing something. Let it.

Reflective listening gets mistaken for parroting. Robotically repeating someone's exact words back to them word-for-word is annoying and hollow. The skill is in capturing the emotional core of what they said and reflecting that back in your own words. "You said you're scared you'll be alone" feels cold. "It sounds like you're really afraid no one's going to want you" lands differently — it shows you actually heard them.

Conflating compassion fatigue with the fatigue of inauthenticity. If being emotionally present for people exhausts you, it's worth asking what exactly is tiring you out. Is it the genuine connection? Or is it the performance of connection — the smile you're forcing, the advice you don't believe, the act of seeming okay when you're not? Those are very different problems with very different solutions.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice the reflection formula. Next time someone shares a problem, respond with "It sounds like you're feeling ____" and fill in the blank with the emotion underneath what they said. Then stop talking.

  2. Sit in the silence. After a reflection, count silently to five before you say anything else. Notice the urge to break the silence with reassurance — and don't give in to it.

  3. Catch yourself reassuring. For one week, notice every time you're about to say "it's going to be okay" or "don't worry" or "you'll be fine." That's your cue to try a reflection instead.

  4. Review a past hard conversation. Think of a time someone came to you upset and the conversation felt flat or ended awkwardly. Ask yourself: did you spend most of it reassuring them or solving their problem? What might have happened if you'd just reflected their feelings back?

  5. Examine your own energy. If you consistently feel low energy or disengaged, take a few minutes to write down what you did this past week that felt genuinely meaningful — where you gave something and felt more alive rather than depleted. Compare that to what drained you. What does the contrast tell you about alignment?
