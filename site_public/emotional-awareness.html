That protective adaptation helps a kid survive, but it doesn't switch off when the danger passes. Understanding exactly what got unplugged — and why — is the first step to plugging it back in.

Why the Brain Shuts Down Emotions in the First Place

Emotions exist for good reasons. AnxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → warns you of danger. Sadness signals to others that you need support. Anger mobilizes you to protect your boundaries. In a healthy environment, expressing these feelings produces real results.

In an abusive or neglectful home, the opposite happens. A child cries and instead of comfort, gets punishment. A child gets angry and instead of being heard, gets hurt worse. The emotional system keeps firing signals that produce no useful outcome — or actively backfire. Since a young child can't rationalize ("my parent is struggling, this isn't about me") the way an adult can, the only option left is to stop feeling altogether.

This shutdown process runs in stages:

  1. Emotional suppression — actively pushing feelings down, stuffing them away
  2. Emotional numbness — the brain automates the suppression; feelings stop arriving
  3. At the time, this is a genuine adaptation. The emotions of severe abuse are overwhelming. Walling them off is how the system protects itself. The problem is that the wall doesn't come down on its own.

What Emotions Actually Do (And What You Lose Without Them)

Once the emotional system goes dark, you lose two things emotions were quietly doing for you all along.

1. Information

Emotions process data faster than conscious thought. Walking into a room and instantly sensing that something is wrong before anyone says a word — that's your emotional system scanning and reporting. The gut feeling that a colleague isn't trustworthy, the subtle unease in a relationship you can't quite explain — these are your brain feeding you real intelligence in the form of feelings.

When you're numb, that feed cuts out. Life starts to feel like you're flying blind.

2. MotivationMotivation is not a feeling, a desire, or a behavior — it is the ability to hold one thought steadily in your mind for a prolonged period of time.Full definition →

Emotions don't just tell you what's happening; they give you the energy to do something about it. Anger, specifically, is what allows you to enforce boundaries and protect yourself. A person who was punished as a child every time they got angry learns: anger makes things worse. The anger circuit gets unplugged. When that child becomes an adult and someone mistreats them, the circuit is still off. They can see, analytically, that they're being mistreated — but they can't feel the anger that would normally mobilize them to act. This is a direct neurological explanation for why people with childhood abuse histories so often end up in abusive adult relationships. It isn't weakness or stupidity. The protective system is simply still running the old code.

Suppressing emotions doesn't make them disappear. You lose the ability to articulate feelings. This matters more than it sounds. Freud noticed that when patients could finally put words to something emotionally charged, their symptoms improved. Language appears to metabolize emotion in some way — it calms the charge. Flow state and productivity break down. Optimal performance — the focused, energized state researchers call flow — requires the left and right hemispheres working together. You need analytical focus and emotional engagement simultaneously. When a task is too easy, you feel bored (emotional signal). When it's too hard, you feel anxious (emotional signal). Reconnecting: Starting with Language

Reversing hemispheric lateralizationThe corpus callosum is the band of neural tissue sitting between the brain's two hemispheres that allows the emotional right brain and the analytical, language-dominant left brain to communicate with each other.Full definition → starts with articulation. Naming an emotion begins to restore the connection between the feeling (right hemisphere) and the language that can work with it (left hemisphere).

This is harder than it sounds for someone who has been numb for years. A useful tool is a feelings wheel — a visual chart of emotions arranged in categories. Many people who genuinely cannot answer "what are you feeling?" can point to a word on a chart and recognize it immediately. That recognition is the beginning.

One signal worth watching: defensive neutral language about painful experiences. Saying "my parents did the best they could" for months without ever saying they did well is a kind of emotional protection — avoiding the grief and anger underneath by staying in the analytical lane. Being able to finally say "they didn't do a great job, and that hurt me" is not an act of disloyalty. It's the moment something starts to move.

Reconnecting: Working Through the Body

Emotions don't only live in the mind. They live in the body — and this opens a second pathway back.

If you're too numb to access feelings directly, start with physical sensation. Ask yourself: - Is there tightness in my chest? - Is there a clenching in my throat? - Do I feel butterflies in my stomach? - Is there tension in my shoulders or jaw?

Research has mapped which physical sensations tend to correspond to which emotions. Recognizing the body pattern can identify the feeling even when the feeling itself isn't consciously available.

More importantly, working with the physical sensation seems to offer real emotional relief. Breathing deliberately into chest tightness, expanding it, releasing — this isn't just relaxation technique. It appears to process some of what's stored there.

Mind-body practices with growing research support include: - Yoga and tai chi — sustained, movement-based practices that reconnect body awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → with emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that promote adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → - Exercise generally - EFT (Emotional Freedom Technique / tapping) — once dismissed as pseudoscience, now supported by some randomized controlled trials; involves tapping specific points on the body to release stored emotional charge - Massage — physical contact as a route to emotional processing

The general principle: if you want emotional health, you have to work on the body, because emotions are partly stored there.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking numbness means healing. Not feeling pain is not the same as being okay. The absence of felt emotion and the absence of emotional activity are completely different things. The emotions are still there, driving behavior — you just can't see them.

Believing that talking about emotions is pointless or weak. This is almost always a conclusion drawn from a childhood where emotional expression made things worse. It felt true then. It's not accurate now, and it has measurable costs.

Confusing analytical understanding with emotional processing. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Use a feelings wheel daily. Once a day — ideally at the same time — pull up a feelings wheel (easily found online) and point to what you're experiencing. Don't try to reason it out. Just look, scan, and pick. Name it out loud or write it down.

  2. Do a body scan when you feel stuck or off. Before reaching for your phone, a drink, or anything else to manage discomfort, pause and ask: Where do I feel this in my body? Chest? Gut? Throat? Sit with the sensation for 60 seconds without trying to fix it.

  3. Breathe into physical tension. If you notice tightness anywhere — chest especially — take a slow breath directed into that area, let it expand, and exhale fully. Repeat three to five times. Notice whether the sensation shifts.

  4. Audit your neutral language. Think about how you describe your most painful experiences — childhood, relationships, failures. Are you always analytical? Always fair to the other person? See if there's a feeling underneath the fairness that never gets named.

  5. Add one mind-body practice to your week. It doesn't have to be elaborate — a 20-minute yoga video, a long walk with attention on physical sensation, or a short tai chi routine. The goal is building the habitBehavior without thought — something you do automatically, requiring no motivation, willpower, or discipline to execute.Full definition → of body awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, not achieving any particular outcome.

  6. Write or speak one true thing you've been protecting someone from. This could be in a journal, with a therapist, or even out loud alone. Something you usually soften. See what shifts when you say it plainly.
