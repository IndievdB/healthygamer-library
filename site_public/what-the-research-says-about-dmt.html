Summary

TL;DR — DMT reliably produces encounters with "otherworldly beings" and other-world experiences in nearly every user, and that pattern is worth taking seriously — but the honest interpretation sits somewhere between pure neurobiology and uncritical mysticism.

This talk walks through a naturalistic field study on DMT experiences, uses it as a springboard to examine what the brain actually does when it "perceives reality," and draws a careful distinction between genuine contemplative insight and the romanticized version that gets sold online. Along the way it introduces a first step toward accessing unusual states through meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → rather than substances.

What the Research Actually Found

The study in question — An Encounter with the Other: A Thematic and Content Analysis of DMT Experiences from a Naturalistic Field Study — interviewed 36 experienced DMT users in depth after their experiences, observing them in real-world (non-clinical) settings rather than a lab.

Two findings stood out:

  • 94% of participants (34 of 36) reported encountering apparent non-human beings during their experience.
  • 100% of participants described entering or emerging into what felt like other worlds.

Understanding why the naturalistic design matters here: a randomized controlled trial controls variables tightly but often produces results that don't hold up in the real world. Naturalistic studies sacrifice that control in exchange for ecological validity — they mirror what actually happens when real people use a substance in real conditions. The bipolar medication quetiapine is a good parallel: in controlled trials it looks highly effective; in practice, side effects like weight gain and cognitive fog cause most people to stop taking it. The finding about DMT is robust precisely because it came from naturalistic observation — these weren't people in a clinical setting being primed for a particular experience.

The near-universal consistency of these reports — across different people, settings, and backgrounds — is the puzzle worth sitting with.

The Brain as an Interpreter, Not a Window

Before jumping to conclusions about what DMT encounters mean, it helps to get clear on what the brain actually does with any perception.

The brain doesn't give you reality directly. It takes raw sensory data and constructs a representation. A few examples that make this concrete:

  • Color is a wavelength of light; the experience of "red" is your brain's interpretation of that wavelength, not the wavelength itself.
  • A solid table is mostly empty space — electrons in orbits. The feeling of resistance when you push on it is electromagnetic force, not matter hitting matter.
  • Phantom limb pain is one of the clearest demonstrations: the limb is gone, there are no nerves there, and yet pain signals are generated and felt. The somatosensory cortex still has neurons mapped to that limb and sometimes fires them. Neurologist Ramachandran famously showed that placing the intact opposite hand in a mirror box — making it look like the missing limb — can eliminate phantom limb pain. Visual feedback alone changes a felt physical sensation.

This has a direct implication for how to think about DMT: the brain isn't just a passive receiver. It actively models the world, and those models can produce vivid, convincing experiences that don't correspond to anything "out there" in a verifiable sense. Cognitive distortions work the same way — a suicidal person who believes their family would be better off without them is experiencing a brain-generated model that is factually wrong, yet it feels completely real from the inside.

"What we see is not actually real. The mind does not produce an accurate representation of reality."

So Are the Beings Real? The Honest Answer

There are two clean interpretations, and neither fully satisfies.

The neurobiological hypothesis says DMT directly stimulates receptors (primarily the serotoninA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition → 5-HT2A receptor) in a way that generates artificial experiences. The beings are a product of a brain temporarily malfunctioning — vivid, but no more real than the pain in a missing hand. On this view, there's nothing "there"; it's a phantom.

The telescope hypothesis is a more interesting alternative: maybe the substance is an instrument, like a telescope, that allows the brain to detect something genuinely present — something that exists at some level or in some form we don't have good language for — which the brain then interprets using its pattern-matching machinery and calls "beings" because that's the nearest category it has.

The telescope analogy is worth unpacking. When you look at the moon through a telescope, the moon appears right in front of your face, even though it's hundreds of thousands of miles away. The instrument distorts the presentation of something real. The moon is genuinely there. The key reason we trust this is conservation of experience — anyone who looks through the telescope sees the moon. That shared, reproducible experience is roughly how we construct what we call objective reality.

DMT experiences show something like this pattern too. The near-universal consistency — 94% encountering beings, 100% entering other worlds — at minimum demands an explanation beyond "random brain noise."

"Is this like a tool that we can use — does it represent reality, like the telescope looking at the moon? Or is it just a phantom limb?"

The honest position is: we don't know. There's no verified, reproducible external signal from these beings. But dismissing the experiences entirely because we lack that evidence may be too quick.

What Meditation Reveals (Without the Drug)

Substances activate neural circuitry that already exists in your brain — they don't build new circuitry. Opioids activate mu receptors. THC activates cannabinoid receptors. DMT activates 5-HT2A receptors. All of these receptor systems are there in every human brain, operating all the time.

This raises a question: can you reach approximate versions of these states without the substance?

Meditation traditions have claimed this for at least 5,000 years. Scientific study of meditation has been around for maybe 30–40 years — a fraction of that. Practitioners across many traditions consistently report unusual experiences along the path of serious practice: out-of-body states, encounters with presences, profound perceptual shifts. These reports have been quietly stripped out of the mainstream, secular wellness version of meditation. Apps and YouTube videos teach you how to reduce cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition →; nobody teaches you what the tradition was actually pointing toward, which is enlightenment.

The meditation version of these experiences tends to be much less dramatic than the drug version — and that's actually worth noting. Where a DMT experience might produce overwhelming, cinematic encounters, the meditative analog is quieter:

"It feels like really normal. It's hard to describe. It's sort of like gravity — you understand it, you account for it, but it's not anything magnificent on a day-to-day basis."

Understanding the "rhythms of the universe" through meditation, for instance, isn't a revelation that arrives in neon lights. It's more like acquiring knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → of gravity — once you have it, you just know how things fall.

Common Pitfalls

Romanticizing the experience before having it. A lot of the people who loudly advocate for spiritual experiences, past lives, or elevated consciousness haven't actually developed a genuine contemplative practice. Something mild happens, they amplify it heavily, and call it transformation. Most of what gets described in online spiritual communities is probably in this category.

Quantum mysticism and borrowed scientific language. Using terms like "vibrations," "dimensions," or "quantum" to dress up intuitions that aren't grounded in either actual physics or honest inner experience is a real trap — and an easy one to fall into. Sounding scientific isn't the same as being accurate.

Dismissing the data because it's uncomfortable. The opposite error is equally bad: ignoring consistent, cross-cultural reports of anomalous experience simply because they don't fit a tidy materialist framework. Decades of meditation traditions reporting similar things can't all be handwaved away just because we don't have a clean scientific mechanism yet.

Conflating "the brain produced it" with "therefore it isn't real." All of your experiences are mediated by your brain. That doesn't automatically mean none of them refer to anything real outside your skull.

Seeking dramatic experiences as a goal. The drug version of these experiences is much more intense and "sexy" than what meditation produces. If you're using psychedelics or pursuing meditation specifically to get a dramatic experience, you're likely to either overshoot and be overwhelmed, or manufacture experiences rather than discovering them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start a basic meditation practice, even five minutes a day. You can't evaluate what happens in deep practice without building the foundation first. Consistency over months matters more than long sessions.

  2. Read Phantoms in the Brain by V.S. Ramachandran. It's one of the most accessible books on how the brain constructs reality, and it gives you a grounded framework for thinking about perception, illusion, and experience before you approach weirder territory.

  3. Read William James's The Varieties of Religious Experience. It's old but serious — James takes anomalous human experiences seriously without abandoning critical thinking. It provides useful vocabulary for distinguishing genuine spiritual experience from delusion.

  4. Notice one cognitive distortion this week. Pick something you believe strongly about yourself or your situation. Ask whether anyone close to you sees it differently. If they do, sit with the question: which representation is your brain constructing, and why? This builds the basic epistemic humility the rest of this territory requires.

  5. When you encounter claims about spiritual experiences — your own or others' — apply the telescope test. Ask: is this more like a phantom limb (the brain generating something with no external referent) or more like a telescope (a tool revealing something real that's just hard to see directly)? You won't always know the answer, but asking the question keeps you honest.

  6. Resist the urge to narrativize unusual experiences immediately. If you have a strange meditative experience or a vivid dream or a moment of unusual clarity, sit with it before reaching for an explanation. The explanation you reach for first will usually be the most culturally available one — not necessarily the most accurate one.
