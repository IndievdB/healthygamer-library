Summary

TL;DR — The world genuinely demands more than it used to, and struggling to keep up isn't a personal failure. The path forward starts with rediscovering who you actually are under all the boxes you've been asked to check.

When someone is taking 20 hours to read a single astronomy chapter, working a job, doing laundry, cooking, grocery shopping, exercising, going to therapy, maintaining relationships, and still getting called irresponsible by their boss — something is wrong with the picture being painted. The instinct is to blame yourself. But that instinct deserves a closer look.

The World Actually Is Getting More Demanding

This isn't a feeling. It's a structural shift that has been building for decades.

The simplest way to see it is credential inflation. A generation ago, a high school diploma got you a decent job. Then you needed a bachelor's degree. Now you need a master's to be competitive for roles that pay $40k a year after six or seven years of school. Entry-level positions requiring two years of experience aren't a punchline — they're the norm. The bar keeps rising not because the work itself changed, but because of mass scale.

When an employer receives a thousand applications instead of fifty, they can't evaluate people individually. They use proxies — degrees, certifications, credentials — to filter the pile. The result is that qualifications get inflated over time, not because they reflect real skill thresholds, but because abundance forces sorting. Online dating works the same way. A checklist of requirements that would have seemed absurd when you only met people through social circles becomes standard when you're swiping through hundreds of profiles.

Add to this: deadlines are faster than ever (a rush job used to be two days; now it's fifteen minutes), social media has measurably worsened mental health across the population, and the self-care required just to stay functional — therapy, exercise, meditation, sleep hygiene — is a response to those pressures, not a luxury. Aboriginal societies didn't need to meditate to manage their stress. That need is a product of the environment, not a sign of weakness.

"The world demands too much of us. That's because every year that goes by, there's going to be one more box you've got to check."

How You Lose Yourself in the Process

The dangerous part isn't just exhaustion. It's what happens when you spend years trying to fit yourself into what other people want.

You check boxes to make your boss happy. You check boxes to qualify for the job. You check boxes to be dateable. You check boxes to seem responsible to your parents. And gradually, the voice inside you that actually knows what you want — the one that says I don't want to apply for that promotion or I'm in college to draw and I haven't drawn in months — gets quieter and quieter.

Sometimes you even succeed. You land the job, the relationship, the income. And you still feel empty, or trapped, or exhausted in a way you're not allowed to name because people keep telling you how lucky you are. That's not a mystery. That's what happens when you've built a life around other people's checklists and lost the thread of your own.

The Two Arrows

There's a concept from Buddhist psychology worth sitting with here: the two arrows.

Life fires an arrow at you — a hard circumstance, a setback, something genuinely outside your control. That's the first arrow, and it hurts. But then, almost automatically, you fire a second arrow at yourself: Why couldn't I handle it? What's wrong with me? My boss managed fine, why can't I?

The first arrow was the situation. The second arrow is the self-blame piled on top of it.

In gaming terms, this maps onto throwing versus tilting. Throwing is making a mistake — maybe unavoidable, maybe just bad luck. Tilting is the spiral that follows the mistake, which usually does far more damage than the original error. What actually loses the game isn't the throw. It's the tilt.

"What screws you is not the throw. It's the tilt."

This is why acknowledging that things are genuinely hard matters. It's not resignation or excuse-making. It's preventing the second arrow. When your boss says "I worked through college just fine," and you feel less than in response — that's a second arrow landing. But if you can hold the awareness that the world they graduated into was materially different from yours, the comparison stops being a verdict on your worth. You're not less than them. You're operating in a harder environment.

Acknowledging reality doesn't make you passive. It stops you from wasting energy beating yourself up for circumstances you didn't create.

Rediscovering Who You Are

Reconnecting with yourself starts with noticing a very small thing: the quiet voice.

Throughout your day, there will be moments where something inside you says I don't want this. The whole world might be pointing you toward applying for the promotion, and there's this faint signal underneath the noise that says no. That voice has often been suppressed for years. It doesn't mean you should always act on it immediately — but you should at least listen to it. Ask it questions. What's actually going on here? What does this resistance mean?

The second step is examining your goals. Write them down. For each one, ask where it actually came from. Dig past the surface answer of "I want this" and ask: Is this goal about what I want, or about what I want other people to think of me?

A useful test: Is this goal about avoiding someone else's judgment? Does it start with I don't want my boss to think... or I don't want my friends to see me as...? Some goals will survive this scrutiny and turn out to be genuinely yours. Others will reveal themselves as borrowed standards. Neither category is shameful — but knowing the difference matters.

The person who sparked this whole conversation knew, on some level, exactly what was theirs: I have no time to draw, which is what I'm going to college for in the first place. That sentence is the thread. That's the voice worth listening to.

This isn't an argument for abandoning responsibilities. You still have to do the astronomy, write the notes, show up to work. But there's a wide spectrum between hedonism and a life so thoroughly shaped by others' demands that nothing in it belongs to you. The goal is to move toward the middle — where you're meeting your obligations and staying connected to what actually matters to you.

Common Pitfalls

Treating the acknowledgment as giving up. Recognizing that the world is objectively harder is not the same as accepting defeat. It's removing the second arrow so you can focus on what you can actually change.

Assuming all your goals are yours. Most people believe their goals come from themselves because they experience them from the inside. The question isn't whether you want the thing — it's why you want it, and whether that why would survive if no one were watching.

Thinking success is binary. The question "why try if I can't succeed?" assumes success is a single moment with a fixed deadline. The attempt itself builds skill, changes what you're capable of next time, and carries its own value. The people who end up succeeding tend to be the ones who were okay with failing — who didn't need to win in order to keep playing.

Using the world's demands as a fixed ceiling. Acknowledging that things are hard is the beginning, not the end. You still have agency over the tilt, over which goals you pursue, and over how much of your life you spend trying to satisfy checklists that were never yours.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Listen to the quiet voice. For one week, whenever you feel a small resistance — a reluctance, an I don't really want this — pause and write it down. Don't act on it immediately. Just notice it and ask: What is this telling me?

  2. Audit your goals. Write down every goal you're currently working toward. For each one, write a sentence about where it came from. Then honestly ask: Is any part of this goal about what someone else thinks of me? Mark those. This isn't about deleting the goal — it's about seeing it clearly.

  3. Name the throw vs. the tilt. When something goes wrong, practice separating the two. The throw is the event itself — what actually happened. The tilt is everything you've piled on top of it in your own head. Write both down. Then ask yourself: what can I actually control from here?

  4. Find the one thing that's actually yours. Look for the equivalent of "I haven't had time to draw." What activity, interest, or way of spending time do you keep sacrificing for obligations? Build even a small amount of it back in — not as a reward, but as a baseline.

  5. Reframe the credential gap. When someone older implies you should be able to handle what they handled, take a moment to genuinely list two or three ways the landscape has changed since they were in your position. This isn't about building resentment. It's about not firing the second arrow at yourself.
