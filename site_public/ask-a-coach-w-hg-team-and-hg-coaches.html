Summary

TL;DR — Coaching isn't therapy, and it isn't advice-giving either. It's a structured, evidence-based process of helping you understand yourself and build the life you actually want — and this talk gives you a clear look at how it works, straight from the coaches themselves.

Four Healthy Gamer coaches — Pax, Julia, Alan, and B — answer questions about what coaching is, how it's structured, how it handles common problems like fear of judgment and relationship triggers, and how one-on-one and group coaching differ. The format is conversational and honest, including the things coaching can't do.

What Coaching Actually Is (And Isn't)

The gym trainer analogy is the cleanest way to understand it: doctors treat illness, and physical trainers help healthy people perform better. Coaching sits in the same relationship to therapy. Therapists treat clinical conditions; coaches work with you to understand yourself, build skills, find purpose, and operate better in the world — without diagnosing or treating anything.

That distinction matters practically. Coaching is:

  • Non-clinical — no diagnosis, no pathology focus, no treatment of illness
  • Collaborative — the coach doesn't solve your puzzle for you; you solve it together
  • Goal-oriented but flexible — goals are a compass pointing north, not a GPS with turn-by-turn directions
  • Phased — there's an emotional/understanding phase, an action phase, and an integrative phase, each building on the last

One coach described the role using Lord of the Rings: "I'm the Sam to your Frodo. I cannot carry the ring for you, but I can be here and support you while you carry it — and if you're having difficulties, I'm going to be there and curse at the ring with you."

The OverWatch VOD review analogy is equally useful. A game coach doesn't play for you. They ask you to narrate your thought process, then work through that — your assumptions, your patterns, your blind spots — not your moment-to-moment mechanics. HG coaching works the same way.

How Goals Actually Evolve in Coaching

Most people come into their first session with a vague sense that something's wrong, or with a very specific goal that turns out not to be the real one. Both are fine.

Goals often start as external benchmarks ("I want to pass this exam," "I want to stop procrastinating") and gradually reveal themselves to be about something more internal ("I want to feel competent," "I want to feel like I belong somewhere," "I don't even know who I am yet"). The coaching process surfaces that shift — not by debunking the original goal, but by asking what's important about this goal?

The most common pattern coaches describe: a client finishes coaching, the coach reads back the goals from session one, and the client says something like, "I didn't actually accomplish that — but I don't care anymore, because I accomplished something I didn't even know I needed." The first goal was often a disguise for the real one.

A few practical implications:

  • Come to each session with some intention — a question, something you want to understand, a direction you want to explore. You don't need a polished goal, just a lean.
  • Don't expect goals to stay fixed. Expect them to sharpen over time.
  • Journaling between sessions accelerates the process significantly.

Dealing With Triggers in Relationships

This is one of the most common issues people bring to coaching. A few important distinctions first:

Not all triggers are clinical traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →. When the word "triggered" is used casually — meaning you get activated, reactive, or flooded by something a partner says or does — that's a coaching-appropriate topic. When trauma rises to clinical severity (think: persistent, debilitating, organizing your life around avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →), a licensed professional should be the first call, not a coach. You don't need a formal PTSD diagnosis to cross that line; the threshold is whether the trauma is complex enough that exploring it without clinical support could do more harm than good.

Within the coaching lane, the approach centers on a few layers:

  1. Break down the chain of events. There's rarely a direct line from "something happened" to "I exploded." There's usually: the triggering event → an emotion → a reaction to that emotion → a behavior → a secondary emotional spiral (guilt, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, self-condemnation). Mapping that chain makes it workable.

  2. Sit with the pain rather than distract from it. Most people assume they know their own pain because it's theirs. But when did you last actually sit with it — no distraction, just observing where it lives and where it comes from? That clarity is what makes it possible to communicate to a partner.

  3. Tell the beast's name. If you have a pattern — say, compulsive urge to check a partner's phone because of past betrayal — naming it to your partner and letting them respond creates a different dynamic than acting on it secretly. The partner's response either shrinks the wound a little or doesn't. Either way, you're dealing with it in the open instead of alone.

  4. Both people in a triggered relationship suffer. It's easy to only think about the person getting triggered. But the partner also needs tools — patience, empathy, and a collaboratively agreed-upon plan for what their role is when a trigger fires. Coaching can work with either person individually.

What coaching won't do: work with both partners at once. That's couples counseling, and for good reason — a single coach can't be objective about two clients simultaneously. If the relationship is the primary issue, couples counseling is the better fit.

Fear of Being Judged: Why "Getting Rid Of It" Is the Wrong Goal

When someone asks how do I get rid of the fear of being judged, that framing itself is worth examining. Fear of judgment is part of being human. The goal isn't elimination — it's operation within the fear.

The coaching questions worth asking:

  • What does confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → actually look like to you? Does it mean not feeling scared, or does it mean moving forward while scared?
  • In what specific situations does the fear spike? What kind of judgment are you imagining — and how do you know that's what's happening?
  • Is the fear preventing you from doing things you actually want to do? What would it look like to pursue those things anyway?

Validation comes first: even without proof that anyone is actually judging you, the feeling is real and it's unpleasant. That gets acknowledged before anything else. Then the work moves toward understanding the pattern's origin, decreasing the emotional intensity over time, and eventually building the capacity to act despite the fear rather than waiting for it to disappear.

One trap: social perfectionism — the implicit belief that you should be universally liked, never negatively judged, and certain of that at all times. That standard can never be met, and coaching doesn't promise to meet it. The realistic outcome is a lower-intensity version of the fear that no longer stops you from doing what you want to do.

Group Coaching: What It Is and What Makes It Different

Group coaching is not cheaper one-on-one coaching. That framing will set you up for disappointment. It's a fundamentally different experience with its own benefits and its own demands.

The ideal group: seven or so strangers from different backgrounds, suffering in similar enough ways, learning to communicate openly and compassionately with each other — without advice-giving, without one person dominating, with everyone accountable to three valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →: presence, authenticity, and compassion.

What makes it work:

  • No real-life consequences. These people aren't your boss, your neighbor, or your partner. You can take social risks, have conflict, and experiment with communication in a way that isn't available in your regular relationships — without burning bridges. That safety is what enables genuine growth.
  • It mirrors real life. How you show up in group is probably how you show up outside of it. That means group becomes a live training ground for the actual relationships that matter.
  • Collective breakthroughs hit differently. When a room full of people is moved by the same thing simultaneously, something happens that can't be replicated in a one-on-one session.
  • Skills transfer outward. The communication and empathy skills you build in group don't stay in group. Clients regularly report using them in their most important relationships.

The demands:

  • You won't have the floor every week. If you feel a session was wasted because you didn't get to talk about your own situation, group probably isn't the right fit right now.
  • You need to genuinely want to be present for other people's struggles, not just your own.
  • It takes longer to ramp up. The first few sessions often feel disorienting. Patience matters.

The highest-functioning groups eventually become self-sustaining — members understand the principles well enough that the coach becomes optional. That's the goal.

Common Pitfalls

Treating coaching as a crisis service. Coaching meets once a week. If your house is on fire right now — academic probation, job on the line this week — coaching can try to help but can't be on-call triage. It's a sustained, longer-term process.

Expecting your goals to be fixed from session one. Goals are a compass, not a map. Treating them as rigid benchmarks you either pass or fail will make you miserable and miss the actual work.

Choosing group because it's cheaper. Group is more demanding in many ways, not less. Cheaper doesn't mean easier or more appropriate for your situation.

Trying to eliminate emotions rather than relate to them differently. Wanting to "get rid of" fear, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, or triggers is understandable but sets up the wrong target. The aim is reduced intensity and increased capacity to function alongside the feeling — not its disappearance.

Waiting for the coach to fix you. The coach doesn't fix anything. You do, with support. Clients who put in effort between sessions — journaling, reflecting, coming in with an intention — get significantly more out of the process.

Quitting after one session because the fit feels off. Rapport takes time to build. If something specific is bothering you, tell your coach. Most issues that feel like incompatibility are actually communication problems that can be resolved. If after honest effort it's still not working, switching is completely fine.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your actual goal underneath the stated goal. Write down whatever problem or goal you'd bring to a coach. Then ask: what would it mean to me if I achieved this? Keep asking that question until you hit something that feels more personal and internal. That's probably closer to the real target.

  2. Map your trigger chain. Pick a recurring situation where you react more strongly than you'd like. Write out the sequence: the event → the emotion → your reaction to the emotion → the behavior that followed → the secondary emotional spiral afterward. Just getting it on paper breaks the feeling that it's a single, instantaneous explosion.

  3. Sit with discomfort for five minutes without distraction. No phone, no music, nothing. Notice where the feeling lives in your body. Notice what thoughts arise. You don't have to solve anything — just observe. Do this once today.

  4. Reframe your relationship with fear of judgment. Instead of asking "how do I stop being afraid of being judged before I do X," ask "how do I do X while I'm afraid of being judged?" Write down one specific thing you've been avoiding because of fear of judgment, and one small version of it you could attempt this week.

  5. Before your next coaching session (or any important conversation), set one intention. Not a goal — an intention. Something like: "I want to understand why this pattern keeps coming up," or "I want to figure out what I actually want here." Walking in with that intention is the single biggest lever you have over how useful the session will be.

  6. If you're the partner of someone who gets triggered, make a plan together. In a calm moment (not during a trigger), ask your partner what they'd want from you when they're triggered. Agree on a role. Write it down. Having a pre-agreed response removes the improvisation pressure in the moment.

  7. Experiment socially in a low-stakes environment. If fear of judgment or social anxiety is a concern, group coaching exists precisely as a training ground. But outside of coaching, identify one low-stakes social situation this week — a comment in a meeting, a message to an acquaintance — where you can try something you'd normally hold back. Treat it as an experiment, not a test you can fail.
