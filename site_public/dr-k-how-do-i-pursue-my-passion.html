Summary

TL;DR — Making the leap into your passion is necessary but not sufficient. The artists who can actually pay their bills aren't just better at their craft; they maintain a minimum competence in a whole set of things that have nothing to do with making art.

Most people who dream of pursuing their passion never take the plunge. Pierre did — he cut his income, let relationships go, and is grinding eight to nine hours a day on his webtoon project. That kind of commitment is genuinely rare. But after talking through his situation, a more practical problem comes into focus: Pierre is working in isolation, spending almost all his energy on the craft itself, and has almost no visibility into the business side of his industry. The internal question he's carrying — am I doing enough? should I keep going? — can't actually be answered from inside the studio.

You've Already Cleared the Hardest Hurdle (But There's Another One)

Most people fantasize about making sacrifices for their art. Pierre actually made them. That deserves acknowledgment, and it changes what the real problem is.

Once you've jumped off the cliff, the question stops being should I jump? and becomes how do I land as well as possible?

The artists who are financially successful — not necessarily the ones making the best art, but the ones who can pay their bills and build an audience — aren't just craftspeople. They have a minimum competence across multiple dimensions beyond the work itself:

  • Social media presence
  • Networking and relationship-building
  • Understanding what their audience actually wants
  • Basic business thinking: who would pay for this, and how do I reach them?
  • Personal discipline: consistent sleep, self-care, showing up reliably

Being devoted entirely to your craft and ignoring all of this is like being the most brilliant doctor in the world but failing to keep up with your documentation and administrative requirements. The medicine is only part of the job. The same is true for art.

The Isolation Problem

Pierre's biggest practical issue isn't his confidence, his mental state, or even his art quality. It's that he has been working like a studio hermit — 70-hour weeks, almost no conversation with the people around him who actually know how this industry works.

He has access to a private Discord server full of working artists who are making a living in his field. He has never asked any of them how they did it.

"You're working in a studio for 70 hours a week without talking to anyone."

That's the diagnosis. All the internal anxiety — am I doing enough, is this going to work, what happens if I fail — partly feeds on the absence of real information. You can't answer the question "am I on the right track?" from inside your own head. You answer it by talking to people who've been where you are.

The people around you in professional networks aren't just moral support. They're a data source. How long did it take them to get their first finished piece published? How do they balance creating versus taking freelance gigs to pay bills? What are editors in this space actually looking for right now?

Authenticity Is Not a Disadvantage on Social Media

Pierre resisted the idea of building a social media presence because the people he saw doing it seemed fake — performing trends, playing a game he couldn't stomach. This is a real and understandable reaction. But it contains a hidden assumption that needs to be challenged.

You don't have to be fake to have a presence. And audiences are increasingly tired of fake.

The story Pierre shared in this conversation — sacrificing relationships, grinding alone, sitting with deep uncertainty about whether any of it will pay off — is exactly the kind of thing people would want to follow. There's a hunger for authenticity, for watching someone navigate a real struggle in real time. That is content. You don't have to manufacture a persona to be on social media; you can just be honest about what you're going through.

The Problem of Premature Planning

Pierre is also burning mental energy worrying about what happens if August 2023 comes and nothing has worked. That worry is understandable, but it's also premature in a specific way: what you should do in August 2023 depends entirely on where you actually are in August 2023.

You might arrive at that date with: - Zero patrons - Four patrons - A hundred patrons - An editor who says "we love it but it needs to be adjusted for this audience" - An editor who offers a contract - A contest win that opens five doors at once

Each of those situations calls for a completely different next step. Trying to plan for failure now, from here, is solving a problem you don't have the information to solve yet. In medicine, you don't decide on treatment before you get the test results.

"What happens in 2023 depends on what happens in 2023."

That doesn't mean the uncertainty isn't hard to live with — it is. But the answer to that uncertainty is not more planning in isolation. It's getting better information now, while you still have time to act on it.

The KPI Problem (You Can't Steer Without Feedback)

There's a business concept called a Key Performance Indicator — a concrete, measurable signal that tells you whether you're on track. Right now, Pierre doesn't have one. He knows he's working hard. He doesn't know if the work is moving him toward success or just keeping him busy.

This is the difference between diagnosis and treatment. Pierre keeps asking "what's the right move?" — but that's a treatment question. The diagnosis question is: what does success in my specific industry actually require, and how do I know if I'm making progress toward it?

Without talking to people in the industry, without understanding what editors want, without knowing how comparable artists built their audiences, you're steering blind. Grinding harder doesn't fix a navigation problem.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking sacrifice for strategy. Giving everything up for your art is meaningful and necessary — but it doesn't guarantee anything on its own. A lot of artists make this mistake: treating the sacrifice itself as the work, when the work is also everything that comes after.
  • Assuming social media requires inauthenticity. Seeing performative, trend-chasing creators and concluding "I can't do that" is valid — but it also means opting out of a whole channel because of a false dichotomy. There is space for honest, low-performance presence.
  • Solving problems before you have the data. Worrying about what you'll do if it fails before you've gathered any real feedback is anxiety pretending to be planning. It costs energy without producing anything useful.
  • Interpreting unfamiliarity as self-sabotage. Pierre wondered if he was sabotaging himself by avoiding the business side. The more accurate frame: he just hasn't done it before. Fear of the unknown is normal, not pathological. You're not broken — you're just new to this.
  • Working in isolation and calling it focus. Cutting yourself off from your professional community to protect your creative time is a trap. Those people are not a distraction; they are the map.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Have one informational conversation this week. Pick someone in your Discord, your network, or your industry who is making a living from their art. Ask them: how long did it take you to get your first paid work? How do you balance creating with everything else? What does your week actually look like? You don't need to broadcast anything — just listen.

  2. Identify your KPIs. Write down two or three concrete, measurable things that would tell you you're on track six months from now. Not "be successful" — something specific, like a number of followers, a number of submissions sent to editors, a response from one publisher. If you can't define what progress looks like, you won't recognize it when it's happening.

  3. Ask your community about the business, not just the craft. Specifically ask: how do you make ends meet? What's your mix of passion projects versus paid work? How did you get in front of the editors or platforms you're working with now? Mine the people you already have access to.

  4. Start building social presence now, not later. Don't wait until the project is finished to plant the seed. Post something small and honest this week — not a polished announcement, just a glimpse of the actual process. You have a story people would follow.

  5. Stop trying to solve August 2023 from today. When the anxiety about "what if it fails" comes up, notice it, let it be there, and redirect your attention to what you can actually do this week. The situation at your deadline will tell you what to do at your deadline. Right now, your job is to gather information and take small consistent steps.

  6. Look up resources from working professionals in adjacent fields. Brandon Sanderson, for example, runs a podcast where successful creators talk about the practical side of turning craft into a career — the stuff nobody romanticizes but everyone who makes it has figured out.
