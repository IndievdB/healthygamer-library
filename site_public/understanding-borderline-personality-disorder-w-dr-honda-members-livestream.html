Summary

TL;DR — Borderline personality disorder (BPD) is not a disorder of random emotional chaos — it is a deeply logical response to early abandonment and betrayal trauma, built on a perceptual distortion that those experiences will repeat forever.

This is a clinical lecture by Dr. Kirk Honda (therapist, professor, and host of the Psychology in Seattle podcast) covering the lived inner experience of BPD, how it develops, how it presents in relationships and therapy, and what genuine recovery looks like. The framing throughout is empathic and mechanistic: once you understand the core wound, every "strange" behavior snaps into place.

The Core of BPD Is Not Instability — It's Constant Pain

The DSM describes BPD as a "pervasive pattern of instability" in relationships, self-image, and emotion. That framing is incomplete, and it misleads clinicians and laypeople alike. What looks like random emotional instability from the outside is, from the inside, a person in constant emotional pain who is white-knuckling their way through ordinary moments.

Think of it this way: the worst moments of a mentally healthy person's life — the first days after a devastating breakup, when intrusive shame is relentless — are roughly equivalent to the best moments for someone with untreated BPD. They are never at a "one out of ten" on the pain scale. They fluctuate between a four and a ten, but zero is not available to them.

When the pain spills over and they react strongly, it looks to outsiders like a sudden irrational outburst. It is neither sudden nor irrational. It is the surface of a sea that has always been churning.

The Emptiness That Can't Be Named

A second core feature — one people with BPD frequently arrive at independently, using the same word — is emptiness. This is not sadness or loneliness. It is the absence of a felt sense of self.

Under normal developmental circumstances, a caregiver helps a young child build the neural connections that let them identify their own internal states: Am I hungry? Bored? Angry? Sad? That constant mirroring — a parent noticing, naming, and responding — is not just emotional support. It is literally constructing the architecture through which a person will later know themselves.

When that mirroring is absent or severely disrupted, the child grows up without a reliable connection to their own inner life. As adults, when they stop and look inward for a sense of who they are or what they need, they find nothing — not darkness exactly, but the absence of something that should be there. It is terrifying. It produces a feeling of not quite existing.

"You're staring through a doorway into a darkness and you think there's nothing there — but there is something there. You just can't see it."

Recovery involves slowly turning up a dimmer switch. After months or years of therapy, a client might report that for the first time, when they asked themselves "what do I need?", something came back. That moment is a milestone.

How Abandonment Trauma Rewires Perception

The core trauma of BPD is abandonment — but that word needs unpacking. A child does not need to be literally left behind. Abandonment can happen:

  • When a parent is chronically depressed, preoccupied, or overworked
  • When abuse is sporadic, which the child experiences as unpredictable rejection
  • When emotional attunement is simply never offered

What makes BPD distinct is how early and thoroughly this abandonment is absorbed. It does not become a bad memory. It becomes a perceptual filter — an operating assumption that abandonment and betrayal are inherent to the world and will happen again. Every subsequent relationship is interpreted through that lens.

This is why the DSM criterion about "frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment" is the most important one. The imagined part is doing enormous work. People with BPD routinely perceive abandonment in situations where it is not occurring — a partner who comes home late, a therapist who seems slightly distracted, a friend who doesn't text back immediately. These register not as minor inconveniences but as confirmation of a lifelong truth: I am alone and about to be left again.

One clinical example: a client became absolutely convinced, over the course of months and then years, that during a brief phone call her therapist had been dismissive and contemptuous toward her. He had not. He had been calm and attentive. But her certainty was complete. This is not psychosis — it is what some early theorists called being "borderline" psychotic, meaning that the delusional certainty is confined to the relational domain, specifically around abandonment and betrayal.

Idealization, Devaluation, and What "Love Bombing" Actually Is

The idealization/devaluation cycle — sometimes called splitting — follows directly from this perceptual distortion combined with the emotional needs of a very young child that were never met.

When someone with BPD meets a person who seems stable and safe, something enormous gets activated: maybe this is finally the one who won't leave. The warmth and intensity they pour toward that person — what online culture calls love bombing — is not usually a calculated manipulation tactic. It is the expression of a need that is developmentally around 18 months old, coming through an adult body. An 18-month-old who feels loved loves you completely and unconditionally. That same totality of feeling is present here.

Then any perceived sign of withdrawal — however small — flips the switch. The same black-and-white thinking that produced idealization now produces devaluation: you are all bad, you have betrayed me, everything good was an illusion. This is identical to how a toddler who has been told it's bedtime suddenly regards a parent who was beloved five minutes ago as the enemy.

"People with borderline are not randomly angry. They're angry because they feel hurt. They feel hurt because they are distorting their perceptions."

From the outside, this looks like gaslighting and manipulation. In most cases it is neither — it is a genuine perceptual reality producing genuine emotions producing behaviors that make no sense without the underlying context.

Attachment Theory: Why the Nervous System Is Wired This Way

The intensity of BPD emotion is not purely psychological — it is neurological. Attachment research describes roughly two adaptive responses to inadequate parenting:

Dismissive/avoidant pattern: When a caregiver is consistently unresponsive, the child learns to attenuate need and emotion. If my signals won't be heard, it costs me to send them. Over time, the neural connections to internal emotional states become muted. These people tend to feel and display less emotion and encode fewer memories — not because they have no feelings, but because the system learned to dampen the signal.

Anxious/preoccupied pattern: When a caregiver is erratically available — sometimes warm, sometimes neglectful or abusive — the child learns the opposite strategy. If I signal louder and more intensely, I may squeeze more attunement out of this unpredictable system. The neural connections to emotion become stronger, faster, and more reactive. This is the pattern most associated with BPD.

The child who learned to cry harder and cling tighter to get needs met does not unlearn that wiring at 18. At 35, the same system fires — instantly and at full intensity — at any cue that resembles what used to precede abandonment.

The Schema Therapy Framework: Three Ways of Coping with a Broken Assumption

Schema therapy offers a clean model for understanding the behavioral patterns that follow from the core BPD belief: abandonment and betrayal are inevitable.

When a core belief this painful is established in childhood, three coping strategies emerge:

  1. Surrender — Accept the schema as truth and live accordingly. Unconsciously seek out partners who will abandon or betray, sabotage good relationships before they can fail "naturally," or manufacture evidence of betrayal when none exists.

  2. Avoidance — Stay out of close relationships entirely so the abandonment can never happen.

  3. Overcompensation — Fight the schema by controlling the environment. Become intensely vigilant about any sign of departure. Attempt to prevent abandonment through monitoring, anger, ultimatums, or coercion.

The third pattern is where behaviors that look "abusive" or "controlling" emerge — not from malice, but from terror. None of these three strategies actually resolve the core pain. They are all adaptations to a wound, not healings of it.

What Therapy With BPD Actually Looks Like

Treating someone with BPD is described without romanticization as one of the hardest things a clinician can do — and also among the most meaningful.

Several dynamics are almost inevitable:

  • The therapeutic relationship will be idealized before it is devalued. The therapist will feel like the best clinician in the world. That is a signal to stay alert, not to relax.
  • Landmines exist. Minor lapses — arriving slightly distracted, forgetting a detail from a previous session — can be experienced by the client as catastrophic betrayal. These ruptures are inevitable; minimizing them and knowing how to repair them is the work.
  • The frame of therapy must be maintained. Clear, consistent limits on contact, scheduling, and the nature of the relationship are not coldness — they are one of the core therapeutic interventions. A person whose world was never predictable needs to experience that boundaries can be dependable.
  • Transference becomes intense. It is common for clients with moderate to severe BPD to develop romantic or sexual feelings toward their therapist. This is an expression of unmet attachment needs flooding the first safe relationship the person has ever had. The therapist's ethical consistency in those moments is the treatment.

Recovery is measured in years, not months, and the markers are subtle. One of the first reliable signs of progress is when a client begins describing their close relationship in realistic terms — complaining about their partner the way everyone occasionally does, rather than swinging between worship and contempt. The goal is what one theorist described as graduating from splitting: accepting that life is imperfect, relationships are mixed, and connection is still worth having.

When it works, the client no longer needs the therapist. The therapist becomes unnecessary — which is exactly the point.

The Stigma Problem and Why It Matters

BPD is heavily stigmatized — not just in popular culture, but among clinicians. Consultation groups include therapists who flatly refuse to treat anyone with BPD. Others take on these clients without recognizing the disorder, become confused or hurt by what feels like inexplicable hostility, and terminate — which re-enacts the core wound.

The term "quiet borderline" emerged online partly in response to stigma: people who have the internal experience of BPD but don't externalize it as aggression felt they needed a different label to avoid being classified as abusive. The instinct behind the term is understandable, but it implies that "regular" borderline means loud or abusive — which is not accurate. Many people with BPD harm no one around them and suffer entirely internally.

The practical consequence of poor clinical training is that people with BPD who enter therapy are disproportionately likely to be misunderstood, misdiagnosed, undertreated, or abandoned by their therapist — precisely because their presentation is most difficult for an undertrained clinician to navigate.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing the behavior for the cause. Anger, emotional reactivity, and controlling behavior look irrational in isolation. They are completely logical once the underlying perceptual distortion is understood.
  • Taking "love bombing" and gaslighting as proof of malice. In the vast majority of cases involving BPD, these are not strategic. They are the outputs of a distorted perceptual system, not a calculated plan.
  • Believing "quiet borderline" means the loud version is abusive by definition. BPD involves suffering, not a personality type defined by mistreating others.
  • Assuming that because someone knows their diagnosis, they can identify it in real time. Even clinicians with training take months to recognize BPD in a new client. Self-diagnosis from DSM criteria is particularly unreliable because the criteria describe surface behavior, not the underlying architecture.
  • Expecting short-term treatment to produce meaningful change. Recovery from a personality disorder is a years-long process. Expecting results in weeks and abandoning treatment early is itself potentially retraumatizing.
  • Clinicians allowing themselves to be seduced by idealization. The feeling of being an exceptionally gifted therapist for a particular client is often a signal to look more carefully, not evidence that the therapy is going unusually well.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you suspect you have BPD or significant abandonment-based reactivity: Before seeking a diagnosis, find a therapist who specializes in personality disorders specifically — not just a general therapist. Interview them: ask how they handle relationship ruptures in therapy, what their approach to BPD is, and what they do when a client becomes intensely upset with them. A clear, confident, non-defensive answer is a positive sign.

  2. Practice the "what do I need?" check-in. Once daily, pause and ask yourself: What am I feeling right now? What do I need right now? Don't force an answer. If nothing comes, sit with the absence. Over time, something will begin to surface. This is the dimmer-switch exercise — it builds the neural connection between you and your inner life.

  3. When you notice yourself in idealization or devaluation, name it. If a relationship feels completely perfect, or you feel someone has completely betrayed you, slow down before acting. Ask: Is it possible this person is neither a savior nor a villain? What would a realistic middle ground look like?

  4. If you're close to someone with BPD: Recognize that what reads as manipulation or gaslighting is almost certainly not volitional. Do not try to argue someone out of their perception in the moment of activation. Calmly maintaining your own stability and not retaliating or fleeing tends to be more helpful than either capitulating or escalating.

  5. If you are a therapist: Treat the therapeutic frame — consistent timing, clear limits on contact, reliable follow-through — as a clinical intervention in itself, not just administrative hygiene. For clients with abandonment trauma, the predictability of the structure is part of what heals.

  6. Study the schema therapy model of core emotional needs. Identify which of your own core needs (secure attachment, safety, guidance, acceptance, autonomy, identity, spontaneous expression, realistic limits) went substantially unmet in your early life. Understanding your own schemas helps you recognize them when they fire — in relationships, in reaction to perceived criticism or rejection, and in therapy.
