What "Fully Healed" Actually Means (And Why the Question Is Harder Than It Sounds)

William's initial definition of healed is: "you can take it without it affecting you." That framing already reveals a problem — it's about managing impact, not restoring something lost.

The honest clinical answer offered here: 80% healed is a reasonable, evidence-backed expectation. Full 100% healing is possible but less certain. What matters more than the exact number is understanding that "healed" isn't one thing. When you have multiple distinct problems — sluggishness, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, hopelessness, existential groundlessness — healing one of them fully is a genuine victory, even if others remain. Think of it like repairing a damaged limb in a video game: if your leg is healed, the movement debuff is gone. That's real progress, even if your arm still needs work.

The reason to have hope isn't inspirational. It's observational: if you've survived everything you've survived and something in you keeps pulling back from the edge, that force is real. It's not nothing. It's worth understanding.

Why Progress Keeps Collapsing: Shields vs. Healing

William describes a painful pattern: he gets better, believes he's fixed, then the weight returns. His psychologist thought he was done. He thought he was done. Then two months later, it came back — not as specific flashbacks this time, but as a constant dragging noise he couldn't locate or name.

The key distinction here is between a shield and actual healing.

  • A shield gives you bonus hit points. It absorbs damage. It can feel exactly like being healthy.
  • But when the shield drops, you're back where you started.

Distraction works like a shield. A relationship that makes you feel like you belong works like a shield. Medication works like a shield. None of these are bad — shields matter, especially when you're drowning — but confusing a shield for healing is what creates hopelessness. You do everything "right," you feel better, the shield fades, and you crash. Then you start wondering if healing is even possible. The hopelessness is completely rational given that experience. You did the work and you're not better. Of course you'd lose hope.

The problem isn't that the treatments are wrong. The problem is that the treatments don't match the layer of damage.

The Five Layers: Why Mismatched Treatment Fails

This is the framework that reframes everything. Based on Pancha Kosha theory — an Ayurvedic model thousands of years old — a human being has five distinct layers of being:

  1. Physical — the body
  2. Energetic — vitality, chi, pranaYour basic unit of life force energy — the vital animating force that keeps you alive, known as qi in the Chinese system.Full definition →
  3. Emotional — feelings, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, grief
  4. Intellectual — thoughts, cognitive patterns, beliefs about yourself
  5. Spiritual — your fundamental sense of whether you exist and belong

The core insight is simple but profound: a wound on one layer needs treatment on that same layer. Applying an emotional treatment to a physical problem gets you a little benefit but not full healing. Applying a physical treatment to a spiritual wound does almost nothing.

Here's how William's specific problems map onto this:

Sluggishness / depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →: This sits at the physical and energetic levels. Talking about your feelings helps a little. Medication helps a lot. What actually heals (rather than just shields) is energetic practice — pranayamaPranayama is the yogic practice of deliberate breath control used to regulate prana — the body's vital energy — and directly influence the nervous system.Full definition →, qigong, yoga, tai chi. These directly work the energetic body. The science is strange but real: tai chi outperforms regular exercise for arthritis in controlled trials. Wim Hof can sit in ice water for an hour through pranayama alone. Something is happening at an energetic level that purely physical intervention doesn't reach. The catch: when you're that depleted, you don't have the energy to do energetic practices. So medication first to get enough function, then use that window to build the practices that will create lasting change.

Shame and negative self-image: This lives at the emotional and intellectual levels. Self-compassion work and CBTA structured, results-focused form of psychotherapy that works by breaking a problem — say, procrastination or social anxiety — down into its three components: thoughts, emotions, and behaviors, then targeting each one…Full definition → (cognitive behavioral therapy) are well-matched treatments here. William has done a lot of this, and it shows — his cognitive distortions are largely resolved. The treatments worked on that layer.

C-PTSD: Here's where most treatment goes wrong. Complex PTSD is not the same as regular PTSD. A single traumatic event can shatter your sense of safety, but it doesn't have to destroy your sense of self. Being told daily as a child that you take up space in the world, that you're useless, that you weren't wanted — that doesn't just create bad memories or negative emotions. It destroys the baseline permission to exist. That's not an emotional problem. That's a spiritual problem.

"You were not given a baseline permission to exist as a human being."

The question isn't why am I here? That's level two. The level one question — the one that has to be answered first — is do I even get to be here?

Most C-PTSD treatment addresses the emotional layer (talk therapy, medication, self-compassion). Those treatments help, which is why people make real progress. But they don't touch the spiritual layer, which is where the core damage actually lives. So the person heals their shame, does their therapy, learns self-compassion — and still feels like a ghost. Still can't fully receive what people say to them. Still wonders what they're doing in a room full of people who care about them. The treatments worked. The wound is somewhere else.

The Ghost Problem: Why Connection Doesn't Land

William describes something many people with complex childhood trauma recognize: even when friends tell him he matters, it doesn't actually land. It's like he's an ethereal presence that others can't quite touch. He knows the words are kind. He can't absorb them.

This is why the relationship that healed him temporarily couldn't heal him permanently. His girlfriend gave him two years of genuine belonging — and that answered the spiritual question yes, you deserve to be here — but she couldn't undo 22 years of being told the answer was no. The spiritual layer was still too fragile. When the relationship declined, the answer slipped back.

You can't receive healing until you're solid enough to be touched. You have to be in the world before the world can repair you.

What Spiritual Healing Actually Looks Like

This is the hardest layer to address because it doesn't respond to analysis. In fact, analytical approaches to spiritual problems are one of the least effective things you can do — using your intellect to solve an existential problem gives you maybe one point of healing when you need ten.

The path into the spiritual layer is meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → aimed at discovering your sense of self — not mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition → in general, not loving-kindness meditation (that's emotional), but practices specifically oriented toward the question: who is this person who is here?

A brief but illuminating exercise is demonstrated: sit up straight, place your hands palm-up in your lap, and simply feel whether your hands feel like you. Then place your right palm over your left with thumbs touching. Notice whether you feel more like yourself. Most people do. That's not a trick — it's a mudra (specifically Bhairava mudra, the posture used by both Buddha and Shiva in traditional iconography), a posture designed precisely to bring you into closer contact with yourself.

The point isn't to answer the question of who you are. It's to get familiar with the road — the experience of moving closer to and further from yourself. To sit with the person inside. And occasionally, without pressure, to ask: does this person deserve to be here?

The goal isn't a philosophical answer. It's direct acquaintance with yourself, repeated often enough that you stop being a ghost and start being someone the world can actually touch.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking progress for completion. Getting emotionally better — less shame, more self-compassion — is real and valuable. It's not the finish line if the spiritual layer is still untouched.

Using analysis to solve spiritual problems. The intellect is good at many things. Answering "do I deserve to exist?" isn't one of them. Thinking harder about it, finding better reasons, building a logical case — these get you very little. The answer has to be felt, not argued.

Thinking medication is healing. Medication for depression is a shield. It's a useful, sometimes necessary shield. But stopping medication and returning to baseline isn't a sign that healing didn't work — it's a sign that the medication was doing what medication does. Don't confuse the absence of the shield for a return of the wound.

Applying one type of treatment to every layer. Talk therapy is valuable. It doesn't fix a physical problem. Medication is valuable. It doesn't resolve a spiritual wound. Matching treatment to layer is the whole game.

Expecting a relationship to do what only inner work can do. Another person can temporarily answer the question "do I deserve to be here?" — that's real and beautiful. But they can't permanently replace the inner work of building your own answer.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start medication strategically, not indefinitely. If you're dealing with neurovegetative depression (heavy body, can't do anything), treat it as a temporary energy boost that allows you to engage with practices that create lasting change. Work with a doctor to plan what you're building while medicated, so stopping isn't just a return to baseline.

  2. Add an energetic practice. Choose one: yoga, tai chi, qigong, or pranayama. Commit to 3–5 sessions a week. Don't expect to feel dramatic results quickly — this is a slow regeneration. The goal is to heal the energetic layer that medication only shields.

  3. Map your problems to layers. Write down the distinct "kinds of bad" you experience. For each one, honestly ask: is this physical, energetic, emotional, intellectual, or spiritual? Then ask: what am I currently doing to address it, and does that treatment actually match that layer?

  4. Practice the Bhairava mudra daily. Sit cross-legged, right palm resting on left palm with thumbs touching. Close your eyes. Notice what it feels like to be closer to or further from yourself. Do this for 3–5 minutes to start. The only goal is familiarity — not insight, not answers. Just learn how to move closer to yourself.

  5. Hold the spiritual question lightly, not analytically. While in the posture above, occasionally ask yourself: does this person deserve to be here? Don't try to answer it with your mind. Just let the question sit with the felt sense of yourself you've found in the practice.

  6. If you have C-PTSD, find a therapist who treats it as a spiritual wound, not just a collection of emotional symptoms. Standard talk therapy will help (one point of healing), but look specifically for clinicians or approaches that address the fundamental sense of self and belonging — the layer where complex trauma actually lives.
