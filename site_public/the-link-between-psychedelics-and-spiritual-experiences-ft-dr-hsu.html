From Atheism to Suicidality to "God Found Me"

Dr. Michael Shu grew up in a household shaped by Cultural Revolution–era China, where his father treated religion as brainwashing and the closest thing to spirituality was rubbing a Buddha statue's forehead before exams. He was hyperactive, disruptive, emotionally unmoored, and by around age 13 had quietly constructed what he describes as a kind of expected-value equation for his life: the negatives (being yelled at, failing in school, getting sent to the principal's office) simply outweighed any pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition → he could identify. He concluded it was better to die than to keep living, and he had a plan.

What interrupted it wasn't therapy or medication or a concerned adult. He heard an audible voice — not a thought, but what he experienced as a genuine external sensory input — telling him this was not the end of his life and that there was hope. He doesn't try to over-explain what it was. What it did was create a crack of openness where there had been none.

The depression didn't lift immediately. In high school he started doing well socially and academically, dating, excelling at tennis. But he noticed something: even with all the ego boosts, there was still a deep emptiness underneath. He couldn't shake the sense that something was missing, and he suspected it had to do with what he'd heard that night.

That suspicion sent him into an unusual kind of intellectual investigation. He read C.S. Lewis and Richard Dawkins back to back. He read apologetics and critiques. He wasn't willing to adopt a belief on comfort grounds, but he also wasn't willing to keep pretending the emptiness wasn't there. He describes being intellectually satisfied by what he found about the historical case for Christianity, but the real turning point came through an emotional experience — watching a music video that depicted his own life story back at him, including the depression, the self-medicating through achievement, and the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → underneath it all. Around age 18, he accepted Jesus as his lord and savior.

Why the Logic of Depression Matters

One of the most important moments in this conversation is a reframe of how to understand depression, especially in young people. When Dr. K hears this story, what strikes him is how logical it sounds. Not an emotional catastrophe, not a cognitive distortion, not a mood disorder in the clinical sense — but a 13-year-old doing arithmetic on his own life and reaching a rational conclusion.

This is a concept Dr. K calls congruent depression: sometimes the depression is appropriate to the circumstances. The life genuinely isn't working. Cognitive-behavioral therapy assumes the problem is a distorted lens, but what if the lens is accurate and the situation just actually is bad? The important follow-up — and it matters enormously for anyone who has ever had this experience — is that a life being objectively bad right now does not mean it cannot improve. Those are two separate claims that often get collapsed into one.

Looking back, Dr. Shu also recognized the emotional dimension of what was happening: tremendous unexpressed anger toward his parents and teachers, which, with nowhere to go, turned inward. He started finding reasons to hate himself. Freud's concept from Mourning and Melancholia — that the root of depression is anger directed against the self — maps closely onto this, and it's a pattern both doctors recognize in their clinical work.

What a Relationship with God Actually Does (The Mechanisms)

Dr. K keeps pressing for mechanism, because as a psychiatrist he wants to understand how something works, not just that it worked. A few answers emerge:

Social connectedness is the most empirically supported piece. By extension, a relationship with God may function therapeutically not because of what God objectively is or isn't, but because of the quality of relating: being received with unconditional love, being seen without judgment, having somewhere to bring your shame. Once you've experienced empathy directed at you, you begin to know what it is and can extend it outward.

Where Psychiatry Misses the Mark on Spiritual Experience

Both doctors share a concern about how the field handles spiritual and mystical experiences: it often pathologizes them reflexively.

Dr. Shu recounts a case where a woman was seen praying in an emergency room. A nurse interpreted this as "responding to internal stimuli," flagged it as possible psychosis, and the chain of events that followed — diagnosis, restraint, hospitalization, a CPS referral — was catastrophic. The woman was praying. That's it.

The underlying problem is diagnostic conflation. Not all voices, visions, or unusual subjective experiences are symptoms of mental illness. The distinguishing features of genuinely pathological psychosis are more specific: content that is isolating or self-destructive, processes like hyperactive pattern-association (where random unconnected events get woven into a threatening narrative), functional decline, and — notably — physiological deterioration. People with untreated schizophrenia die earlier, not merely because of the diagnosis, but because the way the illness reshapes their life trajectory causes cumulative harm.

By contrast, experiences that produce openness, connection, reduced isolation, and expanded compassion — even if they involve hearing a voice or perceiving something non-material — don't fit that profile. Calling a transformative spiritual experience "delusional toxicosis," as some researchers apparently do, may be both scientifically lazy and clinically harmful.

Are Psychedelics and Spiritual Experiences the Same Thing?

This is the question neither doctor claims to answer definitively, but they take it seriously enough to sit with it honestly.

The overlap in phenomenology is striking. People returning from psilocybin or ayahuasca sessions report experiences that sound functionally identical to what contemplatives describe: ego dissolution, a sense of unconditional love emanating from something larger than themselves, contact with a realm that feels more real than ordinary waking life, and a difficulty translating any of it into language afterward. The Good Friday Experiment at Boston's Marsh Chapel — where divinity students were given psilocybin during a religious service — found that most described the experience as congruent with and deepening of their existing spiritual framework.

Machine elves are worth pausing on. Across independent DMT users with no shared cultural background, something like 90%+ report encountering non-human entities — described as small, mechanical, often communicating in ways that bypass language, generally benevolent, and inclined to show the person something about the nature of the universe. The consistency of this across backgrounds is what makes it scientifically interesting. It's not that Christians see Jesus and Hindus see Krishna. Whatever DMT is doing seems to be accessing something more consistent than personal belief, which points away from pure projectionProjection is when something inside you — a feeling, impulse, or trait you can't consciously accept — gets attributed to someone else instead.Full definition →.

One theory is the spiritual antenna model: the brain doesn't generate these experiences so much as it receives them. Psychedelics and certain contemplative practices may tune the antenna rather than create the signal. Dr. K's version of this draws on the difference between the visual cortex (which processes the signal) and visual association cortex (which generates meaning from it). You can artificially stimulate either region and produce experiences that don't correspond to anything in the physical environment — but that doesn't prove the physical environment doesn't exist. It only proves the brain mediates perception. A similar argument applies to non-material perception: the fact that you can induce it with a chemical doesn't tell you whether what's being perceived is real or fabricated.

"Just because I can artificially stimulate neurons and see things — or mess with neurons and not see things — that doesn't mean light doesn't exist. This just means our brain is capable of perceiving the world."

The Problem of Verification (and the Defense of Consensus)

The harder philosophical question is: how do you know whether what you're perceiving in a mystical state is real or simply a very convincing hallucination? Dr. K's answer is consensus of perception.

Scientific truth, at its foundation, is consistent shared human observation. A randomized controlled trial is essentially asking: can multiple observers, under controlled conditions, reliably detect the same effect? Gravity works as a scientific fact because you can run the experiment a million times and the observation never varies. P < .05 is just a formalized threshold for "consistent enough."

The same logic, applied carefully, suggests that the consistency of non-material experience across cultures, time periods, drug states, and meditative traditions is worth taking seriously — not as proof, but as data. The instrumentation to verify it may not exist yet. Crucially, thoughts themselves can't be materially detected either, and yet no one argues that thoughts don't exist.

"There's enough conserved human experience of non-material things that I think that's real. It's just we have no way — and it's so simple, because do thoughts exist? Yes. But we have zero proof of the existence of a thought."

The honest position both doctors land on is one Roland Griffiths (the late Hopkins psychedelic researcher) called negative capability: the willingness to remain in uncertainty without forcing resolution. This isn't agnosticism as a cop-out. It's a recognition that demanding absolute certainty before engaging with something is itself an epistemological choice — and not obviously the right one.

Common Pitfalls

Conflating religiosity with irrationality. The immediate cultural reflex when someone mentions Jesus or God in a clinical or intellectual context is to discount everything else they say. Dr. Shu is a researcher, a clinician trained at Johns Hopkins and Harvard, and someone who stress-tested his beliefs against Dawkins and Lewis before committing to them. Religious experience and scientific rigor are not mutually exclusive.

Assuming all voices or visions are pathological. Psychiatry has a history of overdiagnosing. Praying out loud, speaking about hearing God, or describing a visionary experience should not automatically trigger a psychosis workup. Context, content, trajectory, and function all matter enormously.

Treating psychedelics as a spiritual shortcut without respect for set and setting. The suggestibility during a psychedelic session is real and significant. A therapist or guide who subtly (or not subtly) steers the experience toward their own theological framework is not being neutral, and this can cause harm. The patient's own process needs to lead.

Assuming spirituality is the cause when community is the mechanism. When religious participation improves mental health, the active ingredient may be social connection rather than theology. This matters for how you use it: joining any group that provides genuine belonging may provide similar benefits.

Using the same criterion for non-material experience that you'd use for material evidence. Demanding a brain scan or a double-blind trial before taking seriously the near-universal human report of something like meaning or love or connection is applying the wrong tool to the question.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your own depression for congruence. If you're depressed, ask honestly: is this a distortion of reality, or is it an accurate read of a situation that genuinely isn't working? If it's congruent, the task isn't to think more positively — it's to change the situation. These are different problems requiring different approaches.

  2. Follow the emptiness, not just the achievement. If you've reached external goals (grades, status, relationships, athletic success) and still feel a persistent void underneath, treat that void as information rather than a problem to suppress. It's pointing at something. Get curious about what.

  3. Try one form of contemplative practice for 4 weeks before deciding. Whether it's seated meditation, Christian centering prayer, Sufi dhikr, or something else, you can't evaluate these practices from the outside. The evidence for meditation's clinical effects is robust — commit long enough to actually experience it rather than just think about it.

  4. Read The Varieties of Religious Experience by William James. It remains one of the most rigorous and non-dismissive treatments of what mystical experience actually is, written by someone who took both science and subjective experience seriously.

  5. Before dismissing a spiritual claim, ask what you'd need to observe to take it seriously. This is the negative capability exercise. You don't have to believe something, but practice identifying the difference between "this is false" and "I don't have instrumentation for this yet."

  6. If you work in mental health or adjacent fields: when a patient mentions prayer, spiritual experience, or hearing from God, ask them about it the same way you'd ask about any important relationship. What's the quality of the experience? What does it do for their functioning? Does it isolate them or connect them? Content and trajectory matter more than the category.

  7. If you're considering psychedelic-assisted therapy: research set and setting carefully, prioritize contexts with trained neutral facilitation, and be aware that what you encounter in the experience will be shaped significantly by what you bring into it — your expectations, your fears, your unresolved material. Prepare accordingly.
