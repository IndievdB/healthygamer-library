Summary

TL;DR — Discipline and self-respect aren't things you acquire and then possess; they're things you actively do, over and over again, every single day.

Most people who struggle with discipline are waiting to get it — as if one day it will click into place and run on autopilot. That's not how it works. Discipline is a verb. So is self-respect. Understanding that changes everything about how you approach building them — and why you keep losing them.

Discipline Is a Verb, Not a Trophy

There's a subtle but devastating mistake baked into how most people think about discipline: they treat it as a noun. Something you acquire. A trait you either have or don't have. The thinking goes: once I become a disciplined person, the discipline will just take over and I won't have to fight myself anymore.

That's the big scam.

Disciplining — the actual verb — is the cognitive act of moving from what you want to do toward what you should do. Every single time you face that fork, you either perform the action of disciplining or you don't. There is no finish line where you earn the trophy and coast.

"Don't for a moment think that you will have ever developed discipline. We think it's binary. We think once we cross the finish line, it's done. That's not what discipline is."

Think of it like playing the violin. The first time is hard. With practice it gets easier and more natural. But if you put the violin down for months, your fingers forget. Discipline works on an even faster decay cycle than most skills — because you're not just practicing once a day. You're making hundreds of small decisions every single day, and each one is either a rep of disciplining or a rep of not disciplining.

Why You Lose It So Fast

Most conversations about discipline focus on how hard it is to start. The harder, less-discussed problem is how fast it disappears after you've been doing well for months.

You worked out consistently for six months. You went to therapy for a couple of months. You practiced the violin seriously for a while. Then — it fell apart. Why?

The answer is in the verb. For those six months, you were disciplining yourself — taking a specific cognitive action in response to your impulses. Then the nature of that action changed. You stopped taking it. And just like a garden that goes untended, the weeds rush back in.

"I was gardening for six months and my garden was beautiful. Then I stopped gardening. Why don't we explain it like this? Because this sounds exhausting."

It is exhausting — at first. But the human organism is adaptable. Brushing your teeth at night feels like a chore until it becomes a habit and stops feeling like anything at all. Disciplining becomes less painful the more consistently you do it. The goal isn't to eliminate the effort; it's to make the action familiar enough that the friction decreases.

You Are a Living Thing Fighting Entropy

The reason there's no "being done" with discipline comes down to basic biology. You are not a rock. You are a living organism in constant negotiation with entropy.

Every day you need to eat, breathe, sleep, move. There's no finishing food. There's no retiring from breathing. Life is continuous action. The idea that you'll one day reach a state where no more effort is required — financially, personally, physically — is a comforting fiction.

"You are a living, breathing biological organism that requires investment to stay still. You are fighting against the force of entropy. You can stop acting when you're dead."

This isn't meant to be demoralizing. It's clarifying. Once you accept that maintenance is the nature of being alive — not a sign of failure or weakness — the daily effort stops feeling like something has gone wrong and starts feeling like what life simply is.

Self-Respect Is Also a Verb

The same reframe applies to self-respect. Most people talk about it as something they either have or lack. But self-respect isn't a fixed inner quality — it's the way you respond to yourself in the moments that matter.

Picture this: your boss passes you over for a promotion and gives it to someone else. Your first thought might be that's not fair, I work harder than that person. What happens next is where self-respect lives or dies.

Someone practicing self-respect lets that thought stand. They take it seriously. They say, you're right, you should say something about this.

Someone without self-respect takes that same initial thought and immediately undercuts it: If you deserved it, you'd have gotten it. Stop whining. Work harder. That internal response feels like toughness. It isn't. It's self-abuse wearing the costume of discipline.

"The way that you are talking to yourself is not self-respect. It is not respecting you."

There's also a subtler failure mode: indulgence. Treating yourself like a spoiled child — giving in to every impulse, soothing every discomfort, never holding yourself accountable — isn't kindness. It's a different form of not respecting yourself. We indulge people we're trying to win over, not people we genuinely respect.

Respect looks more like a good coach or a caring parent who holds you to a standard because they believe you're capable of meeting it. The way you respond to your own thoughts, your own needs, your own impulses — that is the practice of self-respect, in real time.

The Modern Adulting Problem

One more thing worth sitting with: the cultural context most young people are operating in has shifted dramatically, and comparing yourself to some imagined "normal adult" is measuring yourself against a standard that largely doesn't exist anymore.

Fifty percent of people under 30 are living with their parents. The traditional path into adulthood — school, job, independence, relationship, in more or less that order — used to be relatively automatic. The structure was built into society like a waterslide: get in at the top, slide to the bottom.

That slide is broken now. Figuring out how to be an adult in today's world is genuinely harder, and the effort required to construct that path yourself is real. Being "behind" isn't a personal failure; it's a generational condition.

"Real adulting is figuring out how to be what you think an adult should be. That's what adulting is now."

This doesn't mean giving yourself unlimited slack. It means directing your energy toward your own path instead of burning it measuring yourself against people who appear to have it together — appearances being what they are.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting to "get" discipline before acting. Discipline doesn't arrive first and then produce behavior. The behavior is the discipline. You have to start doing it to build it, not build it and then start doing it.

  • Confusing harshness with discipline. Tearing yourself down for every failure, calling yourself a loser, and demanding perfection feels like being hard on yourself in a productive way. It's actually self-abuse, and it tends to deepen the cycle rather than break it.

  • Confusing indulgence with self-care. Giving yourself everything you want in the moment — another episode, another scroll session, avoiding hard things — might feel compassionate. But consistent indulgence is the opposite of self-respect.

  • Thinking streaks are self-sustaining. Six months of consistent behavior doesn't bank enough credit to coast. The moment you stop disciplining yourself, the decay begins. There's no accumulated reserve.

  • Comparing your internal experience to others' external appearance. You see what other people are doing; you feel what you're struggling with. That comparison will always be unfair to you.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the moment of impulse. For one day, simply notice every time your mind pulls you toward the easier option — picking up your phone, opening a new tab, leaving your desk. You don't have to resist every one of them yet. Just observe. Name it: this is an impulse.

  2. Practice one deliberate redirect. Pick one recurring impulse and, at least once today, do the cognitive move: notice the impulse, acknowledge it, and choose the other thing. Just one. The goal isn't perfection; it's performing the verb of disciplining at least once consciously.

  3. Audit how you talk to yourself after a small failure. Next time you slip up — skip a workout, waste a few hours, avoid something you should have done — pay attention to your internal response. Is it self-respect (honest, steady, forward-looking) or self-abuse (punishing, contemptuous) or indulgence (dismissive, excuse-making)? You don't have to fix it immediately. Just identify which mode you're in.

  4. Stop asking "how do I get discipline?" and start asking "did I discipline myself today?" Shift from treating it as a destination to treating it as a daily practice. At the end of each day, ask: how many times did I respond to an impulse with the action I should take? How many times didn't I?

  5. Lower the comparison bar — and raise your personal one. Deliberately stop measuring yourself against peers who seem more "together." Instead, compare today's version of you to yesterday's. The only relevant metric is whether you practiced the verb more today than you did before.
