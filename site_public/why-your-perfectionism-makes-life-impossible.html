Summary

TL;DR — Perfectionism isn't about having too-high standards; it's about whose standards you're living by. The fix isn't caring less — it's learning to care about your own opinion too.

Most advice about perfectionism tells you to stop caring what others think, set better boundaries, or just lower the bar. That advice fails because it ignores how your brain was actually wired by your upbringing. There's a neurological difference between two types of perfectionism, and understanding that difference reveals a much more workable path forward — one that doesn't require you to rewire the part of yourself that's actually hardest to change.

Two Types of Perfectionism (and Why They're Not the Same)

There's a meaningful distinction between:

  • Personal Standard Perfectionism (PSP): A high bar you set for yourself, driven by your own valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition → and expectations.
  • External Concern Perfectionism (ECP): A high bar driven by other people's opinions — you're striving to meet their standards, avoid their disappointment, or earn their approval.

Most people who suffer from perfectionism are high in ECP and low in PSP. They're working incredibly hard, but almost entirely in service of how they'll be perceived or judged.

Where It Comes From

ECP tends to grow out of environments where, no matter what you did, you couldn't win. This shows up in a few common patterns:

  • Harshly critical or narcissistic parents who were always disappointed, even when you performed well — meaning your best was never a source of pride, just the expected baseline.
  • Golden Boy/Golden Girl dynamics — an older sibling who excelled, which raised the implicit bar to a level you were expected to match but could never feel good about reaching.

The key insight is this: in those environments, solving the problem didn't work. No amount of trying harder was ever going to make those parents satisfied. So your brain did the only rational thing it could — it stopped engaging with the problem of "how do I improve?" and started protecting you from the pain of falling short.

What Happens in Your Brain When You Make a Mistake

Research comparing people high in PSP vs. high in ECP found something striking when both groups were put through tasks where they were expected to make errors.

When someone high in PSP makes a mistake: - A part of the brain involved in careful attention activates. - Their thinking slows down. - They pay closer attention to what went wrong. - Over time, they make fewer mistakes and improve.

When someone high in ECP makes a mistake: - A region called the medial frontal gyrus (MFG) activates. - This region's job is to redirect attention — it pulls your focus away from what just happened. - You mentally check out, go numb, start thinking about something else. - You don't process the mistake, so you don't learn from it or repair it.

This is the curse of perfectionism in its clearest form: you desperately want to do well, but the moment you fall short, your brain — trained since childhood — abandons the problem. You end up beating yourself up or going numb, but you don't actually fix anything. The very wiring that was once a survival adaptation now makes growth nearly impossible.

"You want to do a great job, but anytime you screw up you don't have the resilience to actually fix the problem. You just end up beating yourself up, numbing yourself out, feeling like there's nothing you can do to win this game."

Why "Just Stop Caring What Others Think" Is Bad Advice

If you've been told to stop caring about others' opinions, here's why that doesn't land: your ECP was forged in an environment where other people's reactions were directly tied to your emotional safety. That wiring runs deep. Telling someone high in ECP to just ignore other people's opinions is like telling someone with a fear of heights to just not feel scared — it's advice that comes easy to people who were never wired that way.

There's a more useful way to think about it. Picture a 2×2 grid:

Low PSP High PSP
High ECP Struggling (probably you) Best outcomes
Low ECP Worst outcomes Doing okay

Moving from high-ECP/low-PSP toward low ECP is genuinely hard — that axis doesn't change easily. But moving toward high PSP? That's the more accessible path. And the surprising finding is that high-ECP + high-PSP actually outperforms high-PSP alone. Your care for how others see you isn't a bug to delete — it becomes fuel when you add genuine self-standards alongside it.

"We don't want to change the way that you think around other people — that's not how we're going to harness perfectionism. What's actually easier is harnessing the personal standard."

The Two-Part Shift

1. Slow down when you make a mistake

The ECP brain's default response to a mistake is to flee — mentally, emotionally, sometimes literally. The antidote is simple but uncomfortable: don't distract yourself. Just slow down.

You don't have to fix the mistake right away. You don't have to figure out what to do. You just have to stay present with it — notice it, sit with it for a moment. That act alone begins to disable the medial frontal gyrus's escape response and lets the rest of your brain start doing what it's actually capable of.

2. Ask yourself what you would be happy with

If you have high ECP, you almost never ask this question. The default mode is: What does everyone else need from me? What would make them satisfied? Your own standards were conditioned out of you early — you had opinions as a kid, and they were dismissed or ignored, so you stopped consulting yourself.

Start asking: If no one else existed, what would I be happy with here? Would a B be okay? Does this need to be perfect, or is this good enough by my own measure?

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking the goal is to eliminate your concern for others' opinions. It's not. That's a near-impossible task, and aiming for it often just leads to not caring about anything at all.
  • Confusing "slow down" with "fix it." When you make a mistake, you're not being asked to problem-solve immediately. You're just being asked to stay in the room mentally instead of fleeing. The fixing comes later, naturally.
  • Waiting until the perfectionism feels manageable before trying these steps. The discomfort of sitting with a mistake is the practice — it doesn't go away before you start, it diminishes because you start.
  • Believing high ECP is purely a liability. In isolation it is — but paired with PSP, that same drive to meet high standards becomes one of the strongest predictors of real-world performance.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the escape reflex. The next time you make a mistake — at work, in a conversation, on a task — notice the urge to mentally check out, change the subject, or distract yourself. Just naming it ("there it is") is enough to interrupt the automatic response.

  2. Practice deliberate slowing. When you notice that urge, don't act on it. Pause for 30–60 seconds and stay with the discomfort. You don't need to analyze the mistake or plan a fix. Just stay present. Do this once per day to start.

  3. Ask the PSP question before every task. Before a project, a conversation, an exam — ask yourself: What would I be happy with here, independent of anyone else's opinion? Write it down if it helps. You're training yourself to have an inner standard, not just an outer one.

  4. Hold both standards at once. After you've identified what you want, note what others might expect too. The goal isn't to ignore one — it's to make sure your own voice is in the room alongside theirs.

  5. Track your mistakes without judging them. Keep a simple log for one week: what went wrong, what you noticed about it, whether you stayed present or escaped. Don't try to fix anything in the log — just observe.
