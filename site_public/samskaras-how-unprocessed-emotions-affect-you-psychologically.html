The Difference Between Distraction and Digestion

Here's the clearest way to see it. A four-year-old gets nipped by a dog, cries, and then gets taken out for ice cream. An hour later she's laughing. The fear seems gone — but it isn't. The next day, she sees a dog and bursts into tears again. The ice cream didn't dissolve the fear; it just gave her mind something else to focus on. An adult in the same situation has something the four-year-old doesn't: a developed intellect that can actually work through the experience. Rather than needing a distraction to feel better, an adult can reflect — I probably shouldn't have reached out to pet a strange dog. Next time I'll ask first. That was a bit stupid of me, but it's fine. Half an hour of genuine processing later, the emotional charge is largely gone. The next day, seeing a dog might produce a small jolt of adrenaline, but there's no triggered breakdown.

The difference isn't willpower or toughness. It's the capacity to metabolize emotion rather than bypass it.

This is why things we went through as children can feel so disproportionately raw when they resurface in adulthood. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Follow the trigger back to its source. The next time you react to something with an intensity that feels out of proportion — an unexplained wave of sadness, anger, or fear — pause and ask yourself: When did I first feel this way? How old was I?   2. Distinguish between distraction and processing. When you feel bad and then feel better, ask honestly: Did I think through what happened and reach some understanding, or did I just redirect my attention? You don't need to judge the distraction — sometimes you need a break — but be honest about whether the processing actually happened.

  3. Revisit "resolved" griefs with fresh eyes. Pick something from your past you believe you've fully processed or forgiven. Sit quietly with it and ask: Is there a younger version of me who might not feel the same way my adult self does? The goal isn't to manufacture suffering — it's to check whether something is actually digested or just covered over.
