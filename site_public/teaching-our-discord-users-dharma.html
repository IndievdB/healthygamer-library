Summary

TL;DR — DharmaDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition → isn't something you figure out alone by looking inward. It's a meeting point between who you are and what the world asks of you.

Most people searching for their purpose treat it like a solo puzzle — something to crack through enough self-reflection. But that approach misses something essential. Dharma, as a concept rooted in Hindu philosophy, is closer to duty than to individual purpose. And duty, almost by definition, requires another party. Understanding that changes how you go about finding yours.

Dharma Is a Two-Way Street, Not a Solo Quest

The biggest gap in how people think about Dharma is that they treat it as purely internal — as if you can sit quietly, look inward long enough, and eventually discover your calling like a buried treasure.

But think about an emergency room doctor. On any given shift, how does that doctor know what their Dharma is? They don't sit in the break room and meditate on it. A patient walks through the door — a car accident victim, someone in anaphylaxis — and that determines what's needed right now. The world tells you.

"Dharma is a meeting between who I am and what the world asks of me."

This doesn't mean you're a passive instrument of whatever society demands. It means your Dharma can't be confirmed without feedback from reality. An AMA posted on Reddit that nobody upvotes and nobody responds to doesn't reveal a need to be filled. The upvotes, the DMs, the people reaching out — that's karmaThe fundamental principle that every action has a cause and every cause produces an effect — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition → at work. Karma here means cause and effect: you put something into the world, and the world responds, and that response is data you genuinely need.

Rosa Parks, MLK, Gandhi — these people didn't consult a self-help book and decide they were destined for greatness. The world showed up with an impossible situation and asked something of them. The question was whether they were prepared to answer.

Karma Is the Feedback Loop That Connects You to Your Dharma

Karma in this context isn't mystical reward-and-punishment. It's simply the principle that actions have consequences — cause and effect. What makes it relevant to finding your Dharma is that karma is how the world communicates what it needs from you.

If you try to figure out your Dharma in isolation, you're cutting off the feedback loop. You can reason all you want about what you're good at and what matters, but until you actually put something into the world and see what comes back, you're guessing.

This is also why Dharma isn't grandiose by default. The examples people reach for — civil rights icons, world-historical figures — are dramatic. But most karma is small. Someone coming to your office every week struggling with depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →. Raising your kids to be decent people. These are enough. The scale of your Dharma is determined by what your specific karma brings you, not by some universal ranking of meaningful pursuits.

Dharma Changes Over Time, and That's Expected

Dharma isn't a destination you arrive at once and stay forever. It evolves as your circumstances, skills, and the demands of the world around you change.

The first Dharma anyone has is to themselves: prepare. This is what the concept of brahmacharyaBrahmacharya is the practice of dwelling in Brahman — the infinite or cosmic consciousness — which in practice means cultivating a state of non-lustfulness, not mere celibacy.Full definition → — the student phase — points to. You can't know what life is going to ask of you ten years from now. So the task right now is to level up the character you're playing. Get physically fit. Get mentally fit. Learn things that interest you — coding, medicine, philosophy, anything — because any of it might become the foundation for a Dharma you can't yet see.

"Level up player one. Make player one the best that you can. The rest is circumstance."

Whether you end up a doctor, a monk, a truck driver, or a parent — the character doing all of those things is still you, and the quality of what you can offer the world is determined by how well-developed that character is. Skills you ignored become limitations later. Experiences you pursued become capacities you didn't know you'd need.

Your Family Situation Shapes Your Dharma — but Doesn't Lock It

The discussion raised a real tension: what about people whose families are broken or even harmful? Isn't placing family at the top of the Dharma hierarchy privileging people who happen to have loving families?

Yes, actually. Family sits at the top of one person's Dharma because that person chose to build one and has a good one. That's not a universal rule.

If you came from a dysfunctional or abusive family, your experiences aren't a deficit — they're a specific kind of preparation. You may be uniquely positioned to help others in similar situations precisely because you've lived it in a way someone with an easy family history hasn't.

Dharma in a difficult family context also doesn't mean endlessly trying to repair something beyond repair. It means doing your genuine best — showing up, extending effort, trying to meet people halfway — and then, if that effort is consistently unreturned, walking away without guilt. You did your part. Dharma is about your actions, not about controlling outcomes.

"Karma is not about an outcome. It's about your actions. Did you do enough? If you did enough, you should be able to walk away without regret."

How to Know Whether Your Dharma Is Actually Right

This is the harder question. What if someone is utterly convinced their Dharma is to spread a harmful ideology? Their conviction feels just as real to them as yours does to you.

The answer isn't a tidy formula, but it starts with tracing the origin of the belief driving your sense of purpose. Where did this conviction come from? If you follow it back honestly, you often find it's rooted in unprocessed hurt. People who feel called to harm or demean others frequently trace that impulse back to pain they haven't fully examined.

That doesn't automatically disqualify every uncomfortable or confrontational conviction — some of the most important actions in history involved people defying what society demanded of them in the moment. Rosa Parks wasn't following society's script. The test isn't "does this upset people?" The test is: can you trace this impulse to a clear, examined place — rather than to anger or wound you haven't sat with yet?

"Try to find the origin of where you got the idea you're thinking about pursuing. If you reflect on it and it comes from hatred or from rational thought, then you'll know how to move forward."

Ego is a related trap. The pull toward a particular path can feel like Dharma but actually be ego in disguise — the desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → to be seen, admired, or loved on a larger scale than regular life offers. Noticing that pull, and being willing to separate it from genuine purpose, is part of the ongoing work.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating Dharma as purely self-determined. Spending years looking inward without actually testing anything against the world keeps you stuck. The world has to weigh in.
  • Confusing grandiosityA personality pattern — not an identity — rooted in low internal self-worth, where the gap between how little you feel you're worth on the inside gets filled by constant external validation.Full definition → with purpose. Dharma doesn't have to be historical or large-scale. Raising children well or showing up consistently for people in your immediate circle is a Dharma.
  • Expecting your Dharma to be fixed. It will change. What you're called to at 22 is not what you'll be called to at 40. Prepare for the version you can't yet see.
  • Conflating Dharma with what earns you admiration. Admiration is about ego. Karma pointing you toward a need is different from people validating your identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →. Notice the difference.
  • Trying to figure it all out before acting. You can't. Preparation matters, but the Dharma only becomes visible when you actually engage with the world and see what comes back.
  • Treating unexamined hurt as a compass. Pain can be motivating, but a purpose built on unresolved anger or hurt without reflection is unstable and potentially harmful. Trace the root first.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Put something into the world and watch what comes back. Post a thought, offer help, start a conversation in your area of interest. The responses — or their absence — are real data about whether there's a need you're positioned to fill.

  2. Audit your current skills and knowledgeVidya is objective, transmissible information — the kind of knowledge that can be written in a textbook, proven to another person, or exchanged between minds.Full definition → honestly. Write down what you know, what you can do, and where your gaps are. This is your character sheet. Then identify one area to level up that genuinely interests you, without worrying yet about how it fits a larger purpose.

  3. Trace the origin of your strongest convictions. Pick one belief or drive that feels central to your sense of purpose. Follow it backward: where did it come from? Was it a formative experience, a wound, a mentor, a moment of recognition? Understanding the source helps you assess whether the conviction is clear-headed or needs more examination.

  4. Separate ego from genuine need. When you feel pulled toward a particular path, ask: what portion of this pull is about being seen, admired, or loved more? What portion is about an actual need you've noticed in the world? Be honest. The ego portion isn't necessarily disqualifying, but it shouldn't be driving.

  5. Do something small for someone who needs it, and notice what happens in you. Not to build a brand — just as an experiment. The internal response (does it feel hollow or genuinely meaningful?) is useful information about alignment.

  6. Read broadly to build your internal compass. Suggested in the talk: Man's Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl, The Count of Monte Cristo, and the Mahabharata — not as scripture but as case studies in people navigating duty, purpose, and the tension between self and world.

  7. Revisit your sense of Dharma periodically, not just once. Set a loose schedule — once or twice a year — to ask yourself whether what you're doing still fits who you are and what you're observing the world asking of you. Expect the answer to shift.
