When a Hard Life Gets Mislabeled

Emergency rooms see patients with six, seven, eight psychiatric diagnoses stacked on their charts. A useful framing for this phenomenon: sometimes people just have a bad life, and then they get labeled. Diagnoses get added on and on, and no one ever removes them.

This matters because the framing changes what you think needs to happen. If someone's distress is fundamentally a psychiatric problem, the solution is psychiatric. But if the root is an exceedingly difficult life — financial instability, unsafe housing, no support system, developmental deprivation — then therapy alone isn't going to cut it, and blaming yourself for not "healing fast enough" is just adding insult to injury.

The Childhood You Weren't Given

One of the quieter devastations in Dom's story gets named directly: he was never allowed to be a child.

We learn who we are and what we want through creative play. You explore, you try things, you find out you're a dog person or a cat person, you discover what excites you and what bores you. None of that happens if you're seven years old running a household while your mom works three jobs, seven days a week.

"You weren't given a childhood. You weren't given an opportunity to play or explore. That in and of itself is devastating."

When that formative exploration never happens, you grow up without a solid sense of self — which shows up later as a kind of shapelessness. Dom described it directly: he adapted his entire personality to whoever he was around to keep the peace, because a happy environment felt safer than an authentic one.

What Untreated ADHD Does to Your Self-Worth

A smart kid with undiagnosed ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition → faces a particular cruelty. You can see clearly that you're just as intelligent as your classmates. You know it. But what takes them an hour takes you four. What comes easily for them is an exhausting slog for you. And because no one has identified why, the only available explanation is: something is wrong with me.

That explanation compounds every year it goes unchallenged. By the time a diagnosis finally arrives in adulthood, there's grief to do — grief for the major you could have studied, the grades you could have earned, the energy you could have saved. That grief is legitimate and it deserves space. But it also tends to curdle into a chronic sense of unworthiness that feels like a personality trait rather than a wound.

Dom was asked whether he was angry about everything that happened to him. His answer was no — more like helplessness and acceptanceAcceptance is the clear-eyed acknowledgment of your present circumstances — not a surrender to them, but the necessary starting point for doing anything about them.Full definition →. But his parents didn't do a good job. He wasn't given a fair start. He worked ten times as hard as his peers for a fraction of the return. That deserves anger. And when that anger has nowhere to go — because getting angry at a mother who was working seven days a week to keep the lights on feels wrong, or a father who was mentally ill — it tends to go inward.

The result is a persistent background hum of "I'm not good enough," "I probably won't get the job," "my skills aren't there yet." Not rage. Not bitterness. Just a quiet, confident self-diminishment that feels like realism.

"As you become angry, as you assign responsibility for your negative situation to others — the point is that honestly, it's not your fault."

That's not about blame. It's about accurate accounting. Locating responsibility correctly is part of how you stop punishing yourself for other people's failures.

The Lifeline Problem

Here's one of the clearest and most practically useful ideas in this whole conversation.

When you've grown up with nothing, and you finally grab something stable — a paycheck, a job, a manager who likes you — you hold on to it with everything you have. You do not let go under any circumstances. Even if the boat is heading toward a waterfall. Even if staying means you never reach shore.

"Once you've got a lifeline, what do you never do? Gamble it. You hold on to it. Even if the boat is going toward the edge of a waterfall and it's going to pull you right over the rocks, you do not let go."

This isn't weakness. It's a rational adaptation to a world where losing the one thing you have means catastrophe with no backup. But it becomes a problem when safety becomes the only value — when "don't lose what I have" crowds out "build something better."

Dom was refusing to apply for UX researcher jobs outside his current company because he feared losing his current stability. But that strategy left him with a single lifeline and no leverage, no alternatives, no way to demonstrate external market value. The "safe" choice was actually the most precarious one — because when you have only one option, you have none.

Financial Security Is Not Separate from Mental Health

This is stated plainly and it's worth sitting with: you can therapize someone who is broke for a decade and not fix the cortisolA stress hormone released by the body during prolonged threat — not the immediate adrenaline spike, but the slower, longer-burning signal meant to keep you alive for the 24–48 hours after a dangerous encounter.Full definition → running through their body.

There's a housing program in New York called Housing First that illustrates this. The old model was: get clean, then get housing. The data said the reverse worked better — give people stable housing, and getting sober becomes easier. Stable external conditions create the foundation for internal change, not the other way around.

"Financial security is so much of mental health."

This isn't an argument against therapy. It's an argument for not treating therapy as a substitute for material stability. If your nervous system is constantly firing because you don't know if you can cover rent, that's not a cognitive distortion. That's an accurate read of a dangerous situation. The goal has to include building real financial security — savings, career growth, options — not just developing a healthier mindset about being broke.

Communicating Across Different Upbringings in a Relationship

Dom's girlfriend grew up in a stable household and wants him to eat better and take care of himself. She's not wrong to want that. But if the gap between their assumptions isn't bridged, that conversation will go badly regardless of how well-intentioned they both are.

She assumes grocery shopping is the obvious solution to not eating well. He knows that buying groceries he might not cook, that might go bad, that represents money he can't recover — is a genuine financial risk, not laziness. She's playing one game; he's playing another.

The practical approach: - Share your full picture — where you came from, where you are, where you want to go - Acknowledge your wins honestly, even when they don't feel like wins - Ask her questions about how she approaches the things she's encouraging you to do — not to argue, but to understand her assumptions - Separate the understanding phase from the planning phase — sometimes you need her to listen, not solve - Be specific about what help actually looks like for you

One small physical detail: don't sit across from each other during hard conversations. Sit next to each other. It changes the dynamic from adversarial to collaborative.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing safety with stability. Having one option feels like security, but it's actually maximum vulnerability. Real security comes from having choices.

Treating every difficult feeling as a psychological problem to be fixed in therapy. Sometimes the stress is accurate. Sometimes the situation actually is precarious. Fix the situation.

Dismissing your own accomplishments because they don't feel like accomplishments. Getting a full-ride scholarship with undiagnosed ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition →, no study skills, financial stress at home, and essentially no parenting is an extraordinary achievement. That it doesn't feel extraordinary is part of the wound, not an accurate assessment.

Letting a partner's encouragement become pressure without first closing the understanding gap. If they don't know what your actual constraints are, their advice won't fit your life, and you'll feel judged instead of supported.

Grieving the alternate life so thoroughly that it blocks movement in the actual one. The grief is real and valid. It also can't be the last stop.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Apply for UX researcher positions outside your current company. Don't negotiate against yourself before anyone else gets a chance to evaluate you. Aim for five to fifteen applications while continuing your current job. External offers don't burn bridges — they often open doors.

  2. Have an honest conversation with your manager. Something like: "I really value working here, and I know we've talked about a UX researcher role. Can you give me a sense of the timeline and likelihood? I want to plan my career thoughtfully, and my preference would be to grow here." An offer in hand from elsewhere strengthens your position internally — it doesn't weaken it.

  3. Start locating your anger accurately. When you notice self-critical thoughts — "I should have figured this out," "why can't I just do this" — try asking: whose job was it to teach me this? Not as a way to stay stuck in blame, but as a way to stop directing punishment at yourself for other people's failures.

  4. Make a factual list of what you've overcome. Not how it felt — what actually happened. No study skills, no parenting support, undiagnosed ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition →, financial precarity, and you got a full-ride scholarship and finished college. Write it out as if you were describing someone else. Then read it.

  5. Before the next health-related conversation with your girlfriend, share your actual context. Not the elevator version — the real one. Where you came from, what your constraints actually are, and what you do want for yourself. Then ask her questions about her own process before jumping to planning.

  6. Set a concrete financial target as a mental health goal. Identify what "enough financial security to experiment" looks like for you — a specific savings number, a specific income level — and treat reaching it as a legitimate part of your wellbeing, not separate from it.
