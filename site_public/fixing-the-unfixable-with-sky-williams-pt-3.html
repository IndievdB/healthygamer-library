Summary

TL;DR — The deepest reason you can't feel loved isn't a character flaw; it's a survival strategy your mind built to protect you from a world where you once had no control. This session works through manipulation, self-blame, and the terrifying prospect of letting other people actually see you.

This is part three of an ongoing conversation with Sky Williams, a content creator working through depression, debt, isolation, and a history of childhood sexual abuse. The session moves from a practical life change (Sky finally moving out of an unhealthy living situation) through a frank examination of manipulation, and then into much deeper territory: a lifelong belief that everything that has ever happened to him is entirely his own fault — including being raped at fifteen.

Why You Manipulate (and What's Actually Driving It)

Manipulation isn't primarily about malice. At its core, manipulation means tailoring information to control another person's behavior. And the reason anyone wants to control another person's behavior is almost always fear — fear of what happens if you don't.

Once you understand that, you have a simple internal compass for detecting your own manipulation: look for fear. When you notice yourself shading a story, omitting information, or carefully framing how you present yourself, ask what you're afraid will happen if you don't. The fear is the signal.

This also explains something counterintuitive:

"Some of the times when you're the most manipulative is when you tell the truth."

Telling the truth isn't automatically honest. If you're selecting which true things to share in order to produce a specific reaction in someone, that's still manipulation. The intent to control the outcome is what matters, not whether the individual facts are accurate.

For Sky, years of financial "generosity" — paying rent for everyone, absorbing debt — was partly manipulation in this sense. He never disclosed how unsustainable it was because he was afraid people would call him an idiot. He was telling true things while carefully concealing the full picture, to control what people thought of him.

The Logic of Extreme Self-Blame

Sky holds a belief so total it's almost a physical law for him: everything that happens to me, good or bad, is a direct result of my actions. When he tries to imagine a world where that isn't true, it sounds like Harry Potter flying in through the window — not just unlikely, but structurally impossible.

This belief feels like it should be a flaw, but it actually does real work for him. It provides:

  • A world that makes sense. If your actions produce outcomes, the world is legible. You hit the spacebar, the character jumps. Every time.
  • Hope and control. If everything bad is your fault, then learning and improving means you can eventually build a safe, loved, comfortable life. You are never truly helpless.

The cost is that it requires you to absorb blame for things that genuinely were not your fault — including things done to you as a child.

This is not stupidity. It's actually the cognitive structure of someone who learned early that having a coherent, controllable world was the only way to stay safe.

Why Abused Kids End Up Blaming Themselves (Theory of Mind)

Young children don't yet have a fully developed theory of mind — the ability to recognize that other people have their own independent thoughts, feelings, and motivations. Before that capacity develops, a child is effectively playing a single-player game. Everyone else functions like an NPC. The child is the only real agent.

In a single-player world, if something terrible happens, there's only one person who could be responsible: you.

When abuse happens during these years, or even in adolescence when the psychological groundwork has already been laid, the brain has no architecture for processing "another person with their own intentions chose to do something harmful to me." The only available explanation is self-blame — and that explanation can calcify into a permanent worldview.

Sky intellectually knows, when presented with a hypothetical victim, that a person who was assaulted is not at fault. But that knowledge doesn't extend to himself. The double standard isn't hypocrisy — it's the exact shape of how this pattern works.

The Hidden Cost: You Can't Be Loved in a Single-Player World

If you're the only agent who matters, love becomes impossible to actually receive. You can't tell the difference between someone genuinely caring about you and someone responding to how skillfully you've managed their impression of you.

"You've never given an opportunity for someone to love you. So how can you possibly be loved? Because at this point you're just fake."

The protection mechanism creates a closed loop. You control the image people see → people respond to the image → you can't trust the response → you have no evidence that the real you is lovable → you control the image more tightly. You never get data to the contrary.

The same logic applies to other areas. Sky has never put 100% into his creative work, because then he'd always have the explanation that he didn't really try. If you hold something back, failure isn't really failure. But it also means success isn't really success, and effort isn't really effort.

The Thing That Actually Requires Courage

The exit from this system isn't insight — it's risk. Specifically, the risk of:

  • Letting other people see you as you actually are
  • Letting them form their own opinion without you managing it
  • Being willing to be hated
  • Being willing to be loved — and having to sit with the fact that it's real

That last one sounds easy. It isn't. If someone loves you despite you doing nothing to earn it, despite all your flaws being visible, that experience directly contradicts the belief that you are fundamentally unlovable. And that contradiction is destabilizing in a way that the comfortable certainty of self-loathing is not.

"There's even a part of you that actually doubts that you're actually a piece of [garbage], but you would rather sit in the comfort of the thought that you're a piece of [garbage] rather than risk the chance that it could be wrong."

Common Pitfalls

Thinking manipulation always involves lying. You can manipulate entirely with true statements by controlling which truths are visible and which aren't.

Treating self-blame as humility or accountability. Total self-blame that extends to things outside your control isn't accountability — it's a control mechanism. Real accountability is specific and accurate, not a blanket absorption of everything that's ever gone wrong.

Expecting insight to feel like relief. When Sky has a breakthrough moment in this session, it doesn't feel good — it feels like an "existential grunt," a short-circuit. Recognizing something true about yourself can be disorienting and even physically uncomfortable before it becomes useful.

Thinking "I know this intellectually" is the same as believing it. Sky can articulate that a 15-year-old assault victim isn't responsible for what happened. He cannot apply that same reasoning to himself. The gap between intellectual knowledge and felt belief is where this work actually lives.

Avoiding the meditation or practice because it feels small. The Om breathing exercise at the end isn't a magic cure. What it produces is a few moments of experiencing yourself as something other than broken. That's the point. You need repeated small experiences of a different self-perception before a new worldview can take root.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice fear before you speak. When you're about to share something — a story, an explanation, a justification — pause and ask: am I afraid of something right now? If yes, name what you're afraid of. That's the manipulation signal. You don't have to change your behavior immediately; just notice it.

  2. Do the Om breathing practice daily, 3–5 minutes, nine rounds. Sit quietly. Take a deep breath and extend the sound across three syllables: Ah — Ooh — Mm, focusing on the physical vibration in your body rather than the sound itself. Do nine of these. After you finish, sit for a moment and notice how you feel about yourself in that moment — before the usual mental chatter returns.

  3. Separate the two questions: "Is this my fault?" and "What can I do now?" Extreme self-blame collapses these into one. Practice asking them as separate questions. The first is about cause; the second is about agency going forward. You can take meaningful action without needing everything to be your fault.

  4. Find one relationship where you deliberately withhold less. You don't have to disclose everything to everyone. But pick one person and let them see one real thing about you that you'd normally manage or hide — and then wait to see how they respond without pre-empting it.

  5. If there's a meditation center or group near you, go. Practicing in community with other people helps maintain consistency and makes the practice feel less isolated than doing it alone in your room.

  6. When you miss a day (or a week), just pick it back up. Missing the practice is not evidence that you're unfixable. It's a normal part of trying something new. Don't let a break become an abandonment.
