Summary

TL;DR — Depression isn't always a broken brain. It's often a rational response to a genuinely difficult world, and taking on too much personal blame for systemic problems makes everything worse.

Most conversations about depression focus on the individual: your neurochemistry, your mindset, your habits. This talk flips that frame. Depression has evolutionary roots that make it functional, not just pathological. It also has social causes — structural, economic, technological — that no amount of individual self-improvement can fix. Understanding which type of depression you're dealing with, and how much responsibility is actually yours, turns out to be one of the most important levers you have.

Why Sadness Exists in the First Place

The brain didn't develop the capacity for sadness by accident. Negative emotions had to provide survival benefits, otherwise evolution would have selected them out.

  • Anxiety and worry let you predict future problems and change your behavior before they arrive. The squirrel burying food for winter is a simple version of this.
  • Sadness after social rejection acts as an internally imposed punishment — it steers you away from behavior that gets you ostracized. People with antisocial personality disorder, who feel little guilt or sadness, end up overrepresented in prison precisely because they lack this internal brake.
  • Grief serves a specific social function: it brings people together around a shared loss, allows venting of negative emotion, and rebuilds connection. Funeral rituals appear across virtually every human culture and even in some animals. The grieving process is so biologically conserved because it actually works — it moves people forward. Those who skip it (the person still fixated on being captain of the football team 25 years later) often get stuck.

The takeaway: feeling sad is not automatically a sign something has gone wrong. It is often a sign your mind is working exactly as designed.

Three Mechanisms That Drive Depression

1. Identity and the hyperactive default mode network

The default mode network is the part of the brain responsible for self-reflection. In people who are depressed, it runs on overdrive and won't shut off. The result is a stream of thoughts focused entirely on yourself — specifically, on what you did wrong.

A healthy mind, after a conflict with someone else, asks: What did they do, and what did I do? A depressed mind asks only: What did I do? It readily accepts 100% of the blame for any problem, regardless of whether that's accurate.

This is why a person living at home at 28 — in a housing market where over half of people under 29 still live with their parents — will often experience that as a personal failure rather than a structural reality.

2. Cognitive distortions

Depression warps perception in predictable ways. A classic example: if you ask a depressed person whether their struggling friend should see a therapist, they'll say yes, absolutely. Ask if they should see one: "No, I don't deserve it. Someone else needs that slot more than me."

Other common distortions: there's no point trying because it won't work anyway; other people can succeed at this but I can't; everything that goes wrong is my fault.

What the internet and social media do is take these individual cognitive distortions and amplify them at scale. When a community of people all reinforce the same distorted belief, it can harden into something closer to a collective delusion — far more resistant to correction than any one person's private thought.

3. Congruent (appropriate) depression

Sometimes depression isn't a malfunction. Sometimes the future genuinely is bleak, and the brain is accurately reporting that.

"Sometimes it's actually appropriate to your life. If I get caught cheating in school and I get kicked out, and I've got two and a half years of loans but I'm not going to get a degree — it's appropriate to be depressed. This is not a malfunction of the mind."

This is called congruent depression (some clinicians call it existential depression). It's distinct from the kind driven by neurochemical imbalance. Importantly, this type tends not to respond well to antidepressant medication — because the instrument measuring your life isn't broken. The measurements are just bleak, because things actually are bleak.

The honest scientific reality: we don't actually know that depression is caused by a neurochemical imbalance. We measure it through questionnaires and clinical interviews, not brain biopsies or spinal taps. We know that raising certain neurotransmitters helps many people — but that doesn't prove the root cause was the chemistry. A phone call telling you a loved one is in the hospital produces a psychological impact first; neurochemical changes follow. Which one is the root? Psychoneuroimmunology research suggests the mind often comes before the brain chemistry, not after.

Also worth knowing: roughly 60% of people who experience depression recover without any medical intervention. That's not an argument against treatment — it's a clue that the causes are broader than brain chemistry alone.

The World Is Actually Making You More Depressed

Depression isn't just getting more common because individuals are weaker. The rate has roughly tripled in recent years. When something shifts on a generational scale, the cause is generational, not individual.

Several structural forces are actively fertilizing depression right now:

  • Economic reality vs. perceived personal failure. More than half of people under 29 live at home — not because they're pathetic, but because wage growth and housing costs have diverged dramatically over 30 years. Yet each of those people, in a depressed state, experiences their situation as a personal shortcoming.
  • Dating apps that were supposed to make finding a partner easier appear to have made it harder — yet people blame themselves for being unable to find a match on a system that may not be optimized for that outcome.
  • Social media comparisons constantly expose you to curated, filtered versions of other people's lives, accelerating the inadequacy loop that depression runs on. Body dysmorphia is rising sharply in men, a population that historically was largely insulated from it.
  • News media incentives reward fear. The brain has a negativity bias, so scary content gets more clicks. Media learns this and serves up more of it. You build tolerance and need something scarier. The result is a world that feels far more catastrophically bleak than it actually is.
  • Repeated large-scale shocks — financial crises, a pandemic, geopolitical instability — pile up in a way that leaves little psychological recovery time between them.

"Oddly enough, part of the problem right now is that individuals are accepting too much responsibility for societal problems."

The Trap of Taking Too Much Responsibility

This is the most counterintuitive point: the push to "take personal responsibility" — usually framed as healthy and mature — can backfire badly when it's applied to problems that aren't actually yours to fix.

When you absorb blame for a structural problem, two things happen. First, you feel worse, because you're holding yourself accountable for something you can't change. Second, you inadvertently let the actual source of the problem off the hook. In a relationship where one person accepts 100% of the fault, the other person has no pressure to change.

The parallel scales up. When individuals absorb guilt for, say, environmental destruction, the organizations with the actual leverage to change outcomes face less pressure.

"No snowflake thinks it's responsible for the avalanche, but we've seen how individual people taking small actions and then other people piling on can actually affect real change."

You do have agency — but it's proportional. Vote. Speak up. Join with others. Those are real levers. Flagellating yourself for systemic failures you didn't cause is not.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming your depression is definitely neurochemical (or definitely not). You can't tell from the inside, and neither assumption is safe. The only way to sort it out is professional evaluation.
  • Treating congruent depression with medication alone. If the problem is that your life circumstances are genuinely difficult, an antidepressant adjusts the instrument without fixing what the instrument is measuring. Dysthymia and existential depression tend to respond much better to life-building than to SSRIs.
  • Reading your internet feed as an accurate sample of reality. People in stable, happy relationships are not hanging out on dating advice forums. The selection bias in online spaces is massive. Bleak communities produce bleak data that feels representative but isn't.
  • Confusing self-compassion with avoiding responsibility. Accepting less blame for things outside your control is not the same as being irresponsible. Calibrating blame accurately frees up energy for the things you can actually change.
  • Waiting for certainty before getting help. You don't need to know whether your depression is "the chemical kind" or "the circumstantial kind" before seeing someone. That's exactly what an evaluation is for.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Get a professional evaluation before self-diagnosing your type of depression. You cannot reliably tell from the inside whether your low mood is driven by neurochemistry, circumstances, or both. See a mental health professional — not necessarily to get treatment, just to get assessed.

  2. Do a responsibility audit. When you catch yourself feeling guilty or ashamed about something, write it down and ask honestly: What percentage of this is actually mine? Try to assign a realistic number. Depression will push you toward 100%. Push back.

  3. Notice when you accept blame that belongs elsewhere. In relationships, at work, in how you explain your circumstances to yourself — practice asking whether you're carrying weight that belongs to someone or something else. Name it explicitly; don't just feel vaguely bad.

  4. Identify your actual sphere of influence and use it. You can't fix the housing market alone, but you can vote, organize, and make small choices that, compounded with others, create pressure for change. Write down one or two levers you actually have in an area that feels hopeless.

  5. Curate your information environment. Deliberately seek out sources that show you progress alongside problems. Recognize the selection bias in whatever communities you frequent online. The fact that everyone in a forum is struggling does not mean everyone in the world is.

  6. If you're stuck on a loss or a past version of yourself, find someone to grieve with. The biological function of grief requires social contact. Talking to someone who shares the loss — or even just shares the experience of loss — is not just emotional release. It's how the brain actually processes and moves forward.

  7. If medication has been suggested and you're hesitant, consider a time-limited trial. Roughly a third of people respond dramatically to antidepressants. If you're in that group, taking a pill for a year could change your life significantly — and you can work with a doctor on tapering off afterward. The fear of dependence shouldn't automatically override a potential major quality-of-life gain.
