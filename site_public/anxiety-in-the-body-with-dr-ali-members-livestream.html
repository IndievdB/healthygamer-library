Anxiety manifests as real, physical sensations — racing heart, muscle tension, nausea, brain fog, shallow breathing, tingling, derealization, even hives. These aren't signs that something is broken in you. They're the outputs of a system that evolved to keep you alive, and understanding that changes everything about how you relate to them.

This piece walks through the biology of the fear response, why anxiety in the body is so hard to suppress, a practical framework (the BRAVER model) for working with it, and a separate section on body-focused repetitive behaviors like hair pulling and skin picking.

Why Your Body Does This (And Why It Isn't Broken)

Fear exists to keep you alive. Your ancestors needed it to run from predators, back away from cliff edges, and stay sharp in genuinely dangerous situations. When a real threat appears, your body transforms rapidly:

  • Heart rate spikes to pump oxygen to your muscles
  • Breathing quickens to bring in more oxygen
  • Muscles tense so you're ready to act
  • Digestion shuts down (food can wait — survival can't)
  • Focus narrows to the threat

This is the fight-or-flight response, and there's also a freeze response (playing dead, staying completely still) and a tend-and-befriend response (protecting others, forming alliances). All of these are adaptive. Four or more of these sensations coming on rapidly is what's called a panic attack. Experiencing them frequently, or living in fear of them, can become panic disorder — something worth discussing with a professional.

The problem with anxiety isn't the response itself. It's that the response fires when there's no actual lion in front of you. Your body runs the full danger protocol for something you're imagining might happen, or for a sensation you noticed inside yourself. That last part is especially important: you notice your heart rate, get anxious about it, notice it more, get more anxious — a feedback loop that makes the sensation feel worse than it would have otherwise.

Why Anxiety Is Not Your Fault (But Is Your Problem)

The tendency toward anxiety comes from three sources:

  1. Biology — Some people are simply born with the volume turned up on their fear response. Higher resting activation, more intense emotional reactions. This is temperament, and it runs in families.

  2. Environment and culture — You learned how to handle emotions from your family, your culture, the language available to you. You can't fully perceive an emotion you don't have a word for. What emotions get expressed, how they're expressed, what you do with them — all of it is shaped by the environment you were raised in.

  3. Experience — Your mind is an association machine. It pairs intense emotional states with whatever happened to be present at the time, even randomly. A man got sick, took cold medication, drank too much caffeine, and drove over a bridge while feeling terrible — and from that point on, bridges triggered panic. His mind connected the dots. Yours does the same.

A fourth factor: skill gaps. If anxiety has led you to avoid certain situations, you never developed the skills those situations would have taught you. AvoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → feeds on itself.

None of this is a character flaw. It just means you have a particular kind of nervous system, shaped by particular circumstances, and you haven't yet had the chance to learn what actually helps.

The BRAVER Model: A Framework for Working With Anxiety

B — Befriend Your Anxiety

The most counterproductive thing you can do is treat anxiety as a villain to defeat. Research bears this out directly: in a study where participants were put in a room with elevated CO2 levels (which can trigger panic), three groups received different instructions. The group told to suppress and fight their sensations did worst — more intense panic, less ability to stay in the situation. The group told to accept and let it be did best.

Suppression fails because it hijacks your attention. All your resources go toward pushing the sensation down and hiding it from others — nothing is left to actually engage with what's in front of you.

A more useful frame: think of your anxiety like an overdramatic but well-meaning friend. C-3PO is annoying. Nobody lists him as their favorite Star Wars character. But you don't throw him out — he has your best interests at heart. You don't have to listen to everything he says. You soothe him, acknowledge him, and sometimes tell him: I know you're trying to help, but I've got this.

When your heart starts racing, instead of fighting it, try: This is my anxiety. I'm feeling this because I'm doing something that matters to me.

R — Remember What Matters

You need something worth being anxious for. Without that, there's no reason to push through discomfort. This comes from Acceptance and Commitment TherapyAn evidence-based psychotherapy, rooted in cognitive behavioral therapy but distinct from it, that pairs radical acceptance of what can't be changed with active commitment to personal values.Full definition → (ACT): if you don't have clear valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →, your default value becomes safety, and safety will always point you back home, away from risk, away from growth.

Values are not goals. Goals change. Values are the kind of person you want to be — they're the compass, not the destination. You can drive cross-country using only your headlights, seeing ten feet ahead at a time, as long as you know which direction to head.

Three ways to find your values:

  1. Imagine your 75th birthday. What stories are people telling about you? What do you want to be known for?
  2. Use a values sort card exercise (printable PDFs are widely available) — sort values by importance until you land on three to five.
  3. Think of someone you genuinely admire. What qualities do you respect in them? Those qualities are clues.

Your values will change over time, and that's fine. What matters is having current clarity, because that's what gives you a reason to face discomfort.

A — Assess Your Thoughts

Most anxious thoughts are background noise that come and go — unless you grab on to them. People who struggle with anxiety tend to give enormous weight and credibility to whatever thought surfaces, especially thoughts about physical sensations.

The goal here isn't to argue with your thoughts or replace them with positive ones. It's to get less stuck on the internal universe and more present in the actual moment.

Two reframes that research supports:

  • "I'm excited" — Fear and excitement are physiologically identical: racing heart, sweating, tunnel focus. The only difference is interpretation. Telling yourself my body is getting excited because something important is happening has been shown to reduce anxiety intensity and redirect attention outward.
  • "I'm built for this" — Your sympathetic nervous systemThe body's fight-or-flight activation system — one half of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes you for intense physical action.Full definition → is doing exactly what it's designed to do. After activation, the parasympathetic system will kick in and bring you back down. Your body knows how to do this.

(Note: if you're ever genuinely worried about a heart attack — especially if the pain spreads toward the jaw or follows physical exertion — see a doctor. Emergency rooms can distinguish between anxiety and cardiac events quickly.)

V — View the Bigger Picture

Whatever you focus on in your body, you notice more. Anxiety hijacks attention inward; the goal is to zoom back out.

Distraction skills from Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) are not avoidance — they're a brief break from your overdramatic friend, so you can come back with more bandwidth:

  • Strong sensory inputs: smell is the fastest sense (the pathway from nose to brain is very short), which makes scents like lavender particularly effective
  • Loud or engaging music
  • Funny videos — creating a different emotional experience actively undoes the physiological state of anxiety
  • Tactile objects (soft textures, etc.)

The goal isn't to escape anxiety permanently. It's to create enough distance that you can re-engage more effectively.

E — Expand Through Experience

"You cannot think your way out of anxiety."

The deepest and most lasting change comes from experience, not insight. Think of anxiety like a covered pot of boiling water. You have three options: remove the pot (avoidance), let it boil over (the "caged lion" — yelling, getting angry, losing control), or remove the lid a little and let some steam out. The last option is where these skills come in.

Emergency anxiety skills — things that bring the pressure down fast:

  1. Big physical movements — Walk, do jumping jacks, vacuum, garden. You have rocket fuel in your body; give it somewhere to go. Sitting still with full activation is one of the worst things you can do.
  2. Progressive muscle relaxation — Intentionally tense a muscle group, hold it for a few seconds, then release. The relaxation response after release is measurable. You can do this invisibly at a desk or in a meeting.
  3. Grounding — Pour your attention into something specific and engaging: five senses, a ranked list of things you love, imagining navigating a familiar place in detail. The goal is to absorb yourself in something outside your internal state.
  4. The dive reflex — When the face (specifically the area covered by a ski mask — eyes and nose) is submerged in cold water, the body triggers a reflex that immediately lowers heart rate to conserve oxygen. Hold for 15–30 seconds. This is a fast, powerful tool for high-intensity anxiety. (Talk to a doctor first if you have a heart condition.)
  5. Sensory shock — Cold shower, head in a freezer, strong peppermint or sour candy. Anything that grabs your nervous system's attention and pulls it outward.
  6. Slow breathing — Rebalances CO2 levels, which directly triggers the relaxation response. Keep it simple: breathe in for five counts, hold briefly, out for five. (Caveat: for some people, focusing on breathing increases anxiety. Experiment.)

For longer-term growth: interoceptive exposures — deliberately triggering the physical sensations you fear in a controlled way (jumping jacks before a practice presentation, spinning in a chair if you fear dizziness, wearing a heavy jacket in a warm room if you fear sweating). The goal is to learn, under your own control, what actually happens — and to find out that you can survive it.

Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviors: Hair Pulling, Skin Picking, Nail Biting

Body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs) — including trichotillomania (hair pulling), skin picking, and nail biting — affect roughly 1 in 50 people, far more common than previously recognized.

These are not simply anxiety behaviors. They're triggered by the full emotional spectrum: stress, yes, but also boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition → (the most common trigger), anger, excitement, and others. They work — they rapidly change how you feel — but with significant side effects: lost time, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, harm to appearance, and for people who ingest pulled hair, a serious medical risk (hair balls called trichobezoars can block the intestines — see a doctor if this applies to you).

The SCAMP model (developed by Dr. Charlie Mansueto) identifies five intervention categories:

  • S — Sensory: Find other sensory inputs that meet the same need. Scalp massage, combing, running rice through your fingers, changing the texture of your hair with product.
  • C — Cognitive: Challenge unhelpful thoughts (hair grows back; skin heals; this doesn't make you broken).
  • A — Affective (emotional): Match the emotion to a skill. Boredom → find something absorbing. Excitement → express it differently. Anger → movement, distraction.
  • M — Motor: Keep hands occupied or create awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → barriers. Rubber fingertips, gloves, Band-Aids, essential oils on wrists so you notice when hands approach your face, Silly Putty to pull instead.
  • P — Place: Change your environment. Cover mirrors that trigger picking. Move tweezers somewhere inconvenient. Reduce access to the trigger.

The underlying issue is the same as with anxiety: the normal homeostatic mechanisms — the body's thermostats — aren't working smoothly. The goal isn't to eliminate the urge but to meet the underlying need in a different way.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to destroy anxiety — The harder you fight it, the more resources it consumes and the stronger it gets. This is well-supported by research and consistently backfires.
  • Confusing "getting better" with "feeling less" — The goal is to function according to your values, not to feel nothing. Anxiety is not the enemy.
  • Overusing mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition → for worry and ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → — For people who already overthink, turning attention further inward often amplifies the problem. Many anxiety sufferers need more external focus, not more internal awareness.
  • Optimization culture thinking — Cold plunges, miracle mornings, supplement stacks, journaling rituals — these can become another form of anxious control-seeking. They can also increase isolation. What actually helps most people is connection and outward engagement.
  • Waiting until you feel ready — You won't think your way to readiness. The learning only happens in the experience.
  • Mistaking values for goals — Goals change and can be reached. Values are the kind of person you're trying to be. They guide day-to-day decisions even when the destination is unclear.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write a letter to your anxiety as if it were an overdramatic friend. Acknowledge what it's trying to do for you, validate the intention, and tell it what you actually need right now. Keep it somewhere accessible.

  2. Do the 75th birthday exercise. Imagine your friends, family, and colleagues celebrating you. What stories are they telling? What do you want to be known for? Write down whatever comes up, then try to distill it into three to five values.

  3. Practice progressive muscle relaxation once today. Start with just your fists: squeeze hard for 5–7 seconds, then release. Notice the difference. Work through one or two other muscle groups (face, shoulders, feet). Do this before bed or when you notice tension building.

  4. Identify your go-to grounding anchor. Pick a category of things you know well and love — spaceships, albums, restaurant dishes, players on a team — and practice running through a ranked list until you have to actually think. Use this the next time anxiety pulls you inward.

  5. Try the dive reflex once. Fill a bowl or sink with cold water. Take a breath, submerge the ski-mask area of your face (eyes and nose) for 15–30 seconds. Notice what happens to your heart rate. (Skip this if you have a heart condition.)

  6. Pick one SCAMP strategy for any BFRBs you experience. If boredom is your trigger, identify one absorbing activity to reach for instead. If it's sensory-driven, experiment with one substitute sensation (scalp massage, textured object, etc.).

  7. The next time you notice a physical anxiety symptom, say to yourself: "This is my anxiety. I'm feeling this because I'm doing something that matters to me." Don't try to make it go away — just name it and ask what the next one step forward is.

  8. Design one interoceptive exposure when you're ready. Pick a physical sensation you fear (racing heart, dizziness, sweating). Find the exercise that produces it (jumping jacks, spinning, heavy jacket). Rate your fear of what might happen (0–100%). Do the exercise. Note what actually happened. Re-rate your belief. Repeat.
