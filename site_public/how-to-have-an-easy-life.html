Summary

TL;DR — Life feels hard not because it's inherently hard, but because no one taught you how to live it. The ancient yogic concept of the three gunas offers a practical framework for understanding why some days feel effortless and others feel impossible — and what you can actually do about it.

Most people assume that some lives are just easier than others — that money, luck, or circumstance explain the difference. This assumption has a quiet cost: it makes us dismiss anyone who claims life can get easier, which means we never learn from them. The actual gap between a hard life and an easy one isn't luck. It's knowledge — specifically, knowing how to live.

Science, for all its value, doesn't fill that gap. Studies can tell you what correlates with happiness across a population — income level, social connection, green space — but they can't tell you how to get those things. Population averages say nothing about what works for a specific human being, which is why medicine still requires clinicians, not just textbooks, to treat individual patients. Science gives you the destination. It doesn't hand you a map.

The deeper problem is that we invest almost no deliberate effort in learning how to live. A student studying calculus spends roughly 216 focused hours in a single year just on that one subject. How many comparable hours has anyone spent studying how to manage their own mind and body? Almost none. We get "bodied" by life and cope as best we can, never pausing to learn intentionally.

Why Hard and Easy Aren't Fixed Categories

The difference between something being hard and easy comes down to one thing: whether you know how to do it. Changing a tire is easy if you know how, impossible if you don't. Building a PC, managing an addiction, doing calculus — each of these moves from "impossible" to "I don't even think about it anymore" once someone learns the relevant skills and practices them enough.

This is an important reframe. "Objectively hard" problems — including opioid addiction, which involves real physiological withdrawal — become manageable, even routine, for people who have worked through the right process. The difficulty wasn't fixed. It was a function of not yet knowing how.

The Three Gunas: Why Some Days Are Just Easier

The ancient yogic tradition identified three fundamental qualities, called gunas, that describe the state of any person's mind and body at any moment:

  • Tamas — inertia, heaviness, sluggishness. The force that keeps things still.
  • Rajas — activity, passion, restless energy. The force that drives movement.
  • Sattva — balance, clarity, ease. The state where the two opposing forces are in equilibrium.

You already know what each of these feels like. Tamasic days are the ones where getting out of bed takes everything you have, where your mind moves like it's pushing through mud. Rajasic days are the ones where you're buzzing with excitement — buying all the gear for a new hobby, firing in twenty directions at once, burning out by noon. And then there are those rare, almost unexplainable days where you just... get things done. No drama. No forcing. That's sattva.

The goal is to spend more of your life in that third state.

Why Motivation and Willpower Make Things Harder

The conventional advice — chase motivation, build willpower — actually points you in the wrong direction.

Motivation is an attempt to harness rajas: you're hoping your mind will happen to want what you want it to do, so you don't have to force it. But this reveals the problem immediately. If you need your mind to feel like doing something before you can do it, you're not in control — you're a passenger hoping the driver picks the right road. Motivation waxes and wanes. Relying on it means your productivity is hostage to something you didn't earn and can't sustain.

Willpower is the antidote to tamas: brute-forcing an inert mind and body into action. It works, sometimes, but it's expensive. Cognitive exertion drains mental energy the same way physical exertion drains physical energy. A life built on willpower is exhausting by design. You may accomplish things, but it'll grind you down.

Neither approach builds a life that feels easy. Both are compensations for an unbalanced mind, not solutions to it.

Building a Sattvic Mind

The actual lever isn't motivation or willpower. It's cultivating sattva — a balanced, clear, responsive mind that will actually follow your intentions. Three practices move you in that direction:

1. Food What you eat has a direct effect on the state of your mind. Heavy, calorie-dense, processed food — anything combining refined carbs and fat — creates a kind of mental fog, increasing inflammation and slowing cognitive function. Light, whole, nutritious food (vegetables, fruit, home-cooked meals) doesn't weigh the mind down. The goal isn't hitting a precise calorie target. The goal is food that doesn't dulling your mental clarity.

2. Mind-body practice Yoga or tai chi aren't primarily about flexibility. They're about training the mind to focus and to follow direction. When you hold a balance pose, your mind must stay present or you fall. You're literally practicing the act of directing your own attention. The more you do this, the more "pliable" the mind becomes — more willing to go where you point it. This is different from ordinary exercise, though movement in general helps. The key element is the mind-body connection, the conscious directing of attention under mild stress.

3. Reducing psychoactive substances Caffeine, alcohol, cannabis, and even many psychiatric medications work by artificially pushing the mind toward one end of the tamas-rajas spectrum or the other. Caffeine doesn't create energy; it blocks the receptors that detect fatigue — you borrow alertness now and pay it back later. Alcohol and cannabis suppress rajas by inducing a tamasic state, which is exactly why they feel relaxing. But none of these produce sattva. They produce chemical imbalance. Almost everyone who cuts out psychoactive substances for 30–60 days reports feeling better. The mind stops ping-ponging and begins to settle.

The Compounding Logic of Satvic Days

You don't have to do all of this perfectly or all at once. The key insight is that satvic actions today make it easier to take satvic actions tomorrow. It works like compound interest, or a chain of dominoes.

Start on a rajasic day — a day when you have some energy and activation — and use that energy to eat well, do some yoga, skip the afternoon coffee. The next day, your gut isn't inflamed, your body isn't in withdrawal, your mind didn't spend the night battling stimulant crash. You wake up 5% more balanced. From that slightly more balanced state, it's a little easier to eat well again. Do yoga again. Ease off the substances again. Each small investment makes the next one slightly less effortful.

The direction matters more than perfection.

Common Pitfalls

  • Dismissing the premise. The automatic reaction — "easy for you to say, you don't know my life" — shuts down learning before it starts. Hard circumstances are real. But treating difficulty as fixed and unteachable guarantees it stays that way.

  • Confusing convenience with ease. Having food delivered and a device that can do everything doesn't make life easier in any meaningful sense. Convenience and a balanced inner life are unrelated, which is why technological progress and the mental health crisis have risen together.

  • Chasing the right feelings before doing the work. Waiting until you feel motivated to act is exactly backwards. Sattva isn't a feeling you wait for — it's a state you build through deliberate, repeated practice.

  • Treating substances as neutral tools. Using caffeine to cover fatigue or alcohol to manage anxiety doesn't solve the underlying imbalance. It swings the pendulum further in the opposite direction and makes the eventual crash worse.

  • Wanting an easy present at the cost of a hard future. Avoiding effort, skipping exercise, staying in comfort — these feel like choosing ease, but they compound into a harder life. The people who end up with genuinely easier lives are the ones willing to embrace difficulty today.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Track your guna state each morning. For one week, spend 30 seconds when you wake up noting whether you feel tamasic (heavy, sluggish), rajasic (restless, scattered), or sattvic (clear, calm). Just observing the pattern builds the self-awareness the framework requires.

  2. Identify one rajasic day this week and invest it. When you notice you have energy and drive, don't burn it all on the exciting new thing. Use part of it intentionally: cook a real meal, do 15–20 minutes of yoga or tai chi, and skip one psychoactive substance you'd normally reach for.

  3. Replace afternoon caffeine with a short nap. If afternoon fatigue is your cue to reach for coffee, try lying down for 10–20 minutes instead. You're listening to the body rather than overriding it, which is a sattvic habit in itself.

  4. Do a 30-day substance audit. Pick one psychoactive substance — caffeine, alcohol, cannabis — and take a break from it for 30 days. Notice how your baseline mental state shifts. You don't have to commit to abstinence forever; the point is to feel what your actual, unmediated mind is like.

  5. Start a Mind-body practice, even minimally. Three rounds of Surya Namaskar (sun salutations) takes under ten minutes. The goal isn't fitness — it's the practiced experience of directing your attention and holding it there. Do it on days you feel rajasic or reasonably balanced; use it to build mental pliability over time.

  6. Audit what you're eating on hard days. Next time you have a day where everything feels impossible, look back at what you ate in the 24 hours prior. Notice whether heavily processed, calorie-dense food preceded the mental fog. Use that as feedback, not guilt.

  7. Invest difficulty now, deliberately. Choose one thing you've been avoiding because it's hard — a difficult conversation, a workout, a project you've been procrastinating on — and do it on a day when you have reasonable energy. Recognize that this friction today is the actual mechanism for making tomorrow easier.
