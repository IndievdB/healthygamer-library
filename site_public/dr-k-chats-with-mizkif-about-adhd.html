The Memory Myth: It's Not Forgetting, It's Not Entering

One of the most relieving reframes in the conversation: people with ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by disordered attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition → don't have bad memories. English misleads you here. When you say "I forgot to close the fridge," the word forget implies something went wrong with retrieval — pulling something out of the library of your mind. But your retrieval is fine. The problem is that the book never made it onto the shelf. Your mind was already somewhere else when the door was left open, so there was nothing to remember.

This distinction matters practically. The goal isn't to improve your memory. It's to improve how much information gets in. And the conversation itself demonstrates exactly how to do that.

Writing Slows the Mind Down

There's a concrete reason writing helps attention and memory: it physically slows the speed of thought.

A thought that takes two seconds to think also takes two seconds to say, but many more seconds to write. During those extra seconds, your mind is sitting on that thought — and the longer a thought is held, the deeper it sinks. Writing is not just a memory aid. It is a mechanism for reducing the speed of a racing mind.

The same principle applies to focusing on a single object (a keyboard key, a picture on the wall). By anchoring attention to something sensory, the mental chatter quiets. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Design low-stimulus environments for important conversations. Before any conversation that matters — with a partner, a friend, a family member — get somewhere with minimal visual and auditory noise. If you're already somewhere distracting, you can say: "What you're saying is important to me. Can we go somewhere quieter so I can actually listen?"

  2. Write things down during or after conversations. Not to keep records — to slow your mind down. The act of writing forces you to hold a thought longer than speaking or listening does, which is when information actually sinks in.

  3. Notice any sensation of warmth or tingling in your forehead; focus your attention there. When thoughts arise, return to the sensation. Start with 3 minutes. Build slowly. Do it at the same time and place each day to avoid feeding the vataOne of the three Ayurvedic doshas, Vata is governed by the elements of wind and ether — making it cold, dry, light, and intensely mobile in nature.Full definition → tendency toward randomness.

  4. Build a multi-task rotation, not a single-task grind. Identify 3–4 meaningful goals or projects (streaming, a game project, a YouTube channel, your relationship). When your attention naturally wanders off one, move deliberately to another. The goal is forward movement on all of them, cycling through as the mind demands.

  5. Reduce short-form content consumption deliberately. Every time you reflexively open Twitter, Instagram, or short video content, you are training your brain's attention-shift circuitry. You don't need to eliminate it, but counterbalance it: spend comparable time on something that holds your attention in one place for longer stretches.

  6. Notice the two idiots. When you feel the urge to shift into joke mode, pause for a second and ask: am I doing this because it's genuinely funny, or am I doing this to escape a feeling? You don't need to stop being funny. You just want to make the distinction consciously.

  7. Look at your diet for gut health. Experiment with cooked vegetables, root vegetables (sweet potatoes especially), and higher-fiber foods. The gut-brain connection is real enough that smoothing out your digestion may have downstream effects on attention and mood. Talk to a doctor before adding supplements.
