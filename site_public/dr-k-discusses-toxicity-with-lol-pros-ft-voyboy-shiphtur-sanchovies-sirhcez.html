Summary

TL;DR — Toxicity in games and self-sabotage in life come from the same root: unmet expectations, ego protection, and the fact that giving up genuinely feels better in the moment than trying again.

A group of League of Legends streamers and former pros — Voyboy, Shiphtur, SirhcEz, and Sanchovies — sit down to unpack why the game's community has become so toxic, why positive players feel like they're fighting a losing war, and what any of this has to do with how people get stuck in their actual lives. The conversation moves from in-game blame-shifting to ego, hopelessness, the seductive relief of quitting, and finally to some concrete tools for rewiring the pattern.

Blame Is a Natural Reflex, But It Has a Cost

When something goes wrong in a game, the immediate instinct is to find an external cause. Someone played badly. The jungler didn't help. The matchup was unfavorable. This is not unique to League — it's what people do in every area of life.

The problem is that blame short-circuits the only mechanism that actually makes you better: looking at what you did wrong. And that's genuinely hard to do in the moment, especially when you have twenty minutes of a losing game still ahead of you and everyone else is already tilted.

"I think it's pretty natural for people to look for reasons outside of themselves, whether it's true or not, to put a lot of the responsibility — a lot of times it turns out to be blame — on others."

Two things make honest self-reflection especially difficult:

  • Ego — admitting a mistake threatens your identity as a competent player (or person)
  • Habit — most people fall into the same routine every day and don't actively try to switch it up

The Scuttle and the GPA: How League Mirrors Real Life

One of the clearest patterns the streamers describe is what happens when something minor goes wrong early: a jungler doesn't get scuttle crab, a tower dive doesn't get support, a small objective gets lost — and someone quits on the game entirely. At three minutes in.

This is not a League problem. It's a human problem.

The person who loses scuttle and ints is doing the same thing as the person who misses one workout and abandons their fitness plan, or loads up a coding tutorial and closes it after twenty minutes. The form is gg go next — the internal declaration that because the game didn't go as imagined, the whole thing is forfeit.

What makes this possible is an expectation that was formed before anything even started. The jungler queued up with a mental script: take scuttle left, take scuttle right, gank mid, snowball, win. When that script gets interrupted, it doesn't just feel disappointing — it feels like everything has been taken away.

"Expectation leads to attachment. Attachment leads to suffering."

This is the through-line. The stronger the mental image of how things were supposed to go, the more devastating any deviation feels, and the more likely someone is to self-destruct rather than adapt.

Why Giving Up Feels Good (And Why That's the Real Problem)

Here's the uncomfortable truth: inning, raging, and quitting feel good. They feel fair. When life (or your team) does something to you, handing that damage back to the universe feels like justice. It's invigorating in a way that staying positive simply is not.

Staying positive, by contrast, is draining. Voyboy describes streaming for four hours while people run it down on his games, having to laugh it off for his audience while genuinely feeling depressed about it. SirhcEz says it "kills the mood" and makes him not want to play. The feeling of being held hostage — trapped in a game for 20–30 minutes with someone actively sabotaging it — is real and cumulative.

The key insight is that willpower is finite. Think of it as a mana bar. Every time you hold back a rage response, you spend mana. Voyboy starts each stream with a full bar; every bounty placed on his head, every targeted int, drains it. At some point the bar runs out, and that's not a character flaw — it's physiology.

The person who ints after losing scuttle isn't weak. They've just run out of mana faster than they realized, often because they didn't know that mana bar existed.

The Two-Arrow Problem

There's an old idea that's directly applicable here: life shoots one arrow at you. The second arrow is the one you shoot at yourself because you got hit by the first one.

Losing scuttle is the first arrow. Running down mid is the second.

The first arrow often can't be avoided. Bad teammates, unfair matchups, actual system failures — these are real. What almost entirely can be controlled is the second arrow. And the second arrow is usually what loses the game.

"Most of what's ruining their lives is not the scuttle. It's the running down mid."

This reframes hopelessness. When someone ints after losing a small objective, they aren't responding to how bad their situation is — they're responding to how hopeless they feel. And that feeling, as everyone on the call acknowledges, often doesn't match reality. A game down one hundred gold is rarely actually lost. A bad test grade doesn't have to end a GPA.

The Ego Trap and the Identity Problem

There's a specific mechanism that locks this cycle in place: the confusion of performance with identity.

If your identity is "I am smart," then studying for a test is a threat. It implies you aren't naturally smart enough. So the only way to protect that identity is to not try — then failure doesn't count. You can tell yourself you could have gotten an A if you'd actually cared.

In League: if your identity is "I am a carry," then adapting after falling behind threatens who you are. Better to int and preserve the narrative that the game was unwinnable anyway.

This is also why positive streamers draw hate. When someone sees SirhcEz succeeding with a chill, consistent approach, it implicitly indicts every choice that person made not to do the same. The most visceral hate often comes from people who recognize, on some level, that a different choice was available to them.

"The thing that inspires the most vitriolic hate is when you see the person making the choice that you didn't make."

Why Positive Players Are Burning Out

The streamers describe feeling like they're in a hopeless war — trying to hold a line that keeps losing ground, with no reinforcements coming. The meta, they say, has shifted toward toxicity being normalized. Positive streamers have been dropping off for years.

Part of this is systemic: when reporting and punishment systems are inconsistent, people learn there are no consequences. Part of it is the isolation of positivity — it's hard to feel like your individual effort matters when the loudest voices are negative.

But there's also something worth naming: the emotional cost of being positive in a toxic environment is genuinely high, and most positive players are absorbing that cost invisibly. Voyboy doesn't show his audience how much the targeted inting hurts. SirhcEz keeps a calm face and says "next game." That suppression has a real cost over time.

Perspective as the Antidote

Every solution the group reaches comes back to perspective — the ability to zoom out from the immediate moment and see the larger picture. It's what stops the loss of scuttle from feeling like the end of the world. It's what lets you stay curious about a losing game instead of panicking.

Perspective is also what allows you to see that the way you respond to a setback matters more than the setback itself.

What makes perspective hard is attachment — the degree to which your identity is bound up in what happens. The more invested you are in a specific outcome, the more your vision narrows when that outcome is threatened. This is literally physiological: adrenaline collapses peripheral vision. When you're tilted, you cannot see the options around the edges. You can only see the thing you lost.

Practices like meditation aren't magic — they're just a way to briefly step off that physiological treadmill and give the nervous system a chance to reset.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking toxicity is about bad people. It's mostly about people running out of mana — emotional resources depleted by a system that doesn't protect them and an ego that conflates mistakes with worthlessness.
  • Believing perspective is just "not caring." Perspective isn't detachment or indifference. It's the ability to care about something without losing yourself in it.
  • Trying to become positive through willpower alone. Willpower is the mana bar. You can't solve a mana problem by willing yourself to have more mana. You have to actually regenerate it — sleep, breaks, practices like meditation.
  • Waiting for the system to fix the culture. Systemic improvements matter, but underestimating the impact of individual consistency is a mistake. The streamers on this call have no idea how many viewers have tilted slightly less because of what they modeled. Neither will you.
  • Conflating "bad at life" with "being bad." Lack of practice is not lack of worth. Most people have spent a decade refining one skill and almost no time studying how they themselves function as a human being. That's a practice problem, not a talent problem.
  • Going all-in on self-improvement immediately. Starting meditation with a goal of thirty minutes a day virtually guarantees failure. The brightest candle burns out fastest. Start with two to five minutes. Anchor it to something you already do.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your expectations before you start. Before your next game — or your next workday, or any high-stakes thing — pause and notice what script you've already written. What do you think is supposed to happen? Name it out loud or write it down. Awareness of the expectation is the first step to not being controlled by it.

  2. Track the second arrow. When something goes wrong today, ask: what did life do to me (first arrow), and what am I doing to myself in response (second arrow)? You can't always control the first. You almost always have some say in the second.

  3. Do a 60-second mood audit after a bad outcome. Instead of immediately reacting, pause. Ask: am I responding to the actual situation, or to my expectation of what the situation was supposed to be? These are often different.

  4. Try alternate nostril breathing for two minutes when you feel tilted. Sit up straight, spine tall, knees lower than hips. Close your right nostril with your thumb and breathe in fully through the left. Switch, closing the left with your ring finger, and breathe out through the right. Breathe in through the right, switch, breathe out through the left. Repeat slowly for two minutes. This directly engages the parasympathetic nervous system and physically interrupts the adrenaline loop that collapses your perspective.

  5. Start a simple self-study practice. At the end of each day, ask: what made today a good or bad day? What specifically happened? How much did I sleep? Did I eat? What was the first thing that went sideways? Do this with the same curiosity you'd use watching a replay. You are the player. Start studying the footage.

  6. Separate your identity from your performance. The next time you make a mistake, try saying "I played that poorly" instead of "I'm bad." One is a data point. The other is a verdict. This distinction is small and genuinely hard, but it determines whether you can learn from the mistake or have to defend against it.

  7. If you meditate: start with five minutes, not thirty. Anchor it to an existing habit — right after waking up, right before queuing for games, right before bed. Consistency over duration, especially at the start.
