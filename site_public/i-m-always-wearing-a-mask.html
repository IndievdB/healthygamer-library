Summary

TL;DR — When autism (or any neurodivergent trait) becomes your entire identity in a social group, you end up performing a character instead of building real relationships. Escaping that pattern means disentangling your self-worth from the label, dropping the self-deprecating act, and taking small risks on authenticity.

Growing up autistic in social environments that didn't understand — or actively exploited — the diagnosis can leave deep behavioral scars. You learn that being laughed at is better than not being included at all, so you lean into it. You become the clown. Then, when that dynamic finally turns toxic enough that you walk away from it, you find yourself unable to connect with anyone at all — because the only social tool you trusted was the one you had to throw out.

This is the trap: the adaptation that got you in the door eventually locks you out of anything deeper.

How a Label Becomes Your Entire Identity

Being introduced as "the autistic guy" rather than by your name is more than a social slight. It sets the terms of every relationship that follows. People aren't befriending you — they're befriending a category.

There's a specific dynamic worth naming: the token neurodivergent friend. A group can feel progressive and accepting by having one autistic member while still treating that person as a sideshow rather than a peer. The label earns you access, but it also caps how seriously anyone takes you. When you express frustration or try to push back, it gets dismissed: "That's just the autism." Your feelings don't count as real data about the relationship.

"You're left in a situation where you have to keep paying the price of being the butt of jokes in order to get some degree of social acceptance."

This is functionally bullying, even when it comes wrapped in the language of inclusion.

The Self-Deprecating Humor Trap

When being laughed at is your ticket to belonging, you start actively engineering it. You play up the diagnosis. You make yourself the punchline before someone else can. It works — people invite you back.

But this strategy has a brutal long-term cost. Humor and autism get fused together in your mind. You can't imagine being funny without also being visibly, performatively "the autistic one." So when you eventually get tired of being disrespected and try to stop doing the self-deprecating bit, you take the humor completely off the table too. You go flat. Quiet. Rigid. And then people can't connect with you at all — not because there's nothing there, but because you've locked the most genuine parts of yourself away.

"The most valuable thing you can offer another human being is something you've taken off the table."

The irony is that the humor probably isn't a symptom of the autism. It's a real quality you have. The two just got tangled together through years of using one to excuse the other.

The Mask That Backfires

After leaving those early toxic groups, the next adaptation is to hide everything — mask so completely that nothing objectionable can get through. Don't be loud. Don't be weird. Be normal.

The problem is that suppressing all personality reads as something worse than awkward. It reads as cold, arrogant, uninterested. People assume you dislike them. You end up pushing people away more effectively by trying to seem acceptable than you ever did by being yourself.

This is the paradox of over-masking: trying to hide what you're afraid will make people reject you becomes the very thing that makes them keep their distance.

What Actually Helps

Accept the diagnosis without being defined by it. There's a meaningful difference between "I am autistic" and "I am the autistic one." You're allowed to wish you weren't autistic. You're allowed to see it as a disadvantage without finding a silver lining. Forced positivity isn't the goal — honest acceptance is. You don't need to perform gratitude for your neurodivergence, and you don't need to be ashamed of it either.

Disclose selectively and early. You don't need to lead with it — "Hi, I'm Robert, I'm autistic" isn't the move. But waiting until someone already thinks you're rude or distant can mean you're correcting a first impression rather than setting a fair one. Telling a manager or a new acquaintance early on ("I sometimes struggle with eye contact — it's not disinterest, it's just how I'm wired") can quietly defuse a lot of misreading. The experience of telling a manager and feeling genuinely understood at work is a preview of what more relationships can look like.

Stop pre-rejecting yourself. One of the quietest ways the fear operates is that you declare the outcome before anything happens. Assuming the person you're talking to sees a robot, or is detecting your autism, or is about to laugh at you — that's not reading the room, it's contaminating it. The other person doesn't get a chance to actually respond to you because you've already decided what they'll think.

It's partly a numbers game. Not every group will be the right fit, and a non-response or a faded connection isn't the same as rejection. People have their own depression, their own chaos, their own reasons for not following up. If you reach out to five different groups, some won't land — and that's fine. You only need one or two to work.

Eye contact may not fully normalize — and that may not matter. Social skills training can improve it, but it might never feel completely natural, and that's a legitimate outcome. What tends to happen is that eye contact matters most in the very early phase of meeting someone. Once a relationship is established, you're going to the movies or hiking side by side anyway, and it stops being a factor. It's a first-impression hurdle, not a permanent ceiling.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing autism awareness with genuine acceptance. More people knowing what autism is doesn't automatically mean they're treating you as a full person. Watch for groups where you're the token — where your value is tied entirely to the label.

Thinking you only bring one thing to the table. "The only reason people kept me around was to make them laugh" is a black-and-white thought, not a fact. Thoughtfulness, self-awareness, and organized thinking are things other people notice and value — often more than you give yourself credit for.

Treating one group's rejection as universal truth. Kids especially are not a representative sample of how humans will respond to you. They can be extraordinarily cruel in ways that have nothing to do with your actual worth as a person.

Over-correcting into flatness. Hiding the humor to hide the autism leaves you with nothing to connect on. The goal isn't to present a neutral, inoffensive surface — it's to present yourself without the self-deprecating wrapper.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your self-deprecating humor. For one week, notice every time you make yourself the punchline. Ask: am I doing this because it's genuinely funny, or because I'm trying to pre-empt someone else doing it? Start distinguishing between the two.

  2. Practice one small early disclosure. In your next new relationship where it feels relevant, mention you're on the spectrum before someone has already formed a negative impression. Keep it matter-of-fact: "I sometimes don't make much eye contact — just how I'm wired, not disinterest." Notice what happens.

  3. Catch black-and-white thinking in real time. When you notice a thought like "they think I'm a robot" or "they all hate me," pause and ask: what's the actual evidence? What's an alternative explanation? Bring specific examples of this to therapy rather than trying to resolve them alone.

  4. Re-engage one piece of genuine personality. Pick something you've been suppressing — a joke, an observation, an interest — and let it out with one person you interact with regularly. You're not performing; you're testing the assumption that it will land badly.

  5. Reframe rejection as filtering, not verdict. When a connection doesn't develop, write down two or three reasons it might have happened that have nothing to do with you (they're busy, they have their own struggles, it was just bad timing). Build the habit of not treating every non-response as confirmation of your worst fears.

  6. Keep the therapy appointment specifically for this work. Bring individual incidents of rejection or awkwardness to sessions rather than abstract worries. Concrete experiences are much easier to process and reframe than general anxiety about whether you're likeable.
