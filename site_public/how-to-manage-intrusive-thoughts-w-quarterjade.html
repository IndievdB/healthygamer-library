Summary

TL;DR — Intrusive thoughts aren't a sign that something is fundamentally wrong with you; they're echoes of old emotional wounds running as background processes in your mind. This session walks through the psychology of intrusive thoughts, where they actually come from, and what it takes to finally quiet them — using the real experience of a streamer named Jody (QuarterJade) as the working example.

The conversation moves from childhood friendship trauma and a volatile father, through Jody's discovery of self-worth via streaming, and lands on a core psychological insight: the confident adult you are today and the frightened child buried in wood chips are both real, and both running at the same time.

What an Intrusive Thought Actually Looks Like

Most people imagine intrusive thoughts as dark, violent, or bizarre urges. But a lot of the time they look much quieter and more familiar than that:

  • You're having a great time with a new friend. Things are genuinely going well. Then, out of nowhere: "When are they going to realize there's something wrong with me and leave?"
  • You say something real and vulnerable in a conversation and immediately: "I shouldn't have said that. That's why people don't like you."
  • Your roommate asks you to move in together and somewhere underneath the excitement: "Be careful. She'll figure out you're not worth it."

That second voice is the intrusive thought. It's unwanted — that's literally what "intrusive" means. You weren't asking for that assessment. You were just trying to enjoy your life. It spawns uninvited, like an enemy spawning through a portal in a game. You fight it off, it comes back. You win sometimes, you don't other times.

"It just comes in and it's like — I but — here's the really scary thing: it actually has a right to be there."

That last part is what makes it so disorienting. Unlike a random anxious thought, intrusive thoughts of this kind are generated by a real belief that's sitting in your subconscious. They're not random noise. They're signal — just signal from the past misfiring in the present.

The Portal Metaphor: Where Intrusive Thoughts Come From

Think of your mind as having two layers: a conscious layer and an unconscious one. When you experience significant emotional pain — especially early in life, before you have the tools to process it — that hurt doesn't disappear. It goes dormant. It becomes, to use a computer metaphor, malware.

That dormant pain doesn't stay quiet. It sits in your subconscious and, under the right conditions, spawns a portal. Through that portal come intrusive thoughts — pop-up ads interrupting whatever you were actually doing.

The trigger isn't random. Your mind is constantly scanning your environment and activating the programs that seem relevant. If you're entering a new friendship that's going well, your mind recognizes the pattern: we've been here before. And it activates the old program — the one that learned, from repeated experience, that people who seem to like you will eventually reject you.

The wound doesn't have to be one dramatic event. It's often the accumulation: childhood friends who suddenly stopped being allowed to see you (because of adult politics you couldn't understand), a bully who you later made peace with and then was cruel to you again anyway, a father who responded to emotional expression with verbal aggression. Each of those experiences taught your mind something. And your mind is just doing its job — trying to protect you from getting hurt the same way again.

"She bullied you once, but what really hurts is that y'all became friends and then she did it again. Like, she had grown up. And y'all were supposed to be friends. So then your mind learns: friends are going to steal my bathing suits."

You're Confident AND Not Confident — And That's Not a Contradiction

One of the most confusing things about this kind of intrusive thought pattern is that it doesn't fit the stereotype of low self-esteem. You can be someone who stands up to your father at 23, who built a successful career from scratch, who dyes their hair pink and moves across the country — and also have a part of you that genuinely believes you're not worth much.

This isn't a contradiction. The mind isn't a single monolithic thing. It's better understood as a fluctuation — different circumstances activate different states. Some environments bring out your confidence cleanly. Others hit a particular frequency that activates the old wound.

The important distinction: a truly low-confidence person believes their words have no merit intrinsically — they think they're dumb, boring, not worth listening to. That's different from someone who knows their words have value, but has learned through repeated painful experience that expressing themselves sometimes leads to punishment or rejection. The second person's hesitation isn't low confidence. It's learned caution from real trauma.

The intrusive thoughts feel like low confidence because they use the language of low confidence. But the thinker generating them isn't your adult self. It's an older, younger part of you that learned a particular lesson a long time ago and never got updated.

The Seven-Year-Old You Is Still Under the Stairs

Here's the useful image: somewhere in your unconscious, there's still a younger version of you sitting under the school stairs, having buried herself in wood chips because she didn't know where else to go during recess. She felt unwanted. She didn't understand why. She was confused and sad and alone. And nobody ever came and really sat with her — not in a way that let her fully feel it.

That little girl never finished grieving. So she's still there. And occasionally, when something in the present rhymes with what happened to her, she sobs and sends a thought your way.

The adult response — humor, reframing, pointing out logically that "it wasn't my fault" — isn't wrong. But it's also not the same as actually comforting her. Laughing is minimizing the pop-up, not closing it. The window is still open. It's still using RAM.

"We've got to let her cry and we just have to be there with her. We don't need to distract her with jokes and tell her everything's okay. We have to sit with her and say: what happened to you is not okay. And it's really really sad that they have done something to make you believe you are not a good person. And you didn't do anything wrong."

This is what self-compassion actually means — not bubble baths and treating yourself, but being willing to hold your own pain without flinching away from it. The reason it's hard is because a lot of people learned early that unguarded emotion was dangerous or inappropriate. If "calculated emotion is the only acceptable emotion," then sitting with raw sadness feels wrong at a deep level — even when you consciously know it isn't.

The Role of Humor (and Why It Isn't Enough)

Using humor to cope with painful things isn't bad. It's often a sign of resilience and intelligence. But there's a specific pattern worth watching for: using laughter to prevent other people from feeling sad for you.

The reason this matters is subtle. If you can't tolerate people pitying you or feeling sad on your behalf, you'll keep steering conversations away from the emotional core of your own story. Which means the emotional work never quite gets done. The wound never gets enough air to heal.

"Until you can sit with the sadness, the intrusive thoughts will continue."

The goal isn't to stop being funny. It's to develop the capacity to also sit in the difficult feeling without immediately escaping it. Both things can be true.

Common Pitfalls

  • Blaming yourself for having intrusive thoughts. The moment the thought appears, many people immediately layer shame on top: "I'm disrespecting my friends by not trusting them. There's something wrong with me for thinking this." That's a second portal opening, on top of the first. It compounds the damage without resolving anything.

  • Mistaking the absence of processing for healing. Understanding intellectually that "it wasn't my fault" is not the same as having processed the emotion. You can have full cognitive clarity about an old wound while still carrying the full emotional weight of it.

  • Thinking you have to choose between being confident and having these thoughts. Both can coexist. The thoughts don't cancel your confidence — they're just evidence of an unresolved emotional echo.

  • Forcing the process. If the part of you that doesn't want to go there is saying "no," forcing it has the opposite effect. The work is patient — sitting nearby, staying present, and letting that younger version of you come out when she's ready.

  • Doing emotional processing only in public. If you're a person who performs, streams, or leads others, you may have strong reflexes around how your emotions land on other people. That's worth keeping. But it can also mean you never have a private space where you can actually fall apart a little. Finding that private space matters.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your portal label. What is the core belief that your intrusive thoughts orbit around? "I am unwanted." "I am not worth as much as people think." "When people really know me, they'll leave." Writing it down precisely gives you something concrete to work with instead of a shapeless fog.

  2. Separate the thought from yourself. When an intrusive thought arises, practice saying to yourself: "There's that portal again" instead of "I'm thinking this because it's true." You are not the thought. The thought is a pop-up from an old program.

  3. Practice sitting with a small amount of sadness without resolving it. When you notice the urge to make a joke or lighten the mood in a conversation about something that genuinely hurt, try pausing for thirty seconds first. Just let it be heavy for a moment. You don't have to stay there indefinitely — just long enough to acknowledge it's there.

  4. Find the kid under the stairs. Recall a specific early memory where you felt genuinely unwanted or rejected — not to analyze it, but to let yourself feel what that child felt. Then speak to her the way you'd speak to someone you love who was going through that. You don't have to do this for long. But do it without immediately following it with a joke.

  5. Try a basic meditation practice to build your tolerance for sitting still. Om chanting is a good entry point because it gives your body something active to do. Sit cross-legged on a folded blanket or pillow so your hips are slightly elevated and your spine naturally straightens. Take a deep breath, open your mouth wide and sound "Ah," close to "Oo," then close your mouth to "Mm." Five rounds. Then sit quietly for sixty seconds without opening your eyes. The goal is not emptying your mind — it's learning that you can exist in stillness without fixing anything.

  6. Consider therapy or coaching specifically oriented around this. Insight about where your intrusive thoughts come from is valuable, but insight alone doesn't close the portal. Sitting with someone who can hold space while you actually feel the old feelings — and not just understand them — is the mechanism by which the underlying belief actually changes.
