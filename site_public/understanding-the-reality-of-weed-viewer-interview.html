Summary

TL;DR — Most people who use marijuana aren't addicts, but that doesn't mean it isn't quietly shaping their lives in ways worth paying attention to. This is a conversation about the grey zone of cannabis use — functional, not catastrophic, but subtly stalling the kind of growth that only happens when you let your negative emotions do their job.

The person in this conversation, Rogan, is a 19-year-old who started using marijuana legally after moving to Canada for school. He uses it daily, it hasn't tanked his grades, and he doesn't feel like it's a "problem" in the clinical sense. But something about it feels off — the magic has faded, and he keeps finding himself alone with thoughts he'd rather not have.

That middle ground — not addict, not totally fine — is where the real conversation needs to happen.

The Majority Don't Fit Either Extreme

When people talk about marijuana, the conversation collapses into two camps: those who are clearly addicted and need help, and those who use casually and are completely fine. Almost nobody talks about the massive middle — the people for whom it's technically manageable but quietly influential.

That middle is where most users actually live. They're not losing jobs or relationships. But they're not unaffected either. The impact is subtler: a slowing of forward motion, a blunting of the friction that usually drives growth.

This is the population worth understanding.

How It Starts: Extracting More Pleasure From Life

Marijuana genuinely does something in the beginning. It amplifies sensory experience — music sounds richer, food tastes better, even mundane things like sitting outside feel interesting. It pulls you into the present moment and quiets whatever mental noise was running in the background.

This is especially useful in a world already saturated with high-dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → stimulation. Video games, social media, and constant entertainment have raised the floor for what counts as "interesting." Low-stimulation activities — a walk, a conversation, just existing — feel flat by comparison. Marijuana temporarily fixes this. It makes a two feel like a nine.

That's a real effect. It's not imaginary, and it's not purely bad. But it sets up a dynamic that gradually inverts.

The Tolerance Trap and the Chronic Debuff

Once you start using marijuana to make boring things tolerable, something shifts. Your baseline capacity to sit with boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition → — without any chemical assistance — shrinks. You become dependent on the enhancement just to feel normal.

Think of it as a debuff. Over time, your unmedicated experience of life drops. The high that used to take things from a 2 to a 9 now barely pushes a 2 to a 5. And your sober baseline has quietly dropped to something like a negative number. You're using marijuana not to enhance life anymore, but just to tread water.

"You originally did this to turn a two into a nine. We used to get a plus seven. Now at this point we're at a minus-five chronic debuff, and then plus four — our net is negative one."

The fix isn't complicated but it requires time: a genuine break from use lets you clear the debuff. Not forever, not necessarily for moral reasons, but to reset the baseline so that ordinary life feels livable again without chemical support.

The Squeezed Lemon Problem

There's a second thing happening alongside toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →. Life offers different-sized "lemons" — sources of meaning, pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition →, and experience. Marijuana can help you squeeze more juice out of whatever lemon you're currently holding. But it can't hand you a bigger lemon.

The transformative experiences — a real romantic relationship, a job that means something, a creative project you're proud of, a memory you made standing next to someone you love — those contain far more juice than anything you can extract from your current situation by getting high. Marijuana keeps you squeezing the same small, dried-out fruit instead of moving toward bigger ones.

"It takes where we are and it allows us to extract more out of it, instead of actually moving us forward to these bigger lemons in life."

When you feel like there's nothing left in the high — like the magic is gone and you're doing it out of habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → — that's often the lemon running dry, not a personal failure.

Negative Emotions Are Not the Enemy

This is the most important piece, and the most counterintuitive.

Negative emotions are not a design flaw. They're the mechanism by which human beings learn, adapt, and grow. LonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, frustration, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, sadness — these are signals. They activate the learning centers of the brain. They create the pressure that makes people change.

When you push negative emotions to the back — through marijuana, video games, or anything else — you don't eliminate them. You just stop processing them. They sit there, accumulating. And because they're not reaching the front of your mind, they're not doing the thing they're designed to do: motivate you to make a change.

The 19-year-old who moves away from home and feels lonely is supposed to feel lonely. That discomfort is what pushes you to reach out, to build something new, to figure out who you are in a new place. When you consistently suppress it, you skip that process. You keep existing, but you don't keep growing.

"They hurt you so much that it prompts you to change, it prompts you to do something different. When we push them towards the back, we move down the route of becoming an NPC."

The emotions don't disappear. They just pile up quietly until they start breaking through — often at the worst moments, like when you're high and your mind finally goes quiet enough to hear them.

Why Men Are Especially Vulnerable Here

Men are statistically more likely to develop substance useThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → problems, and the cultural context around male emotion is part of why.

Growing up, most men receive a consistent message: negative emotions are dangerous, weakness is punished, and asking for help makes you a target. So emotion gets associated with threat. When you then find yourself in a genuinely hard situation — isolated, far from home, without a support system — you have the feeling and none of the tools to handle it.

Nobody teaches you. The expectation is that you'll figure it out. And "figuring it out" often looks like suppression. Then along comes something that makes you feel like everyone else appears to feel — present, relaxed, unbothered. Of course it's appealing.

But the emotions keep building. You never actually fight the battle you can win — the one where you learn to be with your feelings, process them, and move through them. Instead you fight a losing battle: holding them back just well enough to function, but not well enough to grow.

"You're too busy fighting a losing battle to just run away."

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Take a deliberate break of at least two weeks. Not to prove you're not addicted, but to actually observe what your baseline looks like. Notice what feelings come up when use stops. Don't suppress those feelings — that's the data.

  2. Let negative emotions sit in the foreground for short periods. Ask what it's pointing at. It may be trying to tell you something.

  3. Identify what the "bigger lemons" might be. What are the experiences or connections you've been putting off? A relationship, a creative pursuit, a communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition →? These are the things that actually expand your capacity for satisfaction — no chemical assistance required.

  4. Notice the pattern before you reach for it. Before using, ask: am I doing this because something is genuinely fun, or because I'm bored/overwhelmed and want to manage that feeling? You don't have to stop — just notice which one it is. That awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → alone changes the dynamic.

  5. Find one outlet for the emotions you've been pushing back. Journaling, therapy, a trusted person, even songwriting — anything that lets the feeling actually surface and move through you rather than accumulate. The goal isn't to wallow; it's to let the signal do its job so it doesn't have to get louder.
