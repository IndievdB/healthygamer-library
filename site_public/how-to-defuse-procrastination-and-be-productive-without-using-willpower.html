Summary

TL;DR — Procrastination isn't a willpower problem; it's a working memory problem. The fix isn't "just get started" — it's asking yourself "what can I actually do right now?" and picking the best realistic option.

Most advice about procrastination assumes you already know how to do the thing — you just need more discipline. But that framing misses what's actually happening in your brain. When you're stuck, your working memory has locked onto a specific conflict (study vs. video games, for example) and is burning all its resources on that standoff. Understanding this opens up a surprisingly practical exit.

How Working Memory Traps You in a Two-Option Standoff

When you're procrastinating, it doesn't feel like you have a hundred options. It feels like you have two: do the thing you're supposed to do, or do the thing you want to do instead. That's not laziness — that's the architecture of working memory.

Working memory is highly specific. It can only hold a handful of objects at a time, and craving or resistance always attaches to something concrete. You don't crave in the abstract; you crave pizza or the next episode or scrolling your phone. Because of this, when a conflict gets loaded into working memory, it crowds out the awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → that any other options even exist.

There's a whole spectrum of things you could do — go for a walk, text a friend, do ten push-ups, organize one drawer — and a whole spectrum of things you could productively avoid your harder tasks with. But none of those middle options show up when you're stuck in the binary. Your brain is too busy fighting a war it can't win.

Why "Just Get Started" Often Backfires

The common advice is to lower the bar as far as possible: "just open the textbook," "just write one sentence," "just put one cup in the sink." There's an intuition behind this that makes sense — remove the activation energy and momentum takes over.

But there's a hidden flaw. Your brain is always running a cost-benefit calculation. When it considers reading a single line of a textbook on the night before a final, it very rationally concludes: that is a waste of time.

"What is the point of reading one line of a textbook? My brain is going to say that is the biggest waste of time, because there's no point unless we're going to study enough to actually do well on the final. It's like digging a well and the water is 100 feet deep — there's no point in digging 5 feet down. It is literally a waste of effort."

So not only does "just get started" fail to convince your brain the effort is worthwhile, it can actually increase resistance by making the gap between effort and payoff feel ridiculous. The tiniest action signals not enough, which your working memory registers as pointless.

On top of that, if you do manage to get started with the absolute minimum, you're leaving a lot on the table. The work of pulling out the textbook is the same whether you read one line or five paragraphs. The cognitive overhead is identical, but the perceived gain is radically different. Your brain's reward circuitry responds to meaningful progress, not symbolic gestures.

The Motivational Interviewing Principle: Do the Next Best Thing

This is a technique borrowed from addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → psychiatry, where it's used in a phase called motivational interviewingA structured, evidence-based conversational technique used by a trained practitioner to resolve a person's internal conflict and increase their motivation to change.Full definition →. When a patient is addicted to alcohol, telling them "you need to stop drinking, go to therapy, take medication, and attend AA" produces near-total resistance. It's not that the advice is wrong — it's that the ask is too far from where the person actually is.

What works instead is asking: "What can you do?"

Not what should you do. Not what would theoretically be ideal. What can you actually do, right now, given where you are?

Maybe they can commit to not drinking on weekdays. Maybe they can avoid drinking at one specific event this weekend. That's not "good enough" by some abstract standard — but it's genuine movement, and it doesn't trigger the same wall of resistance that the full program does.

The principle translates directly to procrastination:

  • You can't study for four hours → Can you study for thirty minutes?
  • Thirty minutes is too much → Can you do fifteen?
  • What about finishing just one worksheet?
  • What about reviewing your notes from last class?

The goal isn't the tiniest possible action. The goal is the next best thing — as close to the full task as you can actually get away with. You want to maximize the gap you can cover, not minimize the effort to a symbolic level.

"Procrastination is not about what you can do — it is about forcing yourself to do what you should do. You are not operating on what you are actually capable of. You are operating on wishes and hopes and dreams."

Creating Option C (and D and E)

The root of the technique is this: once your working memory has locked in a conflict between two poles — the thing you should do and the thing you want to do — you can't easily win that fight through force. The resistance is already baked in.

So instead of fighting the fight that's already in your head, create options that aren't part of the existing conflict.

Option C, D, and E don't carry the same emotional charge. They haven't been loaded up as "the hard thing" or "the forbidden thing." They're just... options. And because your working memory hasn't already built resistance to them, they're much easier to act on.

This is like negotiating with a five-year-old who won't eat broccoli. You don't win by forcing the broccoli. You win by finding the apple-with-peanut-butter — something in the acceptable middle that nobody thought to mention until you asked.

The question to ask yourself when you're stuck isn't "how do I make myself do this?" It's: "What can I actually get done before I give up and start scrolling?"

The Doorway Effect: Resetting Working Memory Physically

When working memory is stuck in a conflict loop, it's consuming mental resources like a frozen process hogging your CPU. There's a surprisingly simple hardware-level reset: walk through a doorway.

This is called the doorway effect — a real psychological phenomenon that explains why you walk into a room and forget why you came. Crossing a threshold causes working memory to partially reset. The context you were holding gets released.

You can use this deliberately. If you're locked in a procrastination spiral sitting at your desk, the conflict feels enormous and inescapable. Walk out of your room, out of your house, and around the block. By the time you come back, the emotional weight of the conflict will feel meaningfully different — because you've physically cleared the loaded state your working memory was stuck in.

This isn't a metaphor. The internal struggle that felt like a 3v3 death match will literally feel lighter after a few doorways.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking more willpower is the answer. The whole premise of this approach is that willpower is fighting a battle that's already been set up to exhaust you. Adding more force to a broken standoff doesn't change the architecture of the problem.

Mistaking "getting started" for progress. Doing the absolute minimum can actually reinforce the sense that the task is hopeless — your brain notices that one line of studying does nothing to prepare you for Friday's final and correctly identifies it as futile. Don't confuse low activation energy with good strategy.

Only seeing two options. The two-option framing (do the task vs. avoid the task) is a symptom of the problem, not a description of reality. If you feel stuck between exactly two choices, that's your working memory talking — not an accurate map of what's available to you.

Aiming for the floor instead of the ceiling of what you can manage. The target isn't the tiniest action. It's the best action you can realistically pull off. There's a big difference between "what's the easiest possible thing" and "what can I actually do right now."

Action Items & Exercises

  1. The "What Can I Do?" audit. Next time you're procrastinating, pause and write down — don't just think about — five to ten concrete actions you could actually take on the thing you're avoiding. Not the full task, not a token gesture: realistic intermediate steps. Pick the most ambitious one you can genuinely attempt.

  2. Ditch the binary. Identify the two poles of your current conflict (e.g., "study for four hours" vs. "play video games"). Then deliberately brainstorm three to five options that exist between them and aren't part of the original conflict. Write them down physically to get them into working memory.

  3. Size up, not down. When you do decide on a smaller version of your task, resist the urge to go smaller than you need to. Ask: "If I'm going to do this anyway, what's the biggest version of it I could pull off right now?" The overhead of getting started is often the same regardless of scale.

  4. Use the doorway reset. When you feel stuck in a procrastination loop — especially the paralyzed, circular, low-energy kind — get up and walk through at least two or three doorways. A loop around the block is ideal. Notice how the conflict feels different when you sit back down.

  5. Practice the motivational interviewing question weekly. At the start of each week, take one thing you've been avoiding and ask yourself not "how do I make myself do this?" but "what can I actually do on this?" Set a target based on that answer, not on what you think you should be able to do.
