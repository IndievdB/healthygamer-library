Where Entitlement Actually Comes From

The popular assumption is that Karens are entitled because they're rich. The research points somewhere more specific: entitlementA feature of personality (how you interact with the world) rather than identity (how you see yourself) — meaning a narcissist's self-perception is often the exact opposite of their outward behavior: they genuinely…Full definition → is a product of the socioeconomic status you were raised in, not the one you currently occupy.

  • People born into low-SES families who stay there: generally not highly entitled.
  • People born into low-SES families who become wealthy: also generally not highly entitled.
  • People born into high-SES families: much more likely to be entitled, regardless of what happens to them later.

Grow up in a household where Christmas gifts and birthday presents are guaranteed, and you come to expect them. The world becomes a place that is supposed to deliver things to you. When it doesn't, that registers not as bad luck but as an injustice.

There's a second piece: entitled people respond to bad luck with anger. Not disappointment, not resignation — anger. So a Karen who walks into a store that's out of stock didn't just experience a minor inconvenience. To her psychology, she experienced an attack. The explosion that follows isn't irrational given those inner premises; it's completely predictable.

This also explains why the "go to a tire store and ask for pizza" scenario escalates rather than just producing embarrassment. The more objectively wrong the Karen is, the bigger the narcissistic injury, and therefore the bigger the explosion.

The "Speak to the Manager" Mechanism

Why do Karens so reliably demand a manager? A few things are running at once.

First, projectionProjection is the psychological process of taking something you dislike or can't accept in yourself and unconsciously attributing it to someone else instead.Full definition →. People tend to attribute to others the same intentions they would have themselves. Karens are often petty and hierarchical in their own behavior — they gatekeep, they use small power plays. So when someone sets a perfectly reasonable limit on them, they assume that person is also being petty and spiteful. The manager, presumably smarter and more powerful, will see through the employee's pettiness and give the Karen what she deserves.

Second, a childhood template. Many Karens seem to have grown up in households with a dominant authority figure whose word was law. The lesson absorbed was: the person with the most power is the person who determines reality. Escalating to authority isn't an outburst — it's them following the rules of the world as they learned them.

Third, and most importantly, it sometimes works. Managers apologize, override policies, offer refunds. A tantrum that occasionally gets rewarded is a tantrum that gets repeated. The behavior was conditioned, partly by people trying to make the immediate situation calm down.

"Why do people throw temper tantrums? Because they get us what we want."

How the Internet Makes Everything Worse

The Karen videos that go viral are not representative samples of Karen behavior. They're the most extreme moments of the most extreme people on the most extreme days. The internet selects for the top of the distribution by design — the most outrageous content floats, everything else sinks.

This distorts perception in two directions. It makes the audience think Karens are constantly having meltdowns, and it makes the Karens themselves feel targeted and humiliated, which deepens the narcissistic injury and makes future incidents worse.

Beyond that, there's online drift and radicalization. It works roughly like this:

The Karen who films herself berating a store employee may have spent months in online spaces that told her those employees represent everything that's wrong with society.

The radicalization loop then closes: the video goes viral, the Karen feels humiliated, she retreats further into those same online communities for validation, and the cycle tightens.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. When you encounter Karen-like behavior, notice the trigger first. Before reacting, ask: what actually set this person off? Was there bad luck involved (out-of-stock item, long wait, unclear policy)? Understanding the trigger doesn't excuse the explosion, but it gives you information about how to respond without escalating.

  2. Practice holding compassion and a boundary simultaneously. These feel like opposites but aren't. Try a simple formulation: acknowledge the frustration ("I can see you're really frustrated") and then restate the limit calmly ("That said, this is our policy and I can't change it"). The compassion is real; the boundary doesn't move.

  3. If you manage people in service roles, have your employees' backs explicitly. Reinforcing Karen behavior — even to end an unpleasant interaction quickly — trains it. When a manager caves to a tantrum, the Karen learns the tactic works. Not caving, while treating the customer with basic dignity, is the more useful intervention.

  4. Audit your own online environment for drift. Notice whether the communities you participate in actually engage with opposing views or just amplify shared grievances.
