Why Men Specifically Turn to Substances

Men are socialized to be external problem solvers. When something goes wrong emotionally — shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition →, rejection, a bad performance review — the conditioned male response is to fix the external circumstance that created the emotion. Earn the promotion. Prove the critic wrong. Get her approval. Once the external thing is corrected, the painful emotion should dissolve.

The catastrophic flaw in this system: not every painful emotion can be fixed externally. And when external solutions fail, men reach for external substitutes — alcohol, pornography, video games, marijuana — to manage what they were never taught to manage internally.

"We use things outside of us to manage and control our internal emotional state. That's why I think men are three to four times more likely to become addicted to anything than women are."

Compounding this, men are placed in what amounts to a no-win situation culturally. They are told emotional expression is healthy and then punished for expressing certain emotions. They are told to be masculine and to be emotionally available — often by the same culture, often in direct contradiction. No one is taking responsibility for actually teaching men how to process emotions or holding space for them to do so. The result is a generation of men receiving contradictory instructions with no clear path to execute any of them, and no support when they fail.

What's Actually Driving Young Men to Screens

When you look at why a teenager is on TikTok for 17 hours a week or disappearing into the bathroom to watch pornography, the surface behavior is almost never the real story. The technology is filling a gap.

Average age of first exposure to pornography is around nine. A 14-year-old boy watching pornography probably isn't doing so because pornography is intrinsically compelling — he's doing it because he has a crush on a girl and has no idea how to talk to her. The screen is easier than the risk.

The instinct most parents have is to regulate — set time limits, restrict access, take the phone away. This approach consistently underperforms. What actually works is helping your child develop meta-awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and interpret information — about your environment, your body, your emotions, and your own mental processes — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → about their own behavior.

The better parental conversation isn't "you're spending too much time on TikTok" — it's "you've spent 32 hours on TikTok in the last two weeks. How do you feel about that? Is this the life you want to be living?" When a child starts honestly examining why they're drawn to a screen, you get access to what's actually missing. That's the conversation that leads somewhere.

"Don't ask 'do you watch pornography?' Start with 'how much do you watch? How do you feel about that? Is this the kind of life you want to live?'"

When a parent — especially a father — creates space for those conversations, they often uncover something addressable: social anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →, lonelinessA painful emotional state in which you feel disconnected from other people — distinct from simply being physically alone.Full definition →, confusion about how to relate to peers or to romantic interests. Body dysmorphia, in this framework, is one of the most difficult: when someone is addicted to an image of themselves — the version in the mirror after a workout, or the imagined version they're always striving toward — treatment means confronting what they fear they actually are underneath the striving.

Common Pitfalls

Regulating behavior without addressing the root. Taking away the phone, setting screen time limits, cutting off Wi-Fi — these approaches treat the symptom without touching what's driving the behavior. They also tend to create adversarial dynamics with kids that make honest conversation harder.

Treating sobriety as the goal. Getting someone off a substance without rebuilding their life around it tends to leave the underlying emptiness intact. The source of the pain — social isolation, lack of purpose, inability to manage internal states — still demands an outlet.

Assuming emotional expression is always welcome. Men are told to express emotions and then find that certain emotional expressions (anger, frustration, hurt delivered with intensity) are treated as threats or failures. This is a real contradiction, not a cop-out, and it compounds shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → in a particularly vicious way.

Waiting for rock bottom. Many young men romanticize rock bottom as the moment that will finally force change. The limbo before rock bottom — the numb, directionless existence — is actually the most dangerous place because it demands no action while consuming all energy.

Looking outward for the compass. Trying to satisfy contradictory external expectations (be masculine, be sensitive, make more money, be present) is a losing game. The next self-help framework won't fix it either. The signal worth following is internal.
