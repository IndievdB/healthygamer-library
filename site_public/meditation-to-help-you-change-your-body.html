Summary

TL;DR — Triphasic breathing is a structured, learnable technique that trains your body to breathe more deeply and slowly, and doing it consistently produces real, measurable physiological changes — including cardiovascular protection.

Most people breathe shallowly from the chest without realizing it. This talk walks through a specific three-phase breathing practice, explains why it works at the level of basic physiology, and addresses the most common trap meditators fall into once they've had their first meaningful experience.

The Three Phases of Triphasic Breathing

Most people use only a fraction of their lung capacity because they breathe from the chest. Triphasic breathing expands that capacity in three sequential steps:

  1. Abdominal (diaphragmatic) breathing — As you inhale, let your belly push outward. As you exhale, pull your navel back toward your spine. Keep your chest as still as possible. This is the foundation.

  2. Chest expansion — Once your belly is full, consciously expand your ribcage to draw in more air. You'll feel a second "phase" kick in with noticeably more capacity than belly breathing alone.

  3. Clavicular breathing — After expanding the chest, lift your shoulders slightly. This opens the very top of the lungs and adds a final increment — maybe five percent more breath.

The sequence is: belly → chest → shoulders on the inhale, then release on the exhale. Five full rounds is a good starting practice. If you feel dizzy, that's a signal to pause — dizziness during breathing exercises comes from CO₂ dropping too low, too fast.

What Happens to Your Body When You Breathe This Way

Practicing slow, deep breathing gradually trains your resting breathing pattern. Over time you stop defaulting to shallow chest breathing and start breathing more from the abdomen, more slowly, even when you aren't thinking about it.

The respiratory rate during this kind of practice can drop significantly. A seven-second inhale plus a seven-second exhale is a fifteen-second breath — about four breaths per minute. That's a dramatic reduction from the average resting rate of twelve to sixteen breaths per minute, and it isn't hard to reach once you've practiced a few times.

That slower rate also lowers your blood oxygen slightly and lets CO₂ build up. Normally the brain detects elevated CO₂ and signals you to breathe faster to clear it. But with regular practice, your body acclimatizes to this state rather than panicking. It recruits other mechanisms instead of just speeding up your breathing.

"When you meditate on a very regular basis, your body becomes acclimatized to high levels of CO₂, to where it recruits other kinds of mechanisms that don't require rapid breathing."

This acclimatization is thought to be behind the expansive, sometimes "trippy" feeling experienced during deep meditation — not mysticism, but elevated CO₂ in the brain over a prolonged period.

Why Meditation Genuinely Protects Your Heart

This is the part that tends to surprise people, because it sounds like a claim that should require extraordinary evidence — but the mechanism is straightforward physiology.

When your heart muscle doesn't get enough oxygen, it responds in two ways:

  • Vasodilation — it immediately signals the coronary arteries to widen, increasing blood flow in the short term.
  • Angiogenesis — over time, it sends signals to grow new blood vessels around the existing ones, creating alternate routes for blood to travel.

This second response — angiogenesis, or the growth of collateral circulation — is what matters for long-term heart health. Coronary artery disease kills people when a blocked artery cuts off blood supply to heart muscle. But if your heart has already built a network of backup vessels, a partial or even full blockage causes far less damage, because blood can reach the affected tissue through another path.

Slow, deep meditation reduces your oxygen intake gently and chronically — not in a damaging way, but enough to trigger the same signal your heart uses when it senses it's being starved for oxygen. The result is that regular meditators develop richer collateral coronary circulation over time.

"If you've got a blocked coronary artery and you're a regular meditator, the damage from that coronary artery is going to be way less because your body has developed alternate paths of circulation through meditation."

This is the same physiological territory explored by practitioners like Wim Hof, who learned breathing techniques used by Himalayan yogis — people who can regulate their body temperature in extreme cold through breath control alone. The underlying mechanism isn't magic; it's the body's adaptive response to controlled, repeated physiological stress via breathing.

The Most Common Trap: Expecting the Experience You Already Had

Beginners often have a surprisingly good first meditation. The mind is calm, something clicks, and it feels effortless. Then they try again — and it doesn't work the same way.

This is nearly universal, and it's not a sign that you're doing something wrong. It's a direct consequence of how meditation works.

The first time, you had no idea what to expect, so you couldn't chase anything. You just sat with the technique. That openness is exactly what allows the meditative state to arise. But once you've felt it, your mind files that experience away as a goal. The next time you sit down, part of you is checking: Is it happening yet? It's not happening. Something's wrong. This isn't working.

That checking — that layer of evaluation — is the thought that blocks the state you're trying to reach.

"The reason I'm not able to meditate is because I'm trying to meditate, and the reason I'm trying to meditate is because now I understand what meditation is because I did it before."

The hardest meditation experience to have is the second one, not the first.

Common Pitfalls

  • Judging whether it's "working." The moment you're evaluating your meditation, you've left the technique and entered a loop of self-monitoring. That loop is the obstacle.

  • Trying to recreate a previous experience. Attachment to what meditation felt like before creates the exact expectation that prevents it from happening again.

  • Suppressing yawns or other physical responses. If you yawn during breathwork, let it happen and return to the practice. Suppressing it just adds tension.

  • Pushing through dizziness. Dizziness during triphasic breathing means you need a break. It's a physiological signal, not something to ignore or push past.

  • Thinking acclimatization to CO₂ means deliberately restricting your breath. Your respiratory rate drops naturally as you practice. You don't need to force it. Trying to hold your breath or artificially slow yourself down is not the same thing and can be counterproductive.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Learn the sequence before you practice it. Belly out on inhale → chest expands → shoulders rise slightly → full exhale pulling navel back in. Run through it consciously a few times until the order feels natural.

  2. Do five full triphasic breaths. Close your eyes, move through all three phases on each inhale, and exhale fully. If you feel dizzy, pause, breathe normally, and resume when ready.

  3. Check your default breathing pattern. At a few random points during the day, notice whether you're breathing from your chest or your belly. Gently shift to abdominal breathing when you catch yourself defaulting to chest breathing.

  4. Practice before a focused task. A short round of triphasic breathing before work, a workout, or any activity requiring concentration can help you enter a calmer, more present state.

  5. When meditation feels like it isn't working, return to the technique. Don't try to fix the experience or diagnose what's wrong. Just put your attention back on the breath — belly, chest, shoulders. The technique is the path. Stop asking whether it's working and just do it again.
