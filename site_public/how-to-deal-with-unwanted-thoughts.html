Summary

TL;DR — Your mind generates thoughts automatically; you are not responsible for what arises, only for what you do about it.

Unwanted, disturbing thoughts — including violent, sexual, or otherwise taboo ones — are far more common than people realize. The shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → that surrounds them often prevents people from getting help, which makes the problem worse. Understanding what these thoughts actually are, and what they aren't, is the first step toward dealing with them.

Your Mind Is a Thought-Generating Machine

The mind doesn't produce only the thoughts you'd choose. It generates thoughts constantly, triggered by whatever you've seen, experienced, or been exposed to — a turtle in the road, a game your friends are playing, a conversation you had yesterday. You didn't decide to think about any of those things. They just arose.

This matters because most people who suffer from unwanted thoughts carry enormous shame, as if having the thought is the same as being the kind of person who wants to have it. That equation is wrong. You are not responsible for what your mind generates. You are responsible for how you respond to what it generates.

"What makes you a bad person is how you act. We have a Jedi and a Sith Lord inside all of us — it's just which side do you give in to. That's where the money is."

Ego-Dystonic Thoughts: Why Having Them Can Actually Signal Moral Integrity

There's a clinical term worth knowing: ego-dystonic. It describes thoughts that feel alien, repulsive, or deeply at odds with who you believe yourself to be. If you are horrified by a thought your mind produces and you actively resist acting on it, that horror is meaningful.

Ironically, the people who suffer most from intrusive thoughts are often people with a strong moral compass — they notice the thought, reject it, and then feel terrible for having had it in the first place. The suffering itself is evidence that they are not indifferent to doing harm. Fighting a thought every day takes real effort and reflects genuine valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →.

Contrast this with someone who has no such internal conflict about harmful thoughts. The anguish is not a sign of being broken; in many cases it's a sign of caring deeply about being a good person.

What Intrusive Thoughts Can Actually Come From

One of the most important clinical points here is that an unwanted thought is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Jumping straight to a self-label — "I am X kind of person because I have X kind of thought" — skips over a whole range of possible explanations. In clinical practice this is called a differential diagnosisThe clinical practice of generating a full list of conditions that could explain a patient's symptoms — and then systematically ruling them out — rather than jumping to the first explanation that fits.Full definition →: the recognition that many different root causes can produce the same surface symptom.

Some possible origins of intrusive thoughts include:

  • OCDObsessive-Compulsive Disorder is a psychiatric condition defined by intrusive, unwanted, and persistent thoughts (obsessions) that are neutralized by mental or physical acts (compulsions).Full definition → (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder) — Intrusive thoughts, including violent or sexual ones, are a recognized feature of OCD. When the OCD is treated, the intrusive thoughts often decrease or disappear entirely.
  • Traumatic experiences — Particularly in childhood. Thoughts that seem inexplicable in the present sometimes have roots in past experiences that haven't been processed.
  • Normal variation — The full range of human thought is wider than most people assume. Some experiences may simply be part of the natural variation in how minds work.
  • Other compulsive patterns — Thought patterns can become entrenched through repetition and reinforcement in ways that are somewhat independent of their original cause.

Because the same symptom can come from such different places, treating yourself based on a self-diagnosis is genuinely risky. The origin of the thought matters for knowing how to address it.

You Can Cultivate the Direction Your Mind Tends to Grow

Thoughts don't only come from inside you — they're also seeded by your environment. Whatever you surround yourself with shapes what your mind returns to by default.

Think of it like this: if everyone around you is talking about a particular game, your mind will drift there constantly. Put yourself in a cooking camp with people who love food, and a month from now your default mental background will have shifted. The environment you inhabit plants seeds; those seeds eventually bear fruit as recurring thoughts and mental habits.

This is actually a well-established principle in contemplative traditions, including yoga — the deliberate cultivation of a particular kind of mental environment to shape what the mind naturally produces over time.

The practical takeaway: if you want to change the character of your thoughts over time, look seriously at what you're feeding your mind. This isn't a quick fix, and it doesn't work in isolation from other forms of help — but it's a real lever you have some control over.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing thought generation with character. Having a disturbing thought does not mean you are a disturbing person. The thought arose; what you do next is what defines you. Collapsing those two things together creates unnecessary shame and can actually make the thoughts harder to escape.

Self-diagnosing based on the content of thoughts. "I have these thoughts, therefore I am X" skips the entire clinical process of understanding where the thoughts come from. That origin matters enormously for treatment and for genuinely resolving the problem.

Suffering in silence because of shame. These experiences are far more common than people admit. Shame keeps people isolated with something that is actually quite treatable. Professionals who work with intrusive thoughts are not there to judge you — they've seen this before, many times.

Assuming the thoughts are permanent. Intrusive thoughts, including very distressing ones, can lessen and even disappear entirely with appropriate support. Treating them as a fixed feature of who you are forecloses possibilities that are genuinely available.

White-knuckling it alone. Willpower and moral effort matter, but they are not sufficient substitutes for professional help when the thoughts are severe, persistent, and causing real suffering.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Separate the thought from the action — deliberately. When an unwanted thought arises, practice the explicit internal statement: "My mind generated this thought. I have not acted on it. These are different things." This cognitive separation is simple but it directly counters the shame spiral.

  2. Seek a licensed mental health professional. If intrusive thoughts are causing you significant distress, this is the single most important step. Ask specifically about experience with intrusive thoughts and OCD-spectrum presentations. The right clinician can identify the actual origin of the thoughts, which determines the most effective approach.

  3. Audit your environment. Make a list of what you regularly consume and who you regularly spend time with. Ask honestly: what seeds is this environment planting? Then identify one or two changes — even small ones — that would shift the mental diet in a direction you actually want.

  4. Don't self-diagnose. If you find yourself reaching for a label based on the content of your thoughts, write it down as a question to bring to a professional rather than a conclusion you've settled on. The label isn't yours to assign; the clinician's job is to find the actual underlying cause.

  5. Work through the guilt, not around it. The guilt and shame attached to intrusive thoughts deserve real attention — not suppression. In a therapeutic context, processing that guilt directly is often part of what allows the thoughts themselves to lose their grip.
