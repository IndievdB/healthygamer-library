The Real Harm of Brainwashing Isn't the Beliefs — It's the Narrowing

When most people think about brainwashing, they picture a cult or a government injecting dangerous ideas into someone's head. The natural assumption is that the harm comes from what is being implanted. But that framing misses the deeper problem.

The actual psychological damage from brainwashing comes from the process itself — the relentless repetition of any fixed set of ideas. It doesn't matter whether those ideas are sinister propaganda or your own perfectly reasonable worldview. Constant exposure to the same inputs, with no friction, no challenge, no contrary perspective, degrades your mind's ability to function.

Think about what happens to a body that only eats one food. It doesn't matter if that food is nutritious — eat only corn and your eyes stop working, your gut breaks down, your endocrine system falls apart. A healthy body requires dietary variety. The same is true for a healthy mind. It requires a variety of inputs to stay functional.

What the internet is doing, especially through algorithmic content feeds and algorithmically curated comment sections, is the mental equivalent of feeding everyone the same single food indefinitely.

Why This Is Happening Now

The internet has always had echo chambers. That's not new. What has accelerated the damage is the move to algorithmic comment sections.

It used to be that algorithms served you videos you already liked. That was bad enough. But now, when you open the comments, you are served comments that agree with you first — the ones the algorithm predicts you'll resonate with. The example that made this visible: the same video, opened at the same moment, showed two different people comment sections with opposite dominant reactions. Same video. Same time. Completely different social realities.

This is a significant escalation. You're no longer just seeing content that matches your views. You're now seeing what appears to be social consensus matching your views. Your beliefs feel confirmed not just intellectually but socially. And that combination is more powerful, and more narrowing, than either factor alone.

The technology companies doing this are running a live experiment on human cognition with no controls, no ethics review, and no study of downstream health effects. The equivalent would be adding a new chemical to the water supply before anyone has looked at what it does to the body.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Actively seek out the opposing view on things you feel certain about. When you notice a strong opinion — whether it's about relationships, politics, work, or your own limitations — go find the most thoughtful version of the contrary position. You don't have to agree with it. The goal is the cognitive exercise of taking it seriously.

  2. Audit what content you're actually consuming for a week. Notice whether it mostly confirms what you already think or regularly surprises you and challenges your assumptions. If it's almost entirely confirmation, that's the problem to fix — not any specific platform.
