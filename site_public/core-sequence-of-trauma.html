The same pattern of symptoms first observed in shell-shocked soldiers after World War I turned up later in prisoners of war, then in Romanian orphans who were severely neglected, and eventually in people who grew up with alcoholic parents, controlling "tiger" parents, or simply unstable home lives. The common thread isn't the specific event — it's the environment it creates.

The Core Sequence

Step 1 — Coercive control or neglect. When an outside force (a prison guard, a controlling parent, an unpredictable drunk) determines whether you're safe or in danger, your own wants become irrelevant. You stop living from the inside out and start living reactively, scanning constantly for what that external force needs from you.

Step 2 — Physiologic rewiring. Growing up in that environment literally rewires your danger-detection system to be hypersensitive. Small cues — a slight change in someone's tone, a hint of dissatisfaction on a partner's face — trip the alarm. Your body runs on elevated adrenaline and cortisolA stress hormone released by the body during prolonged threat — not the immediate adrenaline spike, but the slower, longer-burning signal meant to keep you alive for the 24–48 hours after a dangerous encounter.Full definition → as a baseline. Even genuinely safe situations feel threatening.

Step 3 — Overwhelmingly negative emotional experience. Because the alarm fires constantly, life fills with anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → and fear. There's a striking example of this: if a partner is 90% at peace after an argument but 10% still resentful, the traumatized person homes in on that 10% and effectively ignores the rest.

Step 4 — Numbness and dissociationDissociation is a state in which you become unplugged from the world around you — analytically aware of what's happening, but unable to connect with it emotionally.Full definition →. You can't fix the source of the fear, so the brain does the next best thing: it turns the feelings off.
