Summary

TL;DR — Having sex for the first time won't fix your life, and a bad first experience doesn't mean you're broken or asexual. It just means sex, like any skill, takes time and conditioning to get right.

A 32-year-old former incel posted about finally having sex after two years of self-improvement work — and hating it. He lost his erection, felt queasy, and worried he might be asexual or had permanently damaged himself from years of pornography and masturbation. This situation is more common than people admit, and it touches on a cluster of myths, physical realities, and psychological traps worth unpacking carefully.

The Myth That Sex Will Change Everything

There's a widespread fantasy — especially strong in incel-adjacent spaces, but honestly present in mainstream culture too — that having sex for the first time crosses some magical threshold. You go in a virgin, you come out confident, complete, and socially transformed. You graduate from "incel to Chad." Everything gets easier.

That's just not how it works.

Society places a strange, outsized value on sex as a milestone. Movies reinforce it constantly: two people kiss under the moon, music swells, cut to the next morning where everyone is glowing and satisfied. That framing conditions people to expect something transcendent. The reality for most people — especially the first time — is that sex is awkward, unfamiliar, and often unsatisfying.

"Sex, just like anything else in life, is going to be awkward and unfulfilling until you get the hang of it."

Think of it like cooking. You can eventually make an incredible meal, but the first time you try a new dish it's probably going to be mediocre at best. That's not a sign something is wrong with you. It's just the nature of learning something physical and relational.

Two specific myths worth naming directly:

  • Myth 1: The first time you have sex, it'll be amazing.
  • Myth 2: Every time you have sex, it'll be amazing.

Neither is reliably true, and believing them sets you up for shame and confusion when reality doesn't match the fantasy.

Death Grip Syndrome: What's Actually Happening Physically

If you've been masturbating for years in a particular way, you have physiologically conditioned your body to respond to a very specific kind of stimulation. This is sometimes called death grip syndrome — your nervous system has been trained, through thousands of repetitions, to require a certain grip pressure and a certain level (or absence) of lubrication to reach climax.

This is essentially Pavlovian conditioning. You haven't broken your body. You've just trained it very specifically, and vaginal intercourse doesn't replicate those exact conditions. When the stimulation doesn't match what your body expects, you don't finish — and then the psychological spiral begins.

The cycle looks like this:

  1. Physical mismatch → can't climax
  2. Confusion and self-judgment → psychological distress
  3. Psychological distress → loss of erection
  4. Loss of erection → shame
  5. Shame → more anxiety about the next attempt

The good news: conditioning can be reversed. The body that learned one response can learn another. It takes deliberate, patient retraining.

Practical steps for retraining:

  • Add lubrication. Vaginal intercourse is naturally lubricated; if you've been masturbating without it, start incorporating lotion or lubricant into solo sessions to begin reacclimating your body.
  • Loosen your grip. The pressure a hand can exert is significantly higher than what you'd experience during intercourse. Deliberately use a lighter grip even when it feels unsatisfying — that discomfort is the retraining happening.

It will feel frustratingly unsatisfying at first. That's expected. You're working against a deeply entrenched pattern. Progress is gradual, not immediate.

The Role of a Supportive Partner

The person in this situation had something genuinely valuable: a partner who already knew his background, remained emotionally present when things didn't go as hoped, and still wanted to see him again. That matters enormously.

"This woman is worth her weight in gold. The ability to understand where you're coming from, be emotionally available, and be patient with you as you go through this journey is amazing."

Not everyone will find a partner that patient — which makes it worth recognizing clearly when you have. This kind of journey isn't one you have to white-knuckle alone. A partner who can be curious and collaborative rather than frustrated or judgmental changes the whole landscape.

Sexually, this also opens up practical options. Intimacy doesn't have to mean a direct leap to whatever felt overwhelming. There are many ways to give and receive pleasure, and if the goal is gradual reconditioning, a partner can be involved in that process rather than excluded from it. You can begin with what works — manual stimulation, for instance — and incrementally bridge toward other forms of contact. Therapists who do sex therapy sometimes get quite detailed about transition points: getting most of the way there using one method, then shifting, so the body slowly learns to complete the loop from a new starting point.

Don't Rush to a Label

When someone has a confusing or negative sexual experience, a natural response is to reach for an explanation. One that comes up quickly is: maybe I'm asexual.

That might be true for some people. But it's worth being careful about adopting that identity immediately after a single bad experience, especially one that has clear physical and psychological explanations.

The problem with going to asexuality forums or spaces right after something like this is that people there — genuinely trying to be supportive — may affirm the label enthusiastically. Their experience resonates with yours, they've found community and peace in that identity, and they want to offer you the same. That's well-intentioned. But if your experience is actually the result of conditioned physical responses and performance anxiety, prematurely anchoring to that label could prevent you from discovering what your sexuality actually is.

"Just because you had a negative experience doesn't mean you're asexual. You may just have had a bad sexual experience and need to recondition your body."

Give yourself time and room to experiment before deciding. Sexuality takes some exploration to understand — one difficult encounter is a data point, not a diagnosis.

A Quick Note on a Persistent Myth

There's a claim that circulates online — particularly in certain male spaces — that a woman who has had many sexual partners will have a physiologically "looser" vagina. This is simply not true anatomically.

The vagina is elastic tissue. Sexual activity does not permanently alter its structure, regardless of frequency or number of partners. The analogy holds: opening your mouth wide to take a big bite of food doesn't leave your mouth hanging open afterward. The body returns to its resting state. None of the physiological mechanisms people imagine here actually exist.

Believing this myth leads people to make unfair judgments about potential partners — and sometimes to end relationships that had real value. It's worth discarding cleanly.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating one bad experience as definitive. A single awkward or unpleasant sexual encounter tells you very little about your sexuality or your future. It tells you more about how unpracticed you are, which is fixable.

  • Jumping to identity labels too fast. Asexuality is real and valid — but so is "I've been masturbating in a very specific way for 18 years and my body needs time to adjust." Don't let an echo chamber answer a question that deserves more time.

  • Trying to push through without addressing the physical conditioning. If death grip syndrome is the underlying issue, just trying harder during intercourse won't solve it. The retraining has to happen deliberately, starting with solo sessions.

  • Dismissing a supportive partner's value. A patient, understanding partner is genuinely rare. Recognize that rather than letting shame push them away.

  • Believing the movie version of sex. If your template for what sex "should" be came from films or pornography, it almost certainly doesn't match how real sex actually unfolds between two people who are new to each other. That mismatch between expectation and reality generates a lot of unnecessary shame.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start retraining solo. For the next several weeks, change your masturbation habits deliberately: introduce lubrication, reduce grip pressure, and slow down. It will feel less satisfying at first. That's the conditioning breaking down — stick with it.

  2. Have an honest conversation with your partner. If they already know your background and have been supportive, they're a resource. You don't have to engineer every encounter alone. Let them know what helps and what doesn't.

  3. Expand what counts as "sex." Stop treating penetrative intercourse as the only valid goal. Explore other forms of physical intimacy with your partner and notice what feels connecting and pleasurable. Use those as the bridge toward reconditioning, not as consolation prizes.

  4. Hold the "am I asexual?" question lightly. Don't answer it yet. Give yourself a few months of patient exploration with a supportive partner before drawing conclusions about your sexuality. Note your experience without immediately labeling it.

  5. Audit your expectations. Write down honestly what you believed sex would feel like the first time. Then write down what actually happened. The gap between those two things is where the shame lives — and naming that gap clearly makes it much easier to stop punishing yourself for not living up to a fantasy.

  6. Be patient with the timeline. Reconditioning a physical response that took 18 years to build won't happen in a week. Approach this the way you'd approach learning any physical skill: consistent practice, gradual progress, no single session as a pass/fail test.
