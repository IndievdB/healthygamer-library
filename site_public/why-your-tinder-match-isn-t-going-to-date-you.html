Summary

TL;DR — Dating apps promised to help you find love faster by filtering out incompatible people, but they actually make it harder. The reason is simple: the things apps can measure have almost nothing to do with what makes a relationship work.

Dating apps feel like progress. You're swiping, matching, messaging — surely something's happening. But there's a gap between what these apps promise, what they can actually measure, and what science says determines relationship success. Understanding that gap explains a lot of frustration that people tend to blame on themselves.

The Original Promise vs. What Actually Happens

The founding argument of dating apps went something like this: meeting people in real life is inefficient. You might date 20 or 30 people over years before finding someone compatible. By filtering for religion, lifestyle, kids, shared interests — all upfront — we'll narrow the field for you. Fewer bad dates, faster path to a real relationship.

It sounds reasonable. The problem is that the opposite has happened.

People now go through hundreds or thousands of profiles to find a single relationship. The app that was supposed to reduce the number of people you wade through has multiplied it enormously. The filters that were supposed to concentrate compatibility have diffused it. The promise was fewer rejections; the reality is astronomically more.

What Actually Predicts Relationship Success

There's real research on what makes relationships work. Things like:

  • Satisfaction, commitment, trust, intimacy, passion, love — the core components of perceived relationship quality
  • Prior dating success — one of the strongest predictors of whether someone transitions from not dating to a committed relationship
  • Attachment style — specifically, attachment avoidance (not anxiety) predicts failure to enter committed relationships, even when other factors are controlled for

Now ask yourself: does any dating app show you another person's attachment style? Does it show you their history of dating success? Does it ask whether you know your own attachment style?

No. What apps collect is what apps can collect — photos, height, stated preferences, a bio. And somewhere along the way, we started assuming that those things correlate with relationship compatibility. They don't. It's like a bank deciding whether to give you a loan based on the size of your car's tires because they can't access your bank account. The measurement is available; it just doesn't mean what you're treating it as meaning.

"Dating apps have this assumption that the crap that you put into your profile has some impact on whether you wind up in a relationship or not."

The profile variables apps ask you to fill in are not the variables that science identifies as predictive of relationship success. So a system built on measuring the wrong things will produce — at best — random results, and at worst, worse results than doing nothing.

How Apps Quietly Changed the Goal

Here's the subtler problem: apps didn't just fail to deliver relationships. They changed what you were optimizing for without telling you.

Early on, the stated goal was relationships. But apps can't measure relationships — those take months or years to develop. What they can measure is matches. And so matches became the metric. Match rates, swipe strategies, profile tweaks, photo selection — all of it is aimed at getting matches, not at finding a partner.

"They changed the instrument of measurement to something that... we weren't trying to maximize our matches, we were trying to maximize our relationships."

More matches does not equal a better chance at a relationship. There's an implicit assumption that it does — that if you just get enough matches, a relationship will eventually come out the other end. But if the matching criteria don't correlate with compatibility, increasing the volume just increases the noise.

Why We Keep Using Them Anyway

If they don't work as advertised, why does everyone use them? Because the cost of using an app for five minutes while sitting on your couch is almost zero. The cost of actually going out to meet people — shower, plans, social energy, potential awkwardness — is high and upfront.

Apps offer the illusion of progress at minimal immediate cost. This is exactly how slot machines work. The odds are terrible, but pulling the lever (or pressing a button) takes almost no effort, and occasionally you get a reward signal — a match notification, a message, a little dopamine hit. The app is designed to feel like forward motion even when it statistically isn't.

"The illusion of progress is so accessible that we just do it very easily."

The problem with low upfront cost is that it disguises the real cost — which accumulates over months and years of going through people without forming anything lasting.

What Apps Are Actually Good For

This isn't an argument that apps have zero value. They genuinely can:

  • Connect people who would never cross paths in their normal social circles
  • Provide a lower-pressure starting point for people with social anxiety
  • Reconnect people from shared pasts who never acted on mutual interest at the time

The issue is using them for something they weren't built to deliver — namely, a reliable path to a committed, healthy relationship — and then blaming yourself when that doesn't materialize.

You May Not Know What You're Looking For

There's one more layer here: apps assume you know your own preferences. You select the filters, you swipe on what attracts you, and the assumption is that your stated preferences align with what would actually make you happy in a relationship.

The research suggests that's often not true. Attachment style, for example, influences who you're drawn to and how you behave in relationships — but most people can't accurately identify their own attachment style without help. Traditional matchmakers had an edge here: they would observe you, form an outside assessment of your personality and weaknesses, and make a recommendation based on that, rather than just taking your self-report at face value.

"There's an assumption that you subjectively know what you're looking for — which is chances are we don't."

This isn't an indictment of self-knowledge. It's just that the variables that predict relationship success are often things we can't observe in ourselves, let alone communicate through a profile.

Common Pitfalls

  • Blaming your profile. If you tweak your photos, rewrite your bio, and change your swiping strategy and nothing changes, that's not because you haven't found the right combination yet. The profile contents don't meaningfully predict match rates or relationship success.
  • Treating matches as progress. A higher match count is not a step toward a relationship unless the matching criteria actually correlate with compatibility — and mostly they don't.
  • Assuming the apps are doing what they said they would. The foundational promise — that filtering upfront reduces the number of people you have to go through — has not been borne out. Treating the app as if it's working as advertised leads you to keep trying harder at something that has a structural problem.
  • Dismissing family input entirely, or accepting it entirely. Family members who have watched you grow up and have more relationship experience than you do sometimes see things you can't. That doesn't mean their criteria (like demanding a "10 out of 10") are right, but the reflex to ignore all external input can be as costly as the reflex to defer entirely.
  • Thinking disownment or relationship ultimatums are permanent. People, especially family, often issue ultimatums they later walk back — particularly as time passes and new circumstances (like grandchildren) create new incentives to reconnect.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what you're actually optimizing for. Write down your goal — is it matches, dates, or a relationship? Then honestly assess whether what you're spending time on (profile tweaking, swipe volume) has a logical connection to that actual goal.

  2. Learn your attachment style. Look up the basics of anxious, avoidant, and secure attachment. Take a validated questionnaire (they're freely available online). This is one of the variables that actually predicts relationship outcomes, and most people don't know theirs.

  3. If you use apps, use them for what they're good at. Let them be a way to initiate contact with people you'd never otherwise meet — then move the relationship off the app as quickly as possible. Don't mistake conversation-starting for relationship-building.

  4. Raise the real upfront cost occasionally. Make a concrete plan — once a month, or more often if you can manage it — to meet people in a context where relationship-relevant qualities are visible: shared activities, social groups, recurring environments. The higher upfront cost comes with actual signal.

  5. If you get feedback after a date, listen carefully. If someone tells you they had a good time but doesn't want to continue, resist the urge to file it away as "their loss." Ask one genuine follow-up question and actually sit with the answer. Patterns across multiple dates are data.

  6. Have an honest conversation with family members who have opinions about your relationships. Ask open-ended questions: What do you think makes a relationship healthy long-term? What would concern you about someone I was seeing? Their answers will tell you both what's worth considering and what's about their priorities rather than yours.
