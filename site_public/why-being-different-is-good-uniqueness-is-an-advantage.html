Summary

TL;DR — You feel like a failure because you're measuring yourself against someone else's life. Once you stop doing that, you can start playing your own game — and you might actually be very good at it.

Most people carry a quiet sense of failure that logic can't touch. You can look at your life, see the things that are objectively fine, and still feel like you're falling behind. That feeling doesn't come from your actual circumstances. It comes from comparing your unique situation to a script that was never written for you. The fix isn't to try harder at the wrong game. It's to figure out what game you're actually playing.

Why Success Doesn't Fix the Feeling of Failure

The feeling of failure is not a logical calculation. It's not a response to your bank balance, your job title, or whether you have a family. There are investment bankers making over a million dollars a year who sit in an office and still feel like failures. The emotion doesn't care about the evidence.

This matters because it means chasing conventional success as a cure for inadequacy doesn't work. You can hit every checkpoint on the traditional path — career, family, stability — and the feeling will follow you there. The emotion is responding to something else entirely: the gap between who you are and who you think you're supposed to be. As long as you're measuring yourself against someone else's template, the gap never closes.

Every Life Is a Different Campaign

Here's a framing that cuts through the noise: every human being who has ever lived has had a completely different set of circumstances, a different inner world, different strengths, different starting conditions. You are not running behind on a shared race. You are playing your own RPG — your own unique campaign with its own villain, its own class, its own skill distribution.

When you try to play someone else's campaign, you are guaranteed to fail — not because you're inadequate, but because that campaign wasn't designed for your character. You're walking onto a golf course and wondering why you can't hit a three-pointer.

"If you're a failure, it's because you're playing the wrong game."

This isn't motivational fluff. It's a structural observation. The traditional script for success — degree, stable career, family, linear progression — is a generalization built from averaging across millions of different people. It doesn't describe any one person's actual optimal path. It describes a kind of statistical center. Most people aren't at that center, and that's not a defect.

Your Flaws Might Be Pointing Somewhere

The RPG analogy goes deeper. In many role-playing games, you can take perk flaws — negative traits that give you extra points to spend elsewhere. Think of your quirks, limitations, and non-traditional qualities that way. The fact that you can't sit in the same chair for eight hours doesn't mean you're broken. It might mean your points got spent elsewhere.

The problem is that most people are so busy blaming themselves for the flaws that they never stop to find the corresponding strengths. You might have a +30 in something you've never used, because you've been trying so hard to be adequate at the things the script demands.

"There's something that you're good at. You're just too busy blaming yourself for not being good at what other people are good at to even stop and figure that out."

Your first job, then, isn't to fix your weaknesses. It's to locate your actual strengths — and those are often hidden in the things you already care about.

The World Is Changing — Your "Useless" Interest Might Not Be

Ten years ago, if you said you wanted to make a living playing video games, the honest answer would have been: tough. The industry had high barriers, few entry points, and a specific pipeline you had to break into. Today, Undertale was made by one person. Axiom Verge was made by one person. Prize pools in competitive gaming exceed those of many traditional professional sports.

The point isn't specifically about gaming. It's that the barriers to publishing, building, and reaching an audience have collapsed across almost every field. You can write a book without a publishing contract (self-publish). You can make a game without a studio (Steam). You can start a channel, a newsletter, a podcast, a course — the infrastructure is already there. The world has quietly reorganized itself in a way that rewards people who pursue non-traditional paths with genuine commitment.

This means that the question "is there a market for what I love?" needs to be asked again, honestly, given the world as it is now — not the world as it was.

Finding Your Path: Start With What You Like

Dharma — a Sanskrit word meaning duty or responsibility — is the idea that there's a role in the world that fits who you actually are. Finding it doesn't require a vision quest. It starts with a simpler question: what do you actually like?

Not what you're supposed to like. Not what's practical. What genuinely interests you, even if it seems useless or frivolous. Start there.

From that starting point, the next step is figuring out how to make it work in the actual world — not by abandoning the interest, but by being creative about structure. A famous example: author Brandon Sanderson worked as a night manager at a hotel while he was writing his early books. Everyone was asleep; he got paid to be present; he could write for eight hours a night while his bills were covered. He didn't need the publishing industry to hand him an opportunity. He engineered his own conditions.

That kind of thinking — starting from where you actually are, not where you think you should be — is the practical move. What does your situation actually allow? What unconventional arrangement might give you the time and stability to pursue what you care about?

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "non-traditional" with "easy." Letting go of the traditional script doesn't mean coasting. Whatever path you pursue has to be done well. Half-hearted work on your own terms is still half-hearted work.

Waiting until you feel ready or legitimate. The feeling that you're a failure for not following the traditional path can paradoxically stop you from starting on your real path. You don't become valid by meeting someone else's criteria and then pivoting. You start from where you are.

Treating your interests as too small to matter. The tendency to dismiss what you genuinely love as "not serious" or "not marketable" is often just internalized comparison. Ask whether that's actually true given the world today, not the world of twenty years ago.

Looking for external confirmation before committing. The traditional path is socially legible — people understand it, and that provides a kind of ambient validation. An unconventional path often doesn't look like anything recognizable, especially at the beginning. That discomfort gets mistaken for evidence that you're doing something wrong.

Confusing acceptance of your path with giving up. Accepting that your path is different isn't resignation. It's the prerequisite for actually making progress on the path that's yours.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your actual stats. Write down your genuine strengths — especially ones you've been ignoring because they don't fit the conventional template. Also write down your real limitations without judgment. Treat it like a character sheet. What did you actually get?

  2. Ask "what do I like?" without filtering for practicality. Just list it. Don't self-edit based on what seems viable. The viability question comes second.

  3. Re-evaluate the market with current eyes. For each interest you've dismissed as non-marketable, spend 30 minutes actually looking at what exists today — communities, platforms, income models, solo creators. Is your old assumption still accurate?

  4. Look for the Brandon Sanderson move. Given your actual circumstances — your schedule, your energy, your financial situation — what unconventional arrangement might let you pursue what matters while keeping the lights on? Think structure, not just motivation.

  5. Start from where you are, not where you should be. Identify one concrete small action in the direction of your actual path that you can take this week. Not a plan. An action.

  6. Notice when you're measuring yourself against someone else's campaign. When the feeling of failure shows up, ask: whose standard am I applying right now? Is this even my game?
