Summary

TL;DR — Your job in dating isn't to make someone like you; it's to show up as yourself and let people self-select. The pressure you feel is mostly coming from the wrong goal.

Most dating anxiety comes from a misunderstanding of what you're actually supposed to be doing when you talk to someone. You've turned a two-way interaction into a performance you have to nail — and that framing makes everything harder. Once you understand what your actual role is, the conversation itself becomes much simpler.

You Are Not a Salesperson for Yourself

The core mistake is thinking it's your job to generate the other person's interest. It isn't. Think of yourself as a street vendor who sets out a table of goods. Your job is to put your stuff on the table. If someone walks by and something catches their eye, they'll stop. If not, they won't — and that has nothing to do with whether your goods are worth having.

When you go into a conversation trying to manufacture someone's interest, two things happen:

  1. You stop being yourself and start performing something you think they want to see.
  2. That performance is cognitively exhausting, which makes you stiff, less fun, and harder to connect with.

Authenticity isn't just morally nice — it's practically effective. When you're focused on genuinely enjoying yourself and expressing who you are, you're relaxed. When you're focused on saying the right thing to make someone like you, you're living a low-grade lie, and people can feel the strain of it.

"Just be who you are and display that, and for the people that are interested they'll respond."

This also applies regardless of gender or orientation. Nobody benefits from contorting themselves into a shape they think will be acceptable. The relationship you end up with would be built on a fiction, and that always costs you later.

The Mechanics: How to Move Past Small Talk

There is a concrete structure that works for getting a conversation off the ground and keeping it moving.

The formula: - Open-ended question - Genuine, investigative follow-up response - A small personal share of your own - Roughly a 2-to-1 question-to-sharing ratio

The point of an open-ended question is that it gives the other person room to actually tell you something about themselves, rather than just answering yes or no and handing the floor back to you. Questions like "What do you do for a living?" are technically open-ended but tend to feel like a job interview. Better prompts invite someone to reveal something about their inner life or recent experience — what's excited them, what a specific period of time was like for them, what they're looking forward to.

Once they answer, don't immediately fire the next question. Follow up on what they actually said. Show curiosity about the specific thing they mentioned. Then, after you've explored that a bit, share something of your own — something real, not something you think will impress them.

Make your intentions clear early. Flirt a little. If a moment of warmth or attraction comes up naturally, say something that acknowledges it rather than suppressing it out of fear. You don't have to be heavy-handed about it — a light, slightly self-deprecating comment that lets the person know you're interested is enough. Then you watch how they respond. If they laugh or lean in, great. If they pull back, you've learned something useful and you can recalibrate.

"Make your intentions known pretty early."

The Most Underused Move: Radical Honesty

If you're stuck and genuinely don't know how to start a conversation, the most powerful thing you can do is just say that — out loud, to the person you want to talk to.

Something like: "For the last ten minutes I've been trying to figure out how to strike up a conversation with you because I think you're gorgeous and I haven't been able to come up with anything. Can you help me out?"

This sounds terrifying, but it works for a specific reason: it's completely authentic. You're not performing. You're not using a line. You're just telling the truth about what's happening inside you, and that's disarming in a way no technique can replicate. The vulnerability itself is the connection point.

Rejection Is Not What You Think It Is

A lot of the fear around approaching people comes from dreading rejection. But what actually happens when someone turns you down politely?

If you were genuine and respectful, there's no real damage. The person who said no will likely think about that interaction later and feel good about it — someone found them attractive and treated them with dignity. You've given them something, even in the "failure" case.

The anxiety about rejection is mostly self-focused: How does this make me look? How embarrassed will I feel? But the actual cost of a graceful, honest approach that doesn't work out is close to zero for you, and it may be quietly meaningful for the other person.

What makes an approach cringe — and actually uncomfortable for everyone — isn't the attempt itself. It's ignoring boundaries, not reading responses, or pushing past a clear signal to stop. That's the thing to avoid, not the approach.

"Even if she turns you down — as long as you're not cringe about it — she's going to wake up the next morning and feel like an attractive person. Which is an awesome thing."

You can also, after a graceful rejection, ask a simple question: "If I think of something good later, can I take another swing?" You're making your intentions known, you're asking permission rather than assuming, and you're showing that you have enough self-respect to try without being pushy about it. She'll either say yes or no, and either way, you've handled it well.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to say the "right" thing. There is no magic line. Searching for the perfect opener keeps you paralyzed while the moment passes. What you say matters less than whether it comes from a real place.

Asking closed or surface-level questions. Yes/no questions and generic openers ("What do you do?") produce flat answers and stall the conversation. They also signal you're not particularly curious about this specific person.

Mistaking performance for confidence. Real confidence isn't projecting certainty or having slick answers. It's being comfortable enough with who you are that you don't need to hide it. Performing confidence is just more inauthenticity with better packaging.

Over-investing in the outcome. When you're laser-focused on whether this person likes you, you stop actually paying attention to them. Genuine curiosity about another human being is far more attractive than someone anxiously trying to manage how they're coming across.

Interpreting every rejection as personal failure. Most of the time, someone not being interested has nothing to do with your worth. They might be in a relationship, have a different type, or just not be in the headspace for it. A rejection is information, not a verdict.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reframe your goal before any interaction. Before approaching someone or sending a message, remind yourself: your job is to put yourself out there honestly, not to manufacture their interest. You are laying out the table; they decide whether to stop.

  2. Practice open-ended questions. Write out three to five questions that invite a real answer — things about a recent experience, something they're looking forward to, or what a particular period has been like for them. Have these in your back pocket so you're not scrambling in the moment.

  3. Use the 2-to-1 ratio. In your next conversation, consciously try to ask two follow-up questions for every one thing you share about yourself. This keeps you curious rather than performative, and it takes pressure off you to fill the silence.

  4. Try the honest opener once. The next time you genuinely want to talk to someone and feel stuck, just say that. Tell them you've been trying to think of how to approach them and couldn't come up with anything. Notice what happens.

  5. Practice graceful exits. If someone signals they're not interested, say something simple and kind — "Thanks for letting me know, enjoy your night" — and mean it. Practicing this removes the fear of rejection, because you have a dignified off-ramp ready.

  6. Flirt early and lightly, then read the room. Don't wait until you've been talking for an hour to signal interest. Drop something warm and slightly playful early, keep it light, and then pay close attention to how they respond. That response tells you everything you need to know about whether to continue.
