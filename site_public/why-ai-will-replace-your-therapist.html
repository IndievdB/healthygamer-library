Summary

TL;DR — Therapy doesn't work for everyone, and the reasons why are more nuanced than "you didn't try hard enough" or "you haven't found the right fit." Understanding where therapy genuinely falls short — and where the patient's own patterns may be part of the picture — is more honest and more useful than either dismissing therapy entirely or defending it unconditionally.

Three practicing psychologists (Dr. Kirk Honda, Dr. Michaela Thorderson, and Dr. K) respond to a raw, detailed post from someone who has tried nearly every evidence-based therapy modality and still feels completely stuck. They then compare their responses to ChatGPT's answer to the same prompt. The conversation surfaces some genuinely uncomfortable truths about therapy, about AI, and about what it actually takes to change.

Life Contains Irreducible Suffering — That's Not a Therapy Failure

The first thing all three clinicians agree on: therapy was never supposed to eliminate suffering. If that's the promise you were sold, the promise was false.

Therapists in training often arrive believing they'll be able to solve their clients' problems. Experienced clinicians have to actively help trainees shed that expectation. The goal of therapy is more modest — not a perfect life, but a reduced suffering-to-pain ratio. Pain is the unavoidable reality of hard circumstances. Suffering is what happens when you refuse to accept that pain, or do things that amplify it. Therapy can reduce suffering. It cannot eliminate pain.

This distinction matters enormously for the person in the original post — and for anyone who has measured therapy against the standard of "complete relief" and found it wanting.

The Common Element Nobody Wants to Name

There's a pattern that clinicians recognize but rarely say out loud: sometimes the patient is the common element.

If someone has dated 15 people and found every relationship unworkable, we don't usually conclude that romance is broken. If someone has had 15 jobs that all fell apart, we don't conclude that employment doesn't work. But when someone has tried 15 therapists and 10 modalities with no improvement, the instinct is often to conclude that therapy is the problem.

"What's the common element? The common element is the patient."

That isn't blame. It's a clinical observation. The question isn't "whose fault is this?" It's "what is this person bringing into every therapeutic encounter that might be getting in the way?"

One specific mechanism: the nocebo effect. If you enter therapy convinced it won't work, you are probably right. Repeated failed treatments don't just leave someone where they started — they actively build a prior expectation of failure. Each new therapist inherits that accumulated skepticism. The first thing an experienced clinician might do with a patient like this is explicitly try to dismantle the expectation that they can fix everything, because that expectation itself is part of what prevents progress.

What Good Clinicians Do That AI Doesn't

One of the sharpest observations in the conversation: when the three psychologists received the prompt, their first move was to ask more questions. ChatGPT's first move was to provide answers.

Effective clinical work requires more information than a single post can provide. The original person lists a long string of modalities — CBT, DBT, ACT, IFS, EMDR, somatic work — but critical details are completely absent:

  • How long were they in each treatment?
  • Were the therapists actually trained and competent in those modalities?
  • Was there ever a clear diagnosis guiding the treatment?
  • Did they try them all back-to-back without a break? (Therapeutic fatigue is real — doing too much of even a helpful thing becomes counterproductive.)

EMDR in particular has documented risk of harm when administered by someone who has only partially learned the technique. "I tried EMDR" and "I received competent EMDR from a fully trained practitioner" are not the same statement. The same applies across the list. There is no quality control in mental health care, which means it's entirely possible someone has tried eight treatments and received substandard delivery of all eight.

What ChatGPT Does Well — and Where It Falls Apart

ChatGPT's response is worth examining carefully because it reveals both the genuine strengths and the meaningful limits of AI in this space.

What it gets right: - It validates without pivoting immediately to new solutions — it doesn't say "have you tried this other therapy?" - It correctly identifies that some needs (the need to have never been harmed, to have always been loved) genuinely cannot be met retroactively, and that this is a legitimate grief rather than a therapeutic failure - It surfaces a possible attachment or relational deprivation pattern at the core of the person's pain — a clinically reasonable hypothesis - Its final suggestion — that community, chosen family, art, shared experience, and "things that aren't trying to fix you" might matter more than formal therapy — is essentially good clinical thinking (it's close to what Acceptance and Commitment Therapy recommends about values-based living) - Its linguistic mirroring of the poster's tone and vocabulary is strikingly effective

Where it breaks down: - Some of its most resonant-sounding phrases are close to meaningless on examination. "Presence alone transforms" sounds profound but doesn't actually communicate a clear idea. This is what happens when a language model assembles words that pattern-match to therapeutic language without grounding them in a coherent clinical concept - It contradicts itself without seeming to notice: it concludes "some pain just is" while simultaneously implying that honest grief leads to "something different starting" - It says the language of "resistance" and "unreadiness" is "often used to preserve faith in therapy" — framing therapists as self-serving. That's an overclaim. Resistance is a real clinical phenomenon. The appropriate critique is that some clinicians use it as an excuse for poor work, not that invoking it is generally a bad-faith move

"It's basically doing ACT, but it's saying ACT is shitty and therapy doesn't work, and then it's doing ACT."

The deeper issue is that ChatGPT's answer feels good to read. That feeling is not the same as the answer being useful. Someone in genuine pain who reads that response may feel seen and validated in the moment without any of their underlying patterns being identified or addressed.

The Thing the Person Is Actually Holding Onto

The most revealing moment in the discussion comes from a community member's blunt response to the original post: your identity has become being broken and unfixable, and healing is subconsciously terrifying because it means losing that identity.

The original poster's reply is striking: they don't reject this. They say the person is right that they're terrified — but not because suffering feels safe. Because moving on feels like abandoning the only thing that has ever felt real. Every time someone says "let go of that need," it feels like amputation.

"What I want isn't to be broken. What I want is the thing that would fix it. And every time someone tells me to let go of that need, it feels like they're asking me to amputate the last piece of myself that still knows what wholeness would look like."

This is clinically significant. The resistance isn't stubbornness. It's the person protecting something that feels load-bearing. The therapeutic question isn't "why won't you let go?" It's "what would you lose if you let go, and how do we grieve that loss rather than just demanding it happen?"

There's a DBT metaphor that fits: recovering from deep psychological pain is like climbing out of hell on a metal ladder. Every rung burns your hands. Each step requires letting go of something that felt like it was holding you. If you've been climbing long enough without reaching the top, it becomes genuinely rational to stop believing there is a top.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the modality list as proof of effort. Naming ten therapies you've tried says very little about the quality, duration, or appropriateness of any of them. "I tried it" and "I received competent, sufficient, well-matched treatment" are different claims.

  • Concluding the system is broken when the common element might be you. This isn't blame — it's taking seriously that patterns of relating, expecting, and engaging follow you into every room. That pattern is also the most direct target for change.

  • Mistaking linguistic resonance for clinical usefulness. A response that sounds exactly right and makes you feel deeply understood is not the same as a response that will actually help you change. AI is very good at the former.

  • Setting the bar at "total relief." If the benchmark is "therapy works only if it eliminates my suffering entirely," almost no treatment will clear it. The meaningful question is whether suffering decreases, coping improves, and life becomes more livable — incrementally.

  • Therapy fatigue without recognizing it. Doing back-to-back intensive treatments for years is not the same as sustained therapeutic work. It can actually impair your ability to benefit from the next treatment.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your treatment history honestly. For each therapy or modality you've tried, write down: how long you were in it, whether the practitioner was fully trained and experienced, and what the target was. Notice whether you have detailed answers or vague ones. Vagueness often signals incomplete trials.

  2. Identify the common element. Across all the things that haven't worked — what did you bring into each one? What was your belief about whether it would work? What did you do when it got hard? You're not looking for blame. You're looking for pattern.

  3. Define what "better" would actually look like. Write a concrete, specific description of what your life would look like if therapy "worked" — not perfect, but meaningfully improved. If your answer involves zero pain, revise it. If you can't write anything down, that absence is information.

  4. Ask whether you're grieving or resisting. There is a real difference between mourning genuine unmeetable needs (e.g., a childhood you deserved but didn't get) and holding onto pain because letting go feels like losing something essential. Both deserve compassion. But they call for different responses.

  5. Look outside the therapy room. Community, shared experience, values-based activity, and genuine mutual relationships can do things that a bounded, paid, hour-long clinical relationship cannot. If relational deprivation is at the root of your pain, ask what you're actively doing to build real connection — not to replace therapy, but alongside it or instead of it.

  6. Before starting a new treatment, surface your expectations. Tell your clinician directly: "I've tried a lot and I don't believe this will work either." A good therapist will work with that openly. A therapist who ignores it or dismisses it is already missing something important.
