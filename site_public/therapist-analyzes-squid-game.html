Summary

TL;DR — Every major character in Squid Game maps onto a real psychological condition, and understanding those conditions through fiction makes mental illness easier to recognize — in others and in yourself.

Squid Game is essentially a pressure cooker that forces people to crack along their pre-existing psychological fault lines. The show exaggerates these conditions into caricatures, but the underlying patterns are real. Walking through the cast one by one reveals how addiction, attachment wounds, trauma, personality disorders, and sociopathy actually look and feel — and why the people who carry them aren't simply bad people.

Gi-hun: Addiction as a Brain Problem, Not a Moral Failure

Gi-hun steals his mom's cash, bets it on horses, wins big, and ultimately turns away from a boarding gate — from his daughter, from safety — to go back and play again. He has every material reason to stop. He can't.

That's the defining feature of pathological gambling, a behavioral addiction. Two specific brain-level tendencies drive it:

  • Impulsivity — difficulty restraining urges in the moment
  • Future discounting — a measurable tendency to choose a smaller reward now over a larger reward later; in research experiments, people with behavioral addictions disproportionately take $1 today over $10 tomorrow

The show handles this well because it makes clear Gi-hun is a good person. That's actually the point. His daughter still loves him. He genuinely cares. Episode 6 is devastating precisely because we've been rooting for him — watching someone decent get corroded by something he didn't choose makes the condition feel real rather than vilifying.

An important note: psychiatric illness isn't binary. Addiction exists on a spectrum. You don't need a ruined life to qualify for help. If you recognize strong impulsivity or a pattern of choosing short-term highs at long-term cost, that's worth exploring with a professional — even if you're still functioning.

Sae-byeok: When the World Never Felt Safe

Sae-byeok is quiet, distrustful, and mostly contained — until something trips a wire and she scalds someone with hot tea. Then she's contained again. This cycling between emotional flatness and sudden volatility, combined with a deep difficulty trusting people, points toward attachment disorder.

Attachment theory explains how early relationships shape whether we experience the world as a safe place. When a child is raised with consistent emotional mirroring — a parent who expresses care when the child is hurt — the child internalizes two things: what I feel is understandable, and other people will show up for me. That becomes the foundation for trust and connection.

When that mirroring is absent, inconsistent, or replaced by anger, the opposite gets encoded. The world becomes a PvP zone. People are threats. Letting your guard down is dangerous.

Someone raised in an environment like that — and growing up in North Korea offers as stark an example as fiction could invent — doesn't develop distrust as a personality flaw. They develop it as an accurate map of the world they grew up in. The problem is that map stays active long after the original environment is gone.

Ji-heon: Complex Trauma Looks Like Being Switched Off

Ji-heon is quieter than Sae-byeok, less volatile, harder to read. She's not paranoid. She's just... absent. Empty. She doesn't seek connection; she barely registers it. Except around the religious man in the game, where something cold ignites in her.

That specific trigger — intense anger directed at religious expression specifically — is a clue. Her history involves an abusive father who was hyper-religious. That anger isn't random; it's her nervous system responding to a pattern it learned to fear.

She's a portrait of Complex PTSD (CPTSD), which differs from standard PTSD in an important way: standard PTSD can develop from a single catastrophic event. CPTSD develops from repeated trauma over time — chronic abuse, ongoing neglect, environments where harm was unpredictable and inescapable.

The hallmarks of CPTSD include:

  • Feeling empty, worthless, or fundamentally different from other people
  • Difficulty forming or sustaining relationships
  • Dissociation — a kind of internal going-offline
  • A damaged sense of self: not just low self-esteem, but genuinely not knowing who you are on the inside

That last piece matters more than it might seem. Having a goal or a purpose requires being a kind of protagonist in your own life. If your internal sense of self was damaged before it could fully form, it's hard to want things, because wanting things requires knowing who the "you" doing the wanting is.

Trauma is also the great chameleon of psychiatry — it can look like depression, like bipolar disorder, like laziness, like checked-out indifference. Ji-heon might easily be misread as depressed. The distinction matters for treatment.

Her willingness to sacrifice herself for Sae-byeok reads as heroism on screen. There's a sadder undercurrent: people with CPTSD often genuinely believe their life is worth less than other people's. That's not self-sacrifice. That's internalized worthlessness.

When Two Broken People Find Each Other

Sae-byeok and Ji-heon pair up. This pattern — traumatized people gravitating toward other traumatized people — shows up constantly in clinical settings, especially in residential trauma treatment.

It makes sense. Two people who feel like half a person can find something like wholeness together. There's a sense of genuine recognition: you don't have to explain yourself here, this person already understands the shape of the wound.

These bonds can be real and powerful. In recovery settings, two people walking a hard road together sometimes build something lasting and genuinely good.

More often, though, two people who both struggle to trust, to connect, and to regulate emotionally don't have the resources to support each other through the stress that any relationship eventually generates. The connection is real; the structural capacity to maintain it is under-resourced on both sides.

This doesn't mean avoiding connection with people who share your experience. It means going in clear-eyed, and ideally with outside support, about what that relationship will require.

Min-yo and Han-mi-nyeo: Borderline Personality Disorder

Min-yo is warm, manipulative, hypersexual, and capable of flipping from devotion to indifference mid-scene. She extracts promises of loyalty and punishes their violation. She's the show's portrait of Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD).

The core of BPD is an unstable sense of identity — specifically, an identity that is almost entirely determined by how other people treat you. If people treat you with love and warmth, you feel loveable and whole. If they're cold or dismissive or absent, you feel worthless and empty. Most people are somewhat sensitive to social feedback; people with BPD are exquisitely, overwhelmingly sensitive to it.

This creates several visible patterns:

  • Mood swings that look like bipolar disorder but are actually responses to perceived relationship shifts
  • Fear of abandonment — attempts to extract promises, guilt-tripping, escalating behavior when someone tries to withdraw
  • Splitting — mentally sorting people into all-good or all-bad categories, with no stable middle ground; this often creates social chaos in group settings
  • Using sexuality or crisis to feel loved and to keep people from leaving

The manipulation that looks so calculated from the outside is largely not conscious strategy. It's a learned survival response: if I can get this person to show they love me, I will feel like someone worth loving.

One important correction to the show's framing: Min-yo is an exaggerated negative caricature. Real people with BPD are not villains. Media portrayals almost always show the worst-case characteristics — and tend to cast women in this role, just as they cast men as sociopaths. People with BPD often respond very well to treatment, particularly Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), which incorporates mindfulness and identity work. With good treatment and stable relationships, people can move well past the threshold of a diagnosable disorder.

Doc Soo and Sang-woo: The Sociopathy Spectrum

Doc Soo kills without remorse, forms alliances only when they serve him, and shows no guilt. Sang-woo does the same things more quietly, with better credentials and a better suit.

Sociopathy (Antisocial Personality Disorder) is characterized by reduced empathy and a weakened internal moral compass. It exists on a spectrum, and — critically — most of the research on it comes from prisons, which means we've been studying its most dysfunctional expression and generalizing from there.

Doc Soo is the low-functioning end. Sang-woo is the high-functioning version that makes it to Seoul National University, to finance, and to near-success before the pressure of a life-or-death game strips away the performance.

High-functioning sociopathy isn't rare in competitive environments. People who can disengage emotionally, tolerate cutthroat competition, and prioritize self-interest in high-stakes situations tend to do well in finance, surgery, executive leadership. Some degree of emotional numbness can be adaptive — a surgeon who falls apart at every complication, or a psychiatrist who carries home every patient death, couldn't function in the job.

Sang-woo isn't purely cold. He shows genuine flashes of empathy and internal conflict. The show tracks this in his facial expressions. That's actually a more honest portrayal than the pure-villain version — most people with sociopathic tendencies feel some empathy; they just discount others' interests more readily and more automatically than most.

Oh Il-nam: What Money Can't Fix

Oh Il-nam is the show's strangest and most philosophically interesting figure: a man with everything who created a death game to feel alive.

The concept at work here is what might be called Buddha syndrome: the experience of achieving material success, security, love, and status — and discovering that none of it produces contentment. Buddha himself described this. He had everything, felt empty, and gave it all up in search of what actually produced happiness.

Research on happiness does show that material security matters — up to a point. It protects against despair, stress, and fear. But beyond that threshold, more money doesn't generate more wellbeing. Success and wealth can coexist with, and sometimes amplify, a profound sense of emptiness.

This is relevant outside of billionaire death-game orchestrators. Celebrities, streamers, people with every apparent advantage report this experience — and are frequently told they're not allowed to talk about it. One of the loneliest things you can do to someone is deny them the right to complain. Suffering doesn't get invalidated by privilege. Compassion doesn't have to be zero-sum.

The healthier resolution, per the Buddhist framing: detachment — from outcomes, from external validation, from the belief that achieving the next thing will finally deliver the feeling you're chasing. Combined with present-focus — directing attention and effort toward action itself rather than what the action is supposed to produce — this seems to be a more reliable path to wellbeing than any accumulation of external goods.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating these diagnoses as binary. You don't have to wreck your life before qualifying for help. All of these conditions exist on a spectrum.
  • Confusing media caricatures with real people. Squid Game portrays the most extreme, most dramatic version of each condition. Real humans with BPD, PTSD, or sociopathic tendencies are far more complex and far more sympathetic than their fictional counterparts.
  • Assuming addiction is a character flaw. The impulsivity and future discounting that drive behavioral addictions are measurable neurological tendencies, not moral failures.
  • Thinking trauma only looks like PTSD. Trauma can present as depression, emptiness, blunted affect, relationship avoidance, emotional volatility, or nothing that looks like trauma at all.
  • Assuming a traumatic bond automatically makes a good romantic relationship. Mutual recognition and understanding are real. That doesn't mean two people with attachment wounds have the resources to sustain a healthy relationship without outside support.
  • Using privilege as a reason to dismiss someone's suffering. Pain doesn't require material justification. Attacking people for being lucky isn't compassion in another direction — it's still attacking people.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify which character you most relate to. Not the diagnosis — the pattern. Do you make impulsive short-term choices you regret? Do you distrust people even when they've given you no reason to? Do you feel empty in ways you can't explain? Starting with a character can be less threatening than starting with a clinical label.

  2. Track your future discounting. For one week, notice every moment you choose a short-term reward over a longer-term benefit. Don't judge it — just notice. This builds the self-awareness that precedes change.

  3. If you grew up in an emotionally unpredictable household, consider whether your current model of "how people work" was built in a genuinely unsafe environment and may not match where you are now. A therapist who works with attachment issues can help you update that map.

  4. If you've been repeatedly traumatized, look into CPTSD specifically. It's distinct from single-event PTSD and is treated somewhat differently. Knowing the right language helps you find the right help.

  5. If you recognize BPD patterns in yourself — mood swings tied to how people treat you, fear of abandonment, difficulty tolerating withdrawal from others — seek out a therapist trained in DBT. It has the strongest evidence base for this presentation.

  6. Try a present-focus practice. Pick one recurring activity (eating, walking, working out) and spend it attending only to the action itself — not what it's for, not what comes after, not how it's going to turn out. This is the practical starting point of the detachment Buddha was describing.

  7. If you're rich, successful, or privileged and feel empty, take that seriously. "You have no reason to feel this way" is not useful information. The feeling is data. Seek help from someone trained in psychological wellbeing, not just symptom management.
