Summary

TL;DR — Life is a sandbox game with no built-in rules, no respawns, and one final boss: you. These 12 rules reframe the way you think about fairness, time, comparison, and personal responsibility using the language of gaming.

Most mental frameworks about life secretly assume it's supposed to work a certain way — that it should be fair, fun, or at least predictable. None of that is built into the game. Once you let go of what life is "supposed to be," you can start actually playing it.

The Foundation: What Kind of Game Is This?

The first thing to settle is what genre of game you're in.

Rule 1: Your spawn point is RNG. Where you're born, your race, your gender, your starting stats — none of that was chosen. It's a crapshoot. This is the modern equivalent of the karma concept: you don't control the hand you're dealt, only how you play it. Accepting this isn't resignation — it's honesty. Spending energy resenting your starting position is energy not spent playing.

Rule 2: Life isn't supposed to be anything — it's a sandbox. The sense of entitlement that says this shouldn't be so hard or I deserve to enjoy this is a confusion about genre. A sandbox game has no objective baked in. You can walk in any direction. That's not a flaw; that's the whole point. Drop the mental image of what life was supposed to look like and look at what it actually is.

Rule 5: There is no respawning, and the servers go offline after 72 years on average. This isn't morbid — it's clarifying. You get one run. The clock is real. That changes how you hold the time you have right now.

The Time Problem (It's Not What You Think)

One of the most paralyzing beliefs is the idea that you've fallen too far behind to catch up. This belief is wrong, and the math is straightforward.

Rule 6: There's no exponential XP requirement to level up, and content doesn't expire.

In most RPGs, leveling up gets harder the higher you go. Life doesn't work that way. The time it takes to read a book is the same whether you're 20 or 50. Four years is what it takes to finish college — whether you start at 18, 28, or 48. A year is what it takes to start putting your life together. Three months is enough to take the first real steps.

You can be a virgin at 30 and sexually active at 40. You can pick up a skill today that you ignored for the last decade. The content is out there. It's not locked behind a gate that closes when you hit a certain age.

"Just because you've wasted some amount of time doesn't mean that you can't do it."

The feeling of being behind is real, but the mechanics of being behind are mostly fictional. Your emotions about lost time don't make it harder to learn something new — they just make you feel bad while learning it.

How to Play With Others

Rule 3: It's your choice whether life is PvP or co-op. Every interaction is a decision point. When someone needs help, do you support them or invalidate them? When you need help, do you ask for it? Complaining about how broken the world is while doing nothing is PvP. Getting up and doing something — even something small — is co-op.

Rule 4: Some enemies are world bosses, and world bosses can't be soloed. Misogyny, climate change, gun violence — no individual can fix these alone. That doesn't mean you do nothing. The way world bosses get defeated is that everyone does what they know in their heart to be completely insufficient.

"No snowflake believes it is responsible for the avalanche."

Your individual action genuinely feels meaningless against problems that large. Do it anyway.

Rule 9: Success depends on your raid guild. The company you keep between your mid-twenties and mid-thirties shapes what you're capable of more than most people want to admit. Time spent with people who are operating at a high level pulls you toward that level. Time spent with people who are drifting pulls you toward that. This isn't a judgment — it's mechanics. If you want to down harder content, find a better guild.

Two Traps to Watch Out For

Rule 7: Beware people selling walkthroughs to sandbox games.

There is no walkthrough for your life. Your stats, your starting equipment, your party members, the enemies you've been aggroed by — this combination has never existed before. Anyone selling you a guaranteed system for turning your life around is selling something that, at best, approximates your situation and, at worst, is pure fiction. This includes well-meaning experts and coaches. Their frameworks may contain useful pieces. None of them is a complete map for your life. You're the only one who can figure that out.

Rule 10: Any build is viable — stop comparing.

Mimicking six different people's strategies because each one looks impressive from the outside is how you end up with no strategy at all. In MOBAs there's a familiar trap: you get destroyed by a hero, you pick that hero next game, you get destroyed again, you blame the next overpowered pick. The same pattern plays out in real life when you keep switching to whatever build looks like it's winning for someone else.

An author is viable. A doctor is viable. Someone who compiles the best YouTube content into a daily channel is viable. The internet has made niche viable in a way it never was before. Pick your build and get good at it. Chasing other people's builds keeps you permanently mediocre at multiple things instead of genuinely skilled at one.

Playing the Current Meta

Rule 11: Exploit the meta. Life has a meta, and it shifts. Conditions that were disadvantages 30 years ago can be advantages now. A shorter attention span and a high-energy, fast-switching mind was a liability in a world built around factory work and long-form reading. That same mind may be well-suited to streaming, fast-content creation, or roles that didn't exist a decade ago.

The bigger meta shift right now: the barrier to publishing, distributing, and finding an audience for almost anything has collapsed. A niche product that would never have found enough buyers through traditional channels can now find exactly the right ten thousand people. Being a dungeon master is apparently a profession. Making a solo indie game is viable. Whatever your thing is, the tools to reach the people who care about it are available and cheap.

The second major shift: automation is steadily removing the need for human labor in enormous categories of work. Entertainment — in the broadest sense — is one of the few areas where human demand keeps growing. That's worth factoring into long-term decisions.

Exploit the meta, but stay alert: every meta gets patched. An investment strategy, a platform, a format — whatever is "overpowered" right now has a finite window. Build skills, not just positions.

You Need Your Own Gear First

Rules 8 & 8a: If you want to carry other people, you need to be geared up yourself.

There's a sincere impulse in a lot of people to fix others before fixing themselves. It feels more generous. It isn't. If you're running at 50% yourself, you can carry someone to 60%. That's not carrying — that's barely helping, and it costs you. The oxygen mask instruction exists for a reason.

Your first responsibility to the world is to become capable, stable, and functional yourself. Not because the world doesn't need help — it obviously does — but because helping from a position of deficit usually produces more damage than good. Get your own situation in order. Then reach back.

The addictive mind loves to use the world's complexity as a reason to delay this. The more variables there are, the more things you can claim you need to figure out before you act. Complexity becomes a hiding place.

"The more complex a problem is, the more the addictive mind can hide in that complexity."

More questions, more paralysis, more time passes. If you notice yourself endlessly adding variables to a problem that has a pretty clear simple action at its core — that's the mechanism at work.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking spawn-point resentment for analysis. Acknowledging that your starting conditions were harder than others is accurate. Treating that as the primary explanation for where you are now is a trap. Both things can be true: the world is unfair and you're the one who has to do something about it.

  • Believing you're too late. Almost always false in the mechanics, almost always vivid in the feeling. The feeling is not evidence.

  • Helping others as a substitute for working on yourself. It feels selfless. It delays the actual work and often produces dependency, not growth.

  • Chasing someone else's build. Especially dangerous when the person selling it seems confident and successful. Their walkthrough fits their character with their stats at their moment in the game. It's a bad guide for yours.

  • Using complexity as a permission structure for inaction. "But what about X" is sometimes genuine inquiry. It's also the most common stall tactic the mind deploys against obvious-but-difficult action.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your spawn-point narrative. Write down the circumstances you most often cite as reasons you're stuck. Then ask honestly: which of these are genuine constraints right now, and which are just the cards you were dealt? Separate the current obstacles from the background conditions.

  2. Name the final boss. Sit with the question: what is actually holding me back? List the real, internal things — not the external ones. Notice how many of the items on the list start with "I don't want to" or "It's hard for me" rather than a genuine external blockage.

  3. Check your mode. For one week, notice every significant interaction and ask: was that PvP or co-op? No judgment — just data. At the end of the week, decide if that's the mode you actually want to be playing in.

  4. Identify one piece of content you've told yourself it's "too late" for. Calculate the actual time it would take to start. Compare that number to your age or circumstances. Is it actually too late, or does it just feel that way?

  5. Look at your raid guild. List five people you spend the most time with. Where are they headed? Are you pulling each other forward or sideways?

  6. Cut one complexity loop. Find one decision or problem you've been "still thinking through" for more than a month. Ask what the single most obvious next action is — not the complete solution, just the next step. Do that step this week.

  7. Pick a build and stay in it for 90 days. Choose one skill, project, or direction and commit to not switching to a different "op strategy" for the next three months. Notice how differently things develop when you stop chasing what's working for someone else.
