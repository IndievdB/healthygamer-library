Summary

TL;DR — When you can't believe a compliment or reassurance no matter how much you want to, it's not a character flaw. Your brain learned, from real experience, that other people's "everything is fine" can't be trusted.

This is a coaching session with Tom, who finds that compliments, reassurance, and validation from people he loves — his parents, friends, partners — simply bounce off him. He knows intellectually that people mean well, but something blocks the message from landing. What unfolds is a close look at where that block comes from, what he does instead to cope, and why that coping strategy quietly makes things worse. The session also draws a sharp and practically useful line between two things most people treat as synonyms: reassurance and validation.

Why Reassurance Bounces Off

Tom's childhood home was chaotic — his father and sister fighting regularly, creating a genuinely unsafe atmosphere. His mother's response was to come to Tom and tell him everything was fine. The problem: it wasn't fine. He could hear the yelling while being told there was nothing to worry about.

The lesson his nervous system filed away: when someone says "everything is okay," that is a signal that something is probably wrong.

This is a rational adaptation to an irrational situation. A child cannot confront the reality of a toxic household head-on — he needs a way to stay sane. The cost is that the coping strategy generalizes. By adulthood, any time someone says the verbal equivalent of "you're doing great, don't worry," Tom's brain pattern-matches it to his mother's false reassurances and quietly rejects it.

"This is how you learned how to reject validation."

If you find yourself asking friends or partners for reassurance and then, the moment they give it, feeling like it didn't help or wasn't real — look for the moment your mind learned that other people's "it's okay" couldn't be trusted. That moment is almost always there.

The Coping Strategy That Backfires

When external reassurance fails, Tom turns inward. He tries to reason his way to feeling okay. The most common conclusion he reaches: work harder, do more research, find more goals.

At first glance this looks constructive. But notice what the logic actually encodes: you only need to work harder if your current effort isn't good enough. So the very act of reaching for the coping mechanism reinforces the original fear.

"The way that you reassure yourself is actually through reinforcing that what you're doing isn't good enough."

The result is a loop: 1. Doubt arises. 2. External reassurance bounces off. 3. Internal conclusion: I must work harder. 4. Working harder implies current effort is insufficient. 5. More doubt.

Tom describes this clearly — the endless grinding produces anxiety, not pride. He ends up doing a lot of things he doesn't dislike, but none of the things that actually matter to him, because staying busy quiets the anxious thought just enough to keep going.

Validation vs. Reassurance — They Are Not the Same Thing

This is the hinge the whole session turns on, and it's worth being precise.

Reassurance tells you that things will be okay. It skips past your current experience and lands in a future where the problem isn't real anymore. Classic form: "No no, you're doing great, everyone thinks you're wonderful, don't worry about it."

Validation acknowledges that your current experience is real and makes sense. It doesn't promise a better future — it sits with you in the present one.

The counterintuitive thing: reassurance, despite sounding supportive, is often actually invalidating. If you say "I'm really struggling," and someone responds "no you're not, everything's fine," they are implicitly telling you that your reading of reality is wrong. That makes you feel more alone, not less.

Validation does the opposite. Even when what's being validated is fear or pain, the person receiving it feels less alone — and that, more than any promise that things will work out, is what actually helps.

"What validation really does — a lot of people think that if I acknowledge someone's negative emotions I'm going to reinforce them, make it worse. But actually when someone is feeling afraid, part of what they're feeling is alone. When you reassure them you make them feel more alone. Whereas when you validate them… what validation really helps people do is acknowledge that negativity, face that negativity, not feel alone in it — and then that actually helps them feel better."

Tom's mother was doing reassurance. When Tom later sat down with her as an adult and said, "the house was toxic and you kept telling me it was fine," and she responded, "you're right, I'm sorry, that was the best I could do at the time" — that was validation. She acknowledged his reality instead of overwriting it. And it was the first time that kind of conversation actually landed.

Where Your Doubt Comes From

There's a second layer worth naming. When one person in a household insists everything is okay while reality says otherwise, the burden of holding reality gets distributed. Someone has to carry the knowledge that things are not fine. In Tom's case, that someone was him.

His doubt wasn't random or pathological — it was the accurate counterweight to his mother's coping mechanism of relentless reassurance. The more she insisted everything was fine, the more his nervous system had to compensate by generating doubt, because the alternative was losing touch with reality entirely.

This means the doubt itself has an origin, and that origin can be found. You don't have to accept "I'm just an anxious person who can't trust people" as a permanent identity. The question to sit with is: when and where did my mind learn to distrust what people tell me?

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking reassurance for support. When someone you care about is struggling, the instinct is to fix their feeling by telling them it'll be okay. This usually backfires. The person ends up feeling like their reality isn't being taken seriously.

Using productivity as a proxy for self-worth. Working harder, researching more, adding more goals — these feel like answers but they encode the problem. If you only reach for them when you doubt yourself, every instance of "working harder" is a small vote for "I'm not enough yet."

Believing you need a checklist to heal. Tom came in wanting practical tips. What he discovered over the course of the conversation was that he had already done the most important things — therapy, a hard conversation with his mother, honest self-reflection — and that his urge for a concrete fix was itself an old habit dressed up as problem-solving. The desire for a five-step plan can be another way of saying I don't trust that what I'm already doing is enough.

Confusing validation with agreement. Validating someone doesn't mean telling them they're right about everything. It means acknowledging that their experience is real and makes sense given what they've been through.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Trace the origin of your distrust. Think back: when did you first learn that someone's reassurance couldn't be trusted? What was the situation? Who was saying "it's fine" when it wasn't? Getting specific about this moment helps you see the rejection of validation as a learned response, not a permanent trait.

  2. Have the conversation you've been avoiding. If there's someone from your past whose false reassurances shaped you, consider talking to them — not to assign blame, but to have your reality acknowledged. What you're looking for from them is validation (they admit what was happening), not reassurance (they insist it wasn't that bad). Tom's conversation with his mother was pivotal precisely because she didn't minimize what he said.

  3. Practice validating other people. When someone around you is struggling, resist the urge to say "it'll be okay." Instead, acknowledge what they're going through: "That sounds really hard," or "It makes sense that you'd feel that way given everything." Practicing this outward trains you to recognize what real validation feels like — which makes it easier to receive.

  4. Notice when you reach for "work harder." The next time you feel doubt and your first instinct is to add more tasks, more research, more goals — pause. Ask yourself: am I doing this because it will genuinely help, or because staying busy quiets the doubt for a little while? You don't have to stop working. Just be honest about what the work is actually for.

  5. Watch for the reassurance-invalidation pattern in real time. The next time someone reassures you and it doesn't land, instead of concluding "I can't be helped," try noticing: they gave me reassurance, not validation. That distinction is information, not a verdict on you.
