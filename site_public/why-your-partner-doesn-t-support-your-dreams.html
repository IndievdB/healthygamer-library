Summary

TL;DR — The people around you reveal how they think by how they judge you, and your dreams stay powerful when you protect them.

This talk covers three interlocking ideas: what it actually means to be judged by a partner based on conditioning rather than who you are, why flexibility is the single best indicator of a healthy long-term relationship, and why delusional dreams are worth chasing — and worth keeping quiet.

Judged by Conditioning vs. Judged by You

When someone looks at one behavior — playing video games, owning a certain phone, driving a certain car — and uses it to draw sweeping conclusions about your character, they aren't actually seeing you. They're running you through a filter built from cultural conditioning.

That's a meaningful distinction. A partner who decides you're a loser because you take a hobby seriously isn't responding to you as a full, complex person. They're responding to an association they absorbed from somewhere else. The same logic applies to anyone who would reject a partner for owning an Android, or pursue one specifically because they're a doctor. The object or title becomes a proxy for a whole cluster of assumptions, none of which are necessarily true.

This doesn't automatically mean you should break up with someone who starts there. The more important question is whether they're flexible. If you can sit down with them, explain your actual situation — "I handle my responsibilities, I play ten hours a week, that's less screen time than most people spend on short-form video" — and they genuinely reconsider, that's a very different person from someone who digs in and stays rigid.

"The more they do that, the harder it is for you to have a healthy relationship — because they're not judging you based on you, they're judging attributes of you as proxies to other things."

Flexibility Is the Relationship Indicator Nobody Talks About

The capacity to change your mind is probably the strongest positive indicator of long-term relationship health. Here's why: if someone demonstrates they cannot adapt, then what you see at the beginning is exactly what you're committing to forever. But if someone is willing to update their views when presented with new experience and evidence, they don't need to be perfect right now. They'll grow with you.

This runs against a lot of what social media promotes. The loudest voices online tend to frame any compromise as weakness or self-betrayal — "don't change for anyone," "if they can't handle you at your worst," and so on. That framing sounds empowering but it quietly destroys the conditions that make long relationships work. Relationships require two people who can adjust to circumstances, to each other, and to the reality that people are not static.

None of this means abandoning your values or accepting mistreatment. It means asking: does this person have the capacity to see me differently as they learn more about me? Because rigidity in one area tends to show up in others.

What a Supportive Partner Actually Looks Like

There's a version of "my partner doesn't support my dreams" that conflates two very different things: a partner who doesn't support you, and a partner who doesn't support a specific behavior indefinitely.

Real support means giving someone a fair shot. It means encouraging them, believing in them, and letting them go for it — genuinely. But support doesn't mean unlimited, unconditional endorsement of every pursuit forever. At some point, a truly caring partner might say: "You've given this eight years and there's no income, no forward movement — is this still serving you?"

The key distinction is whose feelings are driving that conversation. If you're tired and frustrated and just want them to stop, that's not a compass worth following. The right question is whether their life would actually be better if they redirected that energy. A supportive partner thinks about the other person's wellbeing, not their own inconvenience.

"A supportive partner thinks about you, not themselves, not their own frustration."

Delusional Dreams Are Worth Chasing

A delusional dream isn't one you should abandon — it's one where your current capabilities haven't caught up to the goal yet. The response to that gap isn't to give up the dream. It's to close the gap.

Almost every meaningful human advancement started as a delusional dream. Someone decided to attempt the thing that everyone around them considered impossible. They may not have pulled it off, but they moved the line, and someone else moved it further. The entire history of transplant medicine is basically a sequence of people attempting things that sounded insane at the time.

And even when the dream itself doesn't pan out, the pursuit rarely goes to waste. The skills, the grit, the taste you develop, the community you find — these compound into something. The journey is undervalued, not because it's more important than the destination, but because we tend to write it off entirely when the destination isn't reached.

"I think often times we underestimate the value from pursuing a delusional dream. It's not about accomplishing the dream, necessarily."

Keep Your Dream Quiet Until You Have To Share It

If your dream doesn't require you to tell people about it in order to pursue it, keep it to yourself. There are a few converging reasons for this.

Psychologically, Freud noticed that words are a substitute for action. When patients verbalized a violent or obsessive thought, the urgency behind it tended to decrease. Therapy deliberately uses this — talking something out helps release the charge behind it. The same mechanism works against you when you talk about your ambitions before you act on them.

There's also neurological evidence that announcing a goal activates the brain's reward circuitry in a small but real way — meaning you get a taste of the feeling of accomplishment without doing the work. What you end up reinforcing is the talking, not the doing.

From a more contemplative angle, there's also something to the idea that once something is externalized, it no longer fully lives inside you. Mantras in meditation traditions are kept secret precisely because the internal, unspoken nature of them is what gives them their force. When something is shared widely, something of its energy disperses.

So: be hungry for it, close the capability gap, but don't broadcast it prematurely.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "they don't support my dreams" as binary. A partner can love and support you as a person while having real concerns about a specific behavior. Conflating the two leads to unnecessary conflict or unnecessary tolerance.

  • Using flexibility as a weapon. "They should be willing to change" can become a justification for demanding your partner abandon reasonable positions. Flexibility is about capacity to grow, not about winning arguments.

  • Mistaking rigidity now for rigidity forever. Some people need time and evidence to update their views. Give them the chance before concluding they're incapable of change.

  • Confusing talking about your dream with working toward it. The reward hit from announcing a goal can masquerade as momentum. Track behavior, not conversations.

  • Giving up a dream because it currently sounds impossible. The delusional part is a temporary description of your current capability level, not a verdict on whether the goal is worth pursuing.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit how your partner judges you. Think of a few judgments they've made about you — good or bad. Ask yourself: are those based on their direct experience with you, or on cultural assumptions about what a behavior or attribute means? This isn't about scoring points; it's about clarity.

  2. Test for flexibility. If a partner, friend, or family member has written off something important to you, have a calm, specific conversation where you explain your actual situation. Watch what happens. Do they engage with what you said, or do they retreat to their original position? That response tells you a lot.

  3. If you're the skeptical partner, ask a different question. Instead of "am I frustrated by this?" ask "is this pursuit actually harming them, or just annoying me?" Let their trajectory, not your feelings, guide what you say.

  4. Write down your delusional dream — then close the notebook. Articulate it clearly to yourself. Then stop announcing it. Let the energy stay internal and compound.

  5. Identify the capability gap. What specifically makes your dream currently "delusional"? Skill, experience, resources, connections? Name it. That gap is your actual next project — not the dream itself, but the concrete thing that would make it less impossible.

  6. Look at failed pursuits differently. Think of something you tried that didn't work out. Make a list of skills, habits, or relationships that came out of that attempt. This isn't about rationalizing failure — it's about accurately accounting for what the journey gave you.
