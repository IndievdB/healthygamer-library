Summary

TL;DR — You can't change who you are at the core, and trying to will backfire. What you can change is your actions — and that shift begins with acceptance, not self-improvement.

When people start genuinely examining themselves, they often discover uncomfortable things: patterns of dishonesty, ways they've hurt people, gaps between the person they thought they were and the person they've actually been. This discovery brings disgust, and that disgust creates a powerful urge to change who you are. The problem is that this framing — "I need to become a different, better person" — is itself part of the trap. Understanding why requires rethinking what kind of thing a person even is.

You Are Not the Sum of Your Actions

A common assumption is that good actions make you a good person, and bad actions make you a bad person. But this breaks down quickly.

Consider a surgeon who saves 99 lives and then kills someone in a drunk-driving accident. Are they a good person or a bad person? Most instincts want to balance the ledger — but life doesn't work like that. A useful analogy: if one person shoots an arrow ten feet left of a target and another shoots ten feet right, two mathematicians don't get to celebrate a hit. Averaging doesn't work in archery, and it doesn't work in ethics either.

Each action carries its own consequence. They don't cancel each other out.

The framework that holds up better is this: you, as a person, are neither good nor bad. You simply are. All human beings carry intrinsic value that isn't contingent on what they've done. What can be good or bad are your actions — and these are separate from your identity.

This isn't just philosophical nicety. It has real practical stakes. Once you decide you are a bad person, that identity starts shaping everything: you don't apply for the promotion because bad people don't deserve it, you don't ask someone out because bad people don't deserve connection. The identity becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. Labeling yourself is a trap, and the label doesn't even reflect anything real about your core.

The Paradox of Trying to Change Who You Are

Here's where things get genuinely strange: the desire to get over self-disgust is itself powered by self-disgust.

Think about it carefully. Why do you want to change? Because you're disgusted with yourself. So what's fueling the effort to improve? Disgust. The more intensely you pursue "becoming a better person," the more you're feeding the exact thing driving you. The engine of the change project is the problem itself.

"The more you want to change, the more you don't accept yourself, the more disgusted you are with yourself."

This is why people get stuck. They try harder and harder to overcome self-disgust, but every effort reinforces the underlying sense that they are broken and need fixing. The goal of I am bad → I need to become good is not a path forward. It's a loop.

The flip side is equally important: accepting yourself is not the same as giving up. This is the most common misreading of the idea. Acceptance doesn't mean throwing your hands up and saying "I guess this is just who I am." It means clearly seeing where you are — without the story that you're irredeemably broken — so you can actually take the next step.

The clearest example of this working in practice is Alcoholics Anonymous. The first step is standing up and saying, "My name is ___, and I'm an alcoholic." You don't say "I was an alcoholic." You own it, present tense, permanently. That act of full acceptance — not trying to transcend the label but sitting inside it honestly — is paradoxically what creates the foundation for sobriety. Trying to overcome the identity without accepting it first is what fails.

What Acceptance Actually Looks Like

Acceptance in this context means something specific. It's not:

  • "I'm a bad person and always will be" (that's ahamkar — ego crystallizing past actions into a fixed future identity)
  • "It wasn't that big a deal" (minimizing)
  • "I've done enough good things to cancel it out" (the averaging fallacy)

It looks more like:

"I've made a lot of mistakes. I used to think of myself as a good person. I've realized I've done a lot of bad things. I'm not really sure if I'm a good or bad person — and that's okay. Today I can do better."

The focus shifts from what I am to what I do next. You stop trying to resolve an unanswerable cosmic question about your soul and start asking a very answerable practical question: what action can I take today?

Even if — in your worst self-assessment — you conclude you're a bad person, that changes nothing about whether you can do a good thing in the next conversation you have. A "bad person" can still treat someone with respect. A "good person" can still betray someone. The label is beside the point. The action is the point.

The "Time Chamber" Fantasy

A recurring impulse when self-disgust peaks is to retreat: move to a new city, cut off contact, enter a kind of chrysalis and emerge transformed. The appeal is real and understandable — there's a desire to be somewhere clean, somewhere untouched by who you've been, so you can become who you want to be.

The problem is that your mind comes with you.

"You're going to take your problems with you wherever you go. They're your problems."

The time-chamber fantasy carries a hidden error: it treats self-transformation as something that happens away from your actual life, in special conditions, and then gets imported back in. But the conditions of your actual life — the relationships, the friction, the repeated small choices — are precisely where change gets made or doesn't.

This doesn't mean a change of environment is always wrong. If your toxicity is genuinely coming from an external environment, removing yourself from it makes sense. A retreat or a period of focused self-work can be genuinely valuable. But don't confuse changing your location with changing yourself. The self-work happens in the daily actions you take, not in the geography you inhabit.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking self-improvement effort for self-acceptance. Aggressively trying to become better can feel like progress while actually deepening self-rejection. The underlying message of "I must change" is "I am unacceptable as I am."

Accepting the identity, not the actions. There's a difference between accepting that you've done harmful things and accepting that you are a bad person. The first is honest and actionable. The second just cements a damaging self-concept that limits what you believe is possible for you.

Using good deeds to cancel bad ones. This is the averaging fallacy. Each action has its own consequence. Doing something good after something harmful doesn't erase the harm — it just means you've also done something good.

Treating the time chamber as a real solution. Isolation and geographic change can be part of a healthy reset, but treating them as the mechanism of transformation sets you up for disappointment. The disgusted part of your mind moves with you.

Confusing acceptance with passivity. Acceptance is not resignation. Failing a test and accepting that you failed — then studying harder — is acceptance. Failing a test and deciding you're just bad at tests forever is not acceptance; it's defeat dressed up as honesty.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Separate identity from action in your self-talk. When you catch yourself saying "I am a bad person" or "I am someone who does X," try reframing it as "I have done X." This is not denial — it's precision. It keeps the door open for different action next time.

  2. Name the disgust without feeding it. When self-disgust arises, instead of trying to overcome or suppress it, simply acknowledge it: "There is a part of me that is very upset with what I've done. That's real. It doesn't have to be the whole of me." This small act of naming creates distance without suppression.

  3. Ask the action question daily. Rather than asking "Am I a good person?", ask "What is one thing I can do today that points in the right direction?" The cosmic question has no answer. The action question always does.

  4. Write out what you're accepting. Specifically. Not "I'm a bad person" — but "I did not tell people how I really felt. I treated X with disrespect in Y situations." Concrete past actions, not a global verdict on your character. Then write: "Today I can choose differently when ___."

  5. Notice the energy behind your self-improvement goals. When you feel a strong urge to change or fix yourself, pause and ask: where is this urge coming from? Is it disgust? Fear? Genuine curiosity? This isn't to stop you from improving — it's to make sure you're not inadvertently feeding the problem you're trying to solve.

  6. If you're considering a major retreat or move, ask the honest question first. What specifically do you expect to be different in the new place? If the answer involves your own mind — your patterns of thought, your emotional reactions, your relationship to yourself — recognize those will make the trip with you, and begin working on them now, in place.
