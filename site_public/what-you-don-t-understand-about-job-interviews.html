Summary

TL;DR — The reason interviews and speeches go badly is that your mind is on yourself. Shift your focus to what you can offer the other person, and almost everything else fixes itself.

Most people walk into a job interview or a presentation treating it as a performance review — a moment where strangers decide whether they're good enough. That frame puts your brain in a self-monitoring loop that actively degrades your performance. There's a simpler and more effective way to think about it, and it has nothing to do with rehearsing more or appearing more confident.

Your Mind Is in the Wrong Place

When you're anxious about a speech or interview, notice what you're actually thinking about:

  • What if I look like an idiot?
  • Did that answer come out okay?
  • Am I saying "um" too much?
  • Are they going to like me?

Every one of those thoughts is about you. And while your mind is busy monitoring yourself, it's not doing the one thing that would actually make the speech or interview go well — connecting with the person in front of you.

This self-monitoring loop has a compounding problem. The moment you notice yourself saying "um," you panic. The panic makes you say "um" again. Now you're in a vicious cycle, your flow collapses, and you walk away concluding I'm bad at giving speeches. That label — "I'm bad at this" — is where things really go wrong, because you've turned a situational failure into a fixed identity. It's no longer my mind wandered or I got distracted. It's I am someone who cannot do this. That identity then shapes every future attempt before it even begins.

The root cause of all of this is what's called the default mode network — the brain's self-referential circuitry. When it's firing, you're thinking about yourself: your image, your performance, how you're being perceived. And when you're locked inside your own head, you become empathically disconnected from your audience. That disconnection is what makes speeches and interviews feel hollow or stiff, even when the content is solid.

The Reframe: You're There to Give, Not to Get

Here's the shift that changes everything: you were invited.

Nobody gives a speech without being asked. Nobody gets an interview without being called in. That invitation means the people in the room are already there to receive what you have to offer — not to sit in judgment of you. A professor who assigned you a presentation wants to hear what you found. An interview panel called you in because they have a real job that needs doing and they're hoping you might be the right person to do it.

Once you internalize that, the whole frame flips:

"You are there to enrich and explain and share information about something that you know about that other people don't know about."

Even as a medical student presenting to a room full of experienced physicians, there's something in your specific preparation that they may not know. You can offer them something. Hold onto that.

For an interview specifically: the company has a real problem. They need someone. Go in thinking I might be exactly what this company needs — not out of arrogance, but as a genuine orientation toward service. Your job in the room is not to convince them to hire you. It's to show them honestly what you're capable of, and let them make the call.

"Just be authentic — here's who I am, this is what I'm good at, this is what I can do for you. Now you tell me whether that's something you want."

This isn't just motivational advice. It's structurally sound. When your goal becomes offer what I have rather than secure their approval, you stop needing to manage their perception of you in real time. That frees up the mental bandwidth that was going toward self-monitoring and redirects it toward actually communicating.

What Detachment Actually Means (and How to Get There)

This approach is sometimes described as detachment, and that word trips people up. Detachment sounds like not caring — like being cold or indifferent. But genuine detachment isn't about caring less. It's about caring about the right thing.

The mechanism is simple: redirect your attention outward. Instead of asking how am I coming across?, ask what does this person need to understand? What can I actually give them in the time I have?

Those outward-facing questions naturally quiet the self-referential noise. You're not forcing yourself to stop being anxious — you're just genuinely occupied with something else. The anxiety loses oxygen because you're not feeding it.

"The more you step away from your ego, the less you engage the default mode network — and the more natural you're going to feel."

For a speech, the organizing questions become: Who is this audience? What do they need to understand? What can I realistically accomplish in the time I have? These questions actually structure your content for you. They replace the anxious, unfocused rehearsal loop with purposeful preparation.

For an interview, the equivalent is: What does this company actually need? What do I genuinely have to offer them? Where might I be a real asset, and where might I not be?

Social Situations Are a Bit Different

This service-oriented mindset applies well to formal settings like interviews and presentations, but social situations call for a slight adjustment. In a social context, you're not there purely to serve — you're also there to have a good time yourself. Tilting too far toward "I'm here for everyone else" will drain you and make the interaction feel one-sided.

The principle that does carry over is the part about not getting trapped in self-monitoring. At a party, standing in the corner scrolling your phone because you're afraid to look bad at a game is the same mental trap — just in casual clothing. The move is the same: lower the stakes in your own head, admit what you don't know, and participate anyway.

"Hey, I've never played this before — does it matter if I suck?"

Nobody is going to say yes. What actually happens is you get coached, you participate, you potentially have a good time, and the people who were too afraid to join see someone modeling exactly what they wish they could do.

Common Pitfalls

Treating "service orientation" as a performance. If you walk in trying to act like someone who's there to help rather than genuinely shifting your focus, the self-consciousness doesn't go away — it just wears a different mask. This has to be a real reorientation, not a trick.

Confusing authenticity with underselling. Offering what you honestly have isn't the same as downplaying yourself. Know your strengths, communicate them clearly. The point is to stop overselling or performing — not to seem smaller than you are.

Applying the interview mindset to social contexts without adjustment. Pure service-orientation in casual social settings can leave you feeling invisible and resentful. In those contexts, your own enjoyment matters equally.

Labeling yourself after one bad experience. A speech that went poorly means something went wrong in the moment — your attention drifted, the prep was off, the nerves got loud. It does not mean you are someone who cannot do this. The identity label is where the lasting damage happens, not the stumble itself.

Over-rehearsing the wrong things. Memorizing every line trains you to monitor whether you're hitting the script, which pulls attention back toward yourself. Preparation should be about understanding your material and your audience, not achieving word-for-word recall.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Before your next interview or presentation, write down one sentence that answers: "What can I specifically offer these people today?" Keep that sentence somewhere you'll see it in the minutes before you walk in.

  2. Audit your pre-performance thoughts. In the 30 minutes before a high-stakes conversation, notice whether your mental chatter is about you (how you'll come across, what they'll think) or about them (what they need, what you can give). Redirect the self-focused thoughts by returning to your answer from step 1.

  3. For interviews: review your experience honestly from the company's perspective. Don't ask "how do I look on paper?" Ask "what real problems could I solve for them, and where are my honest limits?" Walk in prepared to articulate both.

  4. Practice the outward-focused question set for speeches: Who is my audience? What do they not yet know that I can teach them? What's the one thing I want them to walk away with? Let those questions organize your preparation instead of scripting every sentence.

  5. The next time you're at a social event and feel hesitant to join something, say the thing out loud: "I've never done this / I'm not great at this — can I join anyway?" Notice what actually happens versus what you imagined would happen.

  6. After any speech or interview that didn't go well, write down what specifically happened — not a verdict on your character. "I lost track halfway through," not "I'm bad at this." Keeping the diagnosis situational keeps the door open for improvement.
