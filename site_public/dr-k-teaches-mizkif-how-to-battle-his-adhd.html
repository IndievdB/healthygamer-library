Summary

TL;DR — The biggest obstacle to presence for people with ADHD isn't the scattered mind itself; it's the war you wage against it. This is a deep conversation about why fighting your own attention makes things worse, and what to actually do instead.

If you have ADHD — or just struggle to be present — you've probably noticed that the harder you try to enjoy a moment, the less you enjoy it. The harder you try to relax, the more wired you feel. There's a reason for that, and understanding it changes everything about how you approach your own mind.

Your Mind Has Too Much RAM, Not Too Little

The instinct most people have about ADHD is that the mind is weak or broken — that it can't hold onto things. That's backwards. The problem is closer to the opposite: the ADHD mind is running too many processes at once, not too few.

Think of it like a computer with more RAM than the operating system knows what to do with. It doesn't sit idle — it spawns background processes. Random songs, half-formed memories, the furniture delivery in 30 minutes, a Cartoon Network show you haven't thought about in 15 years — these aren't signs of a failing mind. They're signs of one that won't stop working.

This reframing matters because the solution changes completely depending on the diagnosis. If the problem is weakness, you push harder. If the problem is excess energy with nowhere to go, you give it somewhere to go.

The Alambana: Giving Excess Energy an Exit

In Sanskrit, alambana means "support" — something that anchors the mind during meditation. What this looks like in practice is a fidget toy, a set of prayer beads, a deck of cards to shuffle, or even a railing you run your hand along.

This isn't a cute trick. It's the actual mechanism: when part of your mental energy is occupied by something simple and physical, it stops fueling the internal conflict. Two parts of your mind are no longer fighting each other. One of them went off to shuffle cards.

"Instead of two parts warring with each other, one of the armies goes off and spins a fidget. The tension in the mind reduces, you remove the excess energy, and your mind is left in a calmer state."

You may have already discovered your own version of this. Speedrunning a video game, sorting something with your hands, fidget spinners — if any of these have ever helped you focus, you've independently stumbled onto what contemplative traditions figured out centuries ago.

The practical recommendation: pick one alambana and use it consistently. You can level up with a single tool. The more you train with it, the more effective it becomes at pulling your mind out of its own crossfire.

The Bathtub Problem: Why Fighting Waves Creates More Waves

Imagine telling a kid to hold their hands under the bathwater and push the waves down so the surface goes still. What actually happens when they do that?

More waves.

That's exactly what's happening when you sit at dinner thinking, why can't I just enjoy this? The thought "I should be present" is itself occupying a layer of your mind — and it's the loudest, most intrusive one, right at the surface. It drowns out everything else. You're not just distracted; you're also beating yourself up for being distracted, which is its own separate distraction.

The layers of an ADHD mind at any given moment might look like: - Layer 4: a random song - Layer 3: a memory of a cartoon - Layer 2: what you're supposed to be doing - Layer 1: why can't I be present right now?

Layer 1 is the one that does the most damage. And it's entirely self-generated.

The Observer: Who Is Watching All This?

Here's something worth sitting with. If you can see the layers of your own mind — if you're aware that there's a song playing, a stray thought, a surface concern, all at once — then there's something that's doing the watching. Something that isn't the layers.

That's you. Not your thoughts. The observer of your thoughts.

Most people are only aware of their surface-level mind. The subconscious churns away invisibly. But people with highly active minds often develop an unusual ability: they can actually perceive multiple layers of their own thinking at the same time. That's not a deficit. That's a form of meta-awareness most people don't have access to.

The instruction that follows from this is strange but important: instead of trying to be present with the sushi, try being present with the person who can't be present with the sushi.

You're already in the movie theater, watching a movie of your own life. You can't taste the sushi on screen. But you can fully inhabit the seat you're sitting in. You can be present with the observer.

Acceptance Isn't Giving Up — It's the Only Door In

The hardest part of this is that it asks you to do something that feels like defeat. To say: I can't enjoy this right now, and that's just what's true.

But consider what actually happens when you refuse to accept it. You add energy to an already overfull system. You create tension. You make the conflict worse. You end up not enjoying the sushi and hating yourself for not enjoying it.

"The harder you try to enjoy the sushi, the less you enjoy it. So if the harder you try to fight it, the less enjoyment happens — embrace it. Sit with yourself being unable to enjoy it."

Acceptance removes the tension. When you stop fighting the waves, they calm on their own. This is not a metaphor — it's a description of what actually happens to mental noise when you stop resisting it.

A calm mind can enjoy things. A warring mind cannot. The path to the former runs straight through the thing you've been avoiding: letting yourself be exactly where you are.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing acceptance with resignation. Accepting that you can't enjoy the sushi doesn't mean you give up on ever enjoying anything. It means you stop adding a punishing layer of self-criticism on top of an already busy mind. The goal is to remove tension, not ambition.

Turning relaxation into another task. The moment you say "okay, time to relax," your mind starts tracking whether you're doing it right. If I don't fall asleep in the next 20 minutes I'll only get four hours. Relaxation as a performance is just more mental conflict with a different label.

Expecting the layers to disappear. The scattered, multi-layered mind is not a temporary problem you solve and move past. It's the baseline. The goal isn't to eliminate the layers — it's to stop the top layer from being a war with all the other layers.

Switching alambanas constantly. Random fidget objects help, but training one tool consistently is more powerful. The novelty of a new fidget wears off; the depth of a practiced one doesn't.

Setting "fully present" as the goal. Attaching to complete presence creates an expectation you'll fail to meet, which generates disappointment, which generates more tension. The real goal is more present than before. That's enough. That's actually achievable.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Find your alambana and keep it on you. A set of prayer beads, a deck of cards, a smooth stone, a bracelet — something small, physical, and simple to manipulate. Keep it within arm's reach whenever you eat, have a conversation, or watch something with other people. Use it with one hand while doing the main activity with the other.

  2. Practice the observer. Next time you notice you're not present — at a meal, with a friend, during something that should feel good — don't try to force presence. Instead, just notice the noticing. Say to yourself, internally: There's the guy who can't enjoy this right now. No judgment, no fixing. Just observe.

  3. Let Layer 1 go. When you catch yourself thinking why can't I just be present? — recognize that thought as the wave-pusher. Name it. Don't engage with it. Return to the alambana.

  4. Stop fighting the waves. When something pulls your attention sideways, instead of suppressing it, briefly acknowledge it: There's Camp Lazlo again. Then return. You're not indulging it; you're not warring with it. You're just seeing it and coming back.

  5. Use touch as a grounding tool. Physical sensation — holding a railing, feeling a texture, pressing your feet into the floor — naturally redirects excess mental energy into the body. When you feel particularly scattered, find something to touch and put your attention on the sensation.

  6. Keep the goal small. You are not trying to achieve perfect presence. You are trying to reduce the intensity of Layer 1. That's a tractable goal. Every time you catch yourself fighting your own mind and choose not to, that's a win.
