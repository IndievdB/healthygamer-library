This conversation pulls apart a specific, painful pattern: the person who learned early that being lovable means being useful. They become skilled, versatile, conflict-averse, endlessly accommodating — and somehow still feel fundamentally unwanted. The "Bringing Something to the Table" Trap

When you grow up in an environment where nothing you do is quite enough — perfect grades, every extracurricular, and your father still picks a fight about the toothpaste cap — you arrive at a logical but devastating conclusion: who I am is not the point; what I produce is.

This leads to an exhausting coping strategy. You collect skills. You make yourself useful. You do things that are undeniable — a magic trick, a violin piece, a perfectly edited video — so that people have a concrete reason to keep you around. It feels like confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition →-building, but it quietly reinforces the exact belief you're trying to escape.

"If you stay in your lane and you assure yourself that you bring value to the table and then you show it to them and they accept you — what are they accepting? They're accepting the magic tricks. They're not accepting you."

Every time someone admires the performance and you feel temporarily okay, the machine underneath logs it as more evidence: they liked the trick, not me. The cycle self-reinforces. More skills, more polish, more reasons to deserve company — and the core wound never gets touched.

Where the Belief Is Born

The pattern doesn't start in adulthood. It usually starts in a specific, formative stretch where a child discovers that even doing everything right doesn't produce safety.

For two years, daily conflict and daily crying. Then, somewhere around 13, the crying just stops. Not because anything got resolved — but because nothing was getting resolved, and something had to give. The emotional processing gets shut off. That shutdown is actually an adaptation. It worked, in the sense that it reduced immediate pain and let life continue. But unprocessed emotion doesn't disappear. The years of crying were actually the beginning of processing. What was missing was someone there to help sustain the positive self during those moments. Without that, each hard conversation became another deposit in the wrong account.

Paralytic Security

There's a pattern worth naming directly: the plan to fix yourself privately, fully, before re-engaging with the world.

Stay in my lane. Prove myself first. Once I have something to bring to the table, then I can reach out. Once I'm worthy again, I'll reconnect.

This feels responsible and respectful. It's framed as not wanting to burden or taint others. But underneath it's the same belief from childhood: I need to earn my place before I can occupy it.

"I'm going to face the rest of the world once I know I'm worth it. Once I know I'm ready."

The problem is that worthiness built entirely in private, through solo performance, doesn't actually resolve the wound. The wound is relational. It came from not feeling wanted by people. The only thing that actually addresses it is being imperfect in front of someone and having them stay anyway.

Healing here genuinely depends on other people. Not on performing for them — on showing them the half-formed, unfinished, uncertain version of yourself and letting them respond. That's terrifying precisely because it's the real test. And when someone passes that test — when they show up for you without a magic trick to justify it — the correct response is confusion. Why are they here? I didn't do anything for them. That confusion is the medicine. It needs to happen repeatedly before the old belief starts to lose its grip.

Karma, Closure, and Unfinished Business

On a more practical level, there's a distinction worth understanding between two kinds of healing work.

The first is internal: therapy, journaling, meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition →, emotional processing. This is necessary and valuable. It slowly digests the accumulated residue. But it has limits, because it happens entirely in the room.

The second is external: actually going back to the people and situations that are still open. Not to re-enter anything, not for business reasons — but because unresolved relational wounds don't close on their own. They stay karmically open, as it's put here. You carry them.

Forgiveness is not something you're owed. The other person gets to decide. But there's a version of closure that doesn't require their forgiveness — it requires you trying, being honest about what you miss and regret, receiving whatever anger or hurt they direct back at you, and then being able to put it down. Even a refusal to engage is closure, because at least you know.

"Even if they reject you, I think there would be peace around that. At least you tried."

One conversation, especially years later, is rarely the end of it. Closure revisits. The goal isn't a single definitive scene — it's gradually reducing how much open weight you're carrying.

Self-Judgment and the Permission to Suffer

There's a specific cruelty that happens when you caused harm to others and then feel bad about your situation: you revoke your own right to suffer.

I created this. So I'm not allowed to complain. Other people are allowed to feel pain. Not me.

This sounds like accountability. It's actually a form of punishment that prevents processing. You can believe you made mistakes and still be allowed to grieve what you lost. Those aren't in conflict. Removing your permission to feel bad doesn't pay the debt — it just adds another layer of suppression on top of everything else.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking performance for connection. Building skills to justify your presence feels productive, but it sidesteps the actual fear. People accepting your output is not the same as people accepting you, and on some level you know it.

  • Thinking "staying in your lane" is healing. Waiting until you're fixed before re-engaging is a way of staying safe that also keeps you stuck. The work that needs doing requires other people to be present for.

  • Interpreting neutrality as peace. Describing a feeling as "neutral" when it's actually bittersweet is worth catching. Bittersweet has hurt in it. Calling it neutral is a way of not having to sit with the hurt.

  • One-and-done closure. Grief and relational repair don't work in single conversations. Revisiting matters, especially after significant time has passed and both people are in different places.

  • Believing the wound is entirely yours to fix. Healing from early relational damage often requires relational repair. Doing all the work privately is meaningful, but it has a ceiling.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice when you're performing instead of being present. Before you bring a skill, a piece of content, or a reason to justify your presence — pause. Ask whether you're doing this because it's genuinely what you want to offer, or because you need something undeniable on the table before you feel safe.

  2. Practice showing the incomplete thing. Once this week, share something unfinished — an idea still forming, a feeling you haven't resolved, a question you don't have an answer to — with someone you trust. Let them respond without filling the silence immediately.

  3. Try the Vam / Om chanting practice. Sit upright. Chant Vam slowly, nine rounds, and focus specifically on where the vibration starts and where it ends in your body. Follow with five to seven rounds of Om (A–U–M, mouth fully open to fully closed). After the final round, sit in silence for 60 seconds without opening your eyes. The goal is one-pointed observation, not the absence of thoughts.

  4. Identify one piece of unfinished relational business. Not for the purpose of re-entering anything, but to assess honestly: is there a conversation that is karmically open? Have you expressed what you actually feel — not to get back in, but to give both people a real chance to put it down?

  5. Write down the core belief, then interrogate it. Finish this sentence: "People want me around because ___." If the answer involves something you do rather than something you are, that's worth sitting with. Where did that belief come from? How old were you when you first arrived at it?

  6. Give confusion room to exist. When someone shows up for you without an obvious reason — when they want to spend time with you and you haven't done anything for them recently — resist the urge to immediately figure out what they want. Sit in the confusion. Let it be data.
