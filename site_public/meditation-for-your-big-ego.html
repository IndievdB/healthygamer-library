It's about cultivating real inner experiences, and different techniques work on different things.

Most people picture meditation as sitting quietly, watching thoughts drift by. That framing undersells it dramatically. The two techniques here — one for ego, one for anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → — are meant to produce actual felt experiences: physical sensations, shifting energy, even spontaneous giggling. What Meditation Actually Is (And Isn't)

The popular Western picture of meditation — "observe your thoughts," "be present," "reduce cortisolA stress hormone your body releases in response to danger — not the immediate spike of adrenaline, but the sustained chemical state that keeps you alert for the 24–48 hours after a threat passes.Full definition →" — is true as far as it goes, but it misses what makes the practice worth doing.

The real reason to meditate is cultivating inner experience. When you use a proper technique, you start to feel things that are genuinely hard to describe: a sense of charging up, a wave rippling through your skull, a warmth between your eyebrows. These aren't placebo quirks. They're what meditation is actually designed to produce.

"That's why I meditate — I got into meditation not to attain inner peace. What I loved about it was that people were teaching me stuff that gave me cool experiences."

The neuroscience benefits are real. The cortisol reduction, the improvements in focus, creativity, and empathy — those matter. But they're downstream consequences. Leading with them is a bit like telling someone to play a video game because it improves hand-eye coordination. True, but not the reason anyone actually plays.

The Third Eye Meditation (For Ego)

This technique targets the area between the eyebrows — traditionally called the third eye point — and uses it as a concentrationThe deliberate act of focusing your entire attention on a single object — and holding it there.Full definition → anchor.

How to do it:

  1. Hold your middle finger roughly a centimeter away from the spot between your eyebrows — close but not touching.
  2. Notice whatever sensation arises at that spot. People report tingling, warmth, a sense of pressure, a kind of subtle pulse.
  3. Lower your hand, sit up straight, and place your full attention on that spot.
  4. If concentration is difficult, try imagining your attention as a beam of light moving toward that point — or even radiating out from it.
  5. Stay with that for as long as feels right, then slowly open your eyes.

Even sixty seconds of this, for a first-time meditator, tends to produce something noticeable. That's not an accident — the technique is designed to give you something real to work with quickly.

This technique is specifically associated with ego work. The exact mechanism isn't elaborated here, but the third eye point has long been connected in contemplative traditions to the sense of individual self — the "I" that observes, judges, and defends itself.

What Comes Up When You Meditate

When you sit down and actually try this, you may not just feel pleasant sensations. Sometimes anxiety surfaces. Sometimes there's an urge to giggle nervously. Both of these are worth understanding.

When anxiety appears:

One way to think about anxiety is as energy or movement that's been suppressed. It wants to go somewhere and can't, so it loops. When you meditate, that suppressed energy sometimes rises to the surface.

The important reframe here: ordinarily, you are fighting your anxiety — working to control it, manage it, push it down. In a good meditation session, that relationship can flip.

"We want to create a situation where your anxiety is struggling to stay alive, whereas most of the time your anxiety isn't fighting to keep itself alive — you're fighting the anxiety to keep yourself alive."

That reversal — your anxiety fighting to survive rather than you fighting against it — is a meaningful shift. When it happens, let it. Don't clamp down on what's coming up. That's how it moves through you.

When giggling appears:

Giggling during meditation isn't a sign you're doing it wrong. It's actually a sign something is opening up.

There's a distinction worth drawing here between different kinds of laughter. Humor that involves someone else losing — being mocked, failing, looking foolish — has an edge to it. Even a perfectly crafted, genuinely funny meme carries some of that. Giggling is different. It has no target. It's simple, light, and a little bit uncontrollable.

"Giggling is something people don't do enough. If we think about children who are innocent and pure and happy in the simplest way, they giggle very easily."

In contemplative traditions, the first stage of bliss is sometimes described as exactly this: spontaneous, directionless laughter. If it surfaces during your meditation, let it happen. It's not a distraction — it's the point.

Specificity Matters: Different Techniques for Different Things

One of the most practically useful ideas here is that not all meditation is the same. Western research tends to study "meditation" as a single intervention — like studying "exercise" without distinguishing between sprinting and stretching. The traditions that developed these practices over centuries made fine distinctions.

Different techniques are suited to different aims:

  • The third eye technique is specifically for ego
  • A separate technique (referenced but not fully detailed here) is specifically for anxiety
  • Other techniques address other things entirely

This matters for how you approach your practice. If you've tried meditating before and felt like nothing happened, it's worth asking whether the technique you used was actually matched to what you were trying to address. "Meditation didn't work for me" may really mean "that particular technique wasn't the right tool for that particular problem."

Common Pitfalls

Thinking calm is the goal. If you sit down expecting to feel peaceful and instead feel anxious, tingly, giggly, or strange, you might conclude you've failed. You probably haven't. Those sensations are often exactly what a technique is designed to surface.

Pushing down what comes up. When something uncomfortable arises during meditation — anxiety, restlessness, the urge to laugh — the instinct is to suppress it and return to stillness. In many cases, the right move is the opposite: let it move through you.

Treating all meditation as interchangeable. Picking a random guided meditation app and hoping it addresses your specific issue is a bit like taking a random supplement and hoping it solves a specific deficiency. The technique matters. Exploring multiple traditions and specific practices is worth the time.

Waiting for the long-term benefits before committing. The science-backed long-term benefits of meditation are real, but they're a slow payoff. If that's the only reason you're meditating, you're likely to quit before you see results. The immediate experiential payoff — the laser beam, the giggle, the strange sensation — is a much more reliable motivator.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Try the third eye technique for 60 seconds right now. Hold your middle finger roughly a centimeter from the spot between your eyebrows, notice the sensation, then lower your hand and concentrate on that spot. Don't overthink it — just notice what you feel.

  2. Let whatever comes up during meditation actually come out. If anxiety surfaces, observe the unusual sensation of it struggling rather than you struggling against it. If you feel like giggling, giggle. Suppressing these responses short-circuits what the technique is trying to do.

  3. Notice the difference between giggling and other kinds of laughter in your daily life. When did you last laugh at nothing in particular — not at a meme, not at someone's misfortune, just from simple lightness? That's worth paying attention to as a baseline for where you are emotionally.

  4. Treat technique selection as something worth researching. Different traditions (Tibetan, Vedic, Zen, etc.) developed different tools. The third eye technique here is one example of that specificity in action.

  5. Build a short daily practice around the experiential payoff, not the outcome goals. Instead of meditating to reduce anxiety over the long run, meditate because you want to feel the laser beam charge up again.
