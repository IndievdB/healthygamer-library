What "Processing" Actually Means

Most people use the word processing without a concrete definition. Here's a working framework with three distinct layers:

  1. Emotional impact — the raw feelings the event produces (shock, shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, relief, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →)
  2. Identity conflict — the way those feelings collide with your sense of who you are
  3. Behavioral integration — the adjustments you make going forward, healthy or not

These aren't just three parallel things happening at once. They run in sequence. You can't reliably fix your behavior until you've dealt with the identity layer, and you can't work on identity until you've acknowledged the emotions. Trying to skip straight to "okay, what do I do now?" is why well-intentioned advice like just do it almost never works.

The Identity Layer Is the Hidden Obstacle

Emotions are visible. Behavior changes are visible. The identity conflict in the middle is where people get quietly stuck.

When something bad happens, it doesn't just hurt — it threatens the story you've been telling yourself about who you are. Getting fired when you've always been a hard worker, high-performer, never-failed-anything person creates a direct contradiction:

"There are two kinds of people in this world — people who get promoted and people who get fired, and the people who get fired are people who are incompetent."

That's a societal script most people carry unconsciously. When you get fired, your brain runs that script and it lands like a verdict on your entire identity — even when the circumstances have nothing to do with your competence.

The same pattern shows up everywhere: - The good driver who gets in an accident starts to wonder if they're actually a bad driver - The smart kid with a bad GPA starts to wonder if they're secretly stupid - The dedicated employee who gets fired starts to wonder if they were ever actually good at their job

Until you consciously work through that identity conflict, it leaks into everything. It shows up in how confident you sound in a job interview, whether you apply at all, how you carry yourself.

How to Actually Work Through It

Step 1: Acknowledge the emotions first

This sounds obvious but most people skip it or do a thin version of it. Acknowledgment isn't just privately noting "I feel bad." It means actually sitting with what you're feeling and letting it have some air.

Practical tools: - Journaling — even writing out the situation, as the person in this example did, produces measurable relief. Getting it out of your head and onto a page creates distance and the beginning of objectivity. - Physical movement — walking, going to the gym, spending time outside - Therapy or coaching, depending on severity — a general practitioner can help you figure out which one is appropriate if you're unsure

Step 2: Resolve the identity conflict by reframing accurately

This is the work. The goal isn't to spin a story where you did nothing wrong — it's to arrive at an honest, grounded account of what actually happened, one that doesn't require you to be either a hero or a failure.

A concrete example: when asked about a low GPA in a medical school interview, the answer that worked wasn't defensive and wasn't self-flagellating. It was honest ownership:

"I didn't apply myself. I've worked my ass off since then. If you let me in, I can guarantee I won't slack off — I've done it once and I know the consequences."

For someone who got fired from an acquired company, an honest reframe might sound like: "I was at an employee-owned company that did well enough to get acquired. When the new company started cutting benefits and communicating dishonestly, I raised concerns because that's how I operate — and it's part of why the old company succeeded. They didn't want that kind of feedback. That's a valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition → mismatch, not a performance failure."

The reframe works because it's true. You're not hiding the firing — you're putting it in accurate context.

Step 3: Make careful behavioral adjustments

Once the emotional and identity work is done, behavioral change can actually stick. Before that, behavior adjustments are either too extreme (swinging the pendulum all the way) or too fragile (they collapse under pressure).

The most dangerous behavioral lesson this person could take away: "Never talk to managers about problems." That's the equivalent of never driving again after a car accident. The correct lesson is more nuanced:

  • How you deliver feedback matters as much as whether you deliver it. Constant complaints are noise — people tune them out the way they tune out repeated nagging. Synthesized, focused, constructive feedback is different.
  • Where you work determines whether honest feedback is safe to give. The goal is to find workplaces where it is, not to permanently stop giving it.
  • Taking interviews on company time is a genuine mistake worth correcting — not because it caused the firing in some cosmic sense, but because it muddies the waters of an otherwise clean values conflict.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing processing with just feeling bad for a while. Time passing isn't processing. You can be three years out from something and still have an unresolved identity conflict silently shaping your choices.

Learning the wrong behavioral lesson. After a painful event, the mind tends to swing to absolutes — never date again, never trust a manager, never drive. These feel like wisdom but they're overcorrections driven by unprocessed emotion. You can tell the difference once you've done the prerequisite work.

"Facts over feelings." This framing is popular but wrong. Facts always involve interpretation, and emotions carry real information — your brain's fast-processing system flagging something important. The right frame is facts plus feelings. Ignoring emotions doesn't make you more rational; it just means the emotions influence you without your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

Rushing to behavioral change. Everyone wants to skip to "how do I fix this." But you can't make sound behavioral judgments while you're still in the emotional and identity turbulence. The sequence isn't optional.

Judging before understanding. The mind defaults to quick judgments — this was bad, I was wrong, they were wrong. Judgment is easy; understanding is hard. But understanding has to come first. The question isn't "was this right or wrong" — it's "what's actually here, and what does it mean?"

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write it out, in full. Don't summarize the event — describe it. Include what happened, what you felt, what you thought about yourself in the aftermath. The act of articulating it begins the emotional processing, as the person in this example noticed just from writing their post.

  2. Name the identity conflict explicitly. Finish this sentence: "Before this happened, I believed I was the kind of person who ___. Now I'm not sure that's true." Write down both sides. Don't resolve it yet — just see it clearly.

  3. Write the honest, grounded version of what actually happened — not the defensive version, not the self-blaming version. Aim for the account you'd give to someone you respect who asked you to be completely straight with them. This is the raw material for your reframe.

  4. Before making any major behavioral decisions, ask: "Am I changing this because I've genuinely learned something, or because I'm in pain and want to prevent pain?" The second is a reflex, not a lesson.

  5. Audit the behavioral lesson you're tempted to take. Write it down. Then ask whether it's a targeted adjustment (deliver feedback more selectively) or a total shutdown (never give feedback again). Total shutdowns are almost always signs that processing isn't done yet.

  6. If the emotions feel stuck or unnameable, sit quietly and journal from the prompt: "What am I sad/angry/ashamed about?" Don't judge what comes up. The goal at this stage is observation, not correction. Whatever surfaces is just information — right or wrong doesn't matter yet.
