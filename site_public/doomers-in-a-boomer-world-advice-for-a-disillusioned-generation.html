Summary

TL;DR — The "doomer" worldview isn't irrational or depressed thinking; it's a rational response to being handed a broken formula for life. The real problem isn't the world, and it isn't you — it's autopilot, and the fear of uncertainty that keeps you on it.

A group of self-described "doomers" in their late teens and early twenties sit down to unpack why life feels so relentlessly bleak. What emerges isn't the cognitive-distortion-riddled nihilism you might expect. These are thoughtful, self-aware people who have genuinely tried — ten therapists, failing the same course over and over, withdrawing from school — and keep getting knocked back. The conversation moves through three interlocking problems: living on autopilot, the fear of uncertainty, and isolation. It never fully resolves all three, but the reasoning it produces is worth understanding in full.

The Promise That Turned Out to Be a Lie

A generation was handed a formula: work hard, get good grades, pick a practical degree (engineering, CS, medicine), get a stable job, and happiness will follow. That formula was delivered with total confidence by parents and institutions. And it was genuinely, sincerely meant — it worked for the people giving it.

The problem is that the formula was written for a different world. Parents who came from scarcity — real scarcity, the kind where eight people sleep in an 8x10 room — needed survival strategies, not meaning-making ones. A bachelor's degree could support a family on one income. The stakes and the metrics were completely different.

That world is gone. The formula doesn't transfer. And yet the formula gets passed down anyway, with the same confidence, as though nothing has changed.

"The test has changed. So the answer key from 50 years ago doesn't work anymore."

This isn't the boomers' fault, exactly. But the result is a generation of people playing the wrong character in the wrong game and concluding that the game itself is unwinnable.

Why Your Failure Isn't (Just) Evidence That the World Is Rigged

The doomer frame says: the world is broken, suffering is inevitable, effort is pointless. But when you push on that frame with actual lived examples, something more nuanced surfaces.

Most people in that position don't actually blame the world. They blame themselves. Or they describe something in between — they try, something pushes them back down, they try again, something else goes wrong. After enough repetitions, the conclusion that failure is inevitable stops being a bias and starts being a data-driven inference.

"If you've tried to see 10 therapists and only one has been helpful and the one who was helpful quit — it's reasonable to think the world is kind of a shitty place."

That's not depression distorting your perception. That's pattern recognition.

The trouble is that the pattern is real on the path you're walking. If you've spent years pursuing a degree you don't care about and the best you can ever produce is 50% effort, a 50% result is not proof you're broken. It's proof you're human. Nobody gives their best to something they give no shits about.

Autopilot: How You Survive While Going Nowhere

Autopilot shows up differently for everyone in the conversation — following a parent's academic plan, staying on a career track that feels wrong, filling every empty hour with gaming — but the function is the same: it eliminates uncertainty by eliminating choice. You don't have to decide what you actually want because the path is already set.

The Dota analogy captures it well. Imagine someone told you that the only way to win is to play Anti-Mage, farm for 45 minutes, and go 27-0. You watch Arteezy do exactly that. You pick Anti-Mage every game and go 0-8. Meanwhile, the one time you played support, something clicked — it was more fun, you played better.

But you've never played support in a serious game. You don't know how to win from that position. The guide doesn't cover it. So you keep picking Anti-Mage, keep losing, and conclude that the game is unwinnable.

"All you've been doing is picking Anti-Mage. You never gave yourself the chance to pick a Dazzle or a Lion or an Oracle."

The emotional reason you stay on autopilot isn't laziness. When people imagine turning it off, the immediate response is panic — a specific, physical fear of the unknown. Autopilot feels like the only structure keeping things from collapsing. So you cling to it, even as it slowly destroys any chance of feeling alive.

The Fear of Uncertainty (and What to Do With It)

Autopilot exists to avoid uncertainty. The road you haven't walked is terrifying precisely because you can't run a simulation of it. Your brain models futures based on what it already knows. If you've never played support, your brain genuinely cannot predict how a support game would go. So it defaults to "probably bad."

This isn't cowardice. It's how the mind works. The problem is that you're taking the inability to predict success as evidence that success isn't possible there. It isn't. It just means you don't have enough information yet.

The solution isn't "just try something new." That framing keeps the goal as success, which means failure is still failure. The reframe that actually helps is this: trying something new isn't for success, it's for information. You're playing a few games as support to find out what happens — not to go 27-0 immediately.

When Rune started making music, it wasn't new and fulfilling. It was confusing and weird. The fulfillment came much later, and only because he kept going back to it — not out of discipline, but because something kept pulling him back. That pull is important data. It's telling you something about who you are underneath the autopilot.

The other reframe: trying something new comes with a presumption of failure. You're going to be worse at the unfamiliar path than the familiar one, at first. That's not a sign you made the wrong choice. It's just what new things feel like.

Meaning, Money, and the Mistake of Conflating Them

The boomer formula bundles financial security and personal fulfillment into a single package called "career." Get the right career and you get both. This is almost never how it actually works, and treating it as though it should be sets you up for chronic disappointment.

Financial security is real and legitimate. If you need to do a trades program or a job you don't love in order to pay bills and gain some independence, that's not giving up — that's being practical. But it's also not the destination.

"Financial independence is just money so that you can actually get on to living the life that you want."

The other piece the formula gets wrong is the hobby/job distinction. The barriers to putting creative work into the world are lower than they've ever been. People writing books while working overnight hotel shifts, indie developers selling games on Steam, musicians on Spotify — the world increasingly rewards genuine care and craftsmanship wherever it shows up, not just in credentialed careers.

If you're doing something you care about zero percent, the ceiling on your output is about 50%. If you find something you actually care about, the ceiling rises. That's not motivation-poster logic — it's just how sustained effort works.

Self-Loathing Is Not a Verdict

Rune describes a cycle that's probably familiar: he makes something, likes it for a few hours, then hates it, doesn't touch music for a day, hears something beautiful, gets pulled back in. The self-loathing in the middle feels like a verdict — you're bad at this, you'll never be good at this — but it isn't one.

Self-loathing functions like a song stuck in your head. It arrives, plays for a while, and leaves. The fact that it's playing doesn't mean it's true. The fact that the inspiration returns, again and again, tells you more about who you actually are than the self-loathing does.

"The self-loathing that you have for yourself is not really the real you. It's just a cognitive pattern in your mind."

Whether you continue trying something shouldn't be determined by how you feel about your last performance. It should be determined by whether the pull toward it is still there. Quit for the right reasons — because you genuinely don't care anymore — not because you had a bad day.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing "try something new" with just trying harder. Trying harder at the wrong thing has a ceiling. The question isn't effort, it's direction.

Expecting the new path to feel good immediately. Novelty and fulfillment are not the same thing. New things feel awkward and uncertain. Fulfillment comes after sustained engagement, not at the beginning.

Treating uncertainty as evidence that the path is bad. You can't simulate a life you haven't lived. The inability to predict success doesn't mean success isn't there.

Making financial security the whole goal instead of a means. Getting stable isn't the finish line. Stopping there, and waiting for your job to become meaningful, usually doesn't work.

Calling something OP instead of looking at your own play. Attributing other people's success to unfair advantages can be accurate sometimes, but when it becomes a habit, it's usually protecting you from seeing where you actually have room to grow. Right or wrong, it doesn't help you move.

Treating your self-loathing as accurate self-knowledge. It's a pattern. It lies. It passes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify what you're on autopilot about. Pick one area — school, career, social life — and write down honestly: is this path something you chose, or something you're on because it was the default? No judgment, just observation.

  2. Notice the panic when you imagine getting off autopilot. When you picture an alternative path and feel fear or dread, sit with that for a moment instead of immediately retreating. Ask: is this fear telling me the path is bad, or just that it's unfamiliar?

  3. Try something with the explicit goal of learning, not succeeding. Pick something you've been avoiding because you don't know if you'd be good at it. Give it a few genuine attempts with the framing: I'm collecting information about whether this fits me. Remove success as the metric entirely.

  4. Separate your financial needs from your meaning needs. Write them down in two separate columns. What do you need money for? What do you actually want your life to feel like? Let yourself see that these are two different problems requiring two different strategies.

  5. Track the self-loathing as a pattern, not a truth. Next time the "you're terrible at this" voice shows up, write down the time and what triggered it. Then notice when it leaves. Over a few weeks, you'll see it's cyclical — not a permanent verdict, just weather passing through.

  6. Look for what keeps pulling you back. What have you repeatedly returned to, even after deciding it wasn't worth it? That returning is data. Take it seriously.

  7. Give it more time than feels comfortable. Rune was three years into music before it became deeply fulfilling. Lotus withdrew from school after one bad stretch. The question isn't whether something feels good right now — it's whether the pull is still there and whether you're building something real over time.
