Summary

TL;DR — Your expectations are predictions about what will happen; your standards are what you're willing to accept. Lowering inaccurate expectations reduces suffering without compromising what you value.

Most advice to "lower your expectations" lands wrong because people hear it as "settle for less." But expectations and standards are genuinely different things, and conflating them causes a lot of unnecessary pain. Once you separate them — and add a third concept, your goal — you have a much cleaner map for navigating jobs, relationships, and everyday disappointments.

Expectations Are Predictions, Not Desires

An expectation is your brain's prediction about what will happen next. This isn't a character flaw or a sign of hope — it's a core survival mechanism. The brain evolved to model the future so you can act safely in it. The classic example: an impala drinks from a watering hole it assumed was safe, a crocodile lunges, and the impala survives. From then on, the impala predicts danger at that watering hole. That updated prediction is the whole point.

When a prediction turns out to be wrong — when what you expected doesn't match what actually happened — the brain makes that gap hurt. That's not cruelty; it's a signal to update the model. The size of that pain is roughly proportional to the size of the gap between what you expected and what you got.

"A lot of our suffering in life comes from this prediction error. The gap between what we expect and what we get — that is the size of the pain that we experience."

So when someone keeps expecting their emotionally unavailable parent to finally show up, or expects a friend who has never repaid a loan to pay this one back, the brain keeps getting punished for a prediction it refuses to correct. Mental health research consistently finds that clinging to inaccurate expectations correlates with poorer psychological outcomes — and a large part of what therapy actually does is help people make more accurate predictions about the people and situations in their lives.

Why the Brain Resists Updating

If the brain's whole job is to make accurate predictions, why is it so hard to lower expectations that keep failing?

Because updating a prediction requires sitting with the pain of admitting it was wrong. When that admission would be too painful — for example, accepting that a parent will never offer the love you needed — the brain activates compensatory mechanisms like denial and narcissistic defense. These are not weaknesses; they're the brain's way of protecting itself from an overwhelming realization.

The problem is that these protections also block learning. So the impala keeps drinking from the crocodile pool.

Lowering your expectations genuinely hurts at first, because you have to let go of a hope that felt protective. That's the cost. But the alternative — keeping a prediction wrong indefinitely — produces chronic, recurring pain instead of a single difficult adjustment.

Standards Are a Separate Question

A standard is what you are willing to accept — the minimum (or midpoint) of quality you'll tolerate in a situation. This is not a prediction; it's a value judgment.

These two things are entirely separable:

  • You can expect that your boss will take three weeks to approve your vacation request (accurate prediction based on history), while still holding a standard that timely communication is required in a workplace (what you're willing to accept long-term).
  • You can expect that many people on a dating app will not respond or will cancel plans, while still maintaining a standard that a partner needs to be emotionally available and interested in growth.

Lowering your expectations doesn't mean lowering your standards. It means being honest about what is likely to happen so you're not blindsided by it, while separately deciding what you require.

A concrete example: failing multiple classes and responding by setting a minimum standard of straight A's. The same pattern shows up in job searching (refusing entry-level positions after years of unemployment because the degree "deserves" a senior title) and in dating (requiring a partner who statistically represents one person in a million, while remaining alone).

The fix isn't to abandon what you want. It's to distinguish three separate things:

  1. Expectation — what you predict will happen, based on evidence
  2. Standard — what is good enough; the minimum you'll accept
  3. Goal — what you're ultimately shooting for

You aim for your goal. You enforce your standard. You make your expectations accurate. These are three different operations.

The Wanting-Liking Gap

There's one more piece that makes this genuinely strange: the brain circuits that generate wanting something and liking something once you have it are separate systems.

  • Wanting runs on dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → — it's a forward-looking prediction of how enjoyable something will be.
  • Liking (hedonic experience in the moment) involves the endocannabinoid system.
  • Contentment and mood are more tied to serotoninA neurotransmitter associated with contentment and peace — distinct from dopamine's excitement and pleasure — whose levels are clinically linked to depression and anxiety.Full definition →.

These circuits don't always agree. You can intensely want something that you barely like when you get it. The familiar example is competitive gaming: you want to queue up, you want to win, you want the next game — and then you play and you're miserable. The wanting felt compelling; the experience felt hollow.

"The things that you want, you may not even like. And the things that you like, you may not even want. These are two independent variables."

This matters for standards because it means the person or job or outcome you've decided you must have might not actually produce the contentment you're imagining. Meanwhile, something below your stated standard might genuinely make you happy. That gap between wanting and liking is real, and it's worth being curious about rather than dismissing.

Common Pitfalls

Treating "lower your expectations" as an insult. People resist this advice because they hear it as "give up on what you deserve." But expectations are predictions, not self-worth. Updating a prediction doesn't say anything about what you deserve.

Confusing the goal with the standard. If you refuse to accept anything short of your ideal from the very first step, you block all forward movement. Excellence is built iteratively — from minimum viable to good to great, not from zero to perfect in one jump.

Keeping compensatory standards without recognizing them. If your floor is set in reaction to past failure or shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → rather than genuine values, it's doing more harm than good. Recognizing the source of a high standard is the first step to deciding whether it's worth keeping.

Assuming the wanting-liking gap doesn't apply to you. It does. The partner you've built up in your imagination, the job title you're holding out for — your brain is generating a dopamine-fueled prediction of how satisfying those will feel. That prediction has a mediocre track record.

Denying past evidence to preserve hope. If someone has consistently behaved one way across multiple opportunities to be different, that is the data. Making a different prediction requires denying what you already know.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Make the prediction explicitly. When you're about to enter a situation with emotional stakes — a conversation with a difficult parent, a request to your boss, a first date — pause and ask: Based purely on past evidence, what should I predict will happen? Write it down if that helps. Don't factor in what you want. Just predict.

  2. Map your three layers. For one area of your life right now (relationship, job, friendship), write down separately: (a) what you expect to happen, (b) what your standard is — the minimum you'll accept, and (c) what your actual goal is. Notice where these have been blurred together.

  3. Trace a high standard back to its origin. Pick one standard you hold firmly and ask honestly: Is this standard here because it reflects a genuine value, or because I'm compensating for a failure, insecurity, or shame? You don't have to change it — just know where it came from.

  4. Test the wanting-liking gap. Think of something you currently want badly. Recall the last time you had it or something similar. How did it actually feel in the moment — not in anticipation, but during? Note any difference between the wanting and the experience.

  5. Accept the accurate prediction even when it hurts. If a prediction about someone you care about would cause pain — and the evidence supports it — let yourself feel that once, clearly, instead of cycling through repeated disappointment. The one-time pain of an honest update is smaller than the cumulative pain of repeatedly broken expectations.

  6. Set a process standard, not an outcome standard. Instead of "I will only accept an A" or "I will only apply to senior roles," set a standard around effort and direction: "I will pass this course" or "I will take a role that moves me forward in this field." Aim for your goal; enforce the floor that actually keeps you in the game.
