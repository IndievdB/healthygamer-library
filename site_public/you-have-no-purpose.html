Summary

TL;DR — There is no single "right" career path you're supposed to find. For certain people, the most effective approach is to move through multiple fields deliberately, carrying skills forward and compounding them in unexpected ways.

Most of us are taught that success means picking one career, committing to it for decades, and becoming the best at it. That framing is baked into our language ("my career," not "my careers"), into the success stories we celebrate, and into the advice we give each other. But for a significant subset of people — those with restless, shifting attention who get bored easily and excited about new things — that framework is not just unhelpful, it's actively wrong.

This is a framework for those people: how to understand what you actually are, how to use that nature strategically, and why almost none of your past experience is wasted.

The Binary Trap We Set for Ourselves

We default to thinking about careers in true/false terms: right choice or wrong choice, passion or practicality, committed specialist or unfocused drifter. Even when we allow for multiple options, we treat it like a multiple-choice test — pick one answer and stick to it.

That framing ignores a real category of person. Some people have a genuinely dynamic, shifting mental temperament. They gain momentum in a new domain, plateau or lose interest, and find their energy pulled somewhere else. Most career advice tells these people they're broken, that they need to override that tendency and force themselves to specialize.

"My life improved dramatically once I accepted that I have no singular all-consuming calling and instead just want to do a variety of stuff I kind of like until I eventually die."

There's nothing wrong with that. It's a different operating style, not a deficiency.

Why Being a Generalist Can Actually Win

The conventional wisdom is that hyper-specialization is how you deliver the most value. There's a reasonable argument for it. But it's not the only path — and for some people, it's the worst path.

The real advantage of moving through multiple domains is emergent competence: a combination of skills and perspectives that no pure specialist has, and that creates something genuinely useful precisely because it's not available anywhere else.

Nobody becomes the best content creator, the best doctor, the best gamer, or the best monk in isolation. But someone who combines all four can do things none of those specialists can — apply psychological frameworks to game design, bring meditation practices into high-stress medical rounds, translate family therapy dynamics into executive team consulting.

Game designers who are brilliant at understanding player behavior have largely figured it out without formal behavioral science training. Imagine what someone with both could do. That gap — between what specialists know in their lane and what cross-trained people can see across multiple lanes — is where generalists create value.

The "Dig Multiple Holes" Principle

Variety is not a license for constant shallow dabbling. The key structural rule is: cycle back.

If you get bored of thing A and move to B, then C, try to loop back to A. Don't just keep digging new holes indefinitely. The mental model:

  • Digging 60 holes one foot deep: you never hit water
  • Digging one hole 60 feet deep: you might hit water, but if that spot is dry, you've lost everything
  • Digging four or five holes ten feet deep: highest probability of finding water, and you can hedge against any single dry spot

Over-specialization has a real failure mode. Spending a career becoming the world's best at one narrow thing can leave you completely stranded when that niche disappears — a retail mattress expert when e-commerce eliminates the category, for example.

"Over-specialize and breed in weakness."

Four or five areas of genuine depth, cycled through over time, is more robust than one area taken to an extreme.

Experience Is Worth Less Than You Think (And That's Good News)

One of the most paralyzing beliefs when switching careers is: I've wasted years, and I'll be too far behind to catch up.

Both parts of that are largely false.

On the "wasted years" side: research from medicine shows that quality of practice is largely determined within the first two to five years. Doctors with four years of experience routinely outperform doctors with thirty. The same pattern appears in competitive gaming — players with more total hours frequently underperform players who are newer but more intentional. Experience does not scale linearly with competence.

On catching up: deliberate practice closes gaps faster than accumulated time opens them. The difference between a player with 100 hours who's excellent and one with 1,000 hours who's mediocre isn't hours — it's reflection. The excellent player does the equivalent of replay analysis. They examine what went wrong, identify the specific failure, and correct it. Most people in careers just accumulate hours passively, going through the motions without a personal improvement process.

If you're starting over at 30 or 35, you're not five times behind someone who has been doing it since 22. You're probably one to two years behind, and intentional effort can close that.

Nothing You Did Was Actually Wasted

Every domain you've worked in leaves you with transferable assets, even when the technical content seems irrelevant.

Some assets are universal regardless of field: - Work ethic — the capacity to do things you don't enjoy until they're done - Interpersonal skills — navigating difficult colleagues, de-escalating conflict, reading a room - Detail orientation and reliability — built from high-stakes environments where errors have real consequences - Domain-specific analogies — mental models from one field that illuminate problems in another

Someone who spent years in retail learned to handle difficult customers daily. In a new role, when a colleague or manager is being unreasonable, they have a practiced, automatic response that peers who went straight from school to a professional office don't have. Ten years dealing with sewage plant engineering produces someone unusually detail-oriented, used to high-consequence work, and physically incapable of brushing problems under the rug. Those are real advantages, even to an employer in a totally different field.

The principles you carry are often more durable than the technical content — and the technical content in a new field is usually learnable.

Play to Your Build

Formulas for success are stories about other people. When you try to follow them, you're applying a strategy built around someone else's strengths, temperament, and circumstances to your own situation. It will always be inefficient because it doesn't account for you.

The useful question isn't "what did successful people do?" It's "what does success look like when built around how I actually work?"

If you're intrinsically motivated by novelty and variety, forcing yourself into a decade-long single-track commitment will produce worse results than designing a path that cycles through multiple genuine interests. If your natural inclination is to make things simpler and more efficient, that's not laziness — it's a design sensibility. If you have a background that nobody else in the room shares, that's not a liability. It's the thing you offer that they can't get anywhere else.

"Lean into who you are. Don't follow the formula for success."

Common Pitfalls

Treating variety as an excuse to never go deep. Moving through fields is only useful if you actually develop real competence in each one before moving on. Skimming five topics for a few weeks each produces nothing transferable. The goal is multiple areas of genuine depth, not maximum breadth at minimum investment.

Assuming experience compounds forever. Staying in a career you've outgrown or stopped engaging with because you've "invested so much time" is a sunk-cost trap. The evidence suggests you stopped gaining meaningful competence years before you left — staying longer compounds the loss, not the gain.

Mistaking hours for work. Showing up, doing the job, and accumulating years is not the same as getting better. Without deliberate reflection — examining what went wrong, identifying patterns, adjusting — you're playing on autopilot. The hours are real; the growth is not.

Applying someone else's formula. Success stories are usually told as if the strategy was the cause. They rarely account for the person's specific temperament, circumstances, timing, and the unique combination of factors that made it work for them. What worked for one person may be actively wrong for someone built differently.

Catastrophizing a career change. The gap between where you are and where you'd need to be in a new field is almost always smaller than it feels. Competence develops quickly when it's intentional. Starting over is rarely as costly as staying stuck.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your transferable assets. List every significant thing you've done — jobs, roles, hobbies, training. For each one, write down two or three things you actually got from it: a skill, a mindset, an interpersonal capability, a useful analogy. You'll likely find more cross-application than you expected.

  2. Map your recurring interests. Write down every domain you've been drawn to more than once over your life. Look for patterns and for overlaps between them. These recurring interests are candidates for the "holes worth digging deeper."

  3. Design a cycle, not a ladder. Instead of asking "what should I do with my career?" ask "what are four or five areas I could genuinely develop competence in, and how do they connect?" Sketch how moving between them might work over a five-to-ten year window.

  4. Build a personal improvement process. After any project, difficult interaction, or failure at work, spend fifteen minutes on a written "replay analysis": what happened, what went wrong, what specifically you'd do differently, and what you want to practice next. Don't rely on your organization's feedback process as your only mechanism for growth.

  5. Stop treating years as a measure of competence. When you feel behind someone with more experience, ask specifically: what do they know that I don't? Often the real gap is much smaller and more concrete than "they've been doing this longer." Target the specific gap with deliberate practice rather than trying to catch up on time.

  6. Reframe your "irrelevant" background for an interview or introduction. Take one thing from your past that seems unrelated to where you're going and write a two-sentence explanation of what it actually gives you that someone without that background doesn't have. Practice saying it out loud until it sounds natural.
