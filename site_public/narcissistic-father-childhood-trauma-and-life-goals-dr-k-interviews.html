Summary

TL;DR — When you're paralyzed by a big life decision, the problem usually isn't a lack of information. It's an inability to tolerate uncertainty — and a mind that keeps generating new variables to avoid facing that discomfort.

This is a conversation with JJ (Jobara), a 22-year-old from Bahrain finishing his computer science degree in the US. He grew up with a physically abusive, narcissistic father, was largely raised by his mother and older brother, and is now stuck deciding whether to stay in the US for better career prospects or return home to help his financially struggling family. What starts as a career question gradually reveals something deeper: a pattern of relentless positivity that buries real negative emotion, a lifelong aversion to uncertainty, and a fear that staying in the US means becoming his father.

The Positivity That Buries Pain

JJ describes a genuinely difficult upbringing — years of physical abuse from his father, a mother who worked herself to exhaustion to support the family, a brother who took the worst of the beatings — and yet almost every sentence he speaks turns toward admiration and gratitude. His father is wise. His brother is incredible. His mother is an angel.

This isn't denial exactly. It's something more interesting: a coping pattern so deeply ingrained that it has become his default mode of perception. He blocked out the abuse so thoroughly as a child — in part because he didn't know it was abuse; he thought it was normal — that he never developed access to the anger that should accompany it.

When pushed on this directly, he acknowledges he feels something: "I feel very angry about that guy." But the feeling doesn't stick. His mind returns immediately to appreciation and forgiveness.

"You should have rage in your heart. You should have frustration, anger, disappointment. I think you have guilt. Guilt is the only thing I'm really picking up from you. But there should be a lot of other stuff."

The key insight here is that emotional one-dimensionality is itself a problem. You can love your father and hate what he did. You can forgive someone and still feel the wound. The absence of negative emotion isn't maturity — it's incompleteness. And it creates a blind spot: JJ can't fully understand his own decision-making because a huge driver of his behavior is invisible to him.

The Real Reason the Decision Feels Impossible

JJ frames his dilemma as a career question: stay in the US for better long-term earning, or go back to Bahrain for short-term family stability. But that framing is a decoy.

The actual block is this: staying in the US — focusing on your own career while your brother holds the family together and your mother runs on three hours of sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition → — is exactly what his father would do.

His father retired at 40 to go fishing. He borrowed money from his wife and didn't pay it back. He let her fund the household, the children's school fees, his real estate project. He was physically present but emotionally and financially extractive. Staying in the US and "building your career" looks, from the inside, like becoming that man.

"The reason you don't want to stay is because that's what your dad would do. That's the problem. And so if you stay, you become him."

This is the emotional logic underneath the spreadsheet-style pros-and-cons analysis. JJ isn't really weighing visa logistics and salary potential. He's trying to figure out how not to be his father. And the sad irony is that the objectively better long-term choice for his family — staying, building earning power, sending more money home later — reads to him as abandonment and selfishness.

The correction isn't to ignore his father's shadow. It's to separate the decision from the meaning he's attached to it. Staying doesn't make you your father. What makes someone their father is a pattern of taking without giving, prioritizing their own comfort over their family's wellbeing, and never being accountable. None of that is determined by where you live.

How a Busy Mind Manufactures Complexity

One of the most practically useful observations in this conversation: when you're facing a decision you don't want to make, your mind will generate complexity to avoid making it.

Watch the pattern in real time. Every time the conversation closes in on the actual choice, JJ pivots to a new variable. Should he follow his passion for videography? What about the business minor — does that change things? What about the philosophical question of how many paths a human life can take? What if the H-1B visa situation shifts? What about coding versus management?

None of these are fake concerns. But they're being recruited by the avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → instinct. Each new variable extends the calculation and delays commitment.

"When there is a hard decision, your mind will run to anything it can to complicate the situation. You'll add as many variables as you can to the equation."

This is especially seductive for analytical, philosophically-minded people. Thinking feels like progress. Every new angle feels like it's bringing you closer to the right answer. But you can spend years on this and never move, because the actual obstacle isn't information — it's the willingness to act without certainty.

The Myth of the Right Decision

The framing most people bring to big decisions is: if I think hard enough, I'll identify the correct choice, and then I can act. JJ is stuck inside this framing. He wants to find the answer so he can make the right decision.

The reframe: success comes from damage control, not from making the right call upfront.

This isn't nihilism or "it doesn't matter what you choose." It's a different model of how life actually works. You make a decision with incomplete information. Things go sideways in ways you couldn't have predicted. The skill that determines outcomes isn't pre-decision analysis — it's post-decision adjustment. Can you recognize when something isn't working? Can you change course without collapsing? Can you learn from the wreckage and keep moving?

A future-oriented, highly analytical mind is genuinely good at planning. But it tends to be fragile in execution because it never built the muscle for adjusting mid-flight. It relied on getting it right before takeoff.

"The way to move forward in life is not to find the right decision. You become successful by doing and then learning damage control. It's not prevention — it's damage control."

On the passion versus career question specifically: the framing of "do I pursue videography or take the safe job?" is a false binary. The practical path is to take the job that pays reliably, get genuinely good at it (good enough that you can do 40 hours of output in 20 hours), and build the creative work on the side. The two aren't in competition. JJ has had the skills for this since he was 12, has already demonstrated an audience (80,000 views on a tutorial video he made as a child), and doesn't need to bet his income on it to pursue it seriously.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking forgiveness for resolution. Forgiving someone is a real and valuable thing. But it's not the same as processing the harm they caused. You can forgive your father and still need to grieve what you lost, feel the anger of what was done, and understand how that history is shaping your current choices.

  • Treating emotional positivity as psychological health. Constant gratitude and admiration for everyone around you isn't the same as being okay. Sometimes it's a way of keeping the harder feelings at arm's length. One-dimensional emotion — even when the one dimension is love — is a signal something is being avoided.

  • Confusing analysis with progress. Researching, thinking, and considering more angles feels productive. But if it never terminates in action, it's a sophisticated form of stalling. The volume of thinking does not correlate with the quality of the eventual decision.

  • Letting a parent's failures define what good decisions look like. If your father was selfish and absent, it's easy to code any self-directed choice as "being like him." That logic will sabotage legitimate investment in yourself. What made your father harmful wasn't the category of his choices — it was the pattern of his character.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the buried emotion. Pick one specific thing your parent did — not a category, but a specific incident — and write down what you feel about it without softening the language. See if you can hold both the anger and the love at the same time rather than letting one erase the other.

  2. Identify the avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → variables. The next time you're stuck on a decision, write down every factor you're considering. Then ask yourself honestly: which of these am I raising because they're genuinely relevant, and which am I raising because they extend the time before I have to commit?

  3. Separate the decision from the identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition →. If you're avoiding a choice because it resembles what someone you don't want to be would do, write out explicitly: what actually made that person harmful? Is the specific choice really the issue, or is it a pattern of behavior and character you're afraid of?

  4. Practice making small decisions without optimizing. Deliberately choose a restaurant, a book, a route — without researching it first. Build toleranceSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition → for the feeling of acting without certainty in low-stakes environments.

  5. Set a decision deadline. Pick a date by which you will have made the choice — not the perfect choice, just a choice. Write it down. Share it with someone. The goal is to practice treating commitment as the endpoint, not certainty.

  6. Talk to your family about the guilt directly. Not about the logistics, but about the feeling. Tell your brother and your mother that staying feels like abandoning them. Let them respond. You may find the guilt is based on an assumption about how they'll experience your absence that they don't actually share.
