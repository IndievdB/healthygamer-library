The Adaptive Trap: Why "I Shouldn't Feel This Way" Backfires

When someone spends years absorbing hatred online, the mind finds its best available answer: stop caring. Tell yourself you shouldn't feel hurt. Push the feeling down. Get back to work.

This is genuinely adaptive. It's often the only thing that keeps a person functional. But notice what the strategy actually does — it doesn't resolve the emotion. It makes it dormant.

Think of it like taking ibuprofen for an infection. The fever drops. You feel better. But the moment the medicine wears off, the fever comes back, because the underlying cause was never treated. Telling yourself I shouldn't feel this way is the daily ibuprofen. The wound stays open underneath.

There's a second problem. When someone you love invalidates your feelings — says you shouldn't feel that way — you'd rightfully push back. But when your own mind says the exact same thing to you, there's no pushback. You just absorb the invalidation silently. The message is identical; the source is different. That doesn't make it okay.

"When you feel insecure about yourself and then you tell yourself the same thing — it's a bad message. We just don't see it because it's coming from inside."

Suppression vs. Digestion

There's a crucial difference between sweeping something under the rug and actually processing it.

When you suppress an emotion — distract yourself from it, tell yourself you shouldn't feel it, just push through — the emotional energy doesn't disappear. It gets packaged neatly and stored. It may stop bothering you daily. It may feel resolved. But one gentle question in the right direction and the feelings rush up immediately, because they were never actually gone.

Emotional digestion is different. It means sitting with the feeling long enough for the mind to process it fully. This is what actually happens in good therapy. It's also what you do naturally when you're mature enough to walk yourself through a difficult moment rather than run from it.

A useful image: a child bitten by a dog develops a lasting phobia because her mind isn't yet equipped to process the terror — it just stores it. An adult bitten by the same dog feels that same spike of fear in the first second, but then walks themselves through it: that was scary, I'm okay, maybe be more careful next time. The emotion rises and falls completely. No scar is left.

The self-critical response — what's wrong with you, you idiot — creates a scar. The compassionate, witnessing response dissolves one.

Why We Go Looking for the Thing That Hurts Us

There's a telling pattern: when you're already feeling bad — salty after losing a game, sad after a breakup — you go looking for the thing that will confirm the worst story about yourself. You click through a stranger's chat history. You scroll your ex's Instagram at 2am.

This isn't masochism. It's validation of a feeling you already have but don't know how to sit with.

When the feeling of being unlovable is already active inside you, finding one more piece of evidence for it is strangely relieving. At least the inside and outside match. The alternative — someone telling you you're great, you deserve better — actually feels more uncomfortable, because it contradicts what the wound is telling you.

Noticing this pattern in real time is genuinely useful. When you find yourself clicking into something that's going to hurt, that's a signal: there's already an emotion activated that you haven't sat with yet.

Resistance Is Data, Not an Obstacle

When understanding is blocked, it's often not a problem of logic — it's a problem of readiness. Emotional resistance and intellectual comprehension are connected. When the resistance lowers even slightly, the same explanation that kept bouncing off suddenly lands.

This doesn't mean you should force yourself into emotional territory you're not ready for. It means that when you notice yourself repeatedly unable to grasp something, the question worth asking isn't what am I missing intellectually? but what am I protecting emotionally?

"I was resisting because I genuinely didn't understand. And now that I've understood it, it's different."

High openness with low agreeableness — curious, exploratory, willing to hear things out, but very hard to actually move — is a recognizable cognitive style. It looks like open-mindedness from the outside. It functions more like a sophisticated filter. Knowing this about yourself is useful.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing suppression with healing. Not thinking about something daily is not the same as having resolved it. Dormant pain and digested pain feel similar from the outside and are very different on the inside.

  • Thinking detachmentThe Sanskrit term for non-attachment — specifically, detaching from the outcomes of your actions rather than from the actions themselves.Full definition → means not acting. You can act decisively and still be at peace.

  • Believing the scar defines your whole belief about yourself. Having a small part of you that believes a painful thing doesn't mean you fully believe it. But that small part is real, and ignoring it is what gives it power.

  • Waiting until you're in a bad mood to do the work. The daily grind of stress is not the right time to process a wound. Doing it once a week, with intention, is more useful than trying to analyze every reaction in real time.

  • Assuming the challenge rating of an old wound is as high as it was when you first got it. You are not the same person who was hurt. Your resources — resilience, support, perspective — are vastly greater now. The wound may be much easier to face than you think.

  2. Do one weekly emotional audit. Not every reaction, not every day — pick one moment from the week that produced a stronger emotional response than the situation seemed to warrant. Ask: what does this remind me of? When have I felt this before? Sit with whatever comes up for a few minutes without trying to solve it.

  3. When you catch yourself "going looking" for hurt — scrolling, clicking through someone's history, reopening an old argument — stop and name what you're already feeling before you keep going. The behavior is a symptom. The feeling underneath it is the actual thing worth attending to.
