Summary

TL;DR — Your approach to a conversation determines whether the other person opens up or closes down. Genuine curiosity, not debate tactics, is what actually moves people.

This is a recorded three-way conversation between Dr. K (psychiatrist and founder of Healthy Gamer), political commentator David Pakman, and "Jimmy from Philly" — a self-described proud Trump supporter who regularly calls into Pakman's show. The goal isn't to change Jimmy's mind or diagnose him. It's to actually listen to a person whose views most of the audience finds objectionable, and to model what that kind of listening looks like in practice. What unfolds reveals a lot about where rigid worldviews come from, how survival shapes personality, and why compassion is more useful than argument.

Why Most Political Conversations Go Nowhere

Before Jimmy even says a word, David introduces him with a list of his worst moments: alcohol bottles in frame, an arrest, accusations of domestic violence, a pattern of racist and homophobic language on the show. It's a prosecutor's opening statement dressed up as an introduction.

This is exactly how most political discourse works. You lead with the most damaging framing of the other person, establish that they're a bad actor, and then "engage." What you get back is defensiveness, not dialogue. Jimmy laughs through the whole introduction — not because he's unbothered, but because laughing is how he survives being attacked. He's had decades of practice.

The more productive approach is to set that framing aside and ask a simpler question: How did this person come to see the world the way they do?

How Survival Hardens Into Ideology

Jimmy grew up in Philadelphia, a happy-go-lucky kid who literally skipped down the street until other kids made fun of him for it. He stopped skipping. He learned that being different gets you mocked, that you have to hold your own on the corner, and that the appropriate response to being made fun of is not to feel hurt but to come back harder and funnier.

At 13, after getting into drugs and petty crime, his mother had him placed in a series of group homes and institutions. He hated her for it at the time. He felt abandoned. He watched kids with worse records get picked up by their parents while his mother kept sending him back. His father privately sympathized but didn't intervene, which Jimmy read as cowardice.

He eventually worked through all of this in AA, made amends at his mother's grave at age 38, and came to genuinely respect what she was trying to do for him. But here's the pattern worth noticing:

  • A child who was different got mocked → he learned to mock first, and harder
  • A child who felt abandoned had no choice but to become self-sufficient → he learned that needing people is weakness
  • A child who was sent away when he needed to come home → he learned that the system doesn't have your back, so you'd better not need it to

These aren't irrational conclusions. They're the exact lessons his experience taught him. The problem is that the brain generalizes. What worked as an adaptive strategy in one context gets applied everywhere, including to strangers on the internet, to trans people, to anyone who seems to be asking for special treatment or protection.

"You gotta be on your guard in Philly. You know you're hanging on the corner, you got to be able to hold your own."

The worldview that produces rigid political thinking is often just a survival strategy that outlived the situation that created it.

Anger as the Umbrella Emotion

One of the clearest patterns in the conversation is that Jimmy's emotional range, at least the parts he's willing to show, is extremely narrow. Everything is funny. He's a happy guy. He doesn't feel lonely (though he's surprised to find that true). He hasn't cried in a while.

But watch what happens when the conversation turns to the domestic violence accusation from his ex-partner — the situation that cost him thousands of dollars and his relationship with his daughter. He starts laughing, then gets visibly and genuinely furious. When asked how it feels to talk about it, he says: good.

That moment is important. Anger is accessible to him. Sadness, grief, hurt — those are not. He's had to wall them off because a person who is trying to survive can't afford to fall apart. Anger is useful; it produces action. Grief is not; it produces paralysis.

This isn't a character flaw. It's a very common adaptation in people — especially men — who grew up in environments where vulnerability was punished. The cost of it is that the emotions don't disappear; they just get compressed under the anger, and that pressure has to go somewhere.

The Moment That Cuts Through

There's a brief exchange that lands more than anything else in the conversation. When Jimmy describes the military academy intake officer who pulled him aside privately and said, "If you don't want to come here, I won't make you come here — do you want to be here?" — Jimmy's affect shifts entirely.

He describes that man with something close to warmth. He beat Valley Forge in football later. He's still clearly pleased about it.

What that officer did was simple: he actually asked what Jimmy wanted. He didn't impose a decision. He treated a 13-year-old as someone whose preference mattered. Against a backdrop of being repeatedly sent away without his consent, being asked — and then being listened to — was profound.

"Eternally grateful to that commander, or whatever the hell he was."

The people who got through to Jimmy over his life — the AA sponsor, the military officer, eventually the psychiatrist who noted he was "affable but might benefit from support" — weren't the ones who argued with him or corrected him. They were the ones who took him seriously as a person.

Why Approach Determines Outcome

After the conversation, there's a key observation worth sitting with: Jimmy came across as more open-minded than many viewers expected. That's not because Jimmy is secretly a reasonable person who just needed the right debate partner. It's because the conversation wasn't structured as a debate.

When you approach someone with the intention of catching them in contradictions and proving them wrong, they defend. When you approach someone with genuine curiosity about how they got to where they are, they often — not always, but often — start to reflect.

This applies far beyond politics. It applies to talking to a parent you disagree with, a friend stuck in a bad situation, a colleague with a frustrating attitude. The closed approach produces a closed response. The open approach at least makes openness possible.

"Once you get someone to make an emotional judgment, a nuclear bomb of truth isn't going to make that person change their opinion."

Jimmy says this about the media. It's true about everyone, including the people who are sure they're above it.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking open-mindedness is a trait, not a response. People say "Jimmy was more open-minded than I expected" as if openness is something he either has or doesn't. But whether someone is open or closed in a conversation depends enormously on how you show up in that conversation.

Confusing the map for the territory. Your experiences are real. The conclusions you draw from them are not automatically real. Three girls turning you down for football players is a real experience; "all women only want athletes" is an abstraction your brain built from that data. The abstraction feels as solid as the experience, but it isn't. This is how anyone ends up with a rigid worldview — they're not stupid, they're just generalizing from real pain.

Using compassion as a tactic. There's a version of "listening with an open mind" that is actually just a manipulation strategy — asking questions while already forming your rebuttal. People feel this. It doesn't work. Real curiosity about someone's experience is different from performed curiosity, and the person you're talking to usually knows which one they're getting.

Thinking "I told you so" opens minds. When someone holds a belief that turns out to be wrong and pays a price for it, pointing that out in the moment of their pain does not make them reconsider the belief. It makes them defend it harder. This is especially relevant with health decisions, political choices, and lifestyle disagreements.

Assuming rigidity is stupidity. Political rigidity, especially in people whose lives have been genuinely hard, is often a load-bearing wall. It's doing structural work. Calling it stupid doesn't dismantle it; it just tells the person you haven't understood why it's there.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Before your next disagreement, ask one real question. Not a rhetorical question designed to reveal the other person's error — an actual question you don't know the answer to. "How did you come to believe that?" or "What's the experience that makes this feel true to you?" Then listen to the answer without planning your rebuttal.
