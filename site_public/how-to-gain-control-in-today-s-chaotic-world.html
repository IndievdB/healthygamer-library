Summary

TL;DR — The despair you feel about an uncontrollable world isn't a character flaw; it's a mismatch between your evolved brain and modern life. The path out is radically narrowing your focus to the only thing you've ever actually controlled: yourself.

The world feels increasingly broken — inflation, climate change, dating markets that punish things you can't change, economic systems that trap people before they start. The instinct is to try harder to fix those things, or to collapse under the weight of not being able to. Both responses miss something important: your brain was never built to handle problems at this scale, and the suffering you feel when you try is almost mechanically inevitable.

The good news is that human beings have been here before. As civilization scaled up and problems outgrew individual agency, a coherent answer emerged — not in self-help books, but in ancient philosophy. Understanding that answer, and a simple exercise to start living it, is what this piece is about.

Your Brain Is Running Software for a Different World

For the vast majority of human history, the worst news your brain might receive in a day was that someone in your tribe of 50–200 people got sick, or that the local river was running low. Problems at that scale were actually solvable. You could move camp, share food, help a neighbor. The feedback loop between action and outcome was tight and real.

Now your brain absorbs — every day, often every hour — news about collapsing ecosystems, geopolitical instability, economic systems rigged against you, and social comparison with millions of strangers. Your brain tries to solve these the same way it would solve a drought: what do I do about this? But there is no action it can produce that fixes inflation or climate change. So it keeps cycling, keeps trying, and the repeated failure of that loop is experienced as hopelessness.

This isn't weakness. It's a hardware-software mismatch. Your brain evolved for a low-stimulation natural environment. The Information Age is running on hardware that wasn't designed for it.

What the Bhagavad Gita Got Right

One of the oldest and clearest answers to this problem comes from the Bhagavad Gita, a text from the Hindu tradition. A prince named Arjun is about to enter a civil war where the opposing army includes his grandfather and his beloved teacher. He despairs — even winning means devastating loss. He wants to walk away from the whole thing.

His charioteer Krishna offers him one central piece of wisdom:

"As a human being, you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions. All you are entitled to is the actions themselves."

This is not a consolation prize. It is a precise description of reality. Arjun cannot control whether he lives or dies in the battle. He cannot control whether his grandfather survives. He cannot control the ultimate fate of the kingdom. What he can control is whether he shows up and fights with integrity.

The shift isn't about lowering your ambitions. It's about locating your identity and energy in the only place they can actually live: your own actions. The outcome is not yours. The action is.

The Trap of Outcome-Focused Living

When you focus on outcomes you can't control, a specific and painful pattern emerges:

  1. You want something you cannot directly produce (someone's love, a promotion, a grade)
  2. You try to produce it anyway
  3. You fail, because you cannot control another person's mind or the full causal chain of events
  4. Your brain registers the failure
  5. Repeat — until the brain concludes the goal is impossible
  6. Despair

This is why people trying hardest in dating sometimes do the worst. Every attempt that fails isn't just a setback; it's data the brain uses to conclude that connection itself is impossible for them. The harder they try to control how someone else feels, the more they fail, the deeper the despair.

The same pattern shows up in studying, in job applications, in creative work. The terror of the outcome — failing a class, getting rejected — can become so consuming that it prevents the one thing you actually control: sitting down and doing the work.

The Fist Exercise: Learning What You Actually Control

Here's a simple meditation to make this visceral rather than just intellectual:

  1. Hold your hands out in front of you and make fists
  2. Press them together and clench your whole upper body — feel the tension
  3. Slowly release, bring your arms to your sides
  4. Close your eyes, breathe, and sit with what you feel
  5. Notice: right now, in this moment, what can I actually control?

The answer is: this. Your body, to whatever extent it responds to you. Your attention. The next action you choose to take. That's the full list.

Everything else — whether the video records correctly, whether the person texts back, whether the application gets accepted, whether the world fixes itself — is outside that boundary. Not because you're powerless in some tragic sense, but because that's simply how causality works. You are one node in an enormous web of cause and effect. The node you operate is yourself.

Once that boundary becomes real to you — not just an idea but something you've felt — it changes what you do with your energy.

Focusing Inward Doesn't Mean Giving Up

There's a version of this philosophy that sounds like passivity: nothing matters, I can't control anything, why try? That's not what this is.

The point is that when you stop leaking energy into outcomes you can't control, that energy becomes available for action. And action — consistent, honest, effort-without-attachment — is what actually produces results, often surprising ones.

Applying to medical school three times and getting rejected from over eighty programs is demoralizing if you're attached to acceptance as proof of your worth. It becomes sustainable if your only job is to apply, because applying is the one thing you control. Studying medicine because you want to understand medicine — not to win a grade — can lead to awards you didn't even know to expect.

Finding a relationship by deciding to stop trying to make someone fall in love with you, and instead focusing on becoming yourself, sounds paradoxical. It works for the same reason flow states work: the research on peak performance is consistent that you cannot be focused on the outcome and fully present in the action at the same time.

"The more that we spend energy trying to control things outside of us, the worse things actually become."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking acceptance for resignation. Acknowledging you can't control the outcome doesn't mean the outcome doesn't matter, or that you shouldn't try. It means your trying is not conditional on a guaranteed result. Arjun still fights.

Thinking this is easy or quick. The Buddha reportedly took about ten years working on this. It's a practice, not a switch. Expecting to feel immediate peace after reading about it once is itself an attachment to an outcome.

Using "I can't control it" as an excuse to do nothing. The philosophy is not "therefore I won't act." It is "therefore I will act without requiring a specific result." The action is the whole point.

Applying this only to big problems. The practice starts small — taking a shower, fixing a healthy snack, opening a textbook. The scale of the first action doesn't matter. The orientation does.

Waiting until you feel ready. You don't control whether this works. Neither does anyone else. The only relevant question is whether you try.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the fist exercise today. Sit quietly, make fists, feel the tension, release it, close your eyes, and spend two to five minutes noticing what you actually have control over right now. Do this as a daily anchor, not a one-time thing.

  2. Make two columns. Write down the things currently causing you the most stress or despair. For each one, draw a line down the middle: on the left, what about this situation is within your control? On the right, what isn't? Be ruthlessly honest. Then ask: where is most of your energy going?

  3. Identify one action you've been avoiding because you're afraid of the outcome. A conversation, an application, a creative project. Separate the action from the result in your mind. Commit to the action without requiring the result. Then do it.

  4. Pick one tiny controllable thing and do it immediately after reading this. Not because it will fix your life, but because it locates your energy in the right place. A shower, a walk, a glass of water, ten minutes of reading. The size is irrelevant. The orientation is everything.

  5. When you notice yourself spiraling about world events or others' behavior, use this redirect: What is the one action available to me right now within my actual boundary of control? Do that thing. Return to it every time the spiral restarts.
