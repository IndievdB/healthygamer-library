The Problem with External Standards

Medicine and neuroscience work by averaging responses across large populations. The 2,000-calorie-a-day guideline came from a 1950s survey that asked Americans whether they felt healthy, averaged their diets, and called it science. The diagnostic criteria for depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → came from a committee of researchers who needed a checklist so different studies could compare the same thing—there is no blood test, no brain scan, no biopsy for depression. Same for schizophrenia, addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, and most of psychiatry.

This matters because if you use population averages as your personal benchmark, you are judging yourself against a fictional composite person. The average is not you. When you fall short of "what most people can do," that gap doesn't tell you what you are capable of—it just makes you feel like you're failing.

"If you're talking about individual transformation, you can read as many studies as you want on neuroscience. All of those studies are going to be about the average person. They're not going to be about you."

The counterpoint—raised honestly in the conversation—is that you need some external reference to generate a hypothesis before you can test anything yourself. The answer isn't to ignore science. It's to use it as a starting point, then verify it in your own experience. What works for the average doesn't automatically work for you. What doesn't work for the average might work perfectly for you.

Why the Brain Blocks You (and Why That's Not Stupidity)

The brain is not sabotaging you. It is protecting you according to outdated instructions.

A child gets bullied in a circle of six kids. The brain registers: when people are looking at me, I am in danger. That lesson gets encoded. Years later, at a dinner table with strangers, the same brain fires the same danger signal—heart racing, chest tightening—even though no one is threatening anyone. The person knows the thought is irrational. They can't stop the physiological response. That's not weakness; that's a protection mechanism that got laid down when the play-doh was still soft.

This is the structure of social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, and it applies to almost any fear that derails intention. Jobs, Careers, and Callings

There's a meaningful difference between a job (what society says you should do to be secure) and a calling (what you feel a dutyDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition → or responsibility toward, even when it's painful).

The example in this conversation: streaming is genuinely unpleasant much of the time. Long hours, relentless negativity, trolls, the psychological toll of broadcasting yourself to thousands of people. And yet showing up anyway. Not because it feels good, but because something would be wrong in the world if you didn't.

"Most human beings, when they have an intention, what gets in the way is the negative feelings or negative outcomes. And somehow you are able to do something in spite of those negative feelings. You have stumbled upon the silver bullet—to do something even though it hurts."

This sounds grim, but the point is actually liberating: if negative experience is no longer the thing that stops you, you have enormous freedom. The calling gives you access to that. A job doesn't, because you're always doing it for an external reason, and the moment the external reason weakens, so does your motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →.

A useful question: what are the things that you can do despite them being difficult, that other people complain bitterly about, but that you find manageable or even natural? That asymmetry—where something hard for most people is ordinary for you—is a signal worth following.

Why Video Games Are So Hard to Quit

Video games are intellectually well-matched to the people who play them. A smart kid finishes the math worksheet in ten minutes and has to sit quietly for the rest of the period. The game scales to exactly where they are—too easy and it's boring, too hard and they practice until they're ready.

But what makes games specifically addictive—more than almost any drug—is not the reward. It's the intermittent reward. Every time you use heroin, you get high. Every time you play League of Legends or Fortnite, you might lose. The uncertainty is what hooks the brain. Loot boxes work on exactly the same mechanism. The denial of the reward is more addictive than the reward itself.

The accomplishments feel real in the moment—the dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → response to completing a hard task is genuine—but they tend not to stick. In part because the game's rewards exist inside the game. Outside, the only social reinforcement is a forum post. In the real world, an accomplishment reverberates: it changes how people treat you, what you're allowed to do, who you become. The signal is much louder and longer-lasting.

Common Pitfalls

Using averages as a personal performance standard. "Most people can do five things a day" does not tell you what you can do today. It tells you how to feel bad about doing two things today.

Confusing the cognitive reason with the actual reason. When you ask yourself why you're not doing something, the first answer is almost always a thought: I don't have time, I'm not ready, it's not the right moment. That's probably not the real answer. The real answer is usually a feeling or a physical sensation that shows up when you actually try to do the thing.

Hiding behind noble causes. If you have something uncomfortable to look at in yourself, it is very easy to stay busy doing good work for others instead. The fact that the work genuinely helps people doesn't mean it isn't also functioning as an escape.

Mistaking physical/emotional pain for suffering. A cancer patient can still have pain at level nine and be fundamentally at peace. Pain and suffering are not the same thing. A meditation practice doesn't reduce the pain score—it reduces the suffering, which is why the pain medication use drops dramatically anyway.

Assuming the answer is somewhere in a book. Books and studies generate hypotheses. They don't replace the experiment of living your own life and noticing what actually happens.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down one thing you want to do but aren't doing. Be specific. Don't judge the answer—just get it on paper.

  2. Look at your first explanation for why you're not doing it. It will almost certainly be cognitive ("I don't have time," "it won't work," "I'm not ready"). Note it, but don't stop there.

  3. Get as close as possible to actually doing the thing. Open the job application. Pick up the phone. Start typing the first sentence. Don't complete it yet—just move toward it.

  4. Notice what comes up physically and emotionally as you do this. What do you feel in your chest? Your stomach? Is there a specific emotion you can name, or just a vague sensation? Write that down too. You don't need to solve it yet. Awareness is the entire goal of this first step.

  5. Ask yourself: what hard things do I find easy that others complain about? The things that feel natural to you but seem difficult to most people are a more reliable signal about where your energy belongs than any career quiz or self-help framework.

  6. Before the next conversation or check-in, follow up on steps 1–4. The exercise only works if you revisit what you found. Don't let it sit.
