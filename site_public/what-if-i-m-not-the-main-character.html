Summary

TL;DR — If you grew up being told you were gifted, you may have never actually learned how to feel pride — not because you lack accomplishments, but because the emotional feedback you received made pride impossible to develop.

Most people assume pride is automatic: do something good, feel good about it. But pride is actually a learned emotion, shaped by the specific feedback loop you grew up with. For some people — especially those labeled "gifted" early in life — that feedback loop was quietly broken. The result is a strange emptiness: you work hard, you succeed, and you feel... nothing. Just relief, at best. This piece works through exactly why that happens and what you can do about it.

Why Gifted Kids Often Can't Feel Pride

When adults constantly tell a child they're special, smart, and destined for great things, those words don't just feel nice — they set a floor. Excellence becomes the baseline expectation, not an achievement.

Think of it this way: if expectations sit at the ceiling, the best you can ever do is meet them. You can't exceed them, so there's no gap between "what was expected" and "what you actually did." That gap is where pride lives.

"If perfection is the bare minimum, how can you ever feel proud?"

Getting an A, working out consistently, finishing a hard project — if these are simply what's expected of you, then doing them earns you a pat on the back at best. It doesn't feel like triumph because nobody is treating it as one, including you.

The Other Side of the Coin: Dismissed Shame

Pride and shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → are two sides of the same coin. You can feel proud of the same things you could feel ashamed of — it depends on direction, not category. If you did well at something, you might feel pride. If you did poorly at the same thing, you might feel shame. They're inseparable.

Here's where well-meaning parents accidentally cause damage: when a gifted child struggles or fails, parents often rush to reassure rather than acknowledge. "Don't worry, you're so smart, you'll get it next time." It sounds kind and supportive. But what it actually communicates is: your struggle isn't real.

That kind of reassurance is a form of invalidation. It sweeps the difficulty under the rug. And here's why that matters for pride:

If your parents respond the same way whether you succeed or fail — "you're great, you'll be fine" — your brain never learns to distinguish between the two states. It receives identical feedback regardless of outcome.

"It's sort of like you can't learn the right way to feel, because no matter what happens you're being given the same feedback. Your mind has not learned how to differentiate between pride and shame."

Without the contrast, neither emotion develops properly. You end up not quite knowing what shame feels like (because it was always swept away) and not quite knowing what pride feels like (because success was always expected). The emotional terrain just stays... flat.

Why Unexpected Recognition Hits Differently

There's a revealing pattern in people who struggle with pride: when recognition comes from an unexpected source, in an area they never thought mattered, it breaks through. The baseball Heart Award is a perfect example.

The award didn't come for being smart or disciplined or gifted. It came for something the person hadn't placed expectations on — their presence, their character, their impact on teammates. Crucially, it also came from people who had witnessed the failures, the lost championship, the hard moments. They saw the full picture and chose to honor it anyway.

That's exactly what made it land. Two things were true at once: - Failure was acknowledged, not swept away. - The recognition came in a dimension free from existing expectations.

When praise arrives in a category where you've set no baseline, it can actually register as something you exceeded. That's the emotional gap where pride can form.

The Real Josh vs. the Image of Josh

One of the more quietly damaging effects of being labeled gifted is that other people start projecting an image onto you — and you lose track of the difference between that image and who you actually are.

From the outside, someone who wakes up at 4:30 AM, meditates, exercises, and studies looks like a disciplined, motivated, high-achieving person. The image is inspiring.

From the inside, it's: "I really want to play video games but I guess I'll drag myself out of bed."

The gap between those two pictures is enormous. And here's the thing — the person deserves more credit, not less. The person fumbling toward a better life, fighting a thousand small battles with themselves, winning each one reluctantly — that person has genuinely earned something. The polished image of effortless achievement hasn't.

"Who deserves more pride — the bumbling balloon who struggles and eventually floats in the right direction, or someone who's just... performing?"

Sitting with the honest, unglamorous version of yourself — the one who struggles, resists, and shows up anyway — is where pride actually gets a foothold.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting for the feeling to arrive on its own. Many people assume that if they just keep achieving, pride will eventually show up. It usually doesn't, because the underlying feedback loop hasn't changed. The circumstances don't generate the feeling; the learned framework for interpreting those circumstances does.

Mistaking reassurance for support. Being told "you'll be fine, you're so smart" feels like encouragement but functions as dismissal. If someone in your life (or you yourself) immediately minimizes your struggles, you're not getting the validation that makes eventual success feel earned.

Staying in your lane of giftedness. Gifted kids often unconsciously avoid areas where they're not already good, because performing poorly there would shatter the image. But those are exactly the spaces where pride becomes possible — because the expectations aren't there to block it.

Confusing pride with ego. Going into a new activity hoping to be recognized and admired is still operating inside the expectation trap. The goal is to do something for someone else, where your self-image genuinely isn't the point. That's what makes the feedback feel real when it comes.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Validate your own struggles out loud. When you do something hard — wake up early, work out, finish an assignment you didn't want to do — don't just note the output. Acknowledge the internal resistance you had to overcome. Say it to yourself or write it down: "That was actually hard for me." This is the missing step that makes success feel earned rather than automatic.

  2. Find someone who can witness your difficulty, not just your results. This might be a therapist, coach, or trusted friend. The key quality: someone who won't immediately reassure you that everything is fine. You need at least one person who can sit with you in the hard parts without rushing to fix them.

  3. Move toward activities outside your "gifted" category. Ask yourself before committing to something: "If I did a great job here, would that just be meeting my own baseline expectation?" If yes, that's a low-pride activity for you right now. Seek out something — volunteer work, communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → efforts, helping others directly — where you genuinely don't expect greatness from yourself going in.

  4. Sit with the two images of yourself. Write a short description of how others likely see you, then write an honest description of your inner experience day-to-day — the resistance, the fumbling, the effort. Notice the gap. The second version is the one that deserves credit.

  5. Start with gratitude if pride isn't accessible yet. Pride may not come immediately. That's okay. A lower-stakes entry point: be grateful to yourself for specific efforts. Not the results — the effort itself. "I'm grateful I got out of bed at 4:30 again today." Gratitude for effort is the precursor to pride in accomplishment.

  6. Reflect, don't just consume. Insight without reflection doesn't change behavior. After any meaningful experience — a success, a failure, a conversation — spend a few minutes in a quiet space going over what happened and what it means. This is how internal change actually gets processed and consolidated.
