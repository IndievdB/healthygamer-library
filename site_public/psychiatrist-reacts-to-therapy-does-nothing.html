Summary

TL;DR — Therapy's built-in culture of non-commitment is a real structural problem, not a personal failing on your part. Understanding why it works that way helps you navigate it more effectively.

If you've spent years in therapy feeling like you're talking to a wall — doing all the work, getting your questions deflected back at you, cycling through practitioners with no clearer sense of what's actually wrong or what the plan is — you're not imagining it. There are specific, trainable reasons therapists behave this way, and knowing them changes how you approach getting help.

Why Therapists Dodge Your Questions

The reflexive non-answer — "what do you think we should work on?" — isn't laziness or indifference. It's a trained reflex rooted in a very specific therapeutic tradition.

Therapy as a discipline traces its roots to Freud, who believed that the more neutral the analyst remained, the more material would surface from the patient. The underlying logic: you have stuff buried in you; if the therapist creates the right conditions, it comes out; the coming-out is the cure. From that premise, the therapist's job is to be a mirror, not a guide. Giving direct answers, steering someone toward a decision, saying "I think you should quit your job" — all of that is actively discouraged in training.

The other reason is accountability avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →. If a therapist never orders for you at the restaurant, you can never blame them for not liking the food. Therapy supervision tends to reinforce this: when a patient drops out or doesn't improve, the framing is almost always that the patient "wasn't ready yet" — not that the therapist could have done something differently. There's no standard mechanism for a practice to aggregate patient outcomes, compare them across clinicians, and ask hard questions. Therapists often have no clear feedback loop for whether they're actually helping.

"If I order for both of us at a restaurant and you don't like the food, you're gonna blame me. So what I'm gonna do is sit down and never order a damn thing — so you can never hold me accountable for anything."

The Real Cost of This Approach

This professional culture of non-commitment has a predictable human cost: people who genuinely want answers, a direction, a plan — people who would use advice — cycle through therapists for years and feel increasingly hopeless. The implicit message they receive is "figure out what you need and tell us," but if they could do that reliably, they probably wouldn't need to be there.

There's an important distinction between two therapeutic stances:

  • Bouncing everything back: "What do you think you should do?" used as a permanent first and last move
  • Working together: Trying something, being willing to be wrong, and iterating together based on what doesn't work

The first leaves people stranded. The second actually builds a real therapeutic alliance — one where the clinician has some skin in the game.

Why Giving Bad Answers Is Better Than Giving None

One of the more counterintuitive ideas here: offering an imperfect answer is often more useful than refusing to offer one. When you suggest something and it doesn't work, a few things happen:

  1. The patient sees you're genuinely trying
  2. They have something concrete to react to
  3. Their rejection of your bad idea often reveals what they actually need
  4. The dynamic shifts from "patient waits for therapist to fix them" to "two people working the problem together"

There's a meaningful difference between a clinician saying "I don't know how to help you" and walking away, versus "I don't know how to help you — can you help me help you?" The second version is honest about the limits without abandoning the person. It invites collaboration rather than issuing a referral and moving on.

"I can't do this on my own. I can't fix you. I've tried. But I can't do it on my own, and you can't do it on your own either — so let's do it together."

That shift — from waiting for the expert to fix you, to both parties actively working the problem — is where real progress tends to start.

Why Coaching Has Been Filling the Gap

The rise of life coaching isn't an accident. It exists partly because therapy left a vacuum. Coaches aren't trained in the Freudian non-directive tradition, so they don't have the same reflex toward neutrality. They're more likely to:

  • Offer direct suggestions and external-focus strategies
  • Set concrete goals and measure progress toward them
  • Hold themselves accountable to outcomes in a way formal therapy rarely does

This doesn't mean coaching is uniformly better — and it doesn't mean therapy has no value. Therapy, especially for clinical illness, can be genuinely useful. But therapy as currently practiced isn't designed for someone who walks in saying "I need a plan and I need to understand what we're trying to achieve." Coaching is closer to that.

The honest assessment: measuring outcomes carefully and intervening when a clinician isn't helping their clients is not standard practice in therapy — but it is something a well-run coaching program can do. That accountability structure matters.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming the problem is you. Years of therapists not helping can start to feel like evidence that you're unfixable. It's more often evidence that the match or the modality wasn't right, or that the therapy culture's non-directiveness was a structural mismatch for what you need.

Waiting for the therapist to lead. Because they're the expert, it feels right to let them run things. But many therapists will wait indefinitely for you to lead. You may need to be explicit: "I need us to agree on what we're trying to achieve and how we'll measure it."

Treating a referral as rejection. Being told "I don't think I can help you, try someone else" feels like abandonment. Sometimes it's appropriate honesty — but the bar should be higher than just passing you along. A therapist who says "I'm not sure I know how to help you, but I'm willing to figure it out with you" is offering something meaningfully different.

Expecting solutions without readiness. This cuts the other way: being handed concrete advice ("just do X") doesn't work either if the underlying emotional or psychological state makes following through impossible. "Just talk to five strangers a day" is as useless as "just don't be depressed" if you're not in a position to do it. Good help meets you where you actually are.

Assuming coaching is a scam because it's unlicensed. Licensing guarantees training in a specific methodology, not effectiveness. An unlicensed coach who measures outcomes, adjusts, and genuinely engages with your problem may help more than a licensed therapist running on autopilot.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name what you actually want from treatment. Before your next session, write down: What specific change would tell me this is working? What does "better" look like in concrete, observable terms? Bring this to the session and ask your therapist to help you build a plan around it.

  2. Ask the accountability question directly. Ask your therapist: "How will we know if this is working? What should we expect to see — and by when?" A therapist who can't answer this at all is probably not a good fit for someone who needs structure and direction.

  3. Evaluate the collaboration, not just the content. After sessions, ask yourself: Did I feel like we were working on this together, or did I feel like I was reporting in and getting bounced back? The quality of the collaborative dynamic often predicts outcomes better than the specific technique being used.

  4. Try EMDR if the distress feels physical or instinctive. If talk-based approaches have repeatedly failed to reach the core of your suffering, and the distress feels more like a bodily or automatic reaction than a set of conscious thoughts, look for a therapist specifically trained in EMDR. It's worth one genuine attempt.

  5. Consider a coaching model alongside (not instead of) clinical care. If you have a diagnosable condition, don't abandon professional oversight. But if you're also looking for direction, problem-solving, and someone willing to try things with you, a coaching relationship can fill the gap that therapy's non-directive culture often leaves.

  6. Be explicit that you want a collaborator, not a mirror. At the start of any new therapeutic relationship, say clearly: "I've been in therapy for a long time and I feel like I've been doing most of the work. What I need is someone who will actually try things with me, be wrong sometimes, and keep adjusting. Is that something you're able to do?" Their answer — and how they give it — tells you a lot.
