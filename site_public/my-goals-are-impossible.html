Summary

TL;DR — Feeling like your goals are impossible usually isn't a motivation problem; it's what happens when ego, life planning, and happiness get tangled into one equation that can never be solved.

A 25-year-old doctor — accomplished, driven, deeply anxious — describes feeling like he's climbing a tree with no top. He finished medical school, started working, wants to pursue a PhD and research, but every achievement leaves him feeling like it's not enough. The conversation uncovers why that happens and what to actually do about it. The core insight: three separate problems (ego/insecurity, life planning, and happiness) get fused into one impossible strategy, and the only way out is to pull them apart.

The Tree With No Top

The image that anchors everything: you're climbing, and the first few years feel fine because you're looking at the branch right in front of you. You reach for the next hold, you find it, you move up. That's satisfying.

The crisis arrives the moment you look up and realize the tree doesn't end.

This is what happens when someone transitions from executing tasks to confronting the open-endedness of a career or a life. In medical school, the syllabus tells you what the next branch is. After year four, five, six — the structure runs out and you see an infinite climb. That's not a sign you're doing something wrong. It's a signal that your relationship to goals needs to change, because the strategy that got you here was never designed for an infinite horizon.

The Early Life Crisis Nobody Talks About

Most people have heard of a midlife crisis. What gets less attention is the same thing happening at 25.

The pattern: you adopt a strategy for life — work hard, accomplish things, get rewarded — because you genuinely believe it will make you happy. You grind for years. You rack up achievements. And then one day you look at everything you've built and realize: I'm still not happy. I'm terrified.

That terror has two parts. First: did I waste years on the wrong strategy? Second: if I change strategies now, how do I know the new one will work?

This is the crisis. Not a failure of ambition. A collision between what you were taught success would feel like and what it actually feels like when you get there.

How the Ego Rigs the Game

The ego doesn't compare you to one person. It compares you to the best version of a dozen different people simultaneously — the most athletic friend, the best gamer, the most published researcher, the top student. It constructs a composite ideal that no single human being has ever actually been, and then measures you against it.

You can never win that comparison. That's the point.

But here's the subtler problem with ego: it doesn't honestly claim you can reach the top. If you ask directly, the honest answer is "probably not." What ego actually does is tell you that the insecurity you feel right now will be fixed if you accomplish enough. It whispers: win the Nobel Prize, and you'll finally feel okay about yourself.

"The ego is a consequence of an insecurity... ego doesn't arise when people are confident. It actually arises when people lack confidence — sort of like a defense mechanism."

This is why no accomplishment ever lands. The insecurity wasn't caused by a lack of achievement. It was a feeling of insufficiency that formed early — often in childhood, often in response to how accomplishment and worth were tied together by parents, teachers, or peers. No external award can fix an internal wound. If it could, finishing medical school would have been enough. It wasn't.

Why Accomplishment Doesn't Create Happiness (But We Think It Does)

There's a reason this confusion is so common: it's literally how many of us were raised.

When a child does something well and gets rewarded — a prize, praise, validation — the brain learns: accomplishment → external reward → happiness. That's a reasonable lesson for a child. The problem is that adults don't have a prize committee. Nobody hands you a trophy for doing your job well. The reward structure disappears, but the belief remains.

So you keep accomplishing and feel nothing. The logical conclusion your mind reaches — I need a bigger accomplishment — only deepens the trap.

Carol Dweck's research on mindset points toward what actually works: rewarding effort rather than outcome, focusing on the action rather than the result. The goal isn't to stop caring about your work. It's to find meaning in the doing, not in the prize at the end.

"Most of the happiness in life doesn't come from extraordinary. It comes from normal."

The happiest stretches in the conversation's subject's life were the years in medical school when he was just curious, just learning, just engaged — not when he was thinking about what came next. That's not coincidence.

The Three Things That Need to Be Separated

The core problem is that three distinct things get collapsed into one:

  1. Ego / insecurity — a felt sense of not being good enough, rooted in early experiences
  2. Life planning — decisions about career, goals, direction
  3. Happiness — moment-to-moment contentment and meaning

The ego fuses them like this: I feel insufficient → I need to accomplish X to feel sufficient → therefore my plan is to spend 40 years achieving X → then I will be happy.

That's one equation trying to solve three separate problems at once, and it fails on every dimension: - The insecurity doesn't get resolved by accomplishment - The plan becomes driven by anxiety rather than genuine interest - Happiness never arrives because it was attached to an outcome rather than a way of living

Once you recognize these as three separate problems, each has a more tractable solution: - Insecurity can be worked through directly — therapy, coaching, honest self-examination about where the "not enough" feeling actually came from - Planning is still worth doing, but as a compass, not a contract ("what's my next best move?") rather than a 40-year blueprint attached to a happiness payoff - Happiness is largely about present-moment engagement — research on mindfulness and contemplative traditions converges on this — and it doesn't reliably come from outcomes

"Planning a particular life doesn't necessarily lead to happiness... there are people who have MDs who are happy and people who have MDs who are not happy."

Common Pitfalls

Thinking the solution is to stop having goals. It isn't. The problem isn't ambition or planning — it's assuming the plan will deliver happiness. Keep planning. Just don't hitch your emotional wellbeing to the destination.

Trying to resolve insecurity through more accomplishment. This is the most seductive mistake because it has a logic to it. But the insecurity wasn't born from failure; it was born from a learned belief that your worth depends on results. More results don't fix that belief.

Turning self-improvement into another version of the same trap. Even working on your value system can become: if I complete this internal work, then I'll be happy. That's just ego finding a new ladder. The work needs to be done without treating it as another achievement to unlock.

Comparing yourself to a composite. No living person is the best athlete, researcher, friend, and professional simultaneously. The target your ego builds doesn't exist.

Mistaking normalization for insignificance. If you grew up in a household where saving lives was ordinary, it can feel like nothing. That's a calibration problem, not a measurement of actual impact.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice which direction you're looking. When you feel anxious or stuck, ask yourself: am I focused on the branch in front of me, or am I staring at the invisible top of the tree? This single question is a practical interruption for the spiral.

  2. Separate the three problems on paper. Write three columns: Insecurity/Ego, Life Plan, What makes me happy day-to-day. List what belongs in each. Notice how many things you've placed in the wrong column — especially goals that are really attempts to fix a feeling about yourself.

  3. Trace where "not enough" started. Try to identify when you first began feeling that your worth was tied to accomplishment. Was it a specific environment — school, a parent's reaction, a comparison to a sibling or classmate? You don't need to fully resolve this on your own; a therapist or coach can help, and this is specifically the kind of work worth doing.

  4. Run a happiness experiment. For a week, after significant events — completing a hard task, helping someone, receiving recognition, or spending an ordinary evening — briefly note how you actually feel. Don't assume. Collect real data on what moves the needle for you and what doesn't.

  5. Plan like a chess player, not a cartographer. Instead of mapping the next 40 years, ask: what is the best next move given what I know right now? Apply for the PhD if research genuinely interests you. Don't attach the decision to a story about how it will eventually make you happy.

  6. Read or explore Carol Dweck's work on mindset — specifically the distinction between outcome-based and effort-based thinking. Her book Mindset is the accessible entry point.

  7. If the insecurity feels deep and persistent, consider working with a therapist. The feeling that nothing you do is ever enough — especially when it's been present since childhood — is something that responds well to direct work, often within months rather than years.
