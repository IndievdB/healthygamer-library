Summary

TL;DR — You probably don't lack motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →; your motivation is blocked. The fix isn't to push harder — it's to clear out the unresolved past that's weighing down your unconscious mind.

Most advice about motivation focuses on generating more of it: better habits, stronger willpower, accountability partners. But if you genuinely want to do something and still can't make yourself do it, more motivation isn't the answer. Something is actively blocking the motivation you already have. Understanding what that something is — and how to remove it — is what actually works.

The Difference Between Lacking Motivation and Blocked Motivation

This distinction matters more than it might seem at first. If you lack motivation, then strategies that boost motivation make sense. But if your motivation is blocked, those same strategies do nothing — you're pouring water into a clogged pipe.

The diagnostic question to ask yourself is: Do I actually want to do this thing? If the honest answer is yes, and you still can't move, you don't have a motivation problem. You have a blockage problem. The goal shifts from generating motivation to freeing it.

This might sound abstract, but the psychological equivalent is immediately recognizable. Think about what happens when your to-do list gets out of control:

  • One task to complete → you feel pulled toward it, motivated to act
  • Five tasks → you feel stretched, distracted, slower
  • Twenty tasks → you become paralyzed and can't start any of them

Anyone who has fallen seriously behind at school or work knows this feeling viscerally. It's not that you stopped caring. It's that the sheer accumulation of what needs to be done has frozen you in place. That's not a motivation failure — that's cognitive and psychological overload.

How Your Unconscious Mind Carries the Weight

Here's the mechanism. Your unconscious mind is constantly keeping track of your incomplete tasks. It's why you'll be playing a game or watching something and a thought suddenly surfaces: oh, I need to apologize to that person or I haven't worked on that thing in weeks. Your unconscious is doing that work in the background, all the time.

The problem is that this tracking has a cost. The more your unconscious is managing — the longer and more tangled the list of unresolved things — the less capacity it has to support you when your conscious mind decides to act.

So you sit down and tell yourself: okay, time to work on this. Your conscious mind is on board. But the rest of your being is somewhere else entirely, overwhelmed and occupied. That's the mismatch. Your conscious intention and your unconscious bandwidth are out of sync, and motivation gets caught in the gap between them.

"It's not about finding motivation — it's about unblocking your motivation."

The AA Evidence: Making Amends Actually Works

The most compelling real-world demonstration of this principle comes from Alcoholics Anonymous. An alcoholic's conscious mind often desperately wants to stop drinking. The motivation is clearly there. And yet the behavior persists. That's blocked motivation in its starkest form — not a lack of desire, but a profound inability to act on it.

One of AA's 12 steps is making amends: going back through your past, identifying everyone you've wronged, and doing your best to repair those relationships and right those wrongs. On the surface, this seems unrelated to the physical act of not drinking. Why would apologizing to someone from years ago affect whether you pick up a bottle tomorrow?

But clinically, something remarkable tends to happen. As people work through this step — even imperfectly, even with relapses along the way — their conscious mind begins to gain real traction. People who couldn't get through a morning without a drink find themselves actually able to choose differently. The amends work and the sobriety aren't just correlated; one enables the other.

The explanation is the same principle: the unresolved wrongs, the guilt, the fractured relationships — all of it was living in the unconscious, consuming bandwidth, blocking the motivation that was already there. Clearing that out, piece by piece, frees the person to actually act on what they consciously want.

The Counterintuitive Fix: Look Backward First

If you're stuck right now, the instinct is to focus forward — push harder, try a new system, set a better goal. But the actual leverage is often behind you.

The things that tend to block motivation are unresolved: - Relationships you've let go sour without acknowledgment - Wrongs you've done that you never addressed - Promises to yourself or others that you broke and moved past without closure - Old goals and intentions you quietly abandoned but never consciously let go of

Each of these is an open loop in your unconscious. They're not dramatic or even necessarily serious — but they accumulate. And the accumulation is what creates the paralysis.

The work of unblocking, then, is going back and closing those loops: apologizing where apology is owed, making right what can be made right, and consciously releasing what can't. This isn't about guilt or punishment. It's about freeing up cognitive and psychological space so your unconscious can actually support the direction your conscious mind wants to go.

"The more you settle your past, the more you unburden your unconscious mind, the freer you're going to feel."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking paralysis for laziness or low willpower. When you can't act on something you genuinely want, the temptation is to blame your character. That framing is both inaccurate and counterproductive — it adds more burden instead of removing it.

Trying to push through the block. This is the most common response and the least effective one. If motivation is blocked, adding force doesn't unblock it. You end up exhausted without having moved anywhere.

Chasing motivation-boosting strategies when the real problem is blockage. Hype videos, productivity hacks, and motivational content feel like they should help. Occasionally they produce a short burst. But if the underlying unconscious burden is still there, the boost dissipates quickly.

Expecting the unblocking to be linear or immediate. The AA example is instructive here: people relapse while making amends. That's not failure — it's still the process working. The direction matters more than the pace.

Confusing "making amends" with "dredging up pain." This isn't about ruminating on the past or reopening wounds for their own sake. It's specifically about closing open loops — taking concrete action to resolve things that your unconscious is still holding open.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the diagnostic. For whatever you're stuck on, ask yourself honestly: Do I actually want this? If yes, you don't lack motivation — you're blocked. That reframe alone changes what you do next.

  2. Write an "open loops" inventory. Sit down and list every unresolved thing you're aware of — tasks left incomplete, relationships with lingering tension, apologies never given, commitments dropped without acknowledgment. Don't try to fix them yet; just get them out of your head and onto paper.

  3. Pick one thing from your past and actually address it. Choose a specific person you need to apologize to, or a specific wrong you can begin to make right. Do it this week. Notice whether anything shifts in how much energy you feel toward your current goals.

  4. Practice conscious release for things you can't fix. Some open loops can't be resolved through action — the person is gone, the situation is over. For those, write down what happened, acknowledge it explicitly, and make a deliberate decision to release it. The unconscious responds to intentional closure even when external resolution isn't possible.

  5. When you feel stuck, resist the urge to push forward first. Instead, ask: what from my past might be blocking this? Spend 15–20 minutes with that question before returning to the task itself.
