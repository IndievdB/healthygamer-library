Summary

TL;DR — Your stress system was built for physical emergencies, not emails. To actually turn it off, you have to trick your body into thinking it just survived one.

Modern life is full of stress, but almost none of it is physical. That mismatch is the root of a lot of chronic anxiety, avoidance, and procrastination. Understanding why your body's stress response works the way it does — and how to use its built-in off switch — gives you a surprisingly direct way to break the cycle.

Your Stress System Is Running the Wrong Software

The human stress response evolved for short-term, life-or-death situations. Think being chased by a predator in the wild. The system is elegant and effective — for that context.

The problem is that today's stressors don't look like that at all. Worrying about rent, job security, climate change, or what to say in a difficult email all activate the same stress system, but they're chronic and long-term. Your body has only one stress system, and it was never built for this kind of sustained, low-grade threat. So it fires anyway, stays fired, and never fully shuts off.

This mismatch leads directly to a familiar cycle:

  1. You feel stressed and overwhelmed.
  2. You engage in avoidance behaviors — distraction, scrolling, anything that provides temporary relief.
  3. The actual problem doesn't go away; it gets bigger.
  4. The pile of unresolved stress grows until it crosses a threshold where the discomfort of not acting finally outweighs the discomfort of acting.
  5. You scramble at the last second, do a rushed and mediocre job, beat yourself up, and repeat.

The Built-In Off Switch You're Not Using

Here's what happens in the body during a real physical threat — say, sprinting away from a predator. The brain signals a cascade: CRH → ACTH → cortisol, plus adrenaline. Your heart rate spikes. Blood pressure rises. You're in full fight-or-flight.

But the body doesn't just have an accelerator. It has a brake.

There are pressure sensors in your neck called carotid baroreceptors. When blood pressure surges high enough — as it does during intense physical exertion — these sensors trigger a counter-response: heart rate slows, blood pressure drops, cortisol production decreases. At the same time, intense physical effort releases endorphins, which directly calm the nervous system and activate the parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" side).

This is why a really hard run leaves you wanting to do absolutely nothing afterward. You're not just physically tired — your body has actively shut down the stress response.

"Any time my body gets stressed, it automatically starts the circuitry to de-stress — endorphins, parasympathetic nervous system activation."

The critical insight: modern stress activates the stress response without activating its off switch. When you're anxious about an email, your cortisol and CRH rise, but you're not running, your heart rate doesn't spike enough to trigger the baroreceptors, and you don't release meaningful endorphins. The brake never gets applied. So the stress just... sits there.

Even completing a mental task — finally sending the email, finishing the report — doesn't fully fix it. You might feel some relief, but you're not recruiting the endorphin system or the parasympathetic nervous system. You haven't actually shut off CRH production. Within a short time, the craving for distraction and avoidance creeps back.

The Actual Technique: Vigorous Exercise as a Stress Reset

The practical solution follows directly from the biology: if your stress system has a physical off switch, use it deliberately.

When you're feeling overwhelmed or stressed, do the most vigorous exercise you can manage for as short a time as you can tolerate. The key word is vigorous.

"Try to run as if a monster is chasing you and your life depends on it."

A 90-second all-out sprint, for example. If you can't make it to 91 seconds because you're completely spent, you're doing it right.

Intensity matters far more than duration:

  • Running at 50% of your capacity — does not meaningfully shut off the stress system.
  • Running at 70% of your capacity — shuts it off more.
  • Running at 90–100% of your capacity — recruits the greatest shutdown of the stress response.

The logic is circular in the best way: the harder you push, the more your body perceives physical stress, the harder it compensates once the effort stops — releasing more endorphins, more strongly activating the parasympathetic nervous system, and more effectively halting CRH production.

There's also an unexpected upside for people who aren't in good shape: the less fit you are, the greater the stress reduction you'll experience from a given bout of intense effort. Your body has to compensate harder for the same relative strain. You don't need to be an athlete for this to work; you just need to go hard relative to what you're capable of.

What Happens After You Do This

Once CRH production drops, something important shifts. The craving for avoidance and distraction — the pull toward scrolling, procrastinating, finding anything to do except the hard thing — actually decreases. Those cravings aren't purely a willpower failure. They're partly driven by elevated CRH, which your body is using as an internal signal to seek relief. Shut off the CRH, and the craving quiets.

"Once we shut off CRF production, our desire for cravings, distraction, and avoidance actually decreases — which then makes it easier to actually do the task that was stressing you out in the first place."

This creates a genuinely useful sequence: intense physical effort → stress system shuts down → avoidance cravings drop → you can actually engage with the problem → you make progress → your overall stress load gets smaller.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing moderate exercise with the technique. A light walk or a casual jog won't do what's described here. The intensity threshold matters. If you finish feeling pleasantly warm rather than completely spent, you haven't pushed hard enough to trigger the full physiological response.

Thinking completing the task is enough. Finishing a stressful project provides some relief, but it doesn't fully reset the stress system. Without the physical component, CRH stays elevated and the pull toward avoidance rebounds quickly. The technique isn't a reward for finishing something — it's a tool to make starting possible.

Treating this as a substitute for addressing the underlying problems. Vigorous exercise reduces the physiological noise of stress so you can think and act more clearly. It doesn't make the rent due date disappear. The goal is to break the avoidance cycle so you can actually engage with problems rather than continuing to pile them up.

Waiting until you're calm to start. The whole point is to do this when you're stressed, not as a general wellness habit (though that has its own benefits). The intervention is most useful right in the moment when you notice yourself avoiding, spiraling, or feeling too overwhelmed to start.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Next time you feel the urge to avoid a task, go sprint first. Don't try to push through the avoidance with willpower. Instead, go outside (or find any open space) and run as hard as you possibly can for 60–90 seconds. Recover, then return to the task.

  2. Calibrate your intensity honestly. Ask yourself: "Am I running like something is chasing me, or am I jogging comfortably?" If it's the latter, push harder. The goal is to reach a point where continuing feels nearly impossible.

  3. Notice the shift in your avoidance cravings afterward. After intense exercise, pay attention to whether the pull toward distraction feels weaker. This isn't just anecdotal — it's the endorphin system doing its job. Recognizing it helps you trust the process.

  4. Use this as an on-demand tool, not just a scheduled habit. Keep this technique available as a response to acute stress moments — before opening a difficult email, before a hard conversation, when a task feels impossibly daunting.

  5. Track how the stress cycle plays out for you. For one week, notice when you enter the avoidance loop: what triggered it, how long you avoided, what finally got you to act. Understanding your personal version of the cycle makes it easier to intervene earlier.
