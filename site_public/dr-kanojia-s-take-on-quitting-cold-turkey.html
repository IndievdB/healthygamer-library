Summary

TL;DR — Quitting cold turkey isn't wrong, but it's also not the whole answer. The real goal isn't white-knuckling your way through abstinence — it's building a life that naturally pulls you away from games.

Video game addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → gets treated like alcohol addiction, but the two are fundamentally different problems that probably need different solutions. The cold-turkey playbook was written for substance use disorders, and applying it wholesale to behavioral addictions misses something important: games are actually doing something for you, and you can't just yank that away without consequences.

Why Video Games Aren't Like Alcohol

The dominant framework for beating any addiction comes from substance use disorder treatment — the world of alcoholism, heroin, cocaine. From that tradition comes the idea that abstinence is the only way: you quit, you stay quit, you never touch it again.

That model makes sense for substances. Alcohol is a chemical that physically hijacks your brain's reward system, and for many people, one drink genuinely does restart the cycle. But behavioral addictions — gaming, social media, compulsive Netflix — work differently. They're not foreign chemicals; they're activities that fulfill real psychological needs. The brain circuitry involved overlaps with substance addiction, but the nature of the problem isn't identical.

One piece of evidence worth sitting with: the number of people who have overcome a gaming addiction and gone on to play games in a healthy, non-addictive way is far larger than the number of alcoholics who can return to controlled drinking. That asymmetry matters. It means lifelong abstinence from gaming is not a universal requirement the way it often is for alcohol.

That said — a gaming cleanse of a couple of weeks can genuinely help. The Trap of "I'm Going to Maintain It"

Here is where a lot of people fail, and it has nothing to do with willpower or character.

Someone decides to quit gaming. They feel motivated, committed, fired up. They say: "This time is different. I'm going to maintain it. I'm going to stay off games for a year."

That framing is the problem.

"You can't be sober for a year. All you can do is be sober for a day. And if you do that for 365 days in a row, you will be sober for a year."

"Maintaining abstinence" is not a thing you can do. It's a phantom goal. The moment you frame it that way, you've created a mountain you have to carry every single morning. Every day is another day of fighting yourself with willpower. And at some point — after a hard week, a bad breakup, a brutal work stretch — you'll crack. Not because you're weak. Because you were relying on a resource (willpower) that has a ceiling.

The only real unit of success is the day in front of you. Am I going to play today? That's the only question available to you.

Games Are Doing Something for You — Find Out What

This is the most important piece. Before you try to take gaming away from yourself, ask honestly: what is this game giving me?

Games reliably provide things that are genuinely hard to get elsewhere:

  • ConfidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → and a sense of competence — you're ranked, respected, recognized
  • CommunityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → and belonging — your friends are in there; your social life lives there
  • Intellectual challenge — complex strategy, fast decision-making, mastery
  • Escape from an environment that feels threatening or painful

Consider someone who's autistic, gets bullied at school, and has his only real friendships online through gaming. Telling that person to "just stop playing" doesn't remove an addiction — it removes his social world and leaves him isolated. The game wasn't the problem; it was filling a vacuum the rest of his life couldn't.

If you take gaming away without addressing what it was providing, you don't solve the addiction. You just leave yourself with a hole — less confidence, less community, fewer sources of joy — and the game will be right there waiting when you're at your lowest.

Build a Life That Pulls You Away

The alternative to willpower-based abstinence is this: build a life worth living.

"You don't have to quit gaming. You just need to build a life that pulls you away from the game."

This reframes the whole project. Instead of asking "how do I resist games?" you ask "what would make real life more compelling?"

That means, concretely: - Work or creative projects that challenge you intellectually - Relationships where people want to see you and you want to see them - Physical health that gives you reason to leave your space - Sources of confidence and competence that aren't locked inside a game

When those things exist in your life, games stop being a need and become an option. You can still enjoy a game without being trapped by it. The difference is that you're choosing to play, not medicating a lack.

There's a subreddit called r/outside that treats the real world like an RPG — side quests, skill trees, NPC interactions. It's a useful mental reframe: the real world has everything a good game has, but it's your life. The goal is to engage with it that way.

People who have genuinely and sustainably quit alcohol often describe their sobriety not as a battle they fight every day, but as a preference — my life is actually better this way; I don't want to go back to waking up hungover. That's not willpower. That's what you're building toward with gaming too.

Common Pitfalls

Treating gaming like alcoholism. The playbook isn't identical. You can likely return to gaming in a healthy way eventually. Assuming you must quit forever — when there's no good reason to believe that about your specific situation — sets up an unnecessarily brutal and possibly unnecessary fight.

Confusing motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → with maintenance. The initial rush of commitment feels like fuel. It isn't a plan. "I've made up my mind this time" is not a strategy — it just shifts the burden onto future-you during a hard moment you can't currently imagine.

Removing games without replacing what they gave you. If your confidence, friendships, and sense of achievement all live inside a game, and you take the game away, you haven't freed yourself. You've just emptied yourself out. The vacuum almost always gets filled by the same thing.

Framing success as "maintaining abstinence." This makes every day a potential failure. It also makes you think about games constantly. The better frame is: what am I building today?

Thinking the goal is to conquer gaming. If you wake up every day fighting the urge to play, you haven't solved the problem — you're just managing it with effort indefinitely. The goal is to get to a place where you don't need games, not a place where you're always resisting them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do a two-week gaming cleanse. Not as a permanent commitment — just a reset. Give your dopamine system time to recalibrate so other activities start feeling rewarding again. Notice what you feel during that time. What's missing? What does gaming usually mask or fill?

  2. Ask what your game is doing for you. Write it down honestly. Confidence? Social connection? Intellectual stimulation? Escape from something painful? This isn't an excuse to keep playing — it's a map of what your actual life needs more of.

  3. Work on the deficit, not the symptom. For each thing gaming provides you, identify one concrete way to get it elsewhere. If it's social, find a group activity or reach out to someone. If it's confidence, find a skill or domain to develop. If it's intellectual challenge, find work or projects that push you.

  4. Shrink the goal to today. If you're trying to quit or cut back, don't think about next month or next year. Ask only: am I going to play today? That's the actual unit. String enough of those days together and the bigger goal takes care of itself.

  5. Measure the right thing. Instead of tracking "days since I last played," track what you're building — relationships deepened, skills practiced, experiences had. You're not trying to resist a game; you're trying to construct something worth more than a game.
