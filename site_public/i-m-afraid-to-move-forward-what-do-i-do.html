Summary

TL;DR — Fear of moving forward is often not a signal that you're on the wrong path; it's a signal that the stakes are real. The more important question is whether you're running toward something you care about or away from feelings you're afraid to have.

Chris is 24, has spent five or six years in marketing that he detests, and has just enrolled in a psychology program — a decision he made in under a week. He's afraid he won't be good enough as a therapist, guilty about moving back in with his parents, and ashamed of what his friends and family think. The conversation that follows unpacks why those feelings make complete sense, and why letting them drive your decisions is the real danger.

Fear That Makes Sense vs. Fear That Lies to You

When you've just jumped off a cliff — given up a career, moved backward financially, traded certainty for meaning — fear is the appropriate response. That's not a red flag. That's just what reality feels like when the stakes are high.

There's a useful distinction between fear that's telling you something is wrong and fear that's developmentally appropriate — the kind of anxiety that shows up naturally at any major life transition. Someone who has never been in a serious relationship is going to be afraid of one. That doesn't mean they shouldn't pursue it. It means they're a human being encountering something genuinely uncertain for the first time.

Chris's fear fits this second category. He hasn't finished his education. He's comparing himself to a finished product he hasn't built yet. Worrying that you can't bake a cake when you've only been taught to turn on the oven isn't evidence of failure — it's evidence that you haven't been through culinary school yet.

"You're making an assessment, which is kind of fair because it's your experience, but it's like you haven't been trained in this stuff. You've really only learned step one."

What a Therapist Actually Does (And Why "I'm Bad at Listening" Is the Wrong Worry)

Chris had convinced himself that 80% of a therapist's job is listening — and that because he struggled to help strangers online, he might be constitutionally unfit for the profession. Both of those assumptions deserve scrutiny.

Struggling to help people on the internet has very little in common with doing therapy. Online conversations lack any longitudinal relationship. The person on the other side may need things that listening alone can't provide. The grab-bag nature of cold internet support is not a controlled test of clinical skill.

More importantly, listening is what therapy looks like from the outside. What's actually happening inside a trained therapist's mind is thinking — diagnostic assessment, pattern recognition, hypothesis testing. The reason a therapist can be quiet and still be working harder than anyone else in the room is because their training taught them what to do with what they're hearing.

"Even as I'm talking to you, I'm listening to what you're saying — but actually, I'd say most of our job is thinking."

If you love thinking, and you love connecting with people, that's not a liability. That's the foundation the formal education is designed to build on.

The Real Trap: Making Decisions to Avoid Negative Feelings

Here's the subtler and more important problem. Chris didn't stumble into marketing randomly. He landed there after a streak of painful failures — 50 French university applications, eight months of effort, then having to go home empty-handed. Marketing let him stand in a room with startup CEOs and feel like he wasn't a failure. That feeling was real. The problem is that avoiding the feeling of failure is not the same thing as building a good life.

When you're afraid of feeling shame, you make decisions that keep shame away. You stay at the party where you look successful. You avoid the party where you might feel small. You pick the career that makes you look impressive at family gatherings. And every time it works — you feel less bad — it trains you to keep doing it.

The destination of that strategy is exactly where Chris ended up: six years in a profession he ethically detests, having performed well enough to keep the shame at bay, but not having moved a single inch toward what actually matters to him.

"We live life on autopilot, and the destination is the avoidance of negative emotion at all costs. It's how we pick careers, how we pick relationships, why we use drugs."

This isn't a character flaw — it's what happens when no one ever sat you down and asked why you're making the choices you're making.

The Ferrari vs. the '80s Mazda

The financial argument against becoming a therapist in Indonesia is real. Less money, less social prestige, less stability, more uncertainty. Compared to staying in marketing and running startups, it objectively looks worse on paper.

But the people who chose the Ferrari and hated the job don't look back glad they stayed. Uniformly, what comes out is: I wish I'd made this change ten or fifteen years ago. The Ferrari is real. It just doesn't do anything.

Meanwhile, when you care deeply about work that society undervalues, something counterintuitive tends to happen: you outperform. You show up differently. You get good in a way that people who are just there for the money don't. A psychologist who genuinely cares about mental health in a country that still stigmatizes it isn't just making a noble sacrifice — they're planting themselves in exactly the place where the need is greatest and the competition least interested.

"The same reason it's a risky business to be a psychologist in Indonesia is also exactly why it matters."

The Parents Problem (And What's Really Going On)

The guilt about burdening his parents is the hardest part of the day-to-day for Chris. He asks them if they're disappointed. They say no. He doesn't believe them.

Here's the structural problem: in a universe where the parents are disappointed and a universe where they aren't, they're going to say exactly the same thing. "No, no, it's fine." So asking the question and getting the answer doesn't actually resolve anything.

The reason you can't be reassured by people telling you they're not disappointed in you is that you're disappointed in yourself. External reassurance can't fix an internal verdict. You can be told you're not ugly and still not believe it, because the judgment isn't coming from outside.

The more productive conversation isn't "are you disappointed?" — it's "why aren't you disappointed?" Ask your parents what they actually see when they look at your life. What do they think your struggles are? Do they think you're doing well or poorly, and what's their reasoning? That conversation gives you something real to work with instead of a reassurance you'll immediately dismiss.

And underneath all of it: the goal isn't to never disappoint your parents. The goal is to be able to disappoint them — and be okay with it. Because no one lives a full life without occasionally letting down the people they love. Learning to carry that without it collapsing you is part of becoming an adult.

Your Suffering Is Career Capital

The shame, the failures, the years in the wrong profession, the feeling of being seven years behind 17-year-olds in a university lecture hall — none of this is waste. It's raw material.

Ten years from now, a 24-year-old is going to sit across from you and say they hate their job but feel too guilty to leave. They'll feel like a failure. They'll worry they're disappointing their parents. And you will be able to help that person in a way that someone who went straight from school into a dream career cannot, because you will know exactly what that feels like from the inside.

"It's not just that you care about other people — it's that you know what it is to feel shame, you know what it is to feel like a failure. And you can't see this now, but I sort of can."

Being older than your classmates means you've already failed at things they're still terrified of. Their anxiety about getting a B is not your anxiety. You've been in rooms that didn't work out and you're still here. That's not a disadvantage. That's a head start in a different direction.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking "I feel bad about this choice" for "this is the wrong choice." Fear and shame are not accurate compasses for whether you're on the right path. Sometimes the right path feels terrible, especially at the beginning.
  • Using early, untrained attempts as evidence of future incapacity. Struggling to help strangers online before you've finished your education is not a data point about your career ceiling.
  • Asking for reassurance from others when the real issue is self-judgment. If you don't believe you're okay, no one can tell you enough times to make it stick. The work has to happen internally.
  • Assuming "stable and lucrative" means "safe." A global pandemic, a bad boss, an ethical scandal at the startup you're working for — certainty is often more fragile than it appears.
  • Confusing the absence of negative emotion with being on the right track. Feeling proud in a room full of CEOs while doing work you detest is not success. It's successful avoidance.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify what emotion you're avoiding. When you made a major life decision in the past, ask: what feeling was I trying not to feel? Shame, failure, embarrassment? Name it specifically. This is the first step to stopping it from running your life on autopilot.

  2. Change the conversation with your parents. Instead of "are you disappointed in me?" (which has no useful answer), try: "Can you help me understand why you aren't disappointed? What do you see when you look at what I'm doing?" The goal is genuine information, not reassurance.

  3. Stop using your untrained performance as a benchmark. If you're in the early stages of education or skill-building, you are not yet allowed to conclude that you'll be bad at the thing. Treat your current ability as baseline data, not ceiling data.

  4. Write down what you actually value — not what pays well or looks good. Then look at your last three major decisions and ask honestly: was this chosen for the value, or to avoid feeling a certain way? The gap between the two lists tells you where you've been living on autopilot.

  5. Practice being okay with disappointing someone. Start small. Let someone be mildly let down — miss a social event, deliver a "no" when people expect a "yes" — and sit with the discomfort afterward instead of immediately fixing it. You're building tolerance for a feeling that currently has veto power over your choices.

  6. Give your suffering some credit. Make a list of hard things you've been through that you didn't expect to survive. Note what you now understand because of those experiences that you couldn't have learned any other way. This isn't toxic positivity — it's taking inventory of actual assets you've built.
