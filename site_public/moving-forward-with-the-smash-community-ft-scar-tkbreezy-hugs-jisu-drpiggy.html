What Grooming Actually Looks Like

This is where the conversation becomes most practically valuable. Both Jisu and DrPiggy described their own experiences in enough detail to make the pattern concrete — because, as the discussion notes, you can't recognize something you've never been shown.

Grooming is not a sudden event. It is the slow, systematic erosion of a target's boundaries by someone who almost never crosses a line so clearly that it triggers alarm. The key features:

  • Power imbalance as the foundation. Almost every case involved a gap in age, fame, social capital, or material resources. The person doing the grooming has something the target lacks — status, connections, a place to live, a path into the communityThe Sanskrit word for community, sangha refers to the group of people who support your spiritual or meditative practice — and according to the yogic tradition, it is considered vital to sustaining that practice at all.Full definition →.
  • Isolation. The groomer works to cut the target off from other relationships. In Jisu's case, this was explicit: "He told me behind everyone's back — 'I don't like these people, don't get close to them.'" Without anyone else to reality-check against, the target becomes entirely dependent on the groomer's framing.
  • Gradual normalization of inappropriate content. DrPiggy described being groomed at 14 through AIM messages that incrementally moved from normal conversation toward sexual content — each step small enough to feel unremarkable, cumulatively moving far past any reasonable boundary.
  • The velvet iron. The groomer expresses genuine-feeling warmth and care, but certain things are non-negotiable. The caring feels real in the moment. The control is what's actually real.
  • Secrecy framed as specialness. "This is our thing. Other people wouldn't understand."

"Grooming is the act of taking that fear away — trying to make somebody safe — until their boundaries are gone. And then you can have sex with them if you want, because they think: 'I'm special, we have a special relationship, this person doesn't want to hurt me.'"

A particularly important point: the target's trust is not naivety, it's a manufactured outcome. The adult engineered it deliberately. None of the moral weight falls on the child or the less-powerful person.

DrPiggy also noted that smart predators know exactly where the legal line is. They don't cross it carelessly — they stay just inside it and choose victims they calculate won't be believed or won't speak out. This is why so many of these cases went years without consequence.

Why Victims Stay Silent — and Why They Eventually Speak

Several factors keep people from coming forward:

  1. No proof the legal system recognizes. Jisu had years of chat logs, photos, and willing witnesses. A lawyer still recommended against pursuing the case. The law is designed to be reactive, not preventative, and the standards of proof in sexual violence cases rarely match the reality of how that evidence accumulates.
  2. Fear of not being believed. DrPiggy described trying to raise concerns about specific individuals over years — and being dismissed until multiple men with larger platforms backed her up.
  3. Social ties to the accused. The analogy offered: reducing smoking reduced lung cancer outcomes far more than improving cancer treatment. Structural and cultural prevention beats case-by-case response. That doesn't mean you stop processing cases — it means you also address what's upstream.

Common Pitfalls

"Believe women" means "automatically convict." It doesn't. As DrPiggy says, it means: when you read a story for the first time, don't immediately start looking for reasons to dismiss it. Don't ask if she was flirting, don't demand proof you'd never demand from someone reporting a theft. Give the same default credence you'd give to any person reporting harm.

Waiting for things to be obviously bad before speaking up. They were right. The same principle applies to the current moment.

Thinking it was just a few bad actors. The scale and the time span — stories going back to 2011, 2012 — indicate a cultural and structural problem, not an isolated incident problem. Removing the named individuals doesn't fix the conditions that allowed them to operate.

Believing the apologetic statement. Almost none of the people who issued public responses in July 2020 directly acknowledged what they did. Most deflected, minimized, or framed it as defending their career. The people who genuinely change — as TKbreezy puts it — do it because they recognize the error themselves, not because their image is at stake.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Learn to recognize the grooming pattern. Power imbalance plus isolation plus gradual boundary erosion plus secrecy. If someone important to you is insisting you not get close to others, that's a signal worth examining — not dismissing because you trust them.

  2. Name it in your friend group, privately. If you hear someone use dehumanizing language — "females," casual use of "rape" to mean a game outcome, slurs — say something. You don't have to make it a lecture. "Hey, don't say that around me" is enough to start.

  3. When someone reports discomfort, your first move is to listen. Not to explain the accused's intentions. Not to ask what she did to invite it. Just: what happened, and what do you need right now?

  4. Ask your local tournament organizer about their code of conduct. Do they have one? Who do attendees go to if something happens? Who is responsible for underage attendees at after-parties? If the answer is "no one," that's the gap.

  5. If you're building a local scene, designate someone. Not the tournament organizer running pools — a separate person whose job is to be the person someone can go to. Make sure everyone knows who that person is before the event starts.

  6. If you notice you're being asked to keep things secret, or that someone important to you keeps steering you away from other friendships, treat that as a reason to talk to someone outside the relationship — not as proof something is wrong, but as a reason to get a second opinion from someone who isn't inside the dynamic.

  7. Amplify, don't explain over. If someone shares an experience of harassment or assault publicly, you don't need to contextualize it or explain it to others. Retweet. Share it. Let their voice carry further.

  8. Follow through. The consistent theme in this conversation is that people have big ideas in the immediate aftermath of a crisis and then the energy dissipates. Whatever small commitment you make — talking to a friend, asking your TO about their policies, supporting someone who speaks out — do it and keep doing it, not just while it's the conversation.
