Diagnostic features — four or more of the following point toward a panic attack rather than general anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →:

  • Racing or irregular heartbeat (palpitations)
  • Sweating, trembling, or shaking
  • Shortness of breath, feeling smothered or choked
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Nausea or GI upset
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Sudden temperature swings (chills or hot flashes)
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Derealization — the world around you feels unreal
  • Depersonalization — feeling like you're watching your life rather than living it
  • Fear of losing control, going crazy, or dying

Panic attacks can strike daily for weeks, then disappear for months, or show up roughly once a week or once a month — the pattern varies widely. What's consistent is that they come on fast (minutes) and can last up to an hour or more, and they feel completely unprompted.

Over time, the unpredictability itself becomes the problem. Because an attack can hit anywhere, many people start quietly reshaping their entire lives to ensure they can escape quickly if one hits — avoiding unfamiliar places, skipping flights, staying close to home. In severe cases this contracts into agoraphobia, where leaving the house stops feeling possible at all.

If you find yourself planning life around the possibility of a panic attack, that pattern is itself a sign worth taking seriously. The condition responds well to treatment, so seeking a proper evaluation is the most useful next step.
