Summary

TL;DR — Self-deprecating humor and relentless positivity can secretly reinforce the belief that you're worthless. Real healing starts with stopping the inner bully, not suppressing the wounded part of yourself.

John — content creator known as ObesetoBeast — grew up poor, with drug-addicted parents, time in foster care, learning disabilities, and obesity from childhood. He lost over 150 pounds and built a large following. By every external measure, things are going well. And yet he experiences recurring episodes of feeling completely worthless, shutting down emotionally, pushing away the people closest to him, and wondering why anyone would follow him or stay with him. The conversation works through how those experiences are connected — and why the tools he's used to survive are also what keep him stuck.

The Three People Inside You

Most people who struggle with self-worth think the problem is the part of them that acts out — the part that shuts down, catastrophizes, or says terrible things about themselves. That instinct is understandable. But it's wrong.

Picture three distinct parts:

  1. The wounded child — the seven-year-old crying over multiplication homework, the teenager who couldn't keep up, the kid who felt like a burden and learned the world was unsafe.
  2. The inner bully — the part that immediately jumps in after an episode and says: "Why are you acting like a child? You've accomplished so much, what's wrong with you? That was pathetic."
  3. The observer — the part that can actually watch the first two happening, the awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → that exists outside of body and mind.

The trap is trying to fix #1 by unleashing #2. Every time John felt worthless and pushed people away, he then beat himself up for feeling worthless. And the positive self-talk — "you shouldn't feel this way, look how far you've come" — is just the bully wearing a cheerful mask. The target of the criticism is still you, and the message is still you're not good enough to feel this way.

"You've accomplished so much — why are you so dumb that you can't see it? You're still calling yourself dumb. It's the same thing, it's just layers and layers of the same complex."

Changing the bully comes before changing the wounded child. Once you stop punishing the kid for existing, you can actually start working with him.

The Echo Problem: Why Logic Doesn't Fix It

When John is in one of these dark episodes, he knows logically that he's not worthless. He even says so. But knowing it doesn't help — in the moment, the thought "I'm worthless" hits with full force and nothing anyone says can touch it.

This isn't irrationality. It's something closer to a flashback. When a traumatic emotional memory gets triggered, the brain doesn't experience it as a memory — it experiences it as the present. A veteran who hears a loud bang doesn't think that reminds me of combat; they're back in the jungle, in their 25-year-old body, with their friend dying in front of them.

The same thing happens with emotional wounds. When John's girlfriend mentions an ex, or someone mocks his typing speed in his Discord, the adult John doesn't register a small slight. The seven-year-old comes out of the basement, and that kid genuinely was worthless by every measure available to him: he couldn't do math, he was overweight, he was poor, other kids made fun of him. The emotion is real and was, at the time, accurate.

"Looking back, it's just not true" — actually, looking at now it's not true. When you look into your past, that kid was absolutely in pain, and that pain made sense.

The solution isn't to argue with the echo. It's to acknowledge it: yes, I felt completely worthless back then, and that made sense given what was happening. Fighting the feeling prolongs it. Accepting that you were that kid — and that you deserved to feel that way — is what allows it to move.

How Self-Deprecation Becomes Self-Destruction

John discovered early that if he made fun of himself first, other kids couldn't use it against him. He became the funny fat kid. He earned the nickname "Cupcake" and got it read at graduation. He even got it tattooed on his calf.

That was a brilliant survival strategy. It also had a cost:

"I felt like I was garbage. Because of how much I would make fun of myself, I started to just believe it — you are a joke, you're worthless."

Self-deprecation as armor works by preemptively agreeing with the cruelest possible judgment. The problem is that your brain doesn't distinguish between a joke and a genuine belief. Say it enough — even as a bit, even as a way to deflect bullying — and the assessment sinks in.

The same dynamic shows up in adult life. When John pushes his girlfriend away during an episode ("just break up with me"), he's doing something psychologically sophisticated: he's testing whether she'll stay anyway. If she does, it provides a moment of security. But it also reinforces the underlying belief that he doesn't deserve her affection — because if he did deserve it, the test wouldn't be necessary. Every time she stays despite his worst behavior, it feels good and quietly confirms the premise that he was something to push past.

Gratitude as a Trap

John is deeply grateful. He says "I'm lucky" about almost everything — that his uncle took him in, that his mom got clean, that he was able to lose the weight. That gratitude is real and worth keeping.

But there's a quiet trap inside it. "Lucky" and "deserve" sit at opposite ends of the same axis. If you arrived at your life through luck, the implication is that you didn't earn it — which slides easily into I don't deserve it. The imposter syndromeA persistent belief that your success is undeserved — that you got lucky, fooled people, or benefited from circumstances — rather than from your own effort or competence.Full definition →, the pushing people away, the feeling worthless despite real accomplishment — all of it traces back to the same root: a deep sense that nothing he has is really his.

"If you don't deserve it, that sort of implies a certain value about you as a human being."

Gratitude and deserving aren't opposites. If you order food at a restaurant, you deserve that food — and you can still be genuinely grateful that it tastes amazing. Reclaiming what you deserve doesn't make you ungrateful. It means you've stopped secretly agreeing with the cruelest version of your own story.

Insecure Attachment and Why Reassurance Doesn't Stick

When children grow up in environments where their basic needs aren't consistently met — and a household with two meth-addicted parents in the first years of life clearly qualifies — the brain learns early that the world is unpredictable and unsafe. One consequence: it becomes very hard for reassurance from other people to land. A securely attached child, when startled, can be calmed within seconds by a parent's return. An insecurely attached child takes much longer to regulate, because at the deepest level, the nervous system learned that comfort isn't reliable. You reach out and nothing comes — or nothing reliable comes — so eventually you stop expecting it.

That's why John can hear his girlfriend say he's worthy and valued, and some part of him simply doesn't believe it. Not because she's being dishonest. Because his nervous system learned that message before it had words, and it runs deeper than anything logic can reach.

The path forward isn't more reassurance. It's slowly, repeatedly experiencing that people can stay — and letting that slowly update the older recording.

The problem: saying "it's fine" doesn't actually release anyone from anything. Forgiveness requires acknowledging what happened. If someone wrongs you and you immediately say "no big deal," there's nothing to forgive — the ledger never opens, so it can never close. The guilt stays buried. The wall stays up.

Real forgiveness sounds more like: Yes, those early years were hard. I wish things had been different. I understand you were struggling. And I forgive you. That's harder and more specific, and it's also the only version that actually works — for both people.

You don't even necessarily need to have that conversation out loud. Processing it internally can be enough to let the walls come down. And parents, being parents, tend to feel the difference anyway.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking the goal is to eliminate the dark episodes entirely. The goal is to change your relationship to them — to stop beating yourself up for having them.
  • Using positivity as bypass. "Look how far I've come" can be another form of self-criticism in disguise, if the subtext is so why are you still like this?
  • Confusing "it's okay" with forgiveness. Protecting people from their guilt by minimizing what happened doesn't heal anything — it just buries it.
  • Believing the solution is insight alone. John is unusually self-aware, and that's gotten him halfway. But understanding a pattern doesn't automatically dissolve it; the nervous system needs different experiences, not just different thoughts.
  • Assuming losing weight (or achieving any external goal) will fix self-esteemSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →. If the root is a belief that you're not worthy of love, no external change touches the root. It helps around the edges, but the wound remains.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the inner bully, not just the episode. When you catch yourself in a dark spiral, the reflex is to focus on the spiral itself. Instead, watch for the moment immediately after, when you start judging yourself for having had it. That's where the real work is.

  2. Try alternate nostril breathingPranayama is the yogic practice of deliberate breath control used to regulate prana — the body's vital energy — and directly influence the nervous system.Full definition → when you feel yourself losing control. Fold down two fingers of your right hand, use your thumb to close your right nostril, breathe in. Block the left nostril with your pinky, breathe out, then in. Switch. Repeat for at least nine full breath cycles. The point isn't that it erases the episode — it's that it gives you a small foothold of control in a moment when everything feels automatic.

  3. Practice saying "I felt worthless, and that made sense" instead of "that wasn't true." When the old recording plays, don't argue with it. Acknowledge it. The kid who felt worthless had reasons. Let him have them.

  4. Stop cutting people off from the chance to help you. If you notice yourself saying "I'll figure it out myself" or "I don't want to be a burden," treat that as a signal to do the opposite — ask for help, or at minimum, let someone in. This is especially relevant with family members who want the chance to show up.

  5. Call your mom more. Not to have a big conversation, not to unload everything — just to let her be a part of your life. Let her help where she can. Give her the thing your siblings can't right now: a kid who's okay, who lets her in.

  6. If you experience panic attacks with a sense of impending doom, see a doctor. That specific symptom cluster is treatable, and you don't have to keep living through it at full intensity.

  7. When you catch yourself testing the people close to you — picking fights, pushing them away to see if they'll stay — try to name it in the moment: I'm doing the thing again. You don't have to stop it immediately. Just watch it run its course with some awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, and notice what happens on the other side.
