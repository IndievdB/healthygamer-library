Summary

TL;DR — Most people who've experienced sexual assault don't know what to expect afterward, and that uncertainty itself becomes a barrier to getting help. This piece walks through the psychological, medical, and legal landscape so you can make informed decisions — or help someone you care about do the same.

Sexual assault is an intensely private experience, which means people rarely get oriented to what comes next. The result is that survivors end up isolated, second-guessing their own reactions, and delaying care that could genuinely help them. What follows is a practical map of the psychological aftermath, what actually happens if you go to an emergency room, and what engaging with law enforcement actually looks like — so that none of it has to feel like stepping into the unknown.

The Psychological Aftermath Is More Variable Than You Think

The first and most important thing to understand: there is no single "correct" reaction to sexual assault.

About one-third of people report almost no significant psychological reaction. They acknowledge that something bad happened, they don't feel it has fundamentally broken them, and they move forward. That's not denial — for some people, that's genuinely their experience. On the other end of the spectrum, people develop serious psychiatric conditions. Both responses are real.

A few specific things worth knowing:

The most common emotion is anger, not fear. Around 59% of people feel primarily angry after an assault. Only about 40% report fear or anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → as their dominant emotion. Media depictions tend to show survivors as quiet, withdrawn, and tearful — that's one valid response, but statistically it's not the most common one. If you feel furious rather than devastated, that's not a sign that something is wrong with you.

Physical symptoms are extremely common and often go unrecognized as related to the assault. Up to half of people will experience somatic — bodily — complaints in the days, weeks, or even months afterward. Persistent nausea, diarrhea, stomach pain, or flare-ups of chronic conditions like autoimmune disorders can all be the body's stress response to traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition →. If you don't have flashbacks but you can't keep food down, the two things might be connected.

Guilt and shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → are neurologically predictable, not personal failures. There's a part of the brain wired for counterfactual thinkingThe brain's habit of mentally replaying past events and imagining alternative outcomes — the "what if" and "if only" loop.Full definition → — it replays negative events and asks "what could I have done differently?" This process exists to help us learn from mistakes. After a sexual assault, that same circuitry fires even when the event wasn't your fault. Your brain doesn't automatically know the difference. This is why telling someone "it's not your fault" over and over can paradoxically make them feel more isolated — their brain keeps insisting otherwise, and now they feel like the only person in the room who thinks that way.

DissociationA protective fragmentation of the self in which the brain severs communication between its emotional and analytical hemispheres — numbing feeling so that overwhelming experience becomes survivable.Full definition → is a common and likely protective response. During or after an assault, some people feel disconnected from their own body — like they're watching events from the outside. This is not a sign of weakness or instability; it appears to be the nervous system reducing the intensity of a traumatic experience in real time.

Sexual difficulties afterward are normal and usually temporary. Reduced interest in sex, difficulty with arousal, changes in physical sexual response, and difficulty achieving orgasm are all common after sexual assault. The majority of research suggests these difficulties tend to resolve within about a year, often on their own. For some people they persist longer, and in those cases therapy is effective. The key thing: experiencing these difficulties years later does not mean you're permanently damaged — it means you may need support you haven't gotten yet.

Suicidal ideation is a recognized, relatively common response. The combination of shame, pain, numbness, and guilt can become overwhelming. If those thoughts show up, that is a reason to seek medical care immediately — not a sign that something uniquely terrible is happening to you.

What Actually Happens If You Go to the Emergency Room

The single most recommended step after a sexual assault is to go to an emergency room. Here's the concrete reality of what that looks like.

You walk in. A triage nurse greets you. You say: "I was sexually assaulted." That's it. They take it from there.

The goals of the medical system, as reflected in guidelines from organizations like the WHO and American emergency medicine associations, are:

  1. Make you feel safe
  2. Empower you — meaning you make the decisions about what happens next
  3. Prevent medical consequences — treating injuries, providing medication to prevent STIs, offering post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) for HIV (which works well when started promptly), and providing emergency contraception if relevant

"The key thing to remember is that ultimately you're the boss, and we're going to do or not do whatever you want."

The medical team will likely involve a SANE nurse — a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner — a specially trained clinician who handles the examination. The SANE exam documents injuries, collects evidence (DNA swabs, physical evidence), and preserves that information.

Here is the part most people don't know: you can request that the exam be anonymous. Your name does not have to be attached to the evidence collected. A unique identifier is used instead. If you decide months or years later that you want to pursue legal action, that evidence can still be retrieved. Consenting to a SANE exam does not mean anyone gets arrested. It does not mean you're going to court. It simply preserves your options.

The SANE team can also connect you with follow-up resources — counseling, advocacy, medical check-ins — so you're not leaving the ER with no support structure.

What Engaging With Law Enforcement Actually Looks Like

You can report to police immediately after an assault, or days, weeks, or even years later. You can walk into a police station and simply say you experienced a sexual assault.

Many police departments have a SART (Sexual Assault Response Team) — officers with specialized training in working with survivors. In some settings, the SART and the SANE teams are coordinated, meaning medical and law enforcement support happen together.

A few things the mind commonly says that can be dismissed:

  • "I don't have enough evidence." — That is not your problem to solve. Evidence assessment is the job of law enforcement. Go in and let them determine what's usable.
  • "It wasn't serious enough." — There is no severity threshold required to report.
  • "It was someone I know, or someone I was in a relationship with." — Two-thirds of assault perpetrators are known to the victim. This does not invalidate anything.
  • "Nobody will believe me." — Trained officers are specifically prepared for these dynamics.

"You shouldn't not go to the police just because you don't think there's enough evidence. Your job is to go to the police if you feel comfortable and let them worry about it."

You are in control of the pace. You can report, give a statement, and then ask to take two weeks before deciding whether to press charges. The system, ideally, is designed to hold space for your decision-making, not railroad you into a courtroom.

Common Pitfalls

Showering immediately after an assault. This is completely understandable — many people describe feeling an intense need to feel clean. But it significantly reduces your legal options by eliminating physical evidence. If there's any chance you might want those options later, it's worth delaying.

Waiting too long to seek care because of uncertainty. The longer untreated PTSD or depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → goes unaddressed, the harder life becomes — work suffers, relationships erode, sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → deteriorates. Getting into support early makes a real difference.

Using substances to cope. There is some evidence that using substances after trauma to numb out worsens long-term outcomes, even if it provides short-term relief.

Forcing detailed recounting of the assault. Contrary to what feels intuitive, walking someone through exactly what happened in detail can re-traumatize them. Some amount of healthy distance from the event in the immediate aftermath appears to be protective.

As a friend, imposing your value system. If someone you care about was assaulted and doesn't want to report it, that is their choice — they are the one who has to go through the process. You can share information, encourage medical care, gently note that reporting sooner preserves options — but the pace is theirs to set.

Assuming persistent symptoms mean permanent damage. Sexual difficulties, avoidance, and mood changes that persist beyond a year are not evidence that someone is broken. They are evidence that someone needs (and deserves) support they haven't received yet.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Go to the emergency room, even if you're unsure. You don't have to have made any decisions about reporting or prosecution. Just go, say what happened, and let the medical team walk you through the options. The SANE exam can be kept anonymous. Nothing is locked in by showing up.

  2. Do not shower before seeking medical care if there is any possibility you may want legal options later. Make that decision consciously rather than on autopilot.

  3. If you're supporting someone else, ask first. Don't assume they need you to fix things or push them toward action. Start with: "How can I help?" Then gently orient them to the options — medical care, the anonymous SANE exam, the fact that nothing has to happen right away — and let them lead.

  4. Monitor for delayed physical symptoms. If you or someone you know experiences persistent GI issues, sleep disruption, or chronic fatigue weeks after a traumatic event, consider whether those could be somatic stress responses rather than unrelated health problems.

  5. Don't wait for the "right" level of distress to seek therapy. If you're having trouble with intimacy, relationships, mood, or sleep — even years after an assault — those are appropriate reasons to see a therapist. EMDR and other trauma-focused therapies have solid evidence behind them.

  6. If you're having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, treat that as a medical emergency. Go to the ER or call emergency services. These thoughts are a recognized and relatively common response to assault; they're also treatable.
