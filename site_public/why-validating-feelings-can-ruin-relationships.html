Summary

TL;DR — "Holding space" isn't about validating feelings or solving problems. It's about tolerating someone else's discomfort long enough to let them process it themselves.

When someone comes to you upset, most people do one of two things: jump in to fix the problem, or rush to validate feelings with lines like "that must be so hard." Both responses feel caring, but they're actually driven by something more self-serving — the need to make your own discomfort go away. Holding space is the third option, and it's harder than either of the first two, but it's what people actually need most of the time.

The Real Reason You Fix or Validate

There's an invisible wire running between you and the people you care about. When someone near you feels bad, some of that feeling transfers to you — call it the emotional umbilical cord. This is why you cry at fictional characters in movies even though you know they're not real. It's an automatic, biological phenomenon.

The moment that transferred discomfort lands in you, something kicks in to get rid of it. Two common escape routes:

  1. Fix the problem. If you solve it, they stop feeling bad, the transfer stops, and you feel better.
  2. Validate the feelings. "Oh my god, that's so unfair, you deserve to feel angry." They feel heard, they calm down, the transfer stops, and again — you feel better.

Notice what both strategies have in common: they're both about ending your discomfort, not theirs. The validation especially can become a kind of desperate move — therapize them fast enough that the emotional dumping stops. There's sometimes even a performative quality to it, like you're rushing to check a box so you can be done.

"There is the validating their feelings in order to get them to stop. Like, shut the f--- up, bro. Let me just therapize you so that you feel better."

This isn't to say validation is always wrong — it has a real role in therapy and sometimes in friendship. But when it's reflexive, or when the goal is really to make the other person feel better quickly, it can feel hollow. People often sense it. They feel managed rather than met. The 10-year-old daughter in the example above couldn't even stay mad properly because her dad psychiatrized the anger away before she was ready to let go of it. And that itself was frustrating.

What Holding Space Actually Means

Holding space is intentional inaction. This is the phrase that unlocks the concept, because "doing nothing" sounds passive, even lazy. But intentional inaction is different. A hunter sitting quietly in a blind, waiting for something to happen, isn't doing nothing — they're choosing not to act yet, with full attention. That's the texture of holding space.

The most useful image: imagine you're holding an open bag, and you're letting the other person put whatever they want into it. You're present. You're engaged. You're just not directing anything. They decide what goes in, and when, and in what order. Your job is to hold the bag without dropping it, without taking over, without trying to rearrange what's inside.

This means: - Lean in physically and mentally. Eye contact, body language, small acknowledgments ("hmm," "yeah") — these signal that you're there without hijacking the conversation. - Don't take the lead. The moment you offer a solution or steer toward feeling better, you've stopped holding and started directing. - Let them repeat themselves if they need to. The process isn't always efficient. Sometimes people circle the same thing several times before something shifts.

The deeper point: you are creating a container — a space within which the other person gets to decide what happens. That's what holding space means literally. You are defining the boundaries of a space; they move freely inside it.

"You are creating the space within which they get to decide what to do."

Tolerating the Transfer

The central skill isn't a technique you perform outward. It's an internal act. You have to be able to sit with the discomfort being transferred to you without immediately trying to neutralize it.

This is what makes holding space genuinely difficult. It's not that the emotions are overwhelming — it's that the reflex to make them stop is so fast and so automatic that most people never notice it happening. By the time they realize they've jumped to fix or validate, they've already broken the container.

The practice starts with noticing. When someone comes to you upset, pause before responding and observe what's happening inside you. There's likely a low-grade discomfort — maybe anxiety, maybe a kind of helplessness — and an itch to do something about it. That itch is the signal. It means the emotional umbilical cord is working. It does not mean you have to act.

You don't need to eliminate the discomfort. You need to tolerate it. Hold it the same way you're holding the bag for the other person.

How to Know When You're Done

Holding space doesn't go on forever, and there's an internal signal for when it's run its course.

At some point during a long emotional conversation, the other person may start repeating themselves — the same story, the same complaints, without much new emotional energy behind it. When that happens, pay attention to what you're feeling. If the discomfort that was being transferred has started to fade, and what you're feeling now is closer to boredom or emotional neutrality, that's the indicator. The meaningful processing has probably happened. What's left is more reflexive than felt.

That's the moment you can gently redirect — not by solving the problem, but by reflecting back what you heard and opening a question:

"Here's what I understood about your situation. What are you thinking? How are you approaching this?"

You're not telling them what to do. You're signaling that the holding phase has done its work, and nudging toward whatever comes next.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking validation for holding space. Saying "that must be so hard" isn't automatically holding space. If it's coming from a need to de-escalate the emotional transfer, it's still a form of fixing. Real holding space doesn't have an agenda about when the other person should start feeling better.

Going mentally absent. Holding space is not zoning out while someone talks. If you're checked out, they feel it. The "holding" is active — you're engaged, tracking, present. It just doesn't result in a directive action.

Waiting for permission to hold space. Some people think they need to ask "do you want advice or do you just want me to listen?" before every hard conversation. That's a useful move sometimes, but understanding the three options means you can often read what's needed without turning it into a procedure.

Thinking you need to say something meaningful. Holding space can feel incomplete because it doesn't produce a satisfying output. You haven't solved anything. You haven't even said anything particularly profound. That discomfort — the sense that you should have done something — is worth sitting with too.

Exiting too early. Rushing to hold space for thirty seconds and then offering solutions still breaks the container. The discomfort hasn't transferred fully yet, and the other person hasn't finished. Boredom is the signal to wrap up, not mild awkwardness.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the internal transfer in real time. The next time someone comes to you with a problem, before you respond, pause and check: what are you feeling right now? Name it. Is it anxiety? Helplessness? A pull to fix or reassure? Just noticing this — without acting on it — is the first step.

  2. Identify which mode you default to. Do you usually jump to solutions, or do you rush to validate? Watch a few conversations with that question in mind. Most people have a strong default.

  3. Practice the bag image. In your next emotionally charged conversation, visualize yourself holding an open bag. Your only job is to hold it steady and let the other person put things in. Don't rearrange the bag's contents. Don't fill it yourself. Just hold.

  4. Use your boredom as data. When someone is venting, notice if and when boredom or emotional neutrality kicks in. That's your sign that the heavy processing may be winding down and you can begin to reflect back what you heard and ask a gentle orienting question.

  5. Practice tolerating transferred discomfort in low-stakes situations. Watch something sad or uncomfortable — a hard scene in a film, a difficult conversation between other people — and resist the urge to look away, joke, or mentally exit. Let the feeling sit. This builds the same muscle you use when holding space for someone you love.
