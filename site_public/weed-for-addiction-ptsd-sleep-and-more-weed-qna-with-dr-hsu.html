Summary

TL;DR — Cannabis is far more complex and far more potent than most people assume, and the research does not support using it to treat depression, PTSD, or sleep problems — especially the high-THC whole-plant products most people are actually using.

This is a conversation between two psychiatrists — one with a background in addiction medicine — working through what the science actually says about cannabis: how it works neurologically, what the evidence does and doesn't support, where real risks lie, and how to think clearly about a topic that tends to trigger strong reactions in people.

The Cannabis You're Using Today Is Not the Cannabis Anyone Studied

Almost all the cultural mythology around marijuana — that it's mild, that people have used it safely forever, that "it's just a plant" — was formed around a product with 2–3% THC content. Ancient Chinese medicine, 1960s counterculture, the Woodstock generation: all of it involved cannabis with a completely different chemical profile than what's available now.

Today, whole-plant cannabis averages 20–30% THC. Vape products and concentrates can hit 60–90%. That's a 10- to 30-fold increase in potency.

This matters enormously. Studies done in earlier decades are not straightforwardly applicable to what people are consuming today. The research hasn't kept pace — in part because cannabis is still a Schedule I controlled substance in the US (same category as LSD), making clinical trials genuinely difficult to conduct. And meanwhile, large tobacco companies have purchased many cannabis dispensaries and are applying the same marketing and product-design playbook they used with cigarettes: push potency, play up benefits, obscure harms.

How THC Actually Works in the Brain (And Why It Backfires at High Doses)

THC operates on receptors called CB1 receptors, which are found in several important brain regions:

  • The amygdala — your fear and emotional alarm system
  • The prefrontal cortex — decision-making, motivation, emotional regulation
  • The temporal lobes — associated with visual effects and, at high doses, psychotic experiences

At low doses, THC has an anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) effect on the amygdala. This is real, and it's part of why people reach for it when they're stressed.

At higher doses, the effect reverses. THC becomes anxiogenic — it amplifies fear and anxiety rather than dampening it. This is why some people have panic attacks or paranoid episodes, especially with high-potency modern products.

With chronic use, a different problem emerges. The CB1 receptors in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for identifying and regulating negative emotions — become desensitized and down-regulated. Imaging studies suggest this region may actually shrink in long-term heavy users. When you regularly use a substance to numb emotional states, the neural machinery your brain uses to process those emotions becomes less necessary, and it responds accordingly.

"If I'm using a substance to numb my emotional state, my brain's ability to control and regulate my emotional state becomes less necessary."

This is the core problem with using cannabis to cope with PTSD or depression. It doesn't treat the underlying dysregulation — it outsources it.

What the PTSD and Depression Research Actually Shows

Somewhere between a third and half of people who obtain medical marijuana cards list PTSD as their reason. And many people with PTSD report genuine short-term relief from cannabis. So why doesn't this show up in clinical studies?

Two large Veterans Affairs studies tracked PTSD patients who used cannabis. The findings: people who used more cannabis, more frequently, showed worsening PTSD symptoms over time. One study found that when patients stopped using cannabis, their nightmares and anxiety actually improved.

The neurobiological explanation above predicts exactly this outcome. The amygdala keeps firing; the prefrontal cortex gets blunted; the emotional processing work doesn't get done. Symptoms feel manageable in the short term but worsen in the long term as the brain's own regulation circuitry weakens.

For depression, the picture isn't better. Chronic high-THC use appears to cause hypodopaminergic depression — a state where the dopamine response to everything except the substance is suppressed. You feel flat without it, and the only thing that lifts you to baseline is using again. This looks like the substance treating your depression. It's actually withdrawal.

Recovering from this state after stopping heavy cannabis use can take months to up to a year.

CBD: Genuinely Promising, But Not What You're Probably Getting

CBD (cannabidiol) is a different cannabinoid with a meaningfully different profile. It acts on the CB2 receptor and also on the 5-HT1A serotonin receptor — the same receptor implicated in anxiety, depression, and cognitive flexibility, and possibly in how ketamine works. There is early-stage evidence of potential benefit for social anxiety, PTSD symptoms (nightmares, flashbacks), and insomnia.

But several things are critical to understand:

  1. Therapeutic doses in trials are in the range of 200–400 mg. Most CBD products sold at dispensaries contain 5–10 mg — likely far below any therapeutic threshold.
  2. Dispensary products are largely unregulated. One study examined 25 CBD products from dispensaries; only 3 had CBD content within 20% of what the label claimed. Some contained zero CBD.
  3. The placebo effect is enormous. In antidepressant trials, roughly half the effect is placebo. If you already believe something will help your anxiety, the probability it helps doubles. A product you like and trust, even with zero active ingredient, will feel like it's working.
  4. The only FDA-approved CBD product (Epidiolex) is approved specifically for two rare childhood seizure disorders — not for mental health indications. Insurance won't cover it off-label, and it costs tens of thousands of dollars.

The bottom line on CBD: it's not harmless (at high doses there are GI and liver effects), but it doesn't carry the addiction risk and brain-alteration profile of high-THC products. The evidence is promising but not yet at the level where a physician can responsibly say "yes, do this."

Groups Who Should Avoid Cannabis Entirely

  • Anyone under 25: The developing brain's dopamine pathways, prefrontal cortex, motivation circuits, and motor function can be dysregulated by cannabis use during development. A long-running New Zealand study found a 5–8 IQ-point drop in people who used cannabis heavily from early adolescence — and this did not recover when they stopped later in life.
  • Family history of schizophrenia or bipolar disorder: High-THC cannabis use increases the risk of expressing a genetic predisposition to schizophrenia by four to six times. It also appears to trigger onset earlier in life — and earlier onset generally means worse long-term outcomes.
  • People on certain medications: Smoked cannabis affects liver enzymes that metabolize many medications, including antipsychotics. It can reduce the effective dose of drugs like olanzapine — making it both a direct trigger for psychotic symptoms and a suppressor of the medication that's supposed to prevent them.
  • People with cardiac history or asthma: Associations with arrhythmias and worsening respiratory symptoms.
  • Anyone who is pregnant.

The Addiction Question

Not everyone who uses cannabis regularly becomes addicted, but among people who use it two or three or more times per week, approximately 30% develop addiction. Signs include:

  • Early-morning cravings upon waking
  • Using despite clear occupational or social consequences
  • Escalating doses needed for the same effect
  • Using in situations with obvious risks (before meetings, at work)

A particularly important pattern: when people who are dependent try to stop, the withdrawal — anxiety, depression, anhedonia — can feel worse than the original symptoms cannabis was "treating." This leads many to conclude that cannabis has been helping all along. In many cases, what they're experiencing is withdrawal from a substance their brain has built tolerance to.

The "Successful People Use It" Problem (And Other Cognitive Traps)

There are a few specific reasoning errors worth naming:

Survivorship bias: The people who use cannabis and succeed are visible and vocal. The people who use cannabis and drift into lower functioning, lose motivation, or have psychotic episodes don't end up being the ones cited as examples. Clinicians see the second group far more than the first.

Self-assessment failure: We are remarkably bad at evaluating our own cognitive performance. Studies on sleep deprivation and driving show this consistently. There is no part of your brain that signals "executive function at 60% of normal." The creativity studies are illustrative: people who used cannabis felt more creative, but when researchers looked at the actual output, there was no measurable difference from placebo.

The addicted mind's confirmation bias: Once the brain has attached to a substance, it actively seeks evidence that the substance is beneficial and vigorously dismisses contrary evidence. If your reaction to hearing about cannabis risks is immediate dismissal or a search for counterexamples, that pattern itself is worth examining.

"If you're reacting strongly to this in either direction — start by understanding what that reaction is."

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing "some evidence" with "this is safe and effective": Early signals, open-label trials, and animal studies are how research starts — not how it ends. The media cycle treats preliminary data as confirmation.
  • Assuming CBD from a dispensary is therapeutic: Without regulation, you often don't know what you're actually getting. Sub-therapeutic or zero CBD content is common.
  • Using cannabis for sleep long-term: While THC may shorten sleep onset, it suppresses REM sleep — the stage critical for emotional processing, immune function, and memory consolidation. This is a significant long-term cost.
  • Treating the dose as equivalent to historical or older research: A 25% THC product is not what any prior generation used. Effects, addiction risk, and neurological impact scale with potency.
  • Assuming that because you're using it "medically," you can't be addicted: Medical use label does not change what the substance does to CB1 receptors.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your actual use. Before any other decision, track honestly: how many times per day or week do you use, what product (and approximate THC%), and what you're using it for. Write this down — don't rely on memory.

  2. Identify the function it's serving. For each use occasion you logged, ask: what was I trying to accomplish or avoid? Sleep, social anxiety, boredom, emotional pain, habit? This is not self-criticism — it's information.

  3. Check your reaction to this information. If you found yourself repeatedly wanting to argue with or dismiss what's here, sit with that response. What does it feel like? Where does it come from? This is an exercise in honest self-observation, not self-judgment.

  4. If you're considering stopping or tapering, don't do it cold. Work with a physician or addiction counselor. A gradual taper (roughly 20% reduction every month or two) is significantly more tolerable than abrupt cessation. Know in advance that the first weeks or months may feel worse, not better — and that this is withdrawal, not evidence that you need the substance.

  5. If you're using it for a specific symptom (sleep, anxiety, PTSD, pain), ask yourself: have you ever had a full evaluation for the underlying condition? Sleep issues can stem from apnea, thyroid dysfunction, depression, or poor sleep hygiene — all of which have well-studied treatments. Use this as a prompt to actually address the root issue, not just the symptom.

  6. If you're under 25, or have a family history of psychotic disorders, treat this as a hard stop, not a risk to weigh. The downside risk in these cases — earlier onset of schizophrenia, lasting developmental impact on IQ and motivation — is severe and the potential benefit does not justify it.

  7. If you want to try CBD specifically, consult a physician, look for products tested by a third-party lab (services like ConsumerLab test for actual content), and understand that at typical dispensary doses you are likely well below any therapeutic range that's been studied.
