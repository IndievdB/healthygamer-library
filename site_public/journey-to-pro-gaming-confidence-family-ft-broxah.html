Summary

TL;DR — Confidence isn't something you build alone; it's taught to you first, then grown by stepping into uncertainty. Toxicity in gaming is a real mental health burden, and the antidote starts with understanding where a stable sense of self actually comes from.

This conversation with pro League of Legends player Broxah (Mads Brock-Pedersen) covers his path from a game-boy-wielding five-year-old in Denmark to a world-finalist jungler — but the real substance is about mental strength: where it originates, what undermines it, how toxic online behavior erodes it, and a concrete technique for resetting when negative self-talk takes over.

How a Supportive Family Builds the Foundation

Broxah's story is unusually warm for a pro gamer. His parents played World of Warcraft because he was into it. His mom learned Pokemon mechanics so she could help him past a stuck point. When they decided he was gaming too much, they didn't ban games — they gave him one screen-free day a week, and let him choose which day it would be.

That last detail matters more than it seems. Most parents in that situation restrict access. His parents signaled trust. The implicit message was: I believe you can manage this yourself. That kind of message, repeated over years, becomes the voice in your head.

His parents also separated his performance from his worth. His mom's proudest moments weren't when Broxah was playing the best league in Europe — they were when he gave a thoughtful, professional interview. They made clear they'd be just as happy if he worked at a supermarket, as long as it made him happy. That's the opposite of raising a child as a trophy.

This matters because that unconditional regard is, practically speaking, where confidence is installed.

Where Confidence Actually Comes From

When asked directly where his confidence comes from, Broxah initially says hard work and structure. Both get pushed back on — and usefully so.

Hard work alone doesn't produce confidence. He was working extremely hard when he first went pro and was physically shaking on stage during official matches. Structure helps — a structured mind creates accurate expectations, and meeting your own expectations repeatedly does reinforce belief in yourself. But it's not the root.

The more revealing moment is when he's asked: whose voice is it when you tell yourself you deserve to be here? He can't answer clearly. He says it might be the people he cares about, the ones who trust him. He's not sure it's really his own.

That uncertainty is the answer.

"Confidence is not something you intrinsically do — it's not something you can build all by yourself."

The voice that tells you you can do this starts as someone else's voice — a parent, a mentor, a cousin who shows you the ropes. Over time it becomes internalized, but it has an external origin. People who grew up without that voice being planted don't just lack confidence; they often have the opposite installed instead.

This is important for anyone who looks at a mentally strong person and thinks: why can't I just do what they do? You may not be going into the fight with the same draft.

Growing Confidence by Facing Uncertainty

Here's where you can actually influence your own confidence, regardless of starting conditions.

Broxah describes oral exams — walking in not knowing what topic he'd get — as far scarier than written exams, where he had notes and preparation on his side. But the oral exams, when he passed them, produced a much stronger sense of accomplishment. The written exams, carried by preparation, barely moved the needle.

"The less prepared you are, the more confident you have the possibility of ending up."

This sounds backwards. It isn't. The logic is: when you succeed at something stacked in your favor, you know it was stacked. You were carried. When you succeed blind — no notes, no pro player in your lane, no safety net — you did it. That's where the update to your self-belief actually lands.

The trap people fall into is trying to eliminate uncertainty before they act. They over-prepare, stack the deck, get friends to carry them, never truly expose themselves to a real test. They might get results, but their confidence doesn't grow — because they know, on some level, the result was rigged.

Going into something uncertain, surviving it (win or lose), and waking up the next day fine — that's the mechanism. Broxah describes losing important games and having to tell himself it'll be okay regardless. He could only learn that by losing important games and finding out it was, in fact, okay.

Taking It One Step at a Time Under Pressure

Broxah is careful never to go into a tournament saying "we're going to smash everyone." That's not false modesty — it's a deliberate mental setup. When you declare you must win no matter what, every small thing that goes wrong triggers a spiral: it's over, it's all falling apart. You black out, get frustrated, stop adapting.

Instead, he breaks it down: what do we need to do to reach playoffs? What do we need to do in this specific game? When something collapses two minutes in — and it often does — he's already mentally positioned to adapt rather than panic.

"If I just try to come in prepared, no matter what happens and take it one step at a time, then anything can come my way."

This is closely related to detaching from outcomes and devoting yourself to your actions. The player who goes in demanding a win is actually less resilient than the player who goes in demanding their best effort — because only one of those things is in their control.

Toxicity, Self-Doubt, and the Compounding Effect

As a public pro player, Broxah is reachable on Twitter, Twitch chat, YouTube, Discord. When a team loses a big game, hateful messages arrive from multiple directions at once. Most he can discard. But they pile up.

The damage isn't one message — it's the accumulation activating self-doubt at exactly the moment when external circumstances are already rough. A bad tournament run plus lockdown in LA plus relationship strain plus a hundred hateful DMs produces a specific mental state:

  • Am I good enough for this?
  • Am I letting my team down?
  • Why do I put up with this when I could have a normal job?

The second thread — the fairness one — is separate but equally draining. The feeling that you shouldn't have to be mentally invincible just to do your job, that other people in other professions don't have to absorb this kind of abuse, is legitimate and worth naming.

His coping strategy is two things: remind himself he's there for a reason (that internal voice), and go directly to a coach or teammate for honest feedback — real signal to counterbalance the noise. Getting grounded, direct feedback from people who actually know your work is one of the most practical antidotes to the distortion that mass anonymous criticism creates.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking confidence comes from success. It doesn't, or at least not primarily. It comes from surviving uncertainty, including failure. People who only succeed at things they've rigged in their favor get results but don't build the underlying thing.

Trying to prepare away all uncertainty. Over-preparation is often avoidance in disguise. The oral exam terrifies you precisely because there's no safety net — but that's also why passing it means something. Removing the net removes the growth.

Believing mental toughness is a personal achievement. Broxah is mentally strong in part because he was dealt good cards — parents who treated him as a person with inherent worth, not a performance to be optimized. Comparing yourself to someone like that and concluding you're just weaker is unfair and inaccurate.

Assuming toxic people know they're being toxic. Most people who send hateful messages believe they're being honest or holding someone accountable. They're not waking up thinking time to ruin someone's day. That doesn't make the behavior acceptable, but it does mean "just be responsible" isn't a complete solution.

Setting an all-or-nothing expectation before a high-stakes situation. "I have to win no matter what" is a setup for a mental breakdown the moment anything goes wrong. It trades resilience for an illusion of drive.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Trace your confidence voice. When you hear that internal voice that says you can do this — or its absence — ask yourself: where did that come from? Whose values, whose approval, whose early belief in you is echoed there? This isn't about blaming anyone. It's about understanding your starting point honestly.

  2. Do something without the safety net. Pick one thing you've been over-preparing for or avoiding because you can't guarantee the outcome. Enter the oral exam. Ask without the flowers. Queue up without the smurf account. Go in underequipped on purpose and see what happens. Note how you feel the day after, regardless of outcome.

  3. Use "one step at a time" before high-pressure moments. Before a match, a presentation, a difficult conversation — don't set the goal as winning. Set it as: what is the first concrete thing I need to do well? Then the next. This keeps your mind adaptive when things go sideways, rather than catastrophizing.

  4. Get real signal when noise is overwhelming you. When hateful or critical messages pile up and self-doubt starts activating, go directly to someone who knows your actual work — a coach, a teammate, a trusted colleague — and ask for honest, specific feedback. Replace distorted mass opinion with grounded direct observation.

  5. Practice Bhramari (bee's breath) to reset negative self-talk. Close your eyes. Optionally close your ears with your thumbs. Inhale, then on the exhale hum like a bee for as long as the breath lasts. Repeat for five to nine rounds. The sustained sound occupies enough of your attention that the internal chatter quiets. After the final round, sit quietly with eyes closed for two to five minutes. This doesn't build confidence — it wipes away the active noise so your baseline self can surface. Use it when you're spiraling or before something high-stakes.
