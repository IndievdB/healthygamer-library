Male Loneliness Is a Public Health Emergency That We Keep Mislabeling

Suicide is the leading cause of death for men between roughly 21 and 45, and the rate has climbed around 25% since 1990. Despite that, there is almost no public funding, and the dominant framing — that male suicide is a mental illness problem — is quietly making things worse.

Studies suggest that up to 60% of men who die by suicide show no diagnosable mental illness beforehand. That number matches clinical experience: roughly half of suicidal men seen in practice don't meet criteria for any disorder. What they share instead is hopelessness and thwarted belongingness — they are trying to do what the world asks of them, cannot get traction, and are not permitted to ask for help.

When we call suicidality a mental illness by default, we locate the entire problem inside the individual. We say, implicitly, that the circumstances don't matter, the world doesn't owe you anything, fix yourself. That is exactly what society already tells men. Labeling it as illness just repeats the message with clinical authority.

Two case examples illustrate the range. A high-earning, physically imposing man — successful by every external measure — was approaching suicide because everyone in his life came to him for resources and no one asked how he was doing. A 19-year-old in jail for dealing drugs was suicidal because his family told him a man provides, he had no legal way to do it, and asking for help wasn't something men were supposed to do. Different circumstances, same trap.

The deeper driver is touch starvation, and a surprising amount of it traces back to homophobia. When physical contact between men became culturally coded as sexual, men lost the entire category of platonic touch. The only people left who could touch them were romantic partners and, in childhood, mothers. That collapse means heterosexual women are now expected to meet not just romantic needs but every human need for physical connection a man has — an impossible load, and one that distorts relationships.

Meanwhile, someone who tries to offer a hug to a stranger risks being called a creep. Men who try to express need risk being called weak. The one professional space where they are allowed to be vulnerable — therapy — has rules that prevent the therapist from saying "I love you" back when a patient, for the first time in his life, says it to another person.

"We don't let men be taken care of because we expect them to take care of themselves — and even by labeling it mental illness we're reinforcing that, because now it's their responsibility."

The good cry is worth naming specifically. There are two kinds of crying: the bad cry, done alone in a corner, which tends to spiral, and the good cry, which happens in the presence of someone who accepts it — and which consistently leaves people feeling better. Therapy works partly because it is a structured container for the good cry. Most men have never had access to that container and, because no one modeled it for them, don't know it's possible.

Practical things that can actually help right now: ask the men in your life when they last had a good cry. Offer a hug to someone you know well enough. If you're a man, experiment with physical contact with other men — a hand on a shoulder, an arm around someone. The reason this feels strange is entirely learned, not innate.

Nihilism Isn't Weakness — It's a Rational Response to Broken Promises

Millennials were handed a formula — go to college, take out loans, get the credential, succeed — and the formula didn't work. Student loan debt was carved out of bankruptcy law so it couldn't be discharged even in financial collapse, while corporate and commercial real estate debt could. The generation that followed watched that happen and, quite rationally, stopped believing that effort leads to reward.

The word for that cultural condition is doomerism, and it's common enough to have earned its own name. When the structures that were supposed to give life meaning don't deliver, people look for substitutes. The response to this — "buy this supplement, use this app, hack your brain, a scientist will fix climate change" — is not an answer. It's more of the same system that failed in the first place. The uncomfortable truth is that in an environment this broken, each person has to figure out individually what makes their own life worth living. There isn't a formula. Anyone selling one is selling something.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming all suicidality is mental illness. This is the most consequential mistake in the piece. It locates the problem in the person, removes social responsibility, and reinforces the same message already telling men to fix themselves alone. Seeking mental health support is still useful even when there's no diagnosis — an external confirmation that "your reaction is appropriate to an awful situation" can itself be therapeutic.

  • Treating the solution to tech-driven dysregulation as primarily regulatory. Banning TikTok doesn't undo the neural patterns already established, and every other platform has already copied the short-form model. Regulation is worth pursuing and will arrive too late to be sufficient.

  • Confusing living in the present with hedonism. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your relationship with time for one day. Every time you feel anxious, frustrated, or bored, ask yourself: am I thinking about the future or the past? Notice whether there is ever a moment of genuine suffering that is purely about the present second, not a consequence you're anticipating or a failure you're replaying.

  2. Try a "spent" day instead of a "happy" day. Design a day around things that will leave you depleted in a good way — a hard physical activity, a difficult honest conversation, a creative project you've been avoiding. At the end, notice whether the tiredness feels different from how you usually feel.

  3. Ask someone you know when they last had a good cry. You don't need to follow it up with anything elaborate. The question alone often opens something. Stay with whatever comes up rather than rushing to fix it.

  4. Offer physical contact to someone in your life who might need it. This can be as small as a hand on a shoulder. For men specifically: make one instance of platonic physical contact with a male friend this week — a hug, a hand on the back. Notice what resistance comes up in you and where it comes from.

  5. If you're studying or doing focused work, practice present-tense curiosity. Instead of tracking how much you have left or how long you've been at it, pick one thing in the material and ask why it works the way it does. Stay with the question until you actually want to know the answer.
