Summary

TL;DR — Making friends as an adult is hard for everyone, but it's especially hard when your profession is friendship. The deeper problem, though, usually predates your circumstances.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and a veteran Twitch streamer who came in asking why she struggles to form deep friendships. What starts as a practical question about the social dynamics of content creation gradually reveals something older and more personal: a lifelong pattern of being the outsider, a learned tendency to retreat before rejection can arrive, and an internal experience of the world that rarely gets shared with anyone.

When the Problem Is Real, Not Imaginary

The first instinct when someone says "I can't make friends" is to look for something broken inside them — social anxiety, low self-worth, avoidant attachment. Sometimes that's right. But sometimes the circumstances genuinely are hard, and pretending otherwise doesn't help anyone.

The streaming world is a genuinely difficult place to build friendships. Here's why:

  • Friendship and business are the same activity. Collabs are friendships. Friendships look like collabs. Every hangout has a professional shadow over it.
  • Dual relationships are inherently messy. Anytime the same person is both a friend and a professional contact, the lines blur — the same reason therapists don't date patients or why going into business with family rarely ends well.
  • You can never fully opt out of transactional concern. Even if you have zero interest in someone's follower count, you can't prevent them from wondering whether you do.

This doesn't mean the situation is hopeless. But it does mean you're not imagining it. The deck is stacked, and knowing that matters before you start blaming yourself.

The Bungee Cord, Not a Wall

There's a tempting metaphor for feeling disconnected from people: a wall. Something solid and external that keeps you from getting close. But a wall implies someone else built it, or that you're stuck behind it passively.

A more accurate image might be a bungee cord.

You take a step toward someone. Then another. Then they don't mirror the energy back — maybe they're busy, maybe they're just flaky, maybe it's genuinely unclear — and you snap back. Fast. The further into unknown territory you go, the harder the cord pulls.

The cord isn't a character flaw. It's a learned self-protection. At some point, probably well before adulthood, you made overtures toward people who didn't meet you halfway. It hurt. The brain logged that information and built a very efficient early-warning system: don't go too far, because here's what happens when you do.

"You're trying really hard in one direction and not trying at all in another direction."

That's the real pattern. The effort is real. But it's effort at a safe distance — and then retreat at the first ambiguous signal.

How This Starts Earlier Than You Think

The streaming world can feel like the source of the problem because it amplifies everything. But for most people, the pattern predates it by decades.

Think about earlier versions of the same feeling: always being the second-tier friend in school, the one people were happy to include but didn't think to call first. Or growing up around family events where everyone else shared a language — literally or figuratively — that you didn't have access to, for reasons outside your control.

Those experiences teach a child something very specific: my ability to connect depends on factors I can't control. That's not a cognitive distortion. For a kid in those circumstances, it's an accurate read of reality.

The problem is the brain keeps that lesson active long after the original circumstances are gone. It becomes the default framing: the world runs on autopilot, and I can try to navigate within it, but I can't really change how it responds to me.

"Your mind jumps to: what's the scenario where I can't win, despite my efforts?"

This is what external locus of control looks like in practice. Not laziness or pessimism — just a deeply ingrained sense that the meaningful variables are outside your reach.

The Pop-Up Ad Your Brain Serves You

Locus of control is the key concept here. People with an internal locus of control believe that their actions meaningfully shape outcomes. People with an external locus attribute outcomes to forces outside themselves. Neither is always wrong — but when it comes to relationships, defaulting to external attribution keeps you passive.

The insidious part is that the brain generates these thoughts automatically, the same way a browser serves targeted ads based on your history. Your mind has been "browsing" a certain kind of experience — rejection, being the afterthought, overtures that didn't land — and now it preemptively serves you those conclusions.

"Just because there's something on your screen telling you that you're annoying someone doesn't actually make it real. That's the pop-up. It's not coming from reality."

The goal isn't to delete the pop-ups. It's to recognize them as pop-ups — not as accurate reports of what other people are actually thinking.

When you take one step toward someone and your mind immediately jumps to they're going to get so annoyed they tell me to go away, that's not a prophecy. It's cached data from old experiences being pattern-matched onto a new situation.

The Specific Problem With Radio Silence

Most social friction in adult friendships doesn't come in the form of rejection. It comes in the form of nothing. Someone doesn't reply. Plans fall through. An invitation goes unanswered.

Radio silence is harder to handle than rejection because it's ambiguous. And when something is ambiguous, the brain fills in the gap with its existing fears. If your history tells you that you're the second-choice friend, ambiguity gets interpreted as confirmation.

The practical upshot: when you reach out to someone and don't hear back, try being slightly more explicit rather than going quiet. Something like: "Hey, I've reached out a couple of times and it seems like you've been busy — no big deal. I just wanted to check: do you actually want me to keep inviting you?"

This feels risky because it forces an answer. But most people find that a clear "yes, please keep inviting me, I've just been swamped" is far more useful than the silence they were interpreting as polite rejection. And on the rare occasion someone actually isn't interested, the closure is easier to process than endless ambiguity.

The larger principle: clarify the dual relationship explicitly. In any context where friendship and professional interest overlap, the relationships that work tend to be the ones where people, at some point, make their intentions known out loud.

Normalizing Pain Until You Can't Feel It Anymore

There's another pattern worth naming. When you've been living with certain kinds of difficulty for long enough, they stop feeling like difficulty. They just feel like the weather.

If strangers online are regularly hostile, that becomes background noise. If being the second-tier friend is the chronic experience, it becomes the baseline. If your dad did some genuinely hurtful things, the fact that it doesn't sting anymore might mean you've healed — or it might mean you packed it into a box and put it on a shelf.

"Everyone else on the internet thinks what you go through is terrible and you're a hero for standing up to it, and you're like — it's just my everyday."

That's a signal worth paying attention to. Not because you should be more upset than you are, but because emotional suppression and compartmentalization, while genuinely useful adaptations in the short term, can become a one-trick pony. You get very good at them. And then you find that the same wall keeping the bad stuff contained is the thing preventing deeper connection.

Common Pitfalls

  • Diagnosing this as a streaming problem. The circumstances of streaming make things harder, but if the pattern pre-dates streaming (and it usually does), fixing the circumstances won't fix the pattern.
  • Interpreting "I try" as equivalent to full effort. Trying hard at the safe version of connection — initiating, but snapping back at the first ambiguous signal — is different from staying present through discomfort.
  • Mistaking neutral or ambiguous responses for rejection. Busy people give ambiguous signals. Flaky people give ambiguous signals. The brain fills ambiguity with fear; that's not the same as evidence.
  • Waiting for emotional reciprocation before investing. If you only continue reaching out when the other person has matched your energy exactly, you'll almost always stop early, because most people are slow to warm up.
  • Believing the pop-up ad. The thought "I'm bothering them" feels real and feels like it's about them. It's usually an artifact of your own history.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the bungee cord in real time. The next time you pull back from a social interaction, pause and say to yourself: "I'm retreating right now. Is this because I actually have evidence they're not interested, or because the cord snapped?" You don't have to change the behavior immediately — just notice it.

  2. Try being explicit once. With someone you've been gradually warming up to, try naming the friendship directly. It doesn't have to be dramatic. Something like: "I really enjoy talking with you — I'm genuinely just looking to hang out, no agenda." See what happens.

  3. Ask the clarifying question. If you've reached out twice and heard nothing back, send one more message that makes it easy to answer: "Hey, seems like you've been busy — do you want me to keep inviting you to stuff?" Give them a clear yes/no rather than making them decode your silence, or you decode theirs.

  4. Try abdominal breathing for five minutes, three times a week. Sit up straight. Breathe in through your nose and actively push your belly out. Breathe out and pull your navel toward your spine. The goal is to spend a few minutes not being governed by what other people might be thinking. If a worry surfaces, let it pass like a pop-up ad rather than clicking on it.

  5. When a pop-up arrives, label it. Before a social interaction, or during one, if your mind produces "I'm probably annoying them" — say to yourself, out loud or internally: "That's a pop-up. That's old data, not current reality." You're not trying to make the thought go away. You're just declining to treat it as fact.

  6. Consider whether there's more to unpack. If there are relationships in your past — parental, romantic, or otherwise — where you made overtures that didn't land and quietly stopped trying, those experiences may be powering the pattern more than you realize. A therapist is a good place to explore that without it needing to resolve in a single conversation.
