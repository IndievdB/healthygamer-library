Summary

TL;DR — You control your actions, not outcomes. Nearly all avoidable suffering comes from forgetting this.

Most of the pain people carry around difficult situations — a family member's illness, a loved one's addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, an aging parent who won't accept help — comes from a hidden assumption: that if you love hard enough, prepare thoroughly enough, or do the right things, you can control what happens to another person. You can't. Recognizing that clearly isn't cold or defeatist. It's the thing that actually frees you to help.

The Hidden Expectation Behind Your Suffering

When something hard looms — a sister with a terminal illness, a parent who's struggling — the mind quietly constructs a plan. When Mom and Dad are gone, she'll come live with me. I'll make sure Mom doesn't fall apart. These feel like moral commitments, but look closely: they're predictions about outcomes you don't control, dressed up as duties.

The first move is to examine where that expectation came from and whether it's actually true.

Take the assumption that a family member with serious disabilities is best cared for by you, at home, out of love. That assumption might be driven entirely by guilt. Guilt doesn't make you think clearly. Neither does shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → or fear. The problem isn't having those emotions — it's accepting the conclusions they generate without questioning them.

The right question isn't can I do this? It's is this actually the best thing for the person I love? Those are not the same question, and guilt will make you treat them as if they are.

Love Is Not Enough — And That's Not a Failure

This is one of the hardest things to accept: you can love someone completely and still not be able to help them. Parents of people with addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → hear this and it blows their minds. It shouldn't have to — but it does, because we've been taught that love is the variable that determines outcomes.

It isn't. Circumstances matter. Resources matter. And crucially, the choices of the person you're trying to help matter — and those are not yours to make.

You also cannot love your way to unlimited capacity. A physician running on burnout makes mistakes, misses things, stops being compassionate. Someone who stays up all night comforting a sick child and is a zombie the next day isn't doing the child a net favor. Taking care of yourself first isn't selfishness — it's what makes sustained help possible.

"If you're trying to save someone from drowning, it's really easy if you're standing in the shallow end. If you're in the deep end, then it's tough. Take care of yourself first, establish a solid foundation."

You're Helping More Than You Know

When someone you love is struggling and they keep refusing your suggestions — won't go to counseling, won't change their habits, won't take your advice — it's easy to conclude you're not helping. That logic is wrong.

What people in crisis need more than correct advice is to feel less alone. The act of showing up, asking how someone is doing, saying I see you're struggling, how can I support you? — that registers even when the suggestion attached to it gets turned down.

A mother managing the daily grind of caring for a terminally ill child with cerebral palsy, and who hasn't fallen apart yet — that resilience doesn't come from nowhere. Part of the reason she's holding on is probably because someone keeps demonstrating they care. The caring itself is the intervention, not the specific advice.

Rather than offering suggestions and watching them get rejected, try asking open-ended questions:

  • This must be incredibly hard. How are you handling it?
  • What can I do to support you?
  • Have you ever thought about counseling? What do you think it even involves?

Let the other person run the show. You find out what they actually need, and you give them the experience of being heard rather than advised.

The Arrogance of Controlling Outcomes

Here's where things get uncomfortable.

"The depth of human arrogance is to think that you can control another human being — let alone your own life."

When someone asks what do I need to do for my mother to keep her from losing it? — that question contains an assumption that needs to be pulled apart. Whether your mother loses it is not yours to decide. You can show up. You can ask how to help. You can be present. But whether she falls apart in grief? That is not in your hands.

This isn't a reason to do nothing. It's a reason to redirect your energy toward the only thing you actually have: your own actions.

You are entitled to your actions. You are not entitled to the results of your actions.

  • You're entitled to apply for a job. You're not entitled to get it.
  • You're entitled to ask someone out. You're not entitled to a yes.
  • You're entitled to offer help. You're not entitled to have it accepted.
  • You're entitled to show up for someone in grief. You're not entitled to prevent their grief.

When people tie their sense of worth to outcomes, they get devastated by normal variance in life. She said no — I must not be good enough. I didn't get the job — I must be a failure. That's what happens when you confuse what you control with what you don't.

The 10% Principle

Think about a team game where you're one of ten players. You control exactly your own character — roughly 10% of what happens. If you spend the whole game raging at teammates, you don't get better, you don't climb, and you don't win more. If you focus entirely on what your character did wrong and what you can do differently, you climb.

That's the ratio life offers too. You're not 100% of any situation involving other people. You're a meaningful fraction. Focus obsessively on that fraction — improve it, optimize it, show up for it — and the overall outcomes start shifting. Obsess over the outcomes instead, and you're just raging at randos.

What you control: preparing yourself for loss, showing up, asking good questions, taking care of your own foundation, applying again.

What you don't control: whether your loved one heals, whether your mother holds together, whether your help gets accepted, whether the world responds the way you want.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing guilt with obligation. Feeling guilty about not doing something doesn't mean that thing is actually the right call. Guilt is data about your emotional state, not a reliable guide to what's best for another person.
  • Mistaking inaction for the help not mattering. If someone doesn't visibly change or take your advice, it doesn't mean your presence and concern had no effect. It often has a profound effect you never see.
  • Making suggestions instead of asking questions. Telling someone what to do usually triggers resistance. Asking what they need opens a conversation.
  • Treating love as a control mechanism. Loving someone deeply is real and valuable. It doesn't override another person's autonomy, circumstances, or biology.
  • Focusing on outcomes as a measure of your worth. The outcome of your action is not a report card on who you are. You controlled the action. The outcome had other inputs.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your expectations. Write down one situation where you feel stuck or like you're failing someone. For each expectation you hold ("she should get better," "he should accept my help," "I need to prevent X"), ask: Did I actually have control over this, or did I just assume I did?

  2. Separate your actions from their results. For any goal or situation you're anxious about, write two columns: What I control and What I don't control. Practice pointing your energy exclusively at the first column.

  3. Switch from suggestions to questions. The next time you want to help someone who's struggling, try replacing your advice with: "How are you doing with all of this? What would actually be helpful for you right now?" Notice what changes.

  4. Check your foundation. Ask honestly: am I burnt out? Am I in the deep end trying to save someone else? Before pouring more into another person's situation, identify one concrete thing you can do this week to stabilize your own ground.

  5. Show up without an agenda. If someone you care about keeps rejecting your help, consider that simply being present and demonstrating you care — without requiring them to change — might be the most valuable thing you can offer. Try it for two weeks and observe what actually happens.
