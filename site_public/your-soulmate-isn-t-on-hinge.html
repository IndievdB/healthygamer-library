Love Is Not One Thing

Ancient Greek thinkers identified several distinct types of love, and a 1977 research review formalized them:

  • Eros — passionate, fascinated, "crazy about you" romantic love
  • Ludus — playful, game-like love; a competitive or challenging dynamic
  • Storge — love that grows slowly out of friendship and shared history
  • Pragma — practical love based on compatible valuesValues are the things that, when you move toward them, make you feel genuinely fulfilled — not just pleased or validated, but deeply satisfied.Full definition →, goals, and life circumstances
  • Mania — obsessive, anxious, emotionally unstable love that craves constant validation
  • Agape — selfless, sacrificial love rooted in commitment and care

The critical mistake most people make when searching for a soulmate is collapsing all of these into one thing. They treat "finding love" as a single goal, without recognizing that they may be optimizing for one type while accidentally blocking another.

Why Dating Apps Select for the Wrong Brain

Dating apps are fundamentally pragma machines. Before you've exchanged a word with someone, you've already screened their job, religion, political views, whether they want kids, what they look like, and whether your interests overlap. You're doing what matchmaking parents in an arranged marriage do — running a compatibility analysis.

That's not a bad thing on its own. Arranged marriages, when they work, often succeed because pragma provides a stable foundation, and storge (friendship-based love) grows on top of it over decades. But here's the problem: eros and pragma come from different hemispheres of the brain, and activating one suppresses the other.

Eros is right-hemisphere, intuitive, emotional. Pragma is left-hemisphere, analytical, calculative. When you approach a first meeting having already read someone's profile and decided they're a "good match," you've already lit up the analytical circuitry. The brain that falls in love never gets a chance.

"Compatibility is not love. In fact, compatibility is sometimes the opposite of love."

Think about painting. You can't judge what you're painting while you're painting it — the critical mind and the creative mind can't run simultaneously. The same is true when you meet someone. Judging them — even efficiently, even correctly — cuts off the circuitry that would let you fall for them.

The Rate of Intimacy Model

There's a model of romantic passion built around a counterintuitive insight: passion isn't driven by how well you know someone — it's driven by how fast that knowing is accelerating.

Picture a graph with time on one axis and depth of intimacy on the other. When you first meet someone and each conversation reveals something new and surprising, the rate of change is steep. That acceleration — not the total amount of closeness — is what triggers the dopamineA neurotransmitter the brain uses primarily to reinforce behavior, drive motivation, and generate craving — not, as commonly assumed, simply to produce pleasure.Full definition → release that underlies eros.

"When intimacy is rising quickly, passion will be high. When intimacy is stable and no longer increasing, passion will be low."

This is why new relationships feel electric and long-term ones require different maintenance. It's also why dating apps undermine chemistry from the start: you already know so much before you meet that the intimacy curve is nearly flat by the time you have coffee. The rate of change is a sluggish +1 per interaction, when falling in love requires something closer to exponential.

The soulmate stories people tell tend to follow a pattern: they ran into someone unexpectedly, knew very little about them, and then the knowing came fast — and the more they learned, the stronger the pull got. The connection wasn't about shared hobbies or compatible life plans. It was the acceleration itself.

The question worth sitting with: Is this person extraordinary, or am I just starving?

What Replaces the Spark (The Self-Expansion Model)

Eros can't sustain forever — the intimacy curve plateaus, and when it does, passion fades by design. Long-term love doesn't mean eros continues at the same intensity indefinitely; it means other types of love fill in. Pragma, storge, and agape build up around the original spark.

The model that explains the longer arc is self-expansion: the idea that humans are wired to grow, and that at some point a partner stops being someone you're learning about and becomes someone you're growing through.

Early in a relationship, you're both leveling up individually and the connection is accelerating intimacy. Later, the "I" starts to become a "we." Their accomplishments carry your pride. Your milestones belong to both of you. You're building something — a life, a family, a shared history — and the pull of that shared project replaces the biochemical rush of early eros.

"When you meet your soulmate, I'm leveling up over here, my soulmate is leveling up over here, and at some point we join and start leveling up together."

Soulmates, in this framework, aren't just people who felt right at first. They're people with whom you transition naturally from the rate-of-intimacy phase into the self-expansion phase — where the original feeling of "this person was always meant to be part of me" gets confirmed and deepened by the actual life you build.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating a dating profile like a compatibility checklist. Shared interests, similar valuesValues are the things that, when you move toward them, make you feel genuinely fulfilled — not just pleased or validated, but deeply satisfied.Full definition → on paper, and matching life goals are pragma. They can build a good partnership but they don't generate eros. Finding everything "right" about someone before you meet them may actually prevent you from falling for them.
  • Confusing manic love for a soulmate signal. Intense feeling isn't the same as deep connection. If you're emotionally depleted, isolated, or anxiously attached, needs being met can feel like destiny.
  • Expecting the eros phase to last forever. When early passion fades, people often conclude they chose the wrong person. Usually they're just at the normal plateau — the question is whether pragma, storge, or agape can grow in its place.
  • Assuming soulmates share your interests. Many long-term deeply-connected couples barely overlap in hobbies. Storge grows from shared experience over time, not shared taste at the outset.
  • Ignoring what dopamineA neurotransmitter the brain uses primarily to reinforce behavior, drive motivation, and generate craving — not, as commonly assumed, simply to produce pleasure.Full definition → dysregulation is doing to you. If your reward system is constantly overstimulated — screens, porn, substances — you may not be capable of the neurochemical response that eros requires, and nothing about the person in front of you will fix that.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what kind of love you're selecting for. Before your next date or swipe session, ask yourself honestly: am I filtering for eros or for pragma? Notice how much of your selection process is analytical (profession, interests, life goals) versus intuitive.

  2. Create conditions for accelerating intimacy. Go somewhere neither of you has been. Try an activity that's new for both of you. Ask questions that aren't on a profile. Novelty and shared new experience are the raw material for the intimacy acceleration that eros needs.

  3. Limit profile-reading before a first meeting. Consider seeing less before you meet. The brain that falls in love needs room to be surprised. A flat intimacy curve from the start is hard to recover from.

  4. Take your dopamineA neurotransmitter the brain uses primarily to reinforce behavior, drive motivation, and generate craving — not, as commonly assumed, simply to produce pleasure.Full definition → system seriously. If you're heavily reliant on social media, pornography, video games, or substances, recognize that this isn't just a lifestyle issue — it's directly affecting your capacity for romantic passion. Addressing the dysregulation isn't optional if you want to fall in love.

  5. After the early spark, ask the self-expansion question. Once past the eros phase, check in: Are we growing together? Do their wins feel like mine? Are we building something? That's the transition from early love to lasting love, and it's a choice to lean into, not something that happens automatically.

  6. Distinguish hunger from connection. If you've been lonely, isolated, or emotionally unsupported, sit with this question before deciding someone is "the one": Would I feel this strongly if my baseline emotional needs were already met?
