Your Cognitive Fingerprint: Two Axes for Choosing a Style

From working with patients, a clearer picture emerges: people have a cognitive fingerprint — a characteristic way their mind operates — and certain meditative styles match certain fingerprints. Two axes capture most of the relevant variation.

Axis 1: Open Awareness ↔ Focus

Open awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → practices (like Zen) don't tell your mind where to go. You simply observe — the breath, the taste of food, sounds in the room. You're watching what the mind naturally does, not directing it. Neuroscience research confirms these practices produce measurably different brainwave patterns than focusing practices.

Focusing practices (like tratakaFixed-point gazing — holding a soft, half-lidded stare on one object (classically a candle flame) without blinking for as long as possible.Full definition →, where you fix your gaze on a candle flame) narrow attention to a single point and hold it there deliberately.

Ask yourself: do you work better with open-ended observation, or do you perform better when given a clear, specific target? Driven, goal-oriented people often find focusing practices more natural. More contemplative, "sit back and watch" types often flourish with open awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

Axis 2: Grounding ↔ Exploratory

This distinction gets less attention but is equally important.

Grounding practices are designed to evacuate the mind — to pull attention so completely into one physical sensation that anxious thoughts, ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition →, or painful loops simply can't compete. Ice diving is the extreme example: submerging your face in ice water makes every other thought disappear instantly. Less dramatic grounding techniques work on the same principle. These are particularly useful for people dealing with panic attacks or intrusive thought loops — the goal is not to explore those thoughts but to escape them entirely.

Exploratory (inquisitive) practices do the opposite. Instead of silencing the mind, they give it a puzzle to follow. "Notice the temperature of your breath. Where does it travel inside you?" The mind is invited to investigate, and curiosity becomes the anchor. This works well for people who simply cannot force their mind to be still, but who become highly focused the moment there's something to figure out.

Goal-Oriented Practice: The Kundalini Chakra Framework

For people who come to meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → with a specific outcome in mind — "I want to stop losing my temper," "I want to feel less lonely," "I want better intuition" — the Kundalini yoga framework of seven chakras functions as a practical goal-to-practice map.

Think of each chakra less as a mystical energy center and more as a domain of human functioning. The honest caveat: there's no solid scientific evidence that chakras exist as physical structures, but the framework appears to map onto real neurological circuits that different practices actually influence.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your cognitive fingerprint. Sit quietly for five minutes and just observe your mind. Afterward, ask: Did I want to direct my attention somewhere, or was I comfortable watching? Did I want to explore what came up, or did I want to escape it? Your honest answers tell you which quadrant of the two axes fits you best.

  2. Try an open awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → session.   3. Try a focusing session. Light a candle (or find a fixed point on a wall). Set a timer for five minutes. Keep your gaze and full attention on that single point. Notice when attention wanders and bring it back without judgment. This is basic tratakaFixed-point gazing — holding a soft, half-lidded stare on one object (classically a candle flame) without blinking for as long as possible.Full definition →.

  4. Try a grounding technique. When you notice anxious thoughts looping, splash cold water on your face, or hold an ice cube in your hand. Observe how quickly attention shifts to the physical sensation. This is a starter version of the grounding principle.

  5. Try an exploratory breath exercise. Close your eyes and breathe normally. Notice whether the incoming breath feels warm or cool. Then follow it: where do you feel it in your nostrils? Your throat? Your chest? Spend three to five minutes simply following that trail with genuine curiosity.
