The Real Efficacy Picture: Three Groups, Not One Average

Studies typically report something like a 30–50% improvement in depressive symptoms, which sounds modest. But that average obscures what's actually happening:

There's also evidence that belief matters. Hopelessness about whether a medication will work actually reduces its effectiveness. SSRIs Are Not Happy Pills

A common fear is that antidepressants create artificial happiness or emotional blunting. The clinical picture doesn't support this. SSRIsA neurotransmitter responsible for contentment and peace — distinct from the excitement and pleasure driven by dopamine — and for regulating your brain's perception of how hard something is to do.Full definition → have no street value, no abuse potential, and produce no euphoria. They don't make non-depressed people feel better. What they actually do is reduce the frequency, severity, and duration of depressive episodes.

Someone off medication might have one severe episode per year lasting eight months. The same person on an SSRI might have one episode every three years, lasting two or three months, with less impairment. That's the realistic goal — not permanent happiness, but a meaningfully different pattern of suffering.

Coming Off Medication: What the Data Says

About 50–60% of people who stop taking an antidepressant will experience a depressive episode again within one to two years. The flip side: roughly half of people only need SSRIsA neurotransmitter responsible for contentment and peace — distinct from the excitement and pleasure driven by dopamine — and for regulating your brain's perception of how hard something is to do.Full definition → once and do fine afterward.

Who tends to need them longer? People with frequent, severe, or recurring depressive episodes. For them, staying on medication is similar to staying on any other maintenance treatment — stopping it because you feel well is like stopping exercise because you've gotten fit.

Coming off should always be tapered, not stopped abruptly. Quitting cold turkey increases relapse risk. Work with a doctor on the timeline.
