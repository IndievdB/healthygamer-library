Summary

TL;DR — Confidence isn't built by controlling outcomes or collecting successes; it's built by tolerating uncertainty and surviving failure. Until your sense of worth comes from inside rather than from external validation, anxiety will keep growing no matter how many good streams you have.

This is a wide-ranging conversation about what actually drives low confidence, why controlling your environment makes anxiety worse over time, and where the belief that you have to earn your basic human worth comes from. The talk moves from practical streaming anxiety into deep territory — perfectionism, OCD-like control rituals, the need for reassurance in relationships, childhood conditioning, grief, and a sexual assault disclosure — and shows how all of it connects to a single root: worth that was never allowed to be intrinsic.

Confidence Is Not What You Think It Is

Most people assume confidence comes from success. Win enough times, get enough praise, post a selfie and watch the likes roll in — and you'll feel good about yourself. But that's not how it works, and the conversation makes this contradiction very clear.

Imagine you have a good stream. You prepared carefully, you wore your hat, you had coffee with exactly the right amount of creamer. The stream goes well. Do you feel more confident going into the next one?

For a lot of people, the answer is no — it just raises the stakes. Now the next stream has to be at least as good. The good outcome didn't build confidence; it increased anxiety about failure.

This is because the confidence was never really there. What happened was that you controlled enough variables to remove uncertainty, and the stream was good, and you credited the variables. So now you need the variables again next time. That is not confidence. That is dependency on favorable conditions.

"If I'm confident in myself, I don't have to control a whole lot. Confidence is literally the ability to face uncertainty."

Real confidence is the belief that whatever happens, I can handle it. A confident baker doesn't need to check the recipe three times before starting — they just bake, and if it goes wrong, they adjust. The OCD-style rituals (specific cups, specific creamer amounts, specific pre-stream routines) exist precisely because that internal faith is missing. You're fighting a war against uncertainty every single day, and the weapons are control rituals.

Training Wheels Don't Teach You to Ride

There's a useful image here: a child learning to ride a bike with training wheels. The training wheels remove the anxiety of falling, but they also prevent the child from actually learning balance. If the child is scared of falling, adding training wheels reduces that fear — and directly reduces her confidence, because she is now relying on the wheels instead of herself.

Hats. Hoodies. Asking for reassurance. Controlling how you're photographed. These are all training wheels.

"My cookies are my training wheels."

Every time you use a training wheel to reduce anxiety, you accomplish two things at once: you lower anxiety right now, and you grow the anxiety beast for later. The rituals work, briefly. Then they need to happen again. Then they need to be more elaborate. The OCD-like logic of "if I use this cup, the stream will go well" is the natural endpoint of leaning on control instead of developing tolerance.

Taking the training wheels off is terrifying. You might fall. But falling — and getting back up — is the only thing that actually builds confidence. The goal isn't to guarantee you won't fall; it's to develop faith that you can survive the fall.

Why Successes Don't Stick (The Worth Problem)

There's a deeper reason why positive feedback doesn't build confidence: worth that depends on external things is inherently unstable.

If your value as a person is tied to being the top salesperson at your firm, then leaving that job doesn't just change your career — it removes your entire basis for self-worth. If your value depends on people liking your selfie, then one comment about balding doesn't just sting — it threatens the whole foundation.

When other people's opinions carry more weight than your own, any negative comment from a stranger overrides a hundred kind ones. It's not irrationality — it's the logical result of having an external locus of worth. You aren't assigning yourself a fixed value and then checking whether outside opinions match it. You're looking to outside opinions to tell you what your value is.

This is the cycle: 1. Low confidence → anxiety about specific things (appearance, weight, performance) 2. Anxiety → control rituals (hat, hoodie, reassurance-seeking) 3. Control rituals → good outcome 4. Good outcome → confidence stays low (or drops), because the success was credited to the ritual, not to you 5. Anxiety increases → more elaborate rituals

The cycle only breaks when worth stops being conditional.

Where Conditional Worth Gets Learned

This doesn't appear from nowhere. The conversation surfaces several layers of conditioning:

  • Being judged by family for clothing choices ("showing yourself off" in a tank top) taught a young version of you that your moral worth was visible on your body.
  • Watching siblings excel in sports while being cut from the team taught you that love and belonging required measurable performance.
  • Experiencing childhood sexual abuse — and being told to keep it secret — created a sense of being permanently, secretly impure, unable to fit in with a community that prized purity.
  • Growing up in an environment where love was in some ways conditional on conformity made you extraordinarily sensitive to rejection.

None of this means you are broken. It means you were conditioned — your brain was trained by repeated experience to link your worth to external performance and approval. The brain is a pattern-recognition machine; it learned what it was taught.

The tragedy is that one person consistently sent a different message: that you had value as a person, full stop, not contingent on anything. When she died, that anchor disappeared, and there was no replacement for it.

"You had one person who said — actually, whether you wear a hat or makeup or any of that doesn't matter. I care about you for you."

The grief underneath the anxiety is real. Losing the one consistent source of unconditional regard is not a small thing.

Reassurance-Seeking: The Training Wheel That Strains Relationships

One specific pattern worth naming: walking up to someone and asking "are you mad at me? Do you like me? Are you sure?" feels like it should relieve anxiety. And briefly, it does. But notice what happens next — the anxiety comes back, usually higher than before, and you need to ask again. And again.

"The reassurance doesn't fix the root of the thought. It just alleviates anxiety in the short term."

This is because the question "do you like me?" doesn't actually get at what's fueling the anxiety. The fuel is "I don't like myself — so why would anyone else?" Reassurance from someone else can't answer that question, because it's not their answer to give.

Over time, this pattern can exhaust the people around you, and it also reinforces the belief that you can't trust your own perception of reality — you need external confirmation to know whether you're okay.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking control for competence. When you manage your environment carefully and things go well, it's easy to credit the management. The problem is that your actual capacity — your ability to handle things without the rituals — never gets tested, and therefore never grows.

Waiting until you feel confident to act confident. Confidence follows action, not the other way around. Making a joke when you're not sure it'll land, posting a photo because you want to, streaming without the hat — these things come first. The confidence accumulates afterward, from the experience of having survived the uncertainty.

Using "I know better" as a substitute for doing differently. Knowing that reassurance-seeking makes anxiety worse is not the same as resisting the urge to seek reassurance. Knowledge doesn't rewire the pattern; practice does.

Treating every tool as a crutch. The exercise of writing down anxious thoughts and testing them against reality ("Sam has not actually said he hates me") isn't inherently a crutch — it genuinely helps some people recognize that thoughts aren't facts. The problem is when reality-testing becomes its own form of reassurance-seeking rather than a step toward tolerating uncertainty.

Expecting the anxiety to disappear. The goal isn't to eliminate anxious thoughts. It's to stop feeding them, so they stop growing. An unfed thought loses power. A suppressed or controlled-around thought gains it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your training wheels. Make a short list of things you do to reduce anxiety before or during situations that make you nervous (specific routines, clothing choices, reassurance questions, avoidance). These are the places where your confidence is being borrowed rather than built.

  2. Pick one small training wheel and remove it in a low-stakes situation. Don't start with your biggest fear. Start small — take a photo without filtering it, skip one pre-stream ritual, go to one social situation without your usual armor. Notice what happens to your anxiety, and notice that you survived.

  3. Practice urge surfing when the reassurance impulse hits. When you feel the pull to ask "are you mad at me?" or check that someone still likes you, pause. Name what you're feeling ("my anxiety is about an 80 right now"). Ask yourself: can I tolerate this for the next ten minutes without acting on it? Anxiety peaks and then falls on its own — you don't have to do anything to make it go away; you just have to not feed it.

  4. Try ujjayi breathing (or any slow, audible breath) when anxiety spikes. Slow the exhale by slightly constricting the back of your throat so the breath becomes audible. Focus on the sound. This doesn't eliminate the anxious thought, but it creates enough space to observe it rather than be consumed by it. Practice bringing the thought back and using the breath — the goal isn't to banish the thought but to learn you can coexist with it.

  5. When a negative comment hits disproportionately hard, notice the mismatch. One person says something cutting; a hundred said something kind. That disproportionate weight is data about your current relationship to your own worth — not data about reality. You don't have to fix it in the moment. Just notice it.

  6. Pursue therapy, specifically around the early conditioning and any trauma history. The patterns described here — conditional worth, repetition of painful relationship dynamics, difficulty accepting care — don't typically unwind through willpower alone. A good therapist works exactly this territory.

  7. Let someone care about you without explaining why it's selfish. You deserve to be on the receiving end of cupcakes, not just the giving end. Practice accepting care — a compliment, a kind gesture, someone checking in — without deflecting, minimizing, or feeling like a burden. Just say thank you and let it land.
