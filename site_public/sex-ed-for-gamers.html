Normal Sexual Development and Why Expectations Matter

Puberty typically begins around ages 12–13. For men, this means erections, growth of body hair, voice changes, and acne driven by hormonal shifts. For women, it includes breast development, pubic hair, and—most importantly for understanding pregnancy and birth control—the onset of the menstrual cycle.

The story of the patient who thought he could only reach "the first stage of orgasm" because he expected something resembling a fire hydrant isn't just funny. It illustrates something important: pornography is the de facto sex education for millions of people, and it sets wildly unrealistic expectations. That patient was experiencing a completely normal orgasm. He just had no frame of reference.

Unrealistic expectations aren't a moral failure. They're a predictable result of living in a society where real sexual education is sparse and awkward, and readily available media fills the void.

How the Menstrual Cycle Actually Works

Understanding the menstrual cycle matters whether or not you have a uterus—if you're in a relationship with someone who does, this is just basic biology worth knowing.

The average cycle is roughly 28 days. Here's the sequence:

  1. FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) rises and causes a follicle in the ovary to mature.
  2. An LH (luteinizing hormone) surge triggers ovulation—the egg leaves the follicle.
  3. There's roughly a one-week window around ovulation when pregnancy is possible.
  4. If the egg isn't fertilized and implanted in the uterine wall, the body sheds the uterine lining—this is menstruation.

One thing most men are never told: menstruation is one of the only necrotic processes in the human body. Normal cell death (apoptosis) is orderly and painless—like the skin between your fingers disappearing during fetal development. Necrosis is unplanned cell death: inflammatory, painful, and accompanied by the release of pain-signaling substances. When the uterine lining sheds, it's not a tidy shutdown. It's more like internal damage. The bloating, cramps, and mood effects aren't exaggeration. There's a biological reason for all of it.

This cycle is hormonally driven through a feedback loop: low estrogen and progesterone signal the hypothalamus to release GnRH → FSH and LH rise → ovulation occurs → progesterone rises → which then suppresses GnRH again. This loop is directly relevant to how birth control works.

Safe Sex Has Two Separate Goals

When someone says "I'm being safe," they usually mean one of two things—and these are not the same thing:

  1. Preventing STIs
  2. Preventing pregnancy

Treating them as interchangeable is one of the most common mistakes people make. A partner being on the pill does nothing about STIs. Being tested and clean does nothing to prevent pregnancy. These require different tools.

STIs: What You Actually Need to Know

Bacterial STIs (gonorrhea, chlamydia, syphilis) are treatable with antibiotics. The tragedy is that embarrassment leads people to wait, hope it goes away, and delay treatment while the infection does more damage. Syphilis, for example, starts as a painless sore that can come and go—which makes it easy to ignore—but untreated, it can cause nerve damage and spread to the spinal cord. It's curable with penicillin.

Viral STIs (herpes, HIV) are lifelong. The critical thing to understand:

"Even if you've never had symptoms of these things, you can still have them. Your partner can be asymptomatic and you can still get it."

Herpes affects somewhere between 25–40% of the population, and a significant portion of those people will never have a visible sore. Asymptomatic shedding means the virus can be transmitted even without any active outbreak. HIV follows the same pattern—people can carry it and spread it for years before showing symptoms.

The practical implication: if you or your partner have ever had unprotected sex with anyone else, there is a real possibility of asymptomatic infection. Getting tested at least once a year is the baseline recommendation for sexually active people. Condoms are the primary protection against STIs—not birth control pills, not "trusting" your partner, not the absence of symptoms.

How the pill works: estrogen and progesterone in the pill suppress GnRH, which suppresses FSH and LH—effectively stopping the cycle from triggering ovulation. The 3–5% failure rate is almost entirely user error: missed doses, wrong timing. The implant and IUD eliminate that variable.

IUDs in particular are worth understanding. Copper IUDs create inflammation in the uterine wall that prevents implantation. Hormonal IUDs release progesterone locally. Both last years, are removable when you're ready to get pregnant, and require essentially no ongoing action. For most healthy young people who want reliable protection without daily maintenance, this is the strongest option—but it's a conversation to have with a doctor, not a one-size prescription.

A critical medical note: birth control pills increase clotting risk, which raises stroke risk. They're generally not recommended for people over 35, and combining them with cigarette smoking multiplies the danger significantly. Dosing is also calibrated to a particular weight range. Individual health history matters—another reason "I'm on the pill" isn't a simple checkbox but a medical decision.

Common Conditions That Cause Pain (and the Shame Around Them)

A significant number of people experience pain or discomfort related to sex and spend years assuming they're "broken." In most cases, there's an actual medical explanation.

  • Phimosis: The foreskin is too tight to retract, causing painful erections. Rare, but real—and treatable.
  • Vaginismus: Involuntary spasm of the outer vaginal muscles, making penetration painful or impossible. Can have both physical and psychological components (including traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → history). Treatments include vaginal dilators and psychotherapy. Many people with this condition don't know it exists and spend years in pain and confusion.
  • Endometriosis: Uterine tissue grows in locations outside the uterus. When the hormonal signals trigger the necrotic shedding cycle, tissue scattered across the abdomen dies too—causing severe pain that can extend well beyond the pelvic area and profoundly affect mood. Often missed or dismissed.
  • Hypoestrogenism: Low estrogen or low estrogen response in vaginal tissue causes dryness and pain during intercourse. Treatable with lubricants or, in some cases, hormonal options.

The psychological pattern across all of these is similar: person experiences pain → assumes everyone else's experience is normal → concludes something is fundamentally wrong with them personally → shame prevents them from seeking help → the problem continues.

"Death grip syndrome" is not a formal medical diagnosis, but it describes something real: years of masturbation with a tight, dry grip can train your nervous system to require a specific kind of stimulation to reach climax. Sex provides different sensations—generally more lubrication, less pressure—and the body may struggle to respond. The fix involves retraining: lighter grip, more lubrication, and more partnered sex to allow the nervous system to recalibrate.

Common Pitfalls

  • Conflating "safe sex" with just "no pregnancy." A partner being on birth control says nothing about STI status. These are separate problems requiring separate solutions.
  • Assuming no symptoms means no infection. Many STIs are asymptomatic for extended periods. You can have them, spread them, and receive them without knowing.
  • Using pornography as a reference for normal. It isn't. Production involves pharmaceutical assistance, selective editing, and performance incentives that have nothing to do with ordinary sexual experience.
  • Delaying medical care out of embarrassment. A bacterial STI can be cleared with a week of antibiotics. Waiting does nothing except allow it to cause more damage.
  • Treating birth control choice as one-size-fits-all. The pill, implant, IUD, and other options each carry different considerations based on your health history, weight, lifestyle (ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by a disorder of attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition →, smoking, age), and relationship structure. This is a medical conversation.
  • Interpreting physical pain during sex as a character flaw. Pain is usually a symptom of something diagnosable and often treatable. It is not evidence that you or your body are defective.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Get tested. If you've had unprotected sex with any partner who wasn't exclusively with you and confirmed tested, book an STI screening. Once a year is the minimum for sexually active people. It's a simple blood and urine test. Do it.

  2. Have the actual conversation with your partner. Not "are you being safe?" but: Have you been tested? When? Have you ever had sex without a condom with anyone else? Are you on birth control? Do you know which kind and how it works? These questions feel awkward. Ask them anyway.

  3. Research your birth control options before your next doctor's appointment. Use the framework above—condoms for STI protection, plus a separate method for pregnancy prevention if needed. Bring your actual health history (smoking, weight, ADHD, age) into the conversation so your doctor can help you choose.

  4. If sex is painful for you—or has ever been—see a doctor. Not a therapist first, not the internet first. A physician first. Vaginismus, endometriosis, hypoestrogenism, and phimosis are all diagnosable conditions. You don't have to keep tolerating this.

  5. If you're experiencing premature ejaculation, try the behavioral approach: during sex, slow down as you approach climax, pause, let arousal decrease, then resume. Do this consistently. It takes time but has a real track record of improvement in committed relationships.

  6. Audit what shaped your expectations about sex. If pornography has been a primary reference point, recognize that it's a performance genre, not documentation. Notice where your expectations and your actual experience diverge—and treat that gap as a question worth asking a professional about, not a verdict on your worth.
