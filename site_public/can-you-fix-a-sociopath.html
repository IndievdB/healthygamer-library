Summary

TL;DR — You can't control what life hands you, but you can control what you do next. That choice, made repeatedly over time, is what determines your future.

Sometimes life deals you a genuinely terrible hand — an abusive parent, a toxic relationship, a painful rejection from someone whose approval you desperately want. None of that is your fault, and none of it is something you chose. What you do choose, moment to moment, is how you respond. That's the whole game.

This talk uses a real post from a 19-year-old navigating a brutal rejection from their father to unpack two practical frameworks: dharma (doing your duty — no more, no less) and karma (your present actions shape your future, even when you can't see how yet).

Dharma: Do Your Duty, Then Stop

Dharma means making a genuine, solid effort — not an infinite one.

If someone has hurt you, especially a parent, it's worth trying to repair that relationship. It's worth extending forgiveness, making contact, giving them chances to re-engage. That's doing your duty as a child, as a family member, as a human being. But dharma has a limit. It doesn't mean chasing someone's approval forever. It doesn't mean that if the relationship is broken, it's continuously your fault and you need to keep trying until it works.

"Dharma means making a solid effort. It doesn't mean making an effort until the end of time."

There's a useful framing here from a mentor: the doctor who goes the extra mile for a patient goes a mile too far. Your job is to go the mile you're supposed to go — do your duty — and then let the other person do theirs. If they don't meet you there, that's on them. You don't owe them another chance just because they eventually decide they want one.

"They had a year of chances, and that boat has sailed. You don't have to bring the boat back to shore just because they're a few years late."

This is liberating once it lands. You don't need to feel guilty for walking away from someone who had real opportunities to show up for you and chose not to. You tried. That matters. Give yourself credit for it and let them go.

Why You Still Want Their Approval (And Why That's Normal)

One of the crueler things about having an abusive or neglectful parent is that you still want their approval. You know, rationally, that they've treated you badly. You know the relationship is toxic. And yet some part of you still hopes they'll say something kind, still feels the sting when they don't.

That's not weakness. It's not stupidity. It's biology and it's human.

"It's very normal and natural to want the approval of people who are abusive to you. It's why people stay in abusive relationships. It's how we're wired."

We're built to need recognition and care from the people closest to us, especially parents. Wanting that from someone who can't or won't give it isn't a character flaw — it's just a painful part of being human. Recognizing this lets you stop beating yourself up for caring and start directing your energy somewhere more useful.

Karma: What You Do Now Builds What Comes Next

Karma isn't cosmic punishment. It's a simpler, more practical idea: your current actions plant seeds for your future circumstances. You can't control what's already happened to you, and you can't always control how much something hurts. But you can control what you do about it.

The 19-year-old in that post had an urge to drink and self-harm after getting a devastating message from their father. That urge makes complete sense — they were in real pain. Instead, they chose to post online, spend time with their girlfriend and cat, and be with their mom. Those are karmic choices. They don't eliminate the pain, but they build a life that can support you when the next hard thing comes.

"You're entitled to your actions. You're not entitled to the outcomes of your actions."

Here's what's easy to miss: the support structures that person had — the girlfriend, the cat, the relationship with their mom — didn't appear by accident. Each of those came from a previous difficult choice to engage rather than withdraw, to build rather than destroy. The positive karma accumulated over time, quietly, and then showed up exactly when it was needed.

This is why the smallest action matters. You don't see the effect right away, which is exactly why it's hard to trust the process. But five push-ups, a piece of fruit, a short walk, a journal entry — these are real seeds. There's solid evidence that physical activity and time outdoors improve mood and reduce anxiety, not dramatically in the moment, but measurably over time.

The key shift is this: stop letting your actions be dictated by the progress you can see, and start letting them be dictated by the direction you want to go. The scoreboard doesn't update instantly. That doesn't mean nothing is happening.

When You Lose the Fight, That's Okay Too

Some days the depression wins. Some days you give in to the drinking, or the spiral, or the self-destructive pattern you've been trying to break. That happens. It doesn't erase everything else.

"Just because you've lost that fight quite a few times doesn't mean you have to lose that fight tomorrow or today."

Compassion toward yourself isn't a soft idea — it's practical. Self-blame and shame drain the energy you need to make better choices next time. You don't have to be perfect to be moving in the right direction.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing duty with unlimited obligation. A lot of people either cut off difficult relationships too early (before genuinely trying) or stay in them far too long out of guilt. Dharma asks you to make a real effort — not a token one, but not an endless one either. The line is roughly: did I genuinely try? Did they have real chances? Then you've done your part.

Waiting for visible results before acting. Karmic seeds don't sprout immediately. If you only take positive action when you can see it working, you'll stop almost immediately. The action has to come first, and trust that the direction matters even when the destination isn't visible yet.

Thinking you're broken for wanting toxic people's approval. The pull toward an abusive parent's validation is not evidence that you're weak or irrational. It's evidence that you're human. Accepting that makes it easier to work with the feeling rather than being controlled by it.

Using past failures as evidence the future is fixed. It doesn't matter how many times you've changed direction, failed, or lost ground. The only question is what you do today.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your dharma. If there's a broken relationship in your life, honestly ask yourself: have I made a genuine effort to repair it? If yes, give yourself permission to stop carrying guilt for the outcome. If no, consider what one real attempt would look like — and make it, without expectation.

  2. Plant one small karmic seed today. Not a life overhaul — one thing. A walk, a piece of fruit, five push-ups, a few sentences in a journal. Do it without expecting to feel a dramatic difference. That's not the point yet.

  3. When you feel a destructive urge, name the fork. Notice the moment when two paths appear — one toward the harmful thing, one toward something neutral or constructive. You don't have to choose perfectly every time. But practice noticing that a choice exists.

  4. Get outside. There is meaningful evidence that sunlight and time in nature reduce anxiety and improve mood over time. A short walk is not nothing — it's a real intervention, just a quiet one.

  5. Write or talk about the pain instead of swallowing it. Processing difficult emotions through words — a post, a journal, a conversation — is itself a constructive action. It doesn't fix anything, but it channels the energy somewhere real rather than turning it inward.

  6. Redirect the "what's the point" thought. When you catch yourself thinking nothing I do matters, replace it with: what direction do I want to go today? Not where do I want to be in five years — just today. Then do one small thing in that direction.
