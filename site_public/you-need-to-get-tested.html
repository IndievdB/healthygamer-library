Why the Math Is Striking

Each of these conditions is individually relatively uncommon. But they're independent of each other, and there are several of them. When you multiply through the probabilities of not having each one:

  • 90% not diabetic
  • × 96% not hypothyroid
  • × 99% not B12 deficient
  • × 82% not vitamin D deficient (using the subclinical threshold)
  • × ~80% not sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → apneic
  • × ~95% not anemic

…you get roughly 52% of people being free of all of them. Which means roughly 48% of people have at least one.

This is not a small rounding error. It means that almost half the people reading this have a physiological issue that could be explaining symptoms they're attributing to their personality, their mindset, or a psychiatric condition.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Book a primary care appointment. If you haven't had a general check-up in over a year, schedule one. Frame it explicitly as: "I want to rule out physical causes of fatigue and mood issues." You don't need to be in crisis to do this.

  2. Ask for this specific panel of labs: - Fasting blood glucose and/or HbA1c (for insulin resistance / type 2 diabetes) - TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone, screens for hypo- and hyperthyroidism) - Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D) - B12 - CBC with differential (screens for anemia)

  3. Assess your sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → apnea risk before the appointment. Ask yourself: Do you snore? Do you wake up unrefreshed even after a full night? Do you feel sleepy throughout the day despite sleeping? Are you overweight or do you have a thicker neck? If yes to two or more, mention this explicitly to your doctor and ask about a sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → study.

  4. If you're a gamer who rarely goes outside, take vitamin D seriously. Get your level tested rather than guessing. If you're deficient, supplementation is cheap, widely available, and low-risk — but confirm the dose with your doctor based on your actual level.

  5. If you do see a therapist, bring your lab results. A good therapist will factor in what's happening physically. If something turns up, make sure your mental health provider knows — and make sure a physician is managing the physical side.

  6. If you're flagged as subclinical (not quite over the diagnostic threshold): Don't dismiss it. Ask your doctor whether a trial of treatment makes sense given your symptoms. Subclinical hypothyroidism, for example, is sometimes worth treating when a person is genuinely struggling — the answer depends on symptoms, not just numbers.
