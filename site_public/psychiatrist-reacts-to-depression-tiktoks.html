Summary

TL;DR — Coping with depression is a survival skill, but it isn't the same as fixing it. Most people can get substantially better, and one underrated path there is learning to live more authentically.

Depression is one of the most common human experiences, yet the conversation around it is often incomplete — either reduced to a chemical imbalance that a pill should fix, or treated as a permanent condition you simply manage day to day. Neither framing captures the full picture. This piece untangles what depression actually is, why people get stuck in coping mode, and what it might look like to genuinely recover rather than just survive.

The Extension Cord Problem: Coping vs. Fixing

Here's a useful way to think about the difference between coping and actually addressing depression.

Imagine your computer can't reach the wall outlet, so you run an extension cord across the floor to the other room. Problem solved — sort of. The cord is an eyesore, a tripping hazard, and a constant mild annoyance. But it works well enough that you never bother to move the computer. The "real" fix stays permanently on the back burner.

That's what coping with depression often looks like. People figure out over time that getting outside, getting sunlight, going for a walk, starting the day with a routine — these things reduce the weight enough to function. And that's genuinely good. Those habits work for real neurobiological reasons: sunlight affects vitamin D levels, exercise lowers cortisol and reduces inflammation, behavioral activation is an evidence-based treatment. The problem isn't that these strategies exist. The problem is what happens next.

Once you can cope, the urgency to dig deeper disappears. Worse, if you've already tried other approaches — therapy, medication, lifestyle changes — and they haven't fully worked, it's completely rational to conclude that coping is the best available option. That conclusion makes sense. It's also, for most people, probably wrong.

"The interesting thing that happens with people with depression is that a lot of them will learn to adapt, cope, and survive — because they're resilient, because they're strong, because they're adaptable, because they're intelligent. And once you learn how to cope, it goes on the back burner."

From clinical experience: roughly 90–95% of people who develop coping strategies for depression don't need to rely on them indefinitely after a year of real treatment. Things can permanently get better. That's not a guarantee — treatment doesn't work for everyone, and that's a genuine tragedy — but it does appear to be the outcome for most people. The tragedy is stopping at the extension cord.

Depression Is a Physical Illness Too

One of the more important gaps in how depression gets discussed is the assumption that it's purely a mental illness. It isn't.

Physiological factors that directly influence depression include: - Vitamin D levels (and sun exposure more broadly) - B12 and folate - Thyroid function - Inflammation - Cortisol levels

This matters for treatment. Antidepressant medication works remarkably well for roughly a third of people — genuinely life-changing. Another third get some benefit but not nearly enough. And for a third, SSRIs do very little. SSRIs are not magic, and the "neurochemical imbalance" framing is at best an incomplete explanation. Depression has a differential diagnosis — meaning different people experience it for different underlying reasons — and the treatment that helps one person may be irrelevant for another.

The takeaway isn't to abandon medication or to rely solely on sunlight and exercise. It's to hold both, and to be curious about what's actually driving your particular experience of depression rather than assuming your experience maps onto everyone else's.

When Depression Is Your Brain Working Correctly

This is the part that tends to surprise people: sometimes depression isn't a malfunction. It can be your brain doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

Consider someone who comes in saying they have no reason to live — they're isolated, they have no meaningful relationships, they feel deep shame, nothing feels worthwhile. Those statements might be cognitively distorted, yes. But sometimes they're also just true. In that case, prescribing a pill and sending them home isn't the answer. The answer is: let's work on building a life that actually feels worth living.

"Depression is your body saying: I don't want to be this character anymore. I don't want to hold up this avatar that you've created in the world — it's too much for me."

The framing of depression as deep rest — a concept that resonates with how some people describe their own experience — captures something real. When you've been performing a version of yourself for years, putting on a mask at work, holding up an image for the world, your system eventually runs dry. The retreat into depression can be the body's way of forcing a stop.

This doesn't mean depression is something to welcome or embrace indefinitely. But understanding it this way opens a different question: what if the exhaustion isn't about depression at all — what if it's about the performance?

Authenticity as a Treatment

If maintaining a false version of yourself is what drains you, the logical intervention is to stop doing that. Living more authentically — being more of who you actually are, rather than who you feel you need to be — consistently corresponds with reduced depression in clinical experience.

This is genuinely hard. Being authentic means: - Setting boundaries, which invites conflict - Being judged for who you actually are - Possibly giving up things you've built — stability, income, approval — that depend on the performance continuing - Saying what you actually want and feel instead of what keeps people comfortable

None of that is easy, and none of it happens overnight. But there's a meaningful difference between putting down the avatar and retreating from the world, versus putting down the avatar and staying present. The deep rest model involves both — setting down the mask and withdrawing. The question worth sitting with is: what if you set down the mask and didn't have to retreat? What if you stopped running out of energy because you stopped spending energy on pretending?

"Once they start living more authentic lives, they become less depressed — because you don't need to act anymore. You don't need that deep rest. Your gas tank is not empty."

Common Pitfalls

Assuming your depression experience is universal. Depression varies enormously in its causes and what responds to treatment. Someone sharing what worked for them — sunshine, exercise, cold showers, routine — is sharing one data point. It may or may not apply to you, and their confidence in it doesn't change that.

Mistaking coping for recovery. Developing good coping strategies is real progress and worth doing. But if life becomes about managing the condition rather than resolving it, the underlying issue remains. Coping and fixing are different things, and it's worth continuing to look for the latter even after you've gotten good at the former.

Giving up on treatment because it didn't work the first time. SSRIs alone don't resolve most depression. Failing on antidepressants doesn't mean you can't get better — it may just mean that particular tool wasn't the right match, or wasn't sufficient on its own.

Treating depression as purely psychological or purely physical. It's both, and different presentations lean in different directions. A one-size explanation misses real causes that could be addressed.

Confusing "I need to quit everything" with "I need to be more authentic." Authenticity doesn't always mean blowing up your life. It usually means small, consistent acts of being more honest — with yourself and with others — about what you actually value, need, and feel.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your coping strategies, then ask what's underneath them. Write down everything you currently do to manage depression. Then ask: am I doing these things instead of trying to fix it? What would it look like to address the root, not just the symptoms?

  2. Get a basic physiological workup. Ask your doctor to check vitamin D, B12, folate, and thyroid levels. These are cheap, easy to identify, and directly connected to mood. Don't skip this step just because depression feels psychological.

  3. Notice where you're performing. Pick one area of your life — work, a relationship, social media — and ask honestly: who am I pretending to be here? What would I do or say differently if I wasn't managing an image? You don't have to act on the answer immediately. Just observe.

  4. Distinguish between setting down the mask and retreating. The next time you feel the pull to withdraw from the world, pause and ask: is the exhaustion coming from being myself, or from not being myself? If it's the latter, experiment with setting down one layer of performance before retreating, and see what happens.

  5. Don't stop at coping. Keep the sunlight, the walks, the morning routine — those work, and they matter. And simultaneously, stay curious about whether deeper change is possible. Try one thing you haven't tried before: a different type of therapy, a conversation with a psychiatrist about causes rather than just symptoms, or a structured look at what in your life feels fundamentally misaligned with who you are.

  6. If you have suicidal thoughts, get specific about why. Not to dwell, but to identify what it is about your current life that feels unbearable. That answer often points directly at what needs to change — and that's workable information.
