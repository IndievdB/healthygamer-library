Why Survivors Stay Silent (and What "Making a Big Deal" Actually Costs)

When Adrianah describes not wanting to "make a big deal" after being touched while she was asleep at a party, she's not being passive — she's doing a very rational calculation. The person who assaulted her had far more reach and influence in her industry. She had moved across the country to build a career in that same space. Speaking up, even just saying "that made me uncomfortable," meant:

  • Inviting public scrutiny and repeated retelling of the event
  • Risking being blacklisted from events and communities she depended on
  • Opening herself up to having her entire personality and history picked apart for "clues" that it wasn't real

The cost of speaking is enormous and concrete. The benefit is abstract and uncertain. That math keeps a lot of people quiet for a long time.

What Victim-Blaming Actually Feels Like

Victim-blaming isn't just strangers online saying "what were you wearing." It has a specific psychological texture that's worth understanding.

After a sexual assault, most survivors are already blaming themselves. I shouldn't have gone to that party. I shouldn't have been in that situation. That self-blame is happening before anyone else says a word. So when other people then say those same things out loud — "you shouldn't have drunk," "you shouldn't have put yourself in that position" — they aren't introducing a new idea. They're amplifying a voice that's already there, making it louder and harder to argue with.

"I've already told myself these things for so long. So when they say it — what does that do?"

The cruelest version of this isn't the obvious trolls. It's people who seemed to care, sitting in a room saying "are you sure? We know our friend. He wouldn't do that." What that does is take the guilt you've just barely started to set down and hand it back to you repackaged: if you say something, you'll ruin his life, and we'll have to stop being his friend. Suddenly you're responsible not for what happened to you, but for the consequences of disclosing it.

"It's not the act that they committed — it's making you responsible for ruining someone's career."

The "It's Whatever" Reaction Is Normal

One of the most practically important things in this conversation: the most common reaction to sexual assault is not immediate distress. It's numbness. It's it's whatever, that happened, I'm moving on.

This matters for two reasons.

First, survivors often don't recognize what happened to them as assault right away — sometimes for years. Adrianah didn't process that what happened to her at 14 was assault until she was 17, after reading accounts from the Me Too movement. Until then, she was actively contorting herself to make it okay in her own mind: I liked him. It's fine. That's just how things go.

Second, when survivors later come forward and say something was assault, people use that initial "it's whatever" reaction against them: you said it wasn't a big deal before. But the story hasn't changed — only the vocabulary and emotional access have. Memory isn't a recording. It shifts as we process. That's not inconsistency; that's how human beings work.

How Trauma Shapes the Self Over Time

There's a useful image here: imagine a piece of you gets twisted out of shape by a traumatic experience. Instead of twisting that piece back, you twist everything else around it to compensate — to fit back into normal life. For a while this works. But then you try to connect with other people and you don't fit, and instead of tracing the problem back to the original injury, you conclude something is just fundamentally wrong with you.

That's the move Adrianah describes — not being able to form intimate connections and reading it as personal brokenness rather than an injury with a traceable origin. The self-blame loops back around.

On top of this, she grew up in a home where expressing emotional pain was unsafe. Showing weakness led to being accused of attention-seeking. So she learned to cry only in the shower, with the water running so no one could hear. That became the template: emotions are private emergencies you manage alone, and getting through them means suppressing them fast enough to function.

The result is a split between "strong Adriana" who gets things done and "weak Adriana" who feels things — as if feeling things and functioning are mutually incompatible. That split is exhausting to maintain. And it means the only time emotions come out is in explosive bursts, in private, before being quickly buried again.

The Fallout Is Often Worse Than the Event

This is the core insight worth carrying away.

Sexual assault is understood by most people as a single traumatic event. But what causes the deepest and most lasting damage is often everything that follows:

  • Friends who say "I'm so sorry" and then go like the perpetrator's tweets a week later
  • Being pushed into making a public statement before you're ready, by people whose interests weren't yours
  • Having your story "managed" by a boardroom of acquaintances who frame your options as ultimatums

The betrayal and abandonment in the aftermath — from friends, from systems, from people who claimed to be allies — is what compounds trauma into something that reshapes a person's entire relationship to trust, intimacy, and self-worth.

Common Pitfalls

Assuming the reaction should look traumatic. If someone says "it's whatever" after an assault, that is not evidence that nothing serious happened. Numbing and minimizing are extremely common initial responses. Using that calm against them later is a form of gaslighting.

Treating disclosure as the destination. Coming forward does not provide relief from the original pain. It opens a whole new set of battles. Expecting that "speaking your truth" will feel healing in a simple or immediate way sets survivors up for confusion when the opposite happens.

Confusing changing details with a false story. Memory is imperfect. Emotional access to a traumatic experience shifts over time. Someone describing the same event with different language or more specificity years later is not contradicting themselves — they're doing what human beings do.

Centering the perpetrator's feelings in the conversation. The moment someone asks "but do you really want to ruin his life?" they have made the survivor responsible for the consequences of someone else's actions. That's not a neutral question. It reloads the guilt.

Thinking sexual assault is mostly committed by strangers. The large majority of sexual assaults are committed by people the survivor knows — friends, colleagues, partners, family members. "He wouldn't do that, I know him" is precisely why survivors struggle to name what happened.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If someone discloses to you, lead with belief. Before anything else, say: "I believe you. I'm sorry this happened to you. Your feelings are valid." Don't problem-solve, don't investigate, don't accidentally ask questions that sound like doubt.

  2. Learn to recognize victim-blaming in the wild. Any sentence that begins with what the survivor should or shouldn't have done — "you shouldn't have been there," "you were drinking," "you knew what kind of person he was" — is victim-blaming, even when said with concern. Notice when you're about to say one of those things and stop.

  3. Stop using early minimization as evidence later. If someone once said "it wasn't a big deal" and now says it was assault, resist the urge to point that out as a contradiction. Ask instead: "What helped you understand it differently over time?"

  4. If you're a survivor: consider whether you've been splitting your emotional life into "strong" and "weak" modes. Notice if there are emotions you only let out in private, alone, in controlled bursts. This pattern protects you in the short term but prevents healing. Therapy — specifically trauma-informed therapy — can help integrate these parts so you don't have to keep them walled off.

  5. If you're in someone's life as they navigate disclosure: ask what they need before offering action. "Do you want me to help you figure out what to do, or do you just need me to listen right now?" Survivors who get pushed into premature public statements before they're ready often lose control of their own story. Protect their agency.

  6. Read or watch accounts of what the aftermath of sexual assault looks like — not just the event itself. The gap between what people expect recovery to look like and what it actually looks like is where a lot of harm happens, including from well-meaning friends and family.
