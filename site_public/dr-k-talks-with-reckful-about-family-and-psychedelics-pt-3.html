When Something Is Missing, Something Actually Is Missing

Reckful describes the holidays as lonely — even when he's with his family, something feels off. He keeps using the word "small" to describe his family. When that pattern gets pointed out, he realizes it's not really about the number of people. It's that his brother isn't there.

The takeaway is blunt: the reason you feel like something is missing during the holidays is because something is missing. That sounds obvious, but a lot of emotional work involves refusing to let yourself say that out loud. Naming the absence directly — rather than reframing it or explaining it away — is where processing starts.

The same principle applies to family gatherings that go through the motions. When everyone avoids mentioning the person who died, there's what gets described as a "miasma" — a low-level wrongness that everyone feels but no one names. The gathering becomes performance. The antidote isn't more people or more festivities. It's acknowledgment.

What Sharing Does That Silence Can't

There's a consistent principle running through this talk: things that stay hidden compound; things that get shared can transform.

Two examples illustrate this from opposite directions:

  • TraumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → that remains private and secret doesn't diminish over time. It grows. The longer it stays unwitnessed inside you, the worse it gets — even after the traumatic situation has ended.
  • Mantra in spiritual practice operates by the same logic in reverse: a mantra kept secret becomes stronger. The privacy concentrates it.

The mechanism isn't fully explained — it's described as an anthropological principle rather than a clinical mechanism — but the pattern holds across cultures: humans evolved to grieve publicly. Funerals exist for a reason. The grieving process is semi-public because there's something about witnessed grief that allows it to move, whereas private grief calcifies.

When Reckful reads his father's passage on stream, something shifts. He describes it as getting something off his chest. The reading isn't just painful the way it normally is — it has a quality of metabolism: turning raw grief into something that can be integrated rather than just stored.

"These negative feelings, when they exist within us and we don't show them to the world, they actually compound."

Letting Go Is Not the Same as Leaving Behind

One of the more careful distinctions made: you don't have a choice about losing someone. But you do have a choice about letting them go.

Letting go doesn't mean you stop loving them. It doesn't mean you pretend the loss didn't happen. What it means — and this is the harder thing — is that you stop organizing your life around the loss in ways that prevent you from living.

Reckful says he always assumed he wouldn't want children because he's scared they might kill themselves. That fear is understandable. But it also represents a refusal to move toward life — a way of staying loyal to the grief by not building anything new that could be taken away.

His father, in the book, describes feeling that returning to normal life would mean betraying his dead son. Living fully would mean leaving him behind. The problem is that this logic also means abandoning everyone still alive — the other son, the wife, everyone who needs him present.

There's no clean resolution offered here. But the point is made gently:

"You can decide that's the right thing for you and have children one day... and there's gonna be like the other side of the coin."

Integration doesn't erase sorrow. It holds sorrow alongside joy, rather than using sorrow as a reason to refuse joy.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the absence of crisis for integration. Burying grief or distracting from it can look like moving on. It isn't. The samskarA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from past experiences that gets stored in the mind and automatically activates in present situations, distorting your perception of what's actually happening.Full definition → doesn't disappear because you stop thinking about it; it just waits.

Assuming shared grief means public grief. The point isn't that you need to stream your feelings. It's that keeping grief entirely private tends to compound it. Sharing with even one trusted person — a therapist, a family member, a friend — can interrupt the cycle.

Conflating "letting go" with "forgetting" or "betraying." This is the trap Reckful's father falls into in the book and the trap Reckful partially falls into around having children. Choosing to live fully is not a vote against the person you lost.

Expecting psychedelics to do the work of practice. If a substance gives you insight but you don't build the mental habits to act on that insight consistently, the insight fades. The experience and the training are two different things.

Looking to someone else to validate your path. Reckful wants explicit endorsement of his psilocybin use. The pushback isn't about psilocybin specifically — it's about the pattern of needing external green lights to feel okay about your own choices. That pattern is worth examining regardless of the topic.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name what's actually missing. When you feel like something is "off" — at family gatherings, holidays, or just daily life — try to say what it is precisely, rather than just noting the feeling. Sometimes the feeling is pointing at a real absence that deserves to be named.

  2. Share one thing you've been carrying privately. Pick one experience, memory, or loss that has stayed largely hidden. Tell one person. It doesn't have to be dramatic — you're testing the principle that witnessed grief moves differently than silent grief.

  3. Breathe slowly and focus all your attention on that point for two to three minutes. Then lower your hand and continue focusing on that spot. Notice what, if anything, you feel. If a sensation arises, this may be a technique worth returning to daily.

  4. Practice dhaarana with something small. Pick one five-minute task — making tea, walking around the block, washing dishes — and commit to keeping your full attention on it. Every time your mind wanders, gently bring it back. Don't judge the wandering. Just redirect. Do this once a day for a week and notice whether the redirection gets easier.

  5. If your family has a shared loss that goes unspoken at gatherings, consider whether you could name it. Not necessarily in a big confrontational way — sometimes it's as simple as saying "I've been thinking about [person] a lot lately." The goal isn't to make everyone cry.
