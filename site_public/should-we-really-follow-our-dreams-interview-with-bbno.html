Summary

TL;DR — Making it as an artist isn't romantic; it's volume, consistency, and learning to hold two contradictory truths at once without breaking.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and bbno$ (Alex), a Canadian musician who broke through after years of grinding. What emerges isn't a feel-good success story — it's an honest look at what the creative life actually costs, what keeps you sane inside it, and why the standard advice about "following your dreams" skips the parts that matter most.

The Unsexy Formula for Making It

When pushed to explain what it takes to succeed in music, Alex's first instinct wasn't passion or vision — it was a short pause and a grimace.

The actual answer:

  • Make a lot of music. Alex has written roughly 700 songs. About 100 have ever been heard publicly. The rest were necessary garbage that built the skill to make the good ones.
  • Compete with the volume. There are 46,000 new songs uploaded to Spotify every single day. A tiny fraction ever crosses 2,000 streams. You don't beat that math with one careful album every three years.
  • Stay tapped in to what's current. Knowing what sounds good in the abstract isn't enough — you also have to know what sounds good right now, which means paying attention to where popular music is moving.
  • Work every channel. Social media, collaborations, live shows — none of it is optional decoration around the "real" art. It's all the job.

"You make good food all the time, new recipes — why wouldn't people come to the restaurant?"

The 10,000-hours principle is real here: Alex puts it plainly — he completed his first 10,000 hours before he blew up, not because of it.

The Psychological Trap Nobody Warned About

Alex had a song called "La La La" go parabolic. He thought it was the beginning of permanent lift-off. Then it came down, and he wasn't prepared for what that felt like.

The problem isn't ingratitude. It's how the mind works: it doesn't compare where you are now to where you started — it compares where you are now to your most recent peak. So even when the net result of your career is enormously positive, a dip from a recent high feels like failure. Rationally knowing you're ahead doesn't stop the mood from crashing.

This is just as true for streamers watching their viewer count fluctuate, or anyone whose livelihood runs on metrics. The dashboard becomes the mirror you measure yourself in, and that mirror has a brutal short memory.

"Your mind is so damn relative — it always compares to what you've done recently, as opposed to looking at the summation of what you have to be grateful for."

What helped Alex was developing a support group that doesn't yes-man him — friends who will genuinely tell him a song is bad, and who push him to step back from the grind when it's getting to be too much. Having people around you who are honest and who care about your well-being is not a soft bonus; it's load-bearing infrastructure.

The Tightrope: Nothing Matters / Everything Matters

One of the more interesting tensions in Alex's life is that he holds two seemingly opposite beliefs simultaneously — and needs both of them to function.

On one side: nothing matters. Music doesn't need to chart. Songs can be made and never heard. The only things that truly matter are family, real relationships, and personal growth. This isn't nihilism — it's a kind of detachment that protects him from being destroyed by the volatility of social metrics.

On the other side: everything matters. In the music industry, clout is real. Output is real. Showing up consistently is real. You can't just make art in a vacuum and expect an audience to materialize.

Living between those two is the actual job. Let the "nothing matters" side go too far and you stop putting in the work. Let the "everything matters" side take over and the analytics start eating your nervous system.

"You've got two options — you gotta go harder, or you can never slow down. So there's just one."

Alex's way of managing this isn't a perfect system. It's meditation, exercise, ginger tea, physical awareness of when his nervous system is "running too hot," and — critically — the ability to feel the difference between a break he genuinely needs and laziness. He describes it like hunger: you don't have to figure out when you're hungry, your body tells you.

The Flow State Is Real, But You Can't Command It

Alex describes his best creative moments as almost euphoric — ideas come out fast, everything falls into place, there's a certainty that the song is going to connect. This isn't analysis or calculation. It feels more like being a conduit than being in control.

The condition for that state seems to be a combination of: - Absorbing a lot of inputs (trends, other music, life experience, emotions that have been "bottled up") - Letting them sit and ferment — often a change of scenery, sunlight, or an emotional release triggers the outpouring - Getting the ego and the metrics out of the room — the best songs come fast, not from deliberate editing toward perfection

The grinding, analytical side of the work — trend-watching, social media, touring — is real and necessary. But the actual creation of music that connects seems to happen in a different mode entirely. You prepare the conditions, then get out of your own way.

What won't reliably get you there: trying to force it, or being in a depleted physical state. Sleep deprivation, gut problems, voice damage from touring — all of that chips away at the baseline you need to access that creative state. Taking care of your body isn't self-indulgence; it's protecting your ability to do the thing you're actually here to do.

What Alex Respects About Kendrick Lamar

Alex talks at length about what makes Kendrick exceptional, and it's worth pulling out because it points to something about artistic ambition more broadly.

What Kendrick does that almost nobody else manages at his scale: he makes albums that are albums — front-to-back, contextualized, coherent works where each song exists in relationship to the others. In an era of singles, playlists, and algorithmic shuffle, that's almost a countercultural act.

The catch is that you can only afford to do it once you have the fan base to support it. If you're still building, standalone singles will always outperform album tracks numerically. But the aspiration — to make something where the whole is larger than the sum of its parts, where you're painting a picture rather than firing off one-liners — is worth holding onto, even if you can't execute it yet.

Alex's stated goal is to make exactly that kind of record next: go to an island, turn the phone off, and build something he's genuinely proud of as a complete work of art.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until you're "ready" to release. Alex has watched musicians in his city spend four years on an album with no fan base to release it to. Volume of output builds the audience that will care when you make something ambitious.

Confusing the analytics high with the creative high. The feeling when a song blows up and the feeling when you make a great song are different experiences. One is external and temporary; the other is the reason you're doing this. Conflating them is how you end up chasing metrics instead of making good work.

Assuming successful artists feel successful. The roller coaster doesn't get smoother with more success — the stakes just change. Nobody prepares you for the psychological cost of a peak followed by a plateau, or for the fact that your mind will focus on the descent rather than how far you've climbed.

Using love for your work as an excuse not to rest. If you love what you do, it's easier to sacrifice yourself for it, which means the warning signs of burnout get rationalized away. Alex's dad told him directly: you're burning too hard. The people around you will often see it before you do.

Going it alone or surrounding yourself with yes-people. Both are traps. You need people who will tell you the song is bad, who will tell you to go outside, who care about your well-being independently of your output.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Raise your output volume before worrying about quality. If you're making music, writing, or creating anything — set a target for raw volume over the next month. The skill to recognize good work comes from making a lot of bad work first.

  2. Audit your self-assessment source. Notice whether you're measuring yourself against your recent peak or against where you started. Once a week, write down three things that are genuinely better than they were a year ago.

  3. Build in a "nervous system check." Before you decide to push through or grind harder, pause and ask: what is my body actually telling me right now? Practice recognizing the physical signals of overload — not just the cognitive ones.

  4. Establish a support circle with real feedback loops. Identify one or two people in your life who will tell you the truth about your work and your wellbeing. Actively ask them for honest responses, not encouragement.

  5. Separate your "absorption" mode from your "creation" mode. Spend deliberate time taking in inputs — trends, other artists' work, life experience — and then protect separate time for creation where metrics and strategy are off the table. Don't try to do both at once.

  6. Set a physical baseline you protect. Pick the two or three things that most reliably keep you functional (sleep, exercise, diet, whatever it is) and treat them as non-negotiable before the schedule gets built around them — not as what's left over after everything else.

  7. Name the long-term thing you actually want to make. Alex knows he wants to make a front-to-back album that represents his best work. That clarity shapes every smaller decision. What is the version of that for you — and what would you need to protect and prepare in order to make it?
