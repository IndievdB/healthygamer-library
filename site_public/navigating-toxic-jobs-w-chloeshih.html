The Underappreciated Overachiever Archetype

Chloe coins a phrase that lands hard: underappreciated overachievers. These are highly competent, deeply loyal people who show up, move mountains, and get the job done — and consistently find that their contributions are taken for granted, their names left off press releases, and their promotions given to someone else.

The profile looks like this:

  • Extreme reliability — if you're on their team, the work will get done, even if it costs them sleepSleep is the brain's primary maintenance window — the period during which short-term memories stored in the hippocampus (your RAM) get written into long-term memory (your hard drive).Full definition →
  • Strong facilitation instinct — they listen first, gather everyone's perspective, and find the best answer democratically rather than asserting their own take
  • High loyalty — they pour their identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → into their organization
  • Low visibility — they don't self-promote, don't grab credit, and often don't know how to make their work legible to people with power

The painful irony: these are exactly the qualities that get exploited. Because you're so reliable, people treat you as infrastructure. You become the person who "gets it done" — which means you also become the person who takes the meeting notes, plans the happy hour, and absorbs the extra scope. And when any of that falls short, even though it was never officially your job, it gets pinned on you.

"Don't plan the happy hour. No matter what they say, don't take the meeting notes. They're going to ask you. Say no to that. And let the awkwardness sit."

The structural flip side of this archetype is the overappreciated underachiever — someone who speaks first, sets the tone, makes their opinion visible, and gets credit that doesn't entirely match their output. They are not necessarily bad people; they have just learned to communicate in the language that power responds to. More on that below.

What Actually Drives Workplace Toxicity

The most recurring dynamic in Chloe's story isn't random cruelty — it's a specific psychological trigger. She consistently presents as warm, bubbly, and approachable, which leads people to cast her as a "support character." The moment she steps outside that role — asking a reasonable question in a product review, requesting a promotion she's earned, trying to transfer to a different team — the response is shockingly disproportionate.

"I think I was perceived as someone who could make them look good. And when I started to have my own opinions, it was like: stay in your lane."

This maps onto a broader pattern visible across her career: someone carries a lot of unresolved emotional weight, and Chloe — through no particular action — becomes the target onto which it detonates. Walls of all-caps text. Being called a coward for wanting to change teams. Getting yelled at for asking about a promotion she'd been working toward for two years while her manager was "having a bad day."

These responses are disproportionate, which is the tell. Disproportionate reactions usually mean you've triggered something dormant. The problem isn't what you did in the moment; it's that you've accidentally released pressure that was already there.

The clearest structural example: at the startup, executives were openly jockeying for power in a very visible Game of Thrones style. Chloe, being reliably competent and loyal, was recruited by multiple competing factions simultaneously. She was, in her own words, "on both teams" — not scheming, just useful to everyone. When things went badly, she was positioned as the scapegoat for cultural failures she didn't create.

The Pattern That Follows You

One of the harder things to sit with in Chloe's story is how consistent the pattern is. Dance teams in college. Nonprofits. Multiple tech companies. Content creator social circles. In almost every environment, the same arc: she pours herself in, performs at a high level, gets treated poorly by whoever holds power, and eventually exits.

There are two easy stories to tell here. The first: she's just unlucky and has been surrounded by bad people. The second: she's the common variable and must be causing it.

Both are too simple.

What's more accurate is that certain constellations of traits reliably evoke certain reactions from certain kinds of people. Being genuinely competent, visibly warm, and initially deferential makes you attractive to people who want a capable support character. Being outspoken about process and quality, once trust is established, then reads to those same people as a threat or a betrayal. This is not a character flaw — but it is a pattern worth understanding, because the moment you conclude "this has nothing to do with me," you've handed away whatever power you actually had to change the situation.

"To say that you have no influence in this is one of the most dangerous mistakes you can make. Because the moment you say there's nothing I can do about this — if you have some power you can exercise — you give up your power in that moment."

The language of fault is the wrong frame here. Fault implies moral failure. What's actually happening is closer to karmaKarma is the principle that all effects have causes and all causes produce effects — nothing more, nothing less.Full definition → in the Buddhist sense — a set of actions producing predictable consequences. Some of those actions are genuinely virtuous. Some are patterns that could be adjusted without compromising who you are.

Communicating in the Language of Power

One of the most practically useful insights Chloe shares comes from her time at Discord, where she had to stop facilitating and start directing.

At larger companies with many stakeholders, the instinct to gather all perspectives and synthesize them democratically can actually undermine you. When a team is small and you're the product lead, people are looking to you for direction. Asking "what does everyone think?" reads as uncertainty, not openness.

More broadly, different people and organizations receive information in fundamentally different ways:

  • Some respond to metrics and KPIs
  • Some respond to a tight two-to-three sentence narrative
  • Some need a story before they can internalize a number
  • Some need to feel that you've anticipated their objections before they can trust your direction

Defaulting to the format you think best without reading what your audience actually needs is how technically excellent work fails to land. The person who says three sentences in the format the CEO processes wins the room over the person who gave a fifteen-minute data-dense presentation — not because their work was better, but because they spoke the right language.

"Your job as a product person is to know how each stakeholder receives information, code switch, and know what are trigger topics. That's how you move approvals forward."

This isn't manipulation — it's literacy. And it's something underappreciated overachievers often lack, not because they're incapable, but because they're focused on the quality of the work rather than the politics of how the work gets perceived.

Not getting promoted wasn't disappointing; it was evidence that she was worthless. The startup that imploded wasn't a company that failed; it was her failure.

This is how a lot of people in tech (and beyond) are operating. The career becomes the container for all self-worth, which means a layoff doesn't just cost you a job — it costs you your sense of who you are.

Not as hobbies that distract from career, but as genuine sources of self-worth. So when the Discord layoff came, the container didn't shatter — because it wasn't the only container.

Practical advice for people without a content backup:

  1. Know your financial runway. Understand your severance, your COBRA options, and how much time you actually have. The financial panic amplifies everything else.
  2. Tell people. The instinct to hide it — even from partners, roommates, family — is extremely common and extremely costly. Hiding it means you can't receive help. And receiving help from someone actually bonds them to you more strongly than helping them does.
  3. Own the narrative publicly, if you can. Chloe posted about her layoff the night it happened — not as a victim, not as a warrior, just as someone being honest about an uncertain moment. That honesty opened doors.
  4. Don't internalize the company's failure as your failure. Layoff decisions at large companies are often less targeted than they feel. Spreadsheets, randomization, budget math. It is not always a verdict on you specifically.
  5. Let your support network actually support you. Being alone through this doesn't protect you. It just makes everything harder.

The Victim-to-Hero Arc (and What Comes After)

Chloe describes a psychological journey that maps onto a pattern that comes up repeatedly:

  • Stage one: I'm the victim. These things happen to me. I have dark stories. I've been wronged.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking "it's not my fault" for "I have no role in this." These are not the same thing. Something can be genuinely unfair and you can still have patterns that make it more likely to happen to you. Giving up agency in the name of not blaming yourself is one of the most costly mistakes you can make.

Pouring your entire identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → into your job. This is especially common in high-achievement tech culture, where TC, title, and company name become the whole self-concept. The problem isn't ambition — it's single-point-of-failure identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → construction.

Doing the extra work that wasn't asked of you, then feeling resentful when it isn't rewarded. The happy hour, the swag, the meeting notes — nobody asked you to do these things, you got tapped because you're reliable, and now you're the organizer instead of the strategist. Learn to let the silence sit.

Communicating in your preferred format rather than the format your audience processes. The work matters, but if it can't be received, it doesn't land. This is a skill, not a compromise of integrity.

Staying in the victim or hero narrative too long. Both are stories you tell about yourself rather than ways of simply being yourself. The most authentic version of you is available when you're not performing either.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → pillars. List the things that contribute to your sense of self-worth. If your job is more than two-thirds of the list, start deliberately building the others now — before a layoff makes it urgent.

  2. Name your communication defaults. When you present work or make a case for something, what format do you default to — data, narrative, questions, synthesis? Then identify: is that the format the people you're trying to influence actually respond to? If not, experiment with theirs.

  3. Stop volunteering for invisible labor. The next time someone asks you to plan something social, take the notes, or handle logistics because "you're so good at it" — practice letting the silence sit. Say nothing, or say "I don't have the bandwidth for that right now." Notice what happens.

  4. Tell one person. If you're currently dealing with job loss, a difficult work situation, or shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → about your career circumstances and hiding it — tell one person this week. Not to perform vulnerability, just to let one real person into the actual situation.

  5. Map your reaction patterns. Think back across jobs, schools, or organizations. If there's a recurring dynamic — being treated as a support character who overstepped, or routinely not getting credit, or regularly being on the receiving end of disproportionate anger — write it out without framing it as "I was wronged" or "I deserve better." Just describe what happened. What actions preceded each incident? What signals might you have been sending?

  6. Practice asserting a take before gathering everyone's input. In your next team setting, try leading with your own direction rather than opening with "what does everyone think?" Notice the difference in how the room responds. Being a good listener and being a clear leader are not mutually exclusive — but you may need to practice shifting between them deliberately.

  7. If you've been laid off, crunch the numbers before catastrophizing. Calculate your actual runway including severance, savings, and any freelance options. Financial panic distorts everything. Knowing the real number gives you a timeline to work with instead of a cliff to fall off.
