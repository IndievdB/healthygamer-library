Why Avoiding Suffering Makes Things Worse

Western psychology tends to treat suffering as a problem to be eliminated. If you're depressed, the goal is to become undepressed. If you're anxious, the goal is to stop feeling anxious. The underlying logic is simple: move away from pain, move toward pleasureThe experience of pleasure that comes from satisfying desires — roughly associated with dopamine and the brain's reward circuitry.Full definition →.

But there's a flaw in that logic that shows up clearly in gaming addiction. Gaming is, at its core, a way to escape discomfort — boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition →, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, failure, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →. And yet for most people who game compulsively, the gaming itself creates more of those things. The very coping mechanism makes the original wound worse.

Buddhism offers a different frame. Rather than saying "you deserve to feel good," it starts from the uncomfortable premise that suffering and death are the two things every human being is genuinely entitled to. That's not nihilism — it's a setup for a more honest question: if you can't outrun suffering, what do you actually do with it?

The answer is Dharma.

What Dharma Actually Means

Dharma is a Sanskrit word that translates roughly as duty or responsibility. The idea is that instead of organizing your life around avoiding pain or maximizing pleasure, you organize it around what you're here to do — your obligations to the people and world around you.

This might sound abstract, but the psychological mechanism behind it is concrete. When people act in service of something greater than themselves, they access a kind of strength that they simply can't generate by trying to feel better. Think about what parents are capable of: they'll sacrifice sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition →, money, comfort, and sometimes far more, without hesitation, for their children. The source of that capacity isn't willpower. It's rootedness in duty.

"If you as a gamer can figure out what your Dharma is, if you can be rooted in your Dharma and grounded in it, you'll be amazed at how much strength you get out of that."

This isn't limited to parenting. People who've accomplished extraordinary things throughout history — for good or ill — have typically been driven by a cause larger than personal comfort. The energy that Dharma unlocks is real, and it's available to anyone who finds something genuinely worth serving.

Gamers, interestingly, already understand sacrifice. In games, players routinely give up resources, time, and in-game advantages for teammates or objectives they care about. That capacity exists. The question is whether it's pointed at anything that matters outside the game.

The Real Problem: No Meaning, No Fuel

For a lot of young people deep in gaming, the hard truth when they stop and look at their lives is that there isn't a clear answer to the question what am I here to do? There's no one who depends on them. No project they're building. No cause they're serving.

That's not a character flaw — it's a situation. But it's also the root of the problem. Without meaning, there's no motivational fuel. And without fuel, the default is to return to whatever gives the fastest sense of reward, which for most people in this situation is the game.

"The second you figure out what your meaning is, you're going to start to get more motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →, you're gonna start to get that fuel — you'll start to get energy."

This reframes addiction treatment entirely. The goal isn't just to stop gaming. The goal is to fill the void that gaming is filling — not with another distraction, but with actual responsibility and meaning.

Why Your Analytical Mind Can't Solve This Alone

Here's where gamers specifically get stuck. Gaming selects for and rewards analytical thinking — pattern recognition, logical deduction, optimization. Gamers tend to be smart, and they apply that intelligence to questions about their own lives.

But meaning doesn't come from analysis. You can't think your way to Dharma. The logical conclusion of "I should go for a hike to find meaning" is "that's irrational, hikes don't produce meaning." And that conclusion is technically correct and completely useless.

There are parts of the mind — more intuitive, more emotional, less verbal — that are capable of processing experience and surfacing meaning, but only if you give them something to work with. Those parts don't engage when you're sitting in a chair in front of a screen. They engage when you're out in the world, taking in new sensory input, being physically present somewhere that isn't your room.

Nobody can fully explain why sitting by a stream, going to an art museum, or spending time with people produces insight. But it does. The brain takes in a diversity of experience and, somewhere below the level of conscious reasoning, starts making connections. Meaning emerges not from thinking harder but from living wider.

"You will be amazed at what your brain is capable of when you give it a diversity of experience."

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until you feel motivated to go outside. Motivation doesn't precede action here — it follows it. You won't feel ready. Go anyway.

Expecting a specific activity to "work." The point isn't that hiking gives you your life purpose in some mystical way. The point is that novel experience creates the conditions for your non-analytical mind to do its job. Any experience counts.

Treating Dharma as an intellectual exercise. You can't find your duty by thinking about what your duty should be. You find it by living more and paying attention to what you feel responsible for when you're actually in the world.

Assuming that because no one currently depends on you, Dharma is irrelevant. The absence of current responsibility doesn't mean there's nothing to move toward. It means you need more experience, not less.

Confusing symptom treatment with root treatment. Cutting screen time or using app blockers might help at the margins, but they don't address the absence of meaning that makes gaming so appealing in the first place.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write down your responsibilities. Sit down with a piece of paper and answer honestly: What is my duty as a human being? Who, if anyone, depends on me? What was I put on this earth to do? Don't judge the answers or the lack of them. The point is to make the current state visible.

  2. Notice if the page is mostly blank. If you can't fill it, that's diagnostic information — not a reason for despair, but a signal that you need more experience in the world before the answers can emerge.

  3. Do one thing outside your usual environment this week. It doesn't matter what. Volunteer somewhere. Go to a museum. Meet someone for coffee. Take a walk somewhere you haven't been. The specific activity is less important than the fact of being somewhere new and present.

  4. While you're out, carry one quiet question. Don't try to answer it analytically. Just let it sit in the background: Do I have any responsibility here? Is there anything I feel drawn to protect, contribute to, or be part of? Pay attention to what comes up, emotionally rather than logically.

  5. Repeat the experience with variety. Different places, different people, different contexts. You're giving your intuitive mind a richer dataset to work with. The goal over time is to notice patterns in what lights something up — those patterns point toward Dharma.

  6. Return to the paper periodically. As you accumulate experience, revisit what you wrote. Add to it. Let it become a living document of what you're discovering matters to you.
