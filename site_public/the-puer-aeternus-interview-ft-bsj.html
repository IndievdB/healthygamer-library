Summary

TL;DR — Puer aeternus (the eternal boy) is often a symptom pointing at something deeper: a wound that taught you your worth is conditional on being perfect. This conversation traces one person's journey from recognizing the classic puer patterns — keeping options open, avoiding dreary work, hedging bets — to uncovering the root that fed all of it.

BSJ, a former Dota 2 pro and content creator, reached out after recognizing himself in the puer aeternus framework: the dread of ordinary tasks, the inability to commit to one path, the sense that grinding work is beneath him. What unfolds goes somewhere neither person planned. By the end, the puer stuff is almost beside the point — because BSJ has already largely moved through it. The real material is what was underneath all along.

What Puer Actually Looks Like From the Inside

Most people who fit the puer pattern don't walk around thinking "I believe I'm above this." The internal experience is subtler and more slippery. It shows up as:

  • A task feeling not worthy of your time, even when you consciously know it matters
  • Doing six things at once so that when none of them fully lands, there's always a built-in excuse
  • Throwing in the towel the moment conditions aren't ideal — re-rolling the starting position until it's perfect rather than playing a flawed hand
  • Attributing your lack of results to everyone around you: if my teammates had done what they were supposed to do, I'd be playing at the highest level

That last one is worth sitting with. The puer version of potential is easy to live up to — as long as everyone else does their job perfectly. It's a way of keeping a pristine image of yourself intact while the external world absorbs the blame.

"Your potential is easy to live up to if everybody else does what they're supposed to do."

The teammate-blaming meme in competitive games isn't just a gaming phenomenon. It's a recognizable pattern of absolving yourself of responsibility by pointing at circumstances. The flip side of this is blaming your past self — treating a mistake at minute five as the reason you stop trying at minute twenty-five. The logic is: I should have just avoided this situation, so there's no point learning how to navigate it now. You'd rather restart from a clean position than solve the problem in front of you.

The 100 or 0 Problem

One of the clearest manifestations BSJ describes: rehearsing a conversation in Dutch a hundred times on the way to a coffee shop, then switching to English the moment the barista gives any signal that he's not a native speaker.

This is the perfectionism operating in real time. It's not just about Dutch. The same mechanism shows up in:

  • Making a mistake in a seven-step combo and deliberately failing the remaining four steps
  • Planning a perfect trip to Disneyland and spending the whole time anxious that it's not matching the version in his head — even while genuinely enjoying himself
  • Quitting a game path entirely once conditions aren't pristine

The pattern is binary: if it can't be 100, it becomes 0. Not 90, not 70 — zero. And because zero is easier to achieve than 100, it gets chosen constantly. What looks like laziness or avoidance from the outside is actually an extremely high and rigid internal standard that makes anything short of perfection feel like failure.

"If it's not 100, you'd rather have it be zero."

This also explains the hedging — doing five things simultaneously. If you're spread across five commitments, you can never really fail at any one of them. There's always a reason things didn't go perfectly. It creates internal excuses preemptively.

Where Perfectionism Comes From

About halfway through, a simple question lands differently than expected: What makes perfection so important to you?

The answer, after a pause: I think I was ingrained with an idea that the more perfect I was, the more my dad would love me.

This is the load-bearing piece. The perfectionism isn't a personality quirk or a productivity strategy — it's a survival adaptation. When the message a child receives is that celebration is always conditional ("you made eight out of ten free throws; let's talk about the two you missed"), the child learns that love and approval are things to be earned through performance. And earned imperfectly at that, because the bar keeps moving.

The cruelty of this isn't malice — BSJ is clear that his father loved him and did his best. The damage doesn't require bad intentions. A teammate can ruin your game without meaning to.

"Your game is still [messed up] and that's still going to be an L when you're done. That doesn't change."

The most striking moment in the conversation is a track meet story. BSJ started the final relay leg five to ten meters behind, ran one of the finest races of his life, and lost by a hundredth of a second. His coach's response: "You did all that just to lose?" A random official — someone BSJ had never met — walked over and said it was one of the finest runs he'd seen. BSJ cried. He still tears up telling the story.

That stranger gave him something for free that he had been working his whole life to earn from one specific person.

Blame as a Path to Healing — Not a Verdict

A counterintuitive move: the suggestion to try blaming his dad. Not to demonize him, not to strip away the genuine love and care that was also present — but because:

"You can't forgive if you never blame. If we never admit that there was a problem, we can never forgive and move past it."

BSJ pushes back instinctively. He's built a sophisticated framework in which nothing is really anyone's "fault," because fault implies sole responsibility, and sole responsibility lets you avoid examining your own role. It's a genuinely adaptive intellectual structure — and also a way of never having to feel angry at the people who hurt him.

The deeper point: when you don't put blame where it belongs, it ends up with you. When the mid feeds and you blame yourself for the loss, there is no amount of self-correction that fixes the actual problem. Scapegoating yourself while protecting others prevents any real growth from happening.

What makes this hard to see from the inside is that the refusal to blame was learned very early, and it has since been intellectualized into something that looks like maturity. "I don't blame anyone; I only ask what part I played" sounds healthy. Sometimes it is. But when it becomes a structure that makes it impossible to acknowledge a legitimate injury, it stops serving you.

The Loneliness of Growth

A theme that emerges near the end: as BSJ has genuinely worked through the puer patterns — committing to one project, moving in with his girlfriend, building something that matters to him — he's found himself increasingly disconnected from the people around him.

"The more I've learned, I feel like I'm less understood by the people I interact with regularly."

He's never said it out loud before, and it feels contradictory to say it now when his relationship is going well. But that's how it actually works. Leveling up doesn't mean arriving somewhere with a full new social world waiting. It often means a period where the old connections no longer fit and the new ones haven't been built yet.

This loneliness isn't a sign something is wrong. It's a sign something is changing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking intellectual frameworks for emotional processing. BSJ knows, analytically, that being hard on himself doesn't help. He can explain it clearly. But that top-down knowledge isn't the same as working through the thing that generates the behavior. You can reframe your way around pain without ever touching it.

  • Confusing "no one is at fault" with emotional health. Releasing blame can be wisdom. It can also be a way of never feeling the anger that belongs somewhere specific. If you've built a sophisticated philosophy of non-blame, it's worth asking whether that philosophy is protecting you or protecting someone else.

  • Thinking puer is the final destination. Moving past the eternal boy patterns — committing to things, stopping the hedging, taking the plunge — is real progress. But it tends to uncover whatever was underneath. The puer patterns are often adaptive responses to a deeper wound, not the wound itself.

  • Expecting pride to feel automatic. If you were taught that approval must be earned and the bar was always moved, pride doesn't accumulate passively. You have to actively work to recall it. And when someone offers it freely, it can hit with a force that seems disproportionate — because it's filling something that's been empty for a long time.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your 0-or-100 moments. For one week, notice when you abandon a task, conversation, or game after making a mistake. Ask: am I learning to solve the problem in front of me, or am I focused on the fact that I shouldn't have made the mistake in the first place?

  2. Separate the analytical from the felt. When you catch yourself explaining why a behavior is unhelpful, pause and ask: what does this actually feel like? Not what you should feel, not what you'd tell someone else — what is the raw experience? Practice naming that before moving to analysis.

  3. Trace your perfectionism to its origin. Ask yourself: when did perfectionism start mattering to me? What did it get me? What did it protect me from? You don't need a therapist to sit with those questions, though a therapist helps.

  4. Try, specifically, to feel anger at the people who deserve it. This is not about blaming them publicly, confronting them, or canceling the love you have for them. It's an internal exercise. Think of a specific moment where someone let you down. Allow the emotion of that wasn't okay to exist, without immediately moving to "but they did their best." Both things can be true. Practice holding them simultaneously.

  5. Let the lonely period be what it is. If you're growing faster than your current social environment, resist the urge to either shrink back or to paper over the loneliness with busyness. Name it. It's a sign of transition, not failure.

  6. If you have an unresolved relationship with a parent, consider what it would mean to have one honest conversation — not to assign blame as a verdict, but to name what your experience was. If that person struggles to hold the conversation, that's information too. You don't need their acknowledgment to acknowledge it yourself.
