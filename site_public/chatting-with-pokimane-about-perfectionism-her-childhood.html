The Faucet, Not the Bucket

When you feel like crying all the time and the crying never actually resolves anything, you're not dealing with a full bucket of emotion that needs emptying. You're dealing with a faucet — a continuous source of pressure that refills almost immediately.

This matters because most advice about emotional release assumes the bucket model: let it out and you'll feel better. But if your distress is regenerating, the problem isn't that you haven't cried enough. The problem is what's producing the pressure in the first place.

The Disease of "The Shoulds"

The central concept here is what gets called "a case of the shoulds" — an orientation toward life that is governed entirely by what you should be, should do, should feel, and should have accomplished by now.

This isn't just perfectionism in the casual sense. It functions like a disease with predictable symptoms:

  • If you accomplish the should → resentment (you sacrificed something real for an external standard)
  • If you fail the should → shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → (you didn't meet the standard)
  • The should is never satisfied, so you move compulsively to the next fixable problem

"You've got a wolf the size of an elephant and you just can't feed it enough anymore. It's hungry and it's saying: it's not enough, I'm still hungry, you should do better."

The compulsive problem-solving that can look like drive and discipline is often this beast demanding the next meal. Fix your relationships → fix your work → fix yourself. The self becomes the final altar of sacrifice because everything external has already been offered up.

Where "Should" Comes From

The pattern usually starts early, and it starts with someone important. The hypothesis offered here: at some point you knew something true about yourself, and someone significant — a parent, a culture, a household — communicated that you should be something else instead. You calibrated toward should, then further, then further, until should became your true north and the original self got left behind.

For someone whose parents immigrated with enormous sacrifice and found validation through academic achievement, the math becomes simple very fast: their happiness = your performance. Showing your real feelings — sadness, uncertainty, not-doing-okay — felt like threatening that happiness. So the distressed version of yourself got put in the closet every time a parent came near.

"There was a little girl who had feelings that were completely fair and legitimate, and she got told to shut up and sit in the closet because there was stuff to do."

That closet has been closed for years. The crying that won't stop is not random. It's mourning.

Mourning Is the Opposite of "Should"

Grief and "should" cannot coexist. A "should" says: this person should still be alive. Mourning is the process of moving from that should to acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → — not agreement that it was okay, but acknowledgment that it is.

The same process applies to time lost. If you spent years of identity-forming adolescence performing for others rather than discovering yourself, that loss is real and worth grieving. Feeling sad for yourself, pitying that younger version of you who never got to develop freely — that's not self-pity in the weak sense. That's appropriate mourning, and it's the right direction.

There's also something important about age here: 23 is not as adult as the culture pretends. Identity development happens through experience, friction, and time. Spending ages 16–23 in a high-performance public role — optimizing, producing, pleasing — is not the same as living through those years. Missing that isn't a character flaw. It's a specific loss.

Sacrifice vs. Should

These are not the same thing, and confusing them is costly.

A sacrifice is a choice. You see clearly what you're giving up, you decide it's worth it, and you carry it honestly. You might eat the bran muffin even though you want the cookie — that's a sacrifice.

A should is a pressure. You tell yourself you should want the bran muffin. You believe you're wrong for wanting the cookie. You feel guilty for the desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → itself.

"I have no problem with you doing things that are good for the people around you. But I want you to be honest about whether that's actually what you want. There's a huge difference between making a sacrifice and beating yourself up for not wanting to make it."

You can do things for others and feel good about it. What you cannot sustainably do is devalue yourself for having wants that conflict with what you owe everyone else.

The NPC Relationship Problem

A pattern that emerges with family — and often with others — is what might be called an NPC relationship: scripted, surface-level, predictable exchanges with no real emotional data flowing in either direction.

The instinct to protect parents from your real feelings is loving. It's also wrong as a long-term strategy. Parents who care about you want a two-sided relationship. They want to know when you're struggling, not so they can fix it necessarily, but because being excluded from their child's inner life is its own kind of loss.

The practical suggestion: start by naming it explicitly. Tell them you're grateful. Tell them you feel indebted. Tell them there's a part of you that isn't doing okay. Then ask them directly: when I tell you something is hard, what do you think you need to do about it? That conversation won't be comfortable. It's also not optional if you want to stop feeling alone inside your own family.

Resentment Is Information

When you live inside a system of shoulds, resentment is the inevitable exhaust. You either do the should and feel used, or you don't do it and feel like a failure. Neither path is clean. Feeling jaded, defensive, used by people who are friendly in private and cruel in public — that's not ingratitude or weakness. That's what happens when you've been giving from obligation rather than genuine choice for a long time.

On forgiveness specifically: an apology does not undo damage. Accepting an apology can be genuine and still not constitute full forgiveness. No one is entitled to your forgiveness. You're allowed to say I appreciate the apology, but the damage isn't repaired — without being a bad person for saying it.

Being vs. Becoming

The most counterintuitive point in the whole conversation is this: the problem is not that you haven't improved yourself enough. The problem is that you're treating yourself as a fiction — an architectural diagram of what you should be — rather than as a fact, which is what you actually are.

"The pen is here. It can't be anywhere else. Where's the should in that? The pen is here."

You can't be an inch taller. You can't have spent your early twenties differently. You are exactly what you are, shaped by exactly what happened. That's not resignation. It's the only honest starting point for anything.

What feels like bliss in the moments when the suffering lets up — it's not relief from an external problem being solved. It's the experience of being where you are instead of where you think you should be.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking recognition for change. Knowing you have a "problem-solver brain" or that you live by shoulds doesn't automatically dismantle the pattern. The insight is the beginning, not the solution.
  • Treating acceptance as giving up. Accepting what you are right now isn't the same as deciding not to grow. It's just refusing to treat your current self as a problem to be fixed before you're allowed to exist.
  • Confusing emotional release with emotional processing. Crying until you feel temporarily better is not the same as actually working through what's producing the pressure.
  • Thinking privilege cancels suffering. Having good things in your life does not make your pain illegitimate. The gatekeeping of suffering — you're not allowed to feel bad because look at what you have — is a toxic dynamic that harms everyone it touches.
  • Forgiving prematurely to feel like a good person. Accepting an apology because that's what you're supposed to do and calling it forgiveness doesn't heal anything. Actual forgiveness requires actually processing the damage first.
  • Looking for the rubric. There is no score that means you did enough. Looking for one is the beast asking for more food.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the should, then don't feed it. When you catch yourself thinking "I should do X" or "I shouldn't feel Y," name it out loud if you can. Don't try to fix it or conquer it. Just let it sit there unfed. This will feel uncomfortable. That's what a hungry beast feels like when you stop feeding it.

  2. Have the real conversation with your parents. Start with genuine gratitude. Then tell them there's a part of you that isn't doing okay. Explicitly ask: when I tell you I'm struggling, what do you feel like you need to do? Let the conversation be messy. Don't manage their reaction for them.

  3. Distinguish sacrifice from should in a specific situation this week. Pick one obligation — something you've been doing out of pressure rather than genuine choice. Ask yourself honestly: am I willing to make this sacrifice, or am I just telling myself I should want to? You don't have to change the behavior. Just be honest about which one it is.

  4. Try the AUM chant as a pressure-release practice. Three syllables: ah (open), oo (middle), mm (closed). One slow breath per round, seven rounds. The goal is not silence or peace — it's vibration. Feel where the vibration lands in your body. After the last round, sit quietly and notice: is there anything inside that feels incomplete or wrong? Don't fix it. Just look.

  5. Let yourself be tired. Not as a task — but if exhaustion surfaces during meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → or a quiet moment, don't redirect it. Let it arrive. You have been working very hard for a very long time, including the internal work of keeping everything managed. The tiredness is real, and suppressing it costs something.

  6. Mourn specifically. Think about the years between 16 and now. What experiences did you skip? What version of yourself didn't get to develop? Don't reframe it positively. Don't look for the upside. Just let yourself feel sad about the specific loss. That's mourning, and it's the path toward acceptance — not away from it.
