Summary

TL;DR — Work feels more exhausting than ever because the economic "meta" has been optimized against you, and your own phone may be draining the emotional fuel you need to change anything.

A lot of people working full-time right now are barely breaking even — not because they're lazy, but because the rules of the game changed while the advice they inherited didn't. Understanding why you're so tired, and what's making it worse, is the first step toward doing something about it.

The Rules Changed, But the Advice Didn't

There was a stretch — roughly 1950 to 2005 — of unusual economic prosperity. If you showed up, worked a reasonable amount, and took a few chances, upward mobility was genuinely within reach. Housing was affordable relative to wages. Jobs had pension plans. You could stumble into a dot-com startup and walk out rich. The system had slack in it.

The generation that came of age during that window naturally concluded that moderate effort leads to progress. That's what their lived experience taught them. And so that's what they passed on — "work a little harder and you'll lift yourself up by your bootstraps." It wasn't bad advice for the world they grew up in. It just doesn't describe the world that exists now.

The reason isn't moral failure on anyone's part. It's optimization. Big data, private equity, algorithmic pricing — the people at the top of the economic food chain have figured out the meta. Landlords know, statistically, the maximum rent they can charge before a unit sits vacant. Employers know the minimum salary they can offer before candidates stop accepting. The internet made it easy to apply to a thousand jobs at once, so now everyone does — which means you have to apply to a thousand jobs just to get one. What used to be a competitive advantage became the baseline.

Think of it like a video game where the top players' strategies get published instantly. Everyone copies the optimal build and no one can win with anything else. That's the job market and housing market right now. The "weird stuff that falls into your lap" is largely gone because it's been priced out.

"The meta has been figured out by the people who usually employ you. All of the opportunity that existed — all of the random crap you could fall bass-backwards into 30 years ago — you can't do anymore."

This is worth sitting with. The frustration young workers feel isn't entitlementA personality pattern — not an identity — rooted in low internal self-worth, where the gap between how little you feel you're worth on the inside gets filled by constant external validation.Full definition → in the usual sense. It's the collision between inherited expectations and a structurally different reality. That gap is real, and pretending it isn't doesn't help anyone.

Why Tiredness Isn't Just Laziness

Here's something that changes how you should think about your exhaustion: tiredness is a neurological energy-conservation strategy.

When a bear hibernates, it's not being lazy. Its brain has calculated that searching for food in winter will burn more energy than it yields. The subjective experience of that calculation is overwhelming fatigue. The bear doesn't decide to be tired — its nervous system makes it tired to prevent wasted effort.

Your brain does the same thing. The moment you genuinely conclude that more effort won't improve your situation, your body starts conserving energy. You feel heavier. Getting off the couch feels harder. This isn't weakness — it's your nervous system doing exactly what it evolved to do when the environment looks bleak and effort looks futile.

"The moment that you conclude that additional effort will not change your life, your brain and your body will start to feel more tired."

The problem is we don't live in a seasonal world where spring eventually comes and opportunities return. The bleak conditions are chronic. So the protective tiredness stays on — indefinitely.

The second thing worth knowing: the body adapts. The more you engage with tiring activities, the more tolerant you become. The first week at the gym is brutal; the third week is less so. The same principle applies to any demanding effort. Resistance decreases with exposure. This is the mechanism you can actually use.

Your Phone Is Draining the Fuel You Need Most

Emotions are your primary motivator for behavior. Not logic. If someone makes you angry, you're energized to act. If something fills you with hope, you move toward it. Logic can tell you exactly what you should do and you'll still sit there doing nothing, because logic doesn't fire the engine — emotion does.

Social media and smartphones are burning through that emotional fuel constantly. You watch a two-minute video, feel genuine indignation, connection, or sadness — real emotional activation — and when those feelings dissipate, you're drained. The emotion got spent on the video. There's nothing left to motivate real-world action.

This is why screen time is not passive rest. It feels like rest because you're lying down. It isn't rest because your emotional circuits are working the whole time. You get up from an hour of scrolling more depleted than when you sat down, not less.

This is also one of the most actionable levers you have, which is part of why it's uncomfortable to hear.

The Cycle You Can Actually Change

Here's a pattern worth examining: everything piles up during the week, you're too exhausted to do anything after work, so it all migrates to the weekend. Both days fill up. There's no real rest. You go into the next week already empty. Repeat.

The inverse cycle also exists, and it's achievable:

  1. Reduce phone use — not dramatically, just meaningfully. Give your emotional circuits room to recover.
  2. Do 15 minutes of something productive after work — even when you're exhausted. Not an hour. Fifteen minutes.
  3. Gradually distribute weekend tasks across the week. Small amounts, consistently.
  4. Protect one weekend day. Not immediately — but as a target. One day that has nothing on it.

"Once you have a day off on the weekend, you will be invigorated going into the next week."

Not fully invigorated. But more than before. And that small increase in energy means 20 minutes instead of 15 the next week. Which frees up a little more weekend time. Which gives a little more rest. The cycle can turn in the other direction. It's slow and it's unfair that it has to be this hard, but it does move.

If you're genuinely running on empty no matter what you try, it's worth considering whether something physiological is at play — anemia, hypothyroidism, depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition →, and similar conditions can look exactly like "I'm just tired from life." That's worth checking before you conclude the problem is entirely motivational.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking tiredness for a character flaw. Chronic exhaustion in this environment is a neurological response to a genuinely difficult situation. Treating it as laziness makes you feel worse and doesn't fix anything.

Waiting for the situation to be fixed externally. The system is unfair. That's true. It's also not likely to change fast enough to help you next month. Both things can be true simultaneously.

Thinking rest means scrolling. Passive phone use is one of the most effective ways to feel like you rested without actually restoring the energy you need. They're not the same thing.

Swinging between "I'll push through" and total collapse. All-or-nothing approaches to effort don't build the toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → that makes hard things easier over time. Small, consistent effort does.

Chasing high-variance paths as an escape. Becoming a full-time content creator or going pro in a competitive field are genuinely unlikely outcomes — not impossible, but statistically brutal. Using them as a plan to escape a difficult job situation usually makes things worse, not better.

Assuming your expectations are inflated. Some are. Some genuinely aren't. The key is separating "I deserve easy success" (a belief the system will punish) from "I deserve to have enough time and energy to have a life" (a reasonable human need, even if the current system doesn't support it).

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your screen time honestly. Pull up your daily average on your phone's settings app. Don't judge it — just know the number. Then pick one hour in the evening to have the phone in another room.

  2. Do 15 minutes of one practical task tonight after work. Not when you feel ready. Tonight, tired. Pick something that would otherwise sit on your weekend list. This is the first rep.

  3. Get one potential medical cause ruled out. If you've been exhausted for months and it doesn't track with your sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → or workload, talk to a doctor about thyroid function, iron levels, and mood. It's not a weakness to check — it's information.

  4. Protect one block of weekend time this week. Not the whole weekend. One four-hour block with nothing scheduled. Treat it like a meeting you can't cancel.

  5. Identify one thing you can gradually move from weekends to weeknights. Start with the smallest, least effortful item. The goal is to begin redistributing load, not to clear everything at once.

  6. Notice the emotional hangover after scrolling. For one week, pay attention to how you feel after 30+ minutes on social media versus after 30 minutes of something else — a walk, a meal without your phone, a conversation. You're collecting data on what actually restores you.
