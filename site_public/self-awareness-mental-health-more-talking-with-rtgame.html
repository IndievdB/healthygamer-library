What Anxiety Is Actually Trying to Do

It's your mind running an optimization process. When you're anxious, your brain is generating possible futures and scanning for the best one. 1. It made perfection obviously impossible.

The Hidden Trap in Good Coping

There's a counterintuitive research finding worth sitting with: people who develop very strong coping mechanisms are more likely to stay stuck. The better you are at tolerating a bad situation, the less pressure you feel to actually change it.

Where Existential Dread Actually Comes From

Daniel can date his existential dread to around age 10: watching peers do clubs, join activities, live what felt like fuller lives — while he was too socially anxious to participate. He tried a sailing club, got overwhelmed, quit after a few weeks, and carried that as quiet evidence that life was something happening to other people.

"Life was there to be lived, but you weren't capable of living it. Existence becomes dread."

The dread wasn't philosophical abstraction — it was the felt experience of being an observer to your own life. Books like The Catcher in the Rye resonated not because they introduced the idea but because they gave language to something already there.

The 28-year-old cold-sweat panic ("I've done nothing") isn't a mature adult fear. It's that 10-year-old re-emerging. The present-day self — confident, successful, genuinely living — isn't the one waking up in terror. That's the kid who watched the Sailing Club go on without him. Healing that source is different from coping with the symptom.

Men, Emotions, and the Body

The final thread of the conversation is worth holding separately. The entire architecture of emotional healing — therapy, self-disclosure, emotional vocabulary — was largely built on a population that skews toward women who naturally engage more with emotional language. Men are disproportionately alexithymic (they experience emotions more as physical sensations than as nameable feelings), which puts them at a structural disadvantage in talk-based treatment.

This isn't necessarily a deficiency. It may just mean the most natural channel for male emotional processing runs through the body rather than language. The mind and body run a two-way street: change the physical sensation and the emotion shifts; change the emotion and the physical sensation shifts.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice when you're optimizing. When anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → spikes, ask yourself: What am I trying to pick the perfect version of right now? Naming the optimization goal makes it easier to see that perfect isn't actually available — and that doing something imperfect is still better than looping.

  2. Deliberately blur the good/bad line on one thing. Pick something you're currently anxious about. Write down three genuine ways it could be good and three genuine ways it could be bad. Sit with the fact that it's probably both.   3. Shorten your time horizon on purpose. When you're stuck in anxious future-planning, ask: What would I do right now if I knew I couldn't optimize this? You don't have to nearly die to access that clarity — the question can do some of the same work.

  4. Find where the dread lives in your body. Rather than trying to reason your way out of existential dread or anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →, locate the physical sensation. Where is it? What does it feel like — pressure, heat, tightness? Describe it as specifically as you can. This is especially useful if emotional vocabulary doesn't come naturally to you.

  5. Do the thing you keep putting off. Daniel has known for years he needs physiotherapy. Everyone has a version of that — something a professional has told them to do, that they keep soft-committing to "eventually." Pick one. Contact someone about it tomorrow, not later this week.

  6. If you're identifying with the 10-year-old panic: When you wake up in a cold sweat convinced you've wasted your life, recognize that the voice doing the catastrophizing is an old one. The current-day version of you has already outlived the fears that version was carrying. You don't have to argue it into silence — just notice how old it is.

  7. Consider therapy if you haven't. The fear is that going to a therapist will confirm you're broken. The actual experience, consistently, is the opposite: it's where you find out you were never as broken as you thought.
