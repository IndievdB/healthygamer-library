The Three Components You Actually Need to Work On

Once you accept that motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → is emergent, the practical question becomes: what are the elements that produce it? There are three.

1. Intent

Intent is your why — but more specifically, it's whether your goal is actually yours.

A huge portion of the things people "should" do are goals that were handed to them from the outside: by parents, by culture, by social comparison. I should exercise. I should get better grades. I should wake up at 5 AM. I should become a doctor. These are often perfectly reasonable goals — they just don't belong to the person carrying them.

"If the goal comes from outside of us, which is oftentimes what we'll discover, then chances are it's gonna be very hard to get motivated towards it."

This is why clarifying intent isn't just navel-gazing. It's diagnostic. If your motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → is chronically low for a particular goal, the first question to ask honestly is: do I actually want this, or was I told I should want it? Those feel similar from the inside but produce very different outcomes.

Working on intent means doing the introspective work to strip away conditioned goals and locate what you genuinely care about — building a "why" that is actually yours and therefore actually pulls you forward.

2. Resistance

Even when your goal is genuine, there can still be something blocking you from acting on it. This is resistance, and it has two distinct sources.

Emotional resistance is the gap between wanting something logically and being able to pursue it emotionally. The clearest example: someone who is deeply lonely, genuinely wants a partner, and is still completely paralyzed about going on a date. The logic is airtight. Most goals are framed in purely logical terms (of course I should get an A; of course I should be healthier), but our ability to act on them is not purely logical. Ignoring the emotional layer doesn't make it go away.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your goals for ownership. Take the goals you're currently struggling to pursue and ask, honestly: where did this goal come from? Write down whether each one feels genuinely yours or whether it was absorbed from parents, culture, or social pressure. Don't try to fix anything yet — just map it.

  2. Identify your layer of blockage. For each goal you feel stuck on, try to name which component is broken: Is the why unclear or external (intent)? Is there an emotional or identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → block in the way (resistance)? Or is the goal too abstract to act on (action system)? One of these will feel more true than the others.

  3. Surface the emotional resistance. If you suspect emotional blockage, sit with the question: what am I actually afraid of here? Not the goal itself, but what pursuing it might reveal or cost. You don't need to resolve it immediately — naming it accurately is already useful.

  4. Challenge your identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → labels. Notice the self-descriptions you reach for when you fail to act ("I'm lazy," "I never finish things"). Ask whether these are facts or stories. Then ask what it would mean about you if they weren't true.

  5. Translate one abstract goal into a concrete first step. Pick the most important vague goal in your life right now and ask: what is the single most specific next action I could take? Not a plan — one step. Something that takes less than an hour and has a clear end state. Do that.

  6. Take a personalized approach throughout. Not every principle here will apply to you equally. As you work through the framework, notice which pieces resonate and which feel like they're describing someone else. The relevant components are the ones worth working on.
