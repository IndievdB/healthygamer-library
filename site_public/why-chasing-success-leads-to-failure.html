The Treadmill Isn't a Bug — It's a Feature

The brain didn't evolve to keep you happy. It evolved to keep you alive. Lasting contentment after a single achievement would have been a survival disadvantage; the ancestor who was always a little dissatisfied worked harder, accumulated more resources, and out-competed the one who felt great about the first kill.

This is why the people who are hardest on themselves after a success — who dismiss every compliment, interrogate every win for what could have been better — are often the most objectively successful. One content creator gave a presentation that got a standing ovation. The moment the applause started, their mind was already cataloguing flaws. Their friends told them it was great; they assumed their friends were being kind. So they worked even harder on the next one, and it was better.

That reflex is adaptive. It drives improvement. The goal isn't to kill it.

What's Actually Missing: The Reflection Window

Here's the part nobody talks about: after completing something, there is supposed to be a gap. A pause. A moment where you actually process what just happened.

Modern life is engineered to prevent exactly that. The next short video autoplays. The end-game screen already has a "queue up" button. The loot box you didn't want is immediately followed by an offer to buy another. The entire attention economy is one long project to eliminate the space between experiences.

The sigma grind set person does the same thing voluntarily: get promoted → immediately identify the next target → never stop to ask how the promotion actually felt. The person chasing pleasureHedonics is the experience of pleasure — specifically, the capacity to like and enjoy something in the moment.Full definition → plays ten thousand hours of a video game, never watches a replay, never asks what went wrong, blames teammates, and stays hard-stuck at bronze. Same loop, different flavor.

When you don't stop to process an experience, your brain doesn't extract the lesson from it. You repeat the cycle — not because you're weak, but because the circuit never closed.

What the Agori Sadhus Actually Teach

Agori sadhus are a group of ascetics (you may have seen pictures — enormous dreadlocks, ash-covered, living in cremation grounds) whose practice is the deliberate confrontation with fear and revulsion. Agora means "absence of fear." The way they conquer fear isn't by avoiding frightening things. It's by deliberately pausing inside the experience and observing their own reactions.

They ask themselves four questions every time: 1. What was the build-up to this experience like? How did I feel before? 2. How did I feel during the experience? 3. How did I feel right after? 4. How long did the feeling last?

This isn't a practice designed for monks giving up worldly things. It's a practice designed for people who are in it — who are going to keep doing the thing — but who want to actually absorb what the experience contains. Applied to a promotion, a presentation, a cookie, or a game, the same four questions do the same work: they force the brain to close the loop instead of immediately opening the next one.

What Happens When You Actually Pause

When you force the reflection — even just by sitting with "how do I feel about what I just did?" — a few things happen that seem paradoxical.

You become more content without achieving less. The person in therapy who finally paused after a promotion said something like: "I guess it actually worked out okay." That sounds modest. But that's more satisfaction than they had gotten from any previous win, because it was the first time they had actually let it land.

You stop bleeding self-worth into your goals. When there's no reflection, every goal carries the weight of: if I get this, I'll be okay; if I don't, I'm not okay. When you reflect regularly, the goal stays a goal. It's something you chose to pursue, not a verdict on your worth as a person.

You actually get better at the thing. The gaming analogy is exact. Watching a replay and asking "what went wrong?" is uncomfortable, but it's the only mechanism that produces improvement. Queuing immediately for the next game feels productive but produces nothing.

"Instead of chasing something to fill a void within you, you're going to be like: this is a goal I've set for myself, and my self-worth as a human being is not tied up in its success."

Action Items & Exercises

  1. After completing any meaningful task — work project, gaming session, meal, purchase — stop before the next thing starts. Give yourself two to five minutes. Do not open the next app, queue the next game, or start the next project yet.

  2. Run the four-question sequence on that experience: - How did I feel before doing this? - How did I feel while doing this? - How do I feel now that it's over? - How long did the good feeling (or relief, or satisfaction) actually last?

  3. If you're an achiever: when you hit a goal, bring it up before moving on. If you're in therapy or have a trusted person to debrief with, make the first thing you say about the win, not the next problem. Practice letting it exist in the room for a moment.

  4. If you're stuck in a pleasureHedonics is the experience of pleasure — specifically, the capacity to like and enjoy something in the moment.Full definition → loop: after the next session of whatever you're chasing, sit for two minutes before doing it again. You don't have to stop doing it. Just insert a gap and notice honestly how you feel — not how you should feel.

  5. Watch a replay. Literally or metaphorically — review what actually happened in something you want to improve at. The discomfort of seeing your mistakes clearly is the mechanism of growth that queuing up again immediately bypasses.

  6. Notice when technology removes your pause. When the next video autoplays, when the "play again" button appears — use those as a cue to manually stop and take thirty seconds with the four questions before continuing.
