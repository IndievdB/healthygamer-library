Summary

TL;DR — Helping other people from a place of inner need isn't generosity; it's a way of avoiding yourself. Fix the inside first, and the outside takes care of itself.

There's a certain kind of person who is always the first to check in, the first to notice someone else's struggles, the first to offer a fix. From the outside this looks like warmth and care — and a real part of it is. But underneath it there's often something else running: a hunger that has nothing to do with the other person at all. This talk pulls that pattern apart and traces it back to where it actually starts.

The "Fixer" Pattern and Where It Really Comes From

Some people don't just like helping — they need to help. There's a difference.

When helping someone else is what makes you feel okay about yourself, helping stops being about them. It becomes about satisfying something in you. The practical sign of this is that you start pushing past what the other person actually wants or asked for. You bring up their codependency when they didn't ask for that conversation. You ask how they're coping when they're clearly not ready to talk. You try to fix something they weren't requesting to be fixed.

"Sometimes people will not feel confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → or won't feel good about themselves unless they are able to help other people."

This matters because it means the compulsive helper can end up causing the exact harm they're most afraid of causing. When your need to help runs ahead of the other person's need to receive it, you stop seeing them clearly — you see a problem to solve that also happens to solve something inside you.

The origin of this pattern is worth sitting with: being a fixer often comes from an inability to fix yourself. Going around improving everyone else's life is a way of not having to look at your own. The world becomes a projectionProjection is the psychological process of taking something you dislike or can't accept in yourself and unconsciously attributing it to someone else instead.Full definition → screen. You can see codependency in your friend, emotional avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → in a colleague, fear of vulnerability in an acquaintance — and as long as you're busy diagnosing and treating all of that, you don't have to turn the lens around.

The Oxygen Mask Problem

The airplane instruction exists for a reason. If you try to help someone else with their oxygen mask while yours isn't on, you'll pass out before you've done any good.

The same logic applies here. When you have unresolved things going on in your own mind, your perception of other people gets warped by them. You think you're seeing their situation clearly, but you're actually seeing it through the filter of your own unmet needs. A person who genuinely wants to help someone has to have their own house in order first — not because selfishness is a virtue, but because your clarity about another person depends on your clarity about yourself.

"As long as you have all these issues going on in your head, your mind is gonna warp what you see from the rest of the world."

The practical implication: focus on yourself first. Not as a permanent posture, but as a precondition. When you've done that work, people who need your help will find you. You don't need to hunt for them.

When Helping Becomes Identity

There's a level of this pattern that goes deeper than just behavior — it becomes identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition →. Helping other people becomes who you are, not just something you do.

When that happens, two things break:

  1. It stops being about them. If your sense of self is wrapped up in being a helper, then every situation where someone might need help becomes an opportunity to be yourself. Their need becomes your stage.
  2. You become resistant to receiving help yourself. If getting help from others means having someone look at your stuff — having your self-image disturbed, your lies burned away — that's threatening to the identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition →. So you stay on the giving side of every exchange and keep away from the receiving side.

This is the core contradiction: the person most eager to help others is often the person most closed off to being helped themselves. And that closed-off-ness is the very thing preventing them from becoming someone who can actually help well.

Fear of Being Unlovable and the "When I'm Ready" Trap

Underneath the fixer pattern is often something more personal and more painful: a fear that you are not, as you currently are, worth loving.

This fear doesn't usually announce itself. Instead it shows up as reasonable-sounding delay:

  • I'll ask her out once I've developed my talents more.
  • I'll put myself out there when I have more to offer.
  • I'll be ready when I can show them my best.

The logic feels sound, but look at what it's actually saying: I won't give people the chance to love me until I'm sure they will. And that moment never comes, because the fear isn't about whether you've done enough — it's a core belief that you are not enough.

The "when I'm ready" framing protects against rejection by indefinitely postponing the moment of risk. But it also indefinitely postpones the possibility of being loved. Trying to help people, fixing their problems, being indispensably useful — these can all function as the same delay tactic. If someone valuesValues are the things that, when you move toward them, make you feel genuinely fulfilled — not just pleased or validated, but deeply satisfied.Full definition → you because you helped them, you have evidence of your worth that didn't require the terrifying vulnerability of just being yourself and waiting to see if that's enough.

The answer isn't to wait until the fear is gone. It's to act despite the fear, to let people have the opportunity to love you as you are right now — and to let yourself be hurt when it doesn't work out, sit with that hurt, and try again.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing genuine care with compulsive helping. These can coexist in the same person, which makes it hard to see clearly. The question isn't whether you care — you probably do. The question is what happens inside you when you're blocked from helping. Does it feel fine, or does it feel threatening? The latter is the signal.

Thinking "I just want what's best for them" ends the analysis. Intentions don't override dynamics. Pushing help on someone who didn't ask for it can still cause harm, even if you genuinely want good things for them.

Waiting until you're "ready" to take relational risks. Readiness, in this context, is usually just a disguise for fear of rejection. The conditions for perfect safety will never arrive.

Staying emotionally invested in people who aren't available. Pouring care and attention into someone who is in a relationship, or who can't reciprocate, feels safe precisely because it carries low risk of the rejection you fear most. But it also carries zero chance of the connection you actually need.

Using the work of self-improvement as another avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → strategy. It's possible to spend enormous energy on "fixing yourself" while still never becoming vulnerable enough to let anyone in. Working on yourself is necessary; it is not sufficient.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the hunger. The next time you feel a strong pull to help someone or point out a problem they have, pause before acting. Ask yourself: Is this about them, or does this feel like a hunger in me? Don't judge it — just notice it.

  2. Track the driver, not the action. For one week, when you help someone, ask afterward: Would I be okay if they hadn't needed me? If the answer is no, that's information.

  3. Stop avoiding being on the receiving end. The next time someone offers you help, support, or honest feedback — especially the kind that makes you uncomfortable — let it in instead of deflecting. Discomfort here is a sign you're in the right territory.

  4. Ask someone out (or take a direct relational risk) before you feel ready. Don't wait until you've developed whatever quality you think you're missing. Take the risk as you are. If you get rejected, go find people who will sit with you in that feeling rather than trying to immediately fix it.

  5. Redirect emotional energy toward people who can actually reciprocate. If you're spending significant emotional bandwidth on someone who is unavailable — taken, not interested, or unable to engage — notice that and consciously redirect that energy toward someone who can give something back.

  6. Put your own oxygen mask on first — practically. Make a list of the things you've been meaning to work on in yourself that you keep deferring because you've been focused outward. Pick one. Start there before taking on anyone else's problems.
