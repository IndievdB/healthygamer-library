Summary

TL;DR — Defensiveness makes arguments worse, not better. The way out is to genuinely receive criticism first, then share your side second.

When someone criticizes you and you push back immediately, you're not ending the conflict — you're fueling it. This is especially true if you're neurodivergent, where emotional dysregulation can make the impulse to defend yourself feel overwhelming. There's a specific sequence of steps that actually works, and it starts with understanding why criticism feels so threatening in the first place.

Why Criticism Hits So Hard

The root of defensiveness isn't the criticism itself — it's what your mind does with it. When a critique gets linked to your identity, your emotional response escalates dramatically.

There's a difference between: - "I made a mistake" (an event) - "I am a mistake / I am stupid / I am a failure" (an identity)

The moment a criticism implies something permanent about who you are, your brain treats it like an attack on your survival. You feel compelled to fight back. This is why someone who oversalts food can spiral into "I am a terrible cook" — and then defend that conclusion furiously, even though no one actually said it.

For people with ADHD or autism, this dynamic is compounded by emotional dysregulation. The stress response fires harder and faster, meaning the urge to defend yourself is stronger and harder to interrupt. Growing up neurodivergent often means accumulating a lot of identity-level wounds — years of social friction, exclusion, and correction — which makes this hair-trigger response more entrenched.

The first piece of work, then, is internal: learn to separate the event from the identity. I can be a competent, intelligent person who forgot to send the PowerPoint. Those two things can both be true. When you practice this separation, you lower the emotional temperature before the conversation even starts.

What Happens When You Get Defensive

Defensiveness is an attempt to shut down the other person's attack. The problem is it does the opposite.

When someone sends you a signal — a complaint, a concern, a criticism — and that signal is not received, they do one of two things: they turn up the volume, or they eventually stop communicating altogether. Usually in that order.

"It is too salty. It is too salty. It is too salty. Whatever. I'm never coming over again."

Conflict requires both sides pulling. When you push back, they push harder. When they push harder, you push back more. Defensiveness doesn't extinguish the attack; it confirms to the other person that they haven't been heard yet, so they keep going.

The counterintuitive move is to stop pulling on your end of the rope.

The Phrase That Actually Disarms Conflict

Instead of countering criticism, invite more of it. Two phrases that work:

  • "Can you tell me a little bit more about that?"
  • "What do you mean by that?"

These feel almost absurdly simple, but they do something structurally important: they signal that you are receiving the message, not blocking it. When someone feels genuinely heard, they no longer need to escalate.

This only works if it's real. You have to actually try to understand their experience — what is it like for this food to be too salty? What went wrong for you here? Treat it as information, not as an assault to be deflected.

The goal is to let them talk without pushing back. Once you do this, something shifts. You've validated their experience. The tension releases because you've stopped pulling on your end.

How to Share Your Side Without Sounding Like You're Making Excuses

Here's where people get stuck: it feels deeply unsatisfying to just absorb criticism without saying anything about your circumstances. And you shouldn't have to, permanently. But timing is everything.

The sequence is:

  1. Hear them out fully. Don't interrupt or contradict.
  2. Own what's yours. Acknowledge the actual mistake plainly.
  3. Introduce context as a path forward, not as a defense. Instead of "I didn't send it because my kid was sick" (which sounds like an excuse), say: "I had a rough weekend — my kid was sick — and I need to be more careful on Monday mornings after a hard weekend."

The difference is subtle but important. You're not arguing that the mistake didn't matter. You're using the circumstance to explain the corrective action you'll take. That's what most people actually want to hear. It tells them: this won't happen again, and here's why.

"What are you going to do to fix it next time? That's what most people want to hear. That's what calms most people down."

As a bonus: when you genuinely learn from your mistakes instead of deflecting them, you actually stop making them. Defensiveness short-circuits that learning loop entirely.

The Other Extreme: Accepting All Blame

Some people — especially those who grew up neurodivergent and learned that arguing always makes things worse — swing to the opposite extreme. They accept every criticism reflexively. "Of course it's my fault, I'm an idiot."

This seems like it avoids conflict, but it creates a different problem. When you never push back, people are more likely to criticize you again. They'll start assigning their own mistakes to you. You become the default person to blame.

"I've seen this as well. I feel like they can't win. If they defend themselves, people get mad at them. If they accept all of the responsibility, they always get blamed for everything."

Accepting all criticism wholesale also prevents you from building genuine confidence, because you're treating every complaint as confirmed truth about your identity — which is just the same identity-fusion problem, but in the direction of self-blame rather than self-defense.

The answer isn't "accept everything" or "accept nothing." It's developing enough internal groundedness to evaluate each situation honestly.

The Optional Third Step: Pointing Out Their Role

Once you've genuinely heard someone out and owned your part, there may be a legitimate case that they contributed to the problem too. You can raise that. But only after steps one and two.

"Criticize somebody else after you've owned up to what you did wrong. It'll be way better received."

A useful question here: "Here's what I'm planning on changing — what are you planning on changing?" This signals you're not absorbing 100% of the responsibility, without being dismissive of your own.

One caveat: if you skip to this step too fast — or if you habitually shift blame to others when the fault is mostly yours — this becomes a manipulation tactic rather than a fair reckoning. The sequence only works if you're being honest with yourself.

Common Pitfalls

  • Leading with your excuse. Mentioning mitigating circumstances before acknowledging the mistake reads as defensiveness, even when the circumstances are real and relevant.
  • Asking "what could you do differently?" too early. It's a legitimate question in the right context, but if you haven't genuinely owned your part first, it comes across as blame-shifting.
  • Mistaking capitulation for calm. Saying "you're right, I'm terrible" isn't the same as accepting valid criticism. One is self-punishment, the other is actual learning.
  • Thinking the problem is the other person. The argument usually escalates because both sides are pulling. Your end of the rope is the only one you can let go of.
  • Skipping the internal work. Memorizing phrases like "can you tell me more?" without addressing the identity-level vulnerability means you'll still get flooded emotionally and the techniques will fall apart under pressure.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Trace the identity link. The next time you feel defensive about a criticism, pause and ask: what does this say about me as a person? Write down the exact identity statement your mind jumped to (e.g., "this means I'm irresponsible"). Then deliberately reframe it: "I did an irresponsible thing. That is not the same as being an irresponsible person."

  2. Practice the two phrases. In low-stakes conversations this week, when someone complains or pushes back about anything, try responding with "Can you tell me a little bit more about that?" and see what happens. Get comfortable with the words before you need them in a heated moment.

  3. Reformat how you deliver your side. Take a recent situation where you wanted to explain yourself. Rewrite what you actually said vs. what you could have said — starting with the acknowledgment, then tying the circumstance to the corrective action, not to absolution.

  4. Notice the pendulum in yourself. Over the next week, observe whether you tend toward defensiveness or toward reflexive self-blame. Both are responses to the same identity vulnerability. Knowing which direction you swing helps you calibrate.

  5. Practice the sequence in order. Next time conflict arises: (1) ask a question and listen fully, (2) own your part explicitly, and only then (3) if it's relevant and true, raise what the other person might also examine. Commit to not skipping or reordering the steps.
