Summary

TL;DR — The dating crisis isn't a moral failure or a skill gap; it's a selection pressure event. The post-COVID world sorted people into two groups: those whose social wiring already fit the new environment, and those for whom the new environment eliminated every pathway that used to work.

Dating, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, and isolation are getting measurably worse, and the internet's response is predictably useless: one camp insists it's easy if you just "put yourself out there," the other insists it's hopeless. Both camps are completely certain they're right. The reason neither can convince the other is that they are, effectively, different species living in different ecological niches — and what works in one niche genuinely does not translate to the other.

Natural Selection, Not Personal Failure

Darwin's finches are the right frame here. When he visited the Galápagos in 1835, he found finches with beaks already shaped for specific food sources — nut-crackers and bug-hunters. Natural selection doesn't usually work by forcing individuals to adapt on the fly. What actually happens is that when the environment shifts, the animals already suited to the new conditions survive and reproduce. The ones that aren't suited die out. That's it.

What's playing out in dating culture right now is structurally identical. Some people happen to have the social wiring, the living situation, the physical advantages, or the attachmentA neurobiologically grounded framework explaining how humans develop the capacity to form bonds with others — and how the quality of those early bonds shapes the way you relate to people for the rest of your life.Full definition → style that fits the post-COVID world. They thrive. Others — through no particular fault of their own — were shaped for a world that no longer exists. Their pathways to meeting people have been eliminated one by one.

"This doesn't mean that there are some people who are losers and some people who are winners. What we're seeing is people who are very confused because one person is saying, 'It's not that big of a deal,' and another person is saying, 'It feels impossible.'"

The anger and despair visible in those Twitter threads isn't weakness — it may even be something deeper. There's a question worth sitting with: do our genes have some mechanism for registering that they won't be passed on? No one knows for certain, but the existential dread described in those posts — the sense of being slowly extinguished — is consistent with what that might feel like.

What COVID Actually Changed

Three measurable shifts help explain why the world sorted the way it did.

1. The collapse of unstructured face-to-face time

Research across Australia, England, the Netherlands, New Zealand, and the USA consistently finds that declining unstructured face-to-face time with peers is the underlying driver behind a broad drop in adolescent risk-taking — less drinking, less casual sex, less crime.

Unstructured time is the key word. Hanging out with no agenda — wandering around, running into people, getting bored together — is how social skills are built and how romantic relationships organically begin. That kind of time is essentially gone. Every social interaction now requires someone to decide it's worth leaving their home for. And when the dopamineA neurotransmitter that drives motivation, behavioral reinforcement, and craving — not simply the feeling of pleasure.Full definition → available inside your home (streaming, gaming, food delivery) competes with the uncertain social dopamine available outside, staying home almost always wins.

As unstructured face-to-face time disappears, people become more risk-averse. And dating is inherently risky.

2. Relationships got harder even for people already in them

Studies comparing relationship satisfaction before and after COVID lockdowns found that both dating and married people reported significantly lower scores on satisfaction, love, intimacy, and passion after lockdown. It's not just that meeting people is harder — sustaining bonds is harder too. The people who navigate this best tend to have secure attachment styles or specific skills for deepening emotional connection. Everyone else defaults to what the environment rewards: cycling to the next option rather than working on the current one.

3. The physical and structural landscape shifted

  • 50% of adults under 30 now co-reside with their parents. This single fact creates two completely different worlds. If you have your own place, an invitation back there is natural. If you live at home, that conversation ends things before they start. Nobody in the "just put yourself out there" camp mentions this.
  • Dating apps concentrate attention on the top tier. In a pool of thousands of profiles, physical attractiveness dominates. In a real intro-chem class, you're competing with the people actually in the room with you — which is a very different game.
  • Approaching strangers carries real social risk. One informal poll (not peer-reviewed, but worth noting) found that roughly 18–20% of women reported an approach to the authorities. Whether that exact figure holds up or not, the perception of that risk is real, and it interacts lethally with a generation that already has elevated risk-aversion baked in.

Why the Advice From "Both Sides" Fails

The person who says "just go to church or join a hobby club" is genuinely describing their reality. They have a beak that fits this environment. Their advice isn't wrong for them — it just doesn't transfer, because the person receiving the advice may be working from home, living with parents, having never built unstructured social habits during the years when that development happens, and now facing a social-skills deficit that can't be closed with a motivational tweet.

The reverse is also true. The person saying it's hopeless is also describing their reality accurately. The issue is that both groups assume their experience is universal.

"It's almost as if the advice that one group of people gives literally doesn't translate over into this other group of people."

Four Skills That Actually Help

These aren't a cure. But they're grounded in what unstructured face-to-face time used to build automatically — and they can be practiced intentionally now.

1. Affiliative signals and eye contact

Human connection escalates before words are exchanged. Eye contact, a smile, raising a glass — these are how two people signal mutual interest and begin to close the distance safely. Approaching someone cold, without any of this groundwork, puts the full weight of the interaction on one moment that feels high-stakes for both parties. Practice the escalating dance first. Start low-stakes: a nod to a stranger, small talk in an elevator. The goal is to rebuild a fluency with social signals that used to develop automatically.

2. Ask people out in safe, low-stakes environments

Safety — hers, his, yours — changes everything. A well-lit, populated setting where someone can easily exit the interaction if they want to is the right context. This is why cold approaches at gyms or persistent overtures at work carry higher risk: they create ongoing awkwardness in places someone has to return to. If you do want to ask out someone you share a recurring space with, time it toward the end of that shared context, make your interest clear, and explicitly give them an easy out. "No pressure, I won't bring it up again" is not weakness — it's what makes people feel safe enough to actually say yes.

3. A relaxed, non-resentful demeanor

If you approach someone while carrying the accumulated weight of every prior rejection, that energy transmits whether you intend it to or not. This isn't a call to fake confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → — it's a recognition that working on your internal state is part of the preparation, not a separate project for later.

4. Shared context and warm introductions

Bonds form more easily when two people already have something in common — a shared boss, a shared class, a mutual friend. If you don't naturally share context with the people you want to meet, the most efficient move is to build platonic relationships with people of the gender you're attracted to. A friend saying "this is my buddy — he's a psychiatrist who streams on the internet" does more work in five seconds than any cold opener.

Common Pitfalls

Treating this as a morality story. The natural selection frame is not about winners and losers, or about who "deserves" to date. It's a descriptive observation about environmental fit. Moralizing it in either direction — blaming struggling people for their circumstances, or blaming the environment as corrupt — misses the point and makes the problem harder to solve.

Extrapolating your own experience universally. If things are going fine for you, your experience is real but not representative. If things are going terribly, your experience is also real and also not the full picture. The variance in outcomes right now is genuinely enormous, and the gap is explained by structural factors, not just attitude.

Chasing rule changes instead of skill building. It's tempting, especially in online spaces, to conclude that the right answer is to roll the world back to how it was. That's not actionable. The environment has changed; the practical question is how to navigate it.

Skipping the affiliative-signal stage. Many people who struggle with dating try to shortcut straight to "shoot your shot" — stating intentions directly and mechanically without any of the preceding social dance. This almost always fails and accelerates the cycle of rejection and lowered self-worth.

Ignoring the structural barriers. Conversely, pretending that someone who lives at home, works remotely, and hasn't had unstructured social time since 2019 just needs to "try harder" is not helpful. Structural reality matters.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice affiliative signals daily, outside of romantic contexts. Give someone the nod. Make brief eye contact with a stranger and smile. Make small talk in an elevator or waiting in line. The goal is to rebuild fluency with low-level social signals before you need them to matter.

  2. Audit your unstructured social time. How much time per week do you spend physically with other people, with no set agenda? If the answer is close to zero, identify one recurring situation — a class, a recreational league, a volunteer slot, a regular coffee shop — where low-stakes, unplanned interaction can happen.

  3. Map your warm-intro network. Write down the platonic relationships you have with people of the gender you're attracted to. If that list is short, prioritize building those friendships — not as a strategy to date their friends, but because genuine mixed-gender friendship is both intrinsically valuable and one of the most reliable organic pathways to meeting romantic partners.

  4. Before approaching someone, run the safety check. Is this a public, well-lit place? Can they exit easily if they want to? Will an awkward outcome force you both into repeated contact? If the answers push toward "no, no, yes," wait for a better moment or setting.

  5. Build in an explicit exit for the other person. When asking someone out, make it normal and easy for them to decline: "No pressure at all — if not, no worries." This reduces their risk, which increases the chance they feel safe enough to engage honestly.

  6. Work on your internal state separately from dating attempts. If you're carrying significant anger, resentment, or despair into interactions, those interactions will tend to confirm your worst fears. Addressing that emotional weight — through therapy, structured physical activity, communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition →, or whatever works for you — isn't a detour from dating. It's prerequisite work.
