Summary

TL;DR — Feeling overwhelmed isn't about having too many problems; it's about having too many problems you didn't choose. The fix is counterintuitive: take on more challenges — just ones you pick yourself.

Most people assume that being overwhelmed is a simple measurement of total stress. More problems = more overwhelmed. By that logic, the solution is to reduce your load — drop responsibilities, rest, wait for things to calm down. That instinct is understandable, but it misdiagnoses what's actually happening in your mind, which is why following it tends to make things worse, not better.

The Real Equation Behind Feeling Overwhelmed

The feeling of being overwhelmed isn't produced by the total amount of challenge in your life. It's produced by the ratio between two different kinds of challenge.

Passive challenges are difficulties life throws at you — things you didn't ask for and didn't choose. A health scare, a job loss, a relationship falling apart, financial pressure. These are things happening to you.

Active challenges are difficulties you deliberately take on. Setting a goal, starting a project, committing to a habit. These are things you're choosing to do.

"Your mind feels overwhelmed when your passive challenges outweigh your active challenges."

It's not how hard things are. It's whether you're dealing with more things you chose to deal with versus things you didn't. When life is mostly happening to you — when the majority of your mental and emotional bandwidth is occupied by unchosen problems — your brain registers that as a loss of control. That loss of control is what we experience as being overwhelmed.

Why the Obvious Solution Makes Things Worse

When life is already hard, the natural response is to stop taking on anything extra. Don't start new things. Don't set goals. Just focus on surviving the avalanche in front of you.

The problem is that pulling back from active challenges doesn't reduce the feeling of overwhelmed — it amplifies it. By abandoning the things you were choosing to pursue, you've removed active challenges from the equation entirely. Now your ratio is even more lopsided: 100% passive, 0% active. The feeling of being out of control gets worse, not better.

"When we give up our active challenges, the ratio of passive challenges to active challenges actually goes up — and this actually reinforces the feeling of overwhelmed."

This is how people get stuck. The thing that feels protective — backing off, waiting, doing less — is the very thing that deepens the spiral.

Evidence From Eating Disorders and Depression

This pattern shows up in clinical settings in revealing ways.

In eating disorders, clinicians noticed that the more out of control someone's life became, the more disordered their eating got. When patients were hospitalized involuntarily — stripped of autonomy over their daily lives — their eating disorder symptoms worsened. When people with disordered eating ended up in emotionally neglectful or controlling environments, the same thing happened.

What emerged from working with these patients was a striking insight: controlling food intake was functioning as an active challenge. It was the one domain where they could impose structure and feel agency. Even as the restriction became increasingly punishing, it was doing something psychologically real — counteracting the sense of helplessness in the rest of their life. The eating disorder, as harmful as it was, was the mind's attempt to rebalance the ratio.

A parallel principle appears in behavioral activation, a treatment for depression. Depression often comes with a long list of unchosen, seemingly unfixable problems — isolation, unemployment, lack of direction. The mind says: none of this is in my control, so why bother doing anything?

Behavioral activation works by getting someone to take on any small, chosen challenge. Do the dishes. Do some laundry. Go for a short walk. Nothing that directly addresses the "real" problems.

And yet it works. Not because cleaning the dishes fixes loneliness or job insecurity — it obviously doesn't — but because it shifts the ratio. The person is now choosing to do something, completing it, and experiencing a small but real sense of control. That rebalancing, even from something trivial, is enough to start lifting the depression and restoring the capacity to tackle bigger problems.

"By fixing problems that are not the problems that overwhelm us, it actually makes us feel less overwhelmed."

Why Being Overwhelmed Becomes a Trap

There's a particularly cruel loop that kicks in once you're in a state of overwhelm. When your mind is telling you constantly that things are out of your control, that problems are too big, that you can't fix this — how capable are you of actually tackling those problems?

Not very.

Overwhelm hamstrings you. It tells you the situation is uncontrollable before you even try, which leads to avoidance, which removes whatever active challenges remained, which worsens the ratio, which deepens the overwhelm. It becomes self-fulfilling.

"Once our passive challenges rise and we start to feel overwhelmed, we engage in avoidance behaviors — and the cycle repeats itself."

The way out isn't to power through the feeling with willpower or discipline. That framing keeps you locked on the overwhelming problems, which are often the ones you have the least control over. The way out is to disarm the feeling by changing the ratio — which you can do without touching the big, scary, unchosen problems at all.

The Counterintuitive Fix

Pick a completely unrelated challenge. Something small is fine. Something you genuinely choose — not something else you feel obligated to do. The domain doesn't matter. It just has to be yours.

As you start working on that chosen challenge, something shifts. You begin to feel capable. You feel like an agent in your own life rather than someone being acted upon. And then — gradually — the problems that felt paralyzing start to look more approachable. Not because the problems changed, but because you did.

"Those very same problems that used to feel overwhelming — now you have the confidence to deal with them."

The math in your head has changed. Active challenges are back in the equation. The ratio is no longer so lopsided. The overwhelm loses its grip.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking you have to solve the overwhelming problem first. The core insight here is that you don't. In fact, fixating on the unchosen problems and trying to brute-force your way through them often reinforces the sense of helplessness. The active challenge you pick doesn't need to be related to what's overwhelming you.

Confusing "taking on more challenges" with "adding more obligations." An active challenge has to be chosen. Piling on more things you feel forced to do just adds more passive challenges. The key word is agency — you're deciding to do this.

Dismissing small actions as pointless. The thought "doing the dishes doesn't fix my actual problems" is accurate — and also irrelevant. The goal isn't to solve the problem directly. It's to shift the internal ratio. Small actions do that.

Waiting until you feel better to start. The shift in mindset comes from taking action, not before it. Waiting to feel motivated or in control before doing anything keeps you stuck in the loop.

Treating overwhelm as something to muscle through. Willpower and discipline aimed directly at the source of overwhelm are tools aimed at the wrong target. The feeling doesn't need to be conquered — it needs to be unplugged.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your passive challenges. Write down the things currently overwhelming you. For each one, ask: Did I choose this? If the answer is no, it's a passive challenge. Getting clear on this helps you see the actual ratio you're dealing with.

  2. Pick one small, completely unrelated active challenge. It doesn't need to be meaningful or impressive. It just needs to be chosen by you. Clean one area of your home. Start a short daily walk. Learn something you've been curious about. Cook a meal you've never made. Make sure it's something you're choosing, not something you already feel obligated to do.

  3. Do it before you feel ready. The confidence comes after the action, not before. Start even — especially — when it feels pointless.

  4. Notice the ratio, not the total load. When overwhelm creeps in, don't ask "how do I reduce everything I'm dealing with?" Ask "what can I add that I actually choose?" That's the lever.

  5. Protect your active challenges. When life gets harder, the instinct is to drop your chosen goals first. Resist that. Those chosen challenges are what's keeping the ratio balanced. Losing them is what tips you into overwhelm.
