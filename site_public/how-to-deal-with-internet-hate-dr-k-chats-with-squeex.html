Why Intolerance Keeps Winning Online

The internet doesn't select for truth or compassion — it selects for emotional engagement, and one emotion above all others drives clicks:

"Righteous is the number one emotion that creates engagement. You have to show someone something that is wrong and you have to make them feel right."

This is why calling people out rarely helps. When you publicly condemn someone, you galvanize the people already on your side and entrench everyone who opposes you. The fight becomes about winning, not solving anything. And it's not just anti-South Asian hate that's rising — anti-Jewish sentiment, anti-conservative sentiment, anti-liberal sentiment, anti-white-male sentiment are all increasing simultaneously. Intolerance on the whole is being algorithmically amplified because it works.

The deeper structural problem: people aren't even living in the same informational reality anymore. You can't resolve a disagreement about the dishes if you and your roommate are each looking at a completely different sink. Shared sensory input used to anchor shared reality. Social media has dissolved that anchor, and cognitive bias — always present — now operates on top of fractured information. This is why measuring "who has it worst" becomes nearly impossible and endlessly contested; everyone's feed is a personalized grievance engine.

What Actually Helps (And What Doesn't)

Calling someone out accomplishes one thing reliably: it makes the audience that already agrees with you feel validated. That's not nothing, but it's not the same as making the world safer or the hateful person reconsider anything.

"We've started confusing being offended with being injured, and we've started emphasizing validation over improvement."

There's a useful distinction borrowed from an MIT security guard: making people feel safe is not the same as making people safe. Validation feels good in the moment and changes very little downstream.

What tends to move people is being genuinely understood. Most racism and most toxicity isn't born from pure malice — it's born from ignoranceA fundamental ignorance about the true nature of reality and the self — specifically, the mistaken belief that you are your body and mind rather than something deeper.Full definition →, from hurt that gets redirected, from never having encountered the people being mocked. When someone holds a racist belief and you actually ask them to explain it, push them to trace where it came from, the position often collapses on its own. It can't survive scrutiny because it was never built on careful thought.

"If you really try to understand where their views come from… most of the time people just don't do that. Usually when I do that, I'm amazed by how quickly strongly held positions will fall apart."

This approach requires something uncomfortable: treating the hateful person as a human being whose experience of reality is shaped by forces you may not fully see yet. That isn't the same as agreeing with them.

Representation as a Durable Alternative

Rather than fighting fire with fire, positive representation is a route that doesn't feed the outrage engine. Celebrating your culture, showing up visibly as a happy, grounded person from a group being mocked — this offers the kid scrolling through a sea of hateful content a different version of reality to hold onto.

The goal isn't "this person is wrong." The goal is: here is something true and good that contradicts the lie, and you don't have to be ashamed of who you are.

This matters because when you don't see people who look like you represented anywhere — or when the only representations are stereotypes — the hate starts to feel like the whole story. One visible counterexample can break that spell.

What Meditation Is Actually For (And Why Most Teachers Can't Teach It)

A thread running through the conversation is the phenomenon of self-referential processing — the mental cascade that fires the moment someone looks at you. Attention from another person triggers you to see yourself through their eyes, which splits your focus, floods you with emotion, and pulls you out of the present moment. You stop being in your experience and start performing it.

Meditation, done well, helps you build a stable internal sense of self that you carry with you into those moments — so when the flood hits, you're not swept away by it.

The problem: almost nobody is being taught this well. Most meditation teachers are trained to a certificate, not to a demonstrated level of attainment. Bvari, TM apps, corporate mindfulnessThe capacity to separate self from mind and observe that gap — what Sanskrit calls sakshi bhav, or "witnessing attitude".Full definition → programs — these often teach the mechanics without conveying why you're doing it or what you're actually trying to develop. The result is a practice that feels good for thirty minutes and washes away by lunch.

A better approach starts with knowing your reason to meditate. What in your life are you trying to understand or change? The practice flows from that. A good teacher also modifies the practice based on what you're struggling with — they don't hand everyone the same instructions and walk away.

The deeper principle: focus and judgment cannot fully coexist. When you are completely absorbed in an experience — chanting a mantra, playing a game, being present with a person — the judging mind quiets. Efficacy Is Not the North Star

One reframe worth sitting with: making the biggest possible impact is a trap. Startups, creators, and organizations that optimize hard for "impact" tend to make compromising decisions that pull them far from where they started. Scalability is a word that, when applied to anything human and meaningful, tends to hollow it out.

"I moved away from efficacy as an outcome. I'm going to do what feels good to me, what feels right to me, what feels fun to me. I want to make some impact — but I don't have to make the biggest impact."

The healthiest sign a mental health resource can show is people leaving because their lives got better. Success that looks like dependency is the wrong kind of success.

Optimism, Pessimism, and Just Doing the Thing

The world does appear to be at some kind of inflection point — birth rates declining, economic strain, AI development outpacing governance, climate instability, a lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → epidemic. Whether it tips catastrophically or not is genuinely unclear.

But here's the thing: being optimistic leads to complacency. Being pessimistic leads to despair. Neither state makes you more effective at the thing directly in front of you.

"My relation to the problem is the most important thing. Optimism and pessimism are basically psychological things — do away with all of it. There's certain stuff in your life and in the world around you that can be immediately affected by you today. That's what you should do."

The future dimension torments people. Nobody can predict it. What you can do is solve the problem in front of you today, which will alter the trajectory of the future in ways you won't be able to fully measure — and that's fine.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking validation for progress. Feeling seen and actually improving your situation are different things. Content and conversations that make people feel righteously validated get rewarded by algorithms; that reward has nothing to do with whether anything got better.

  • Assuming calling out bad ideas combats them. It mostly just makes your existing supporters feel good and makes opponents dig in harder. Confrontation without understanding rarely changes minds.

  • Treating silence as complicity — or speech as virtue. Neither automatic silence nor automatic speaking-out is the right answer. The question is what effect your action actually has, not whether you performed the correct gesture.

  • Meditation without purpose or guidance. Doing a mindfulness app for a few months and quitting when it doesn't stick is an extremely common story, and it's mostly a teacher-quality problem. If nobody has explained why you're doing it or connected it to something you actually care about, the practice has no anchor.

  • Optimizing for growth when the goal is depth. The things that serve people most — a five-part lecture series, a genuine long conversation, communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → built around real investment — perform terribly on most platforms. That doesn't mean they're the wrong things to do.

  • Assuming every racial or cultural stereotype is either entirely true or entirely false. The human brain generates patterns from data; that pattern-generator is not a one-to-one map of truth. Many stereotypes have some upstream cause (like selection bias in immigration) without being literally true about a population.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify your actual reason to meditate before starting a practice. What specific quality — groundedness in social situations, less reactivity to criticism, clarity about who you are — do you want to develop? Start there, not with an app's default program.

  2. When you encounter hateful content online, ask before you respond: what outcome am I actually trying to produce? If the answer is "I want to feel righteous" or "I want my followers to feel validated," that's honest — but different from actually changing the situation.

  3. Try the "trace the belief" exercise. The next time you encounter a view you find offensive or obviously wrong, ask yourself: what experience of reality would make this seem true to the person holding it? You don't have to agree. Just see if you can find the internal logic.

  4. Invest in positive representation rather than negative pushback. Celebrate what you love about your background, identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition →, or community. Show that it's possible to be happy and flourishingA deep sense of contentment, peace, and well-being that is neurologically and experientially distinct from pleasure.Full definition → as whoever you are. This reaches the person who needs it without feeding the outrage engine.

  5. Strip the future dimension out of your biggest problems. Take one thing you've been anxious or despairing about and ask: what is the concrete action available to me today? Do that. Don't ask whether it will work.

  6. If you're teaching, coaching, or leading anyone — diagnose before prescribing. Cookie-cutter instruction fails the people who most need help. Find out where this particular person is stuck before offering your standard answer.

  7. If meditation hasn't stuck, look for a teacher rather than a better app. The difference between a practice that transforms something and one that feels good for thirty minutes is usually the quality of guidance and whether someone is adapting the practice to your actual obstacles.
