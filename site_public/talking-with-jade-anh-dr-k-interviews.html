Summary

TL;DR — How you treat yourself is shaped by things that happened long before you had any say in the matter, and self-blame can feel safer than vulnerability even when it quietly costs you everything.

This is a conversation between a psychiatrist and a young woman named Jade — a Twitch streamer in her early twenties who grew up in Germany, spent time in Thailand, and returned home during the pandemic. On the surface she seems self-aware, open, and mostly okay. The session keeps circling something she can't quite name: why she holds herself to a standard she would never apply to anyone else, and what that costs her.

The conversation moves through hypersexuality, trauma, identity, self-blame, and what it actually means to need something — ending with an insight that is simple but surprisingly hard to accept: the capacity to admit you might be broken is itself a sign that you aren't.

Why Hypersexuality After Trauma Isn't Random

Jade mentions she became hypersexual after a sexual assault at 16 — that thoughts and fantasies occupy her mind constantly and feel intrusive and unwanted. The instinct is to treat this as a separate problem to solve. It isn't.

When something deeply violating happens, the mind looks for a way to regain footing. Sexuality becomes a lens because it is emotionally safe — it lets you think about other people without actually connecting to them, trusting them, or needing them. Sex is just sex. There's no vulnerability required. There's no basement you have to go down into.

"I don't think your hypersexual thinking is going to go away unless you have other ways of thinking about people."

The intrusive thoughts aren't a malfunction. They're doing a job: keeping you at a comfortable distance from genuine human connection. The question isn't how to stop the thoughts — it's what they're protecting you from having to feel.

The Architecture of Self-Blame

Jade broke her curfew. She was drunk. She knows this. And so, in her accounting, what happened to her at 16 is partly — maybe mostly — her fault.

This is not unusual. Victims of assault, domestic violence, and abuse almost universally find a way to absorb responsibility for what was done to them. The reason is counterintuitive: blame is a form of control.

If it's your fault, then the world is still a place where bad things happen because of your choices. You can change your choices. That's a livable world. But if someone can harm you for no reason — if you can be walking down the street and something terrible just happens — that's terrifying. Randomness is scarier than guilt.

"If it's their fault then they're still in control, and that can feel good. But if you are a victim you let go of control in your life and you acknowledge that there are people out there that can do things to you that are not your fault — and that's actually terrifying."

So the mind chooses guilt. It's uncomfortable, but it's manageable. Powerlessness is not.

The Double Standard You Don't Notice You're Running

Jade says flatly that being a victim sounds weak to her. She would not say that about anyone else who had been through what she experienced. She says so herself. She knows other people aren't weak for being victims — only she is.

This isn't hypocrisy in the usual sense. A hypocrite lets themselves off the hook. Jade does the opposite: she holds herself to a harsher standard than she holds anyone else. Every flaw she spots in herself, she leans into. When a potential strength is named — courage — she immediately grabs it and reframes it as a deficit: I don't have enough courage yet.

"Your mind is going to look at whichever skill tree you're the worst at."

The pattern is consistent: find the weakness, confirm it, move on. The identity being protected is not I am a good person — it's I am not a victim. And because "not being a victim" is the foundation, any suggestion that she was one feels like a structural threat.

The Seven-Year-Old Who Learned to Count on No One

This is the piece that ties everything together. Long before the assault at 16, something happened when Jade was around seven: she was beaten up by older kids in a public space. People walked by. It took a long time for anyone to stop it.

The lesson her nervous system drew wasn't just other children can hurt you. It was: adults will watch and do nothing. The people who were supposed to protect her didn't. That is a different and deeper wound.

"You can only count on yourself — because if you count on others, it might never happen, or it happens too late."

Once that belief is installed, everything that follows starts to make sense. The self-blame after the assault. The hypersexual thinking that lets her engage with people without needing them. The refusal to identify as a victim, because victims need help and help doesn't come. She became an island not because she's cold or broken, but because an island can't be let down.

Beating Yourself Up as a Safety Strategy

There's a strange but coherent logic to being your own harshest critic. If you wake up every morning and punch yourself in the face — metaphorically — and you survive it, then you already know you can survive the worst thing anyone can do to you. Nothing from outside can land harder than what you do to yourself. The world loses its power to devastate you.

"As long as you're beating the crap out of yourself, people can hate you on Twitch, people can love you on Twitch — you can deal with it, because at the end of the day nothing they can do is worse than what you do to yourself."

This is adaptive. In an environment where people are unpredictable and help is unreliable, it's a reasonable solution. The cost is that you never get to rest. You never get to be wrong in a way that doesn't confirm your worst suspicion about yourself. And you never get to find out whether the world might, occasionally, be kind.

What It Actually Means to Be Broken

Near the end of the conversation, something shifts. Jade is asked whether it's okay to need to be fixed — and then whether she's allowed to be broken. She says yes to both.

"The capacity to accept that you could be broken and that it's okay to be broken is a sign that you're not broken."

This isn't a paradox for its own sake. Someone who is truly defended against their own pain — who insists everything is fine, who performs confidence to cover collapse — cannot say I might be broken and that's okay. The admission requires a kind of honesty and self-acceptance that is itself healthy. Jade has it. She just hasn't been giving herself credit for it.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking self-awareness for being okay. Jade can articulate her hypersexuality, her trauma history, her coping patterns — fluently. That fluency can read as health. But understanding something intellectually and having actually processed it emotionally are different. You can have a very coherent story about your damage and still be running the same old protective strategies underneath it.

Confusing "I don't feel like I need anything right now" with actually being fine. Needs don't disappear when the crisis passes. If your baseline involves constant self-criticism, intrusive thoughts, difficulty trusting anyone, and a sense that you're fundamentally alone — that's not fine. The fire going out doesn't mean the house is in good shape.

Treating self-criticism as self-improvement. There's a meaningful difference between noticing where you can grow and reflexively cataloging your deficiencies. One is oriented toward something. The other is just punishment that happens to use productivity language.

Taking on blame because it feels more controllable than randomness. If you were assaulted, abused, or hurt — and you find yourself working hard to identify what you did wrong — notice that. The calculation is not actually about fairness. It's about maintaining the feeling that the world is predictable.

Stopping therapy when you stop wanting to die. Jade left the one therapist who genuinely helped her once she felt stable enough. Suicidal ideation resolved; therapy resolved. The underlying architecture — the self-blame, the isolation, the distrust — stayed untouched.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the double standard in real time. For one week, when you catch yourself criticizing yourself for something, ask: Would I say this to a friend who had been through the same thing? Don't try to fix the criticism yet. Just notice the gap.

  2. Audit your explanations for bad things that happened to you. Pick one difficult event and write out honestly: whose fault do I believe this is? Then write out what you would tell someone else in the same situation. Compare the two. You don't have to change your answer — just see the difference clearly.

  3. Practice tolerating uncertainty about how you'll be treated. The self-punishment strategy exists because it makes the world predictable. Try one small exposure to unpredictability: share something real with someone you marginally trust and don't control the outcome. Let them respond however they respond.

  4. Consider returning to a body-based practice — Qigong, Tai Chi, yoga, or even slow walks with no phone. Trauma lives in the body. Practices that connect breath, movement, and attention give it somewhere to process that isn't just more thinking.

  5. Ask yourself once a week: "Am I being kind to myself?" Not kind in terms of buying things or taking days off — kind in the way you'd be kind to a person. If the honest answer is I don't really know how to do that, sit with that answer rather than rushing past it. That not-knowing is the starting point, not a failure.

  6. If you've stopped therapy because the crisis passed, reconsider. The best time to work on the underlying patterns is not when you're in freefall. Stability creates the bandwidth to actually look at things. Find someone direct — someone whose feedback you can feel, not just intellectually process.
