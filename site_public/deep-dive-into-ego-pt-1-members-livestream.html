Why the Ego Distorts Reality

Once ahamkara is active, it doesn't sit quietly in the background. It takes the incoming signals of the real world and filters them through the lens of past experience. The result is that you're often not responding to what's actually in front of you — you're responding to your mind's interpretation of it.

A concrete example: at 15, having been bullied repeatedly, a friendly girl sitting down to talk feels like a trap. The objective event (a kind person making conversation) gets filtered through a history of humiliation, producing paranoid thoughts and anxious emotions. The girl was just being nice. The suffering was entirely self-generated.

"There is the real world out here, and then there is something that we add to it. This is what ahamkara does."

This matters practically in two ways:

  1. It degrades decision-making. If you miss a promotion and your ego tells you "the boss is biased, I'm amazing," you might job-hop — only to find the same pattern everywhere, because the real issue was never addressed. Removing ego from the equation allows you to ask an honest question: Do I actually need to perform differently, or is the environment genuinely the problem?

  2. It creates suffering even in success. Michael Phelps winning four gold medals and then experiencing suicidal ideation the next morning is not a paradox once you understand ahamkara. The ego that drove him to the top is the same ego that now has nowhere to go but down. Klishta — The Mental Coloring

A klishta is the meaning, evaluation, or emotional charge your mind adds to a neutral stimulus. The word translates roughly as "coloring."

Two pens sit on a desk. Objectively, they're two pieces of plastic and ink. One writes more smoothly. That's a fact. But the mind goes further: "this one is terrible, that one is superior, I'm annoyed that I ended up with the bad one." The additional layer — the annoyance, the judgment, the sense of deprivation — is klishta.

An unanswered text is an absence of a text. That's the entire objective reality. The mind adds: "they don't like me, this relationship is one-sided, I should never reach out first again." None of that is in the text's absence. All of it is produced by the mind.

Common Pitfalls

Or it might be ahamkara projecting jealousy, fear, or past experience. The conclusion is the same; the methodology is entirely different. The question to ask is not whether the conclusion is correct, but what is generating it.

Arguing intellectually against these ideas.
