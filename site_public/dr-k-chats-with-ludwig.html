Where Confidence Actually Comes From

A useful reframe surfaces here. The confidenceSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → that looks impressive from the outside often isn't "I know I'll succeed." It's closer to "whatever happens, I'll be able to work with it."

The reasoning: the sun goes down and comes back up. You live the next day, and the next, and the next. Even genuinely bad outcomes — a career ending, something embarrassing leaking, a business failing — eventually become the new terrain you navigate from. That's not toxic positivity; it's a realistic observation that people adapt. The catastrophe you're bracing for is almost never the end of the story.

This is structurally different from believing you won't fail. It's believing failure isn't terminal. That belief frees you to try things, because the downside is survivable.

The Flow State: Three Conditions That Actually Matter

Flow gets described vaguely a lot. Here it's broken into three concrete requirements:

  1. Mind in the present — not projecting into the future (what if I lose?), not replaying the past
  2. Absence of ego — not thinking about how you're being perceived, whether you're good or bad, what winning would mean for your identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition →
  3. One-pointedness — full attention on the single task at hand

The third condition is what arousal (the right level of challenge) provides externally. If a task is too easy, your mind wanders. If it's too hard, your mind scatters across worst-case scenarios. The sweet spot isn't about difficulty for its own sake — it's about inducing undivided attention.

What kills flow in competition isn't losing; it's the moment you start thinking about what winning would mean. A player who's been grinding a perfect set starts imagining the victory, the identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → shift, what a reverse sweep would mean — and that's when the collapse begins. The game hasn't changed. The mind has left the present.

"When you're not thinking about winning or losing is when you play your best game."

This applies identically to creative work, athletic performance, and conversation. The flow state is available in any domain where these three conditions are met — including, apparently, a well-timed bathroom stop on a long road trip.

"Should" vs. What You Actually Want

Living a life organized around should produces two outcomes, and both are bad:

  • When you fulfill the should, you don't feel good — because the satisfaction belongs to whoever's standard you were meeting, not to you
  • When you fail the should, you feel guilty

It's a heads-you-lose, tails-you-lose situation. And the deeper problem is that most of what people think they want — career success, a good relationship, a respectable lifestyle — was also absorbed from outside. Advertisements enter through your senses. Social norms enter through observation. Very little of what feels like a want originated in a vacuum.

The alternative isn't to reject all external influence (impossible) or to live without standards. It's to move from adherence to a borrowed rulebook toward understanding the game. Once you understand how human psychology actually works — how happiness is generated, what flow requires, what causes burnout — you can make choices that align with who you are rather than who you think you're supposed to be.

A useful analogy: when someone asks which character to play in a game they're new to, the honest answer is "whoever you feel most comfortable with." That's not avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition → — it's recognizing that alignment with your own style matters more than playing the theoretically optimal pick. Life is the same.

Eudaimonic happiness (roughly serotonergic): contentment-based, sustained, and driven by four specific things: - Self-determination — choosing your own direction - Self-expression — putting something of yourself into the world - Importance — feeling like what you do matters - Effort — doing something that requires real engagement

The practical upside: you don't have to overhaul your life to access eudaimonic happiness. Writing one chapter instead of one game session — not out of guilt, but as a genuine choice — can produce more lasting satisfaction than the session would have.

Regret Is a Thought, Not a Verdict

And a wish is just a thought. That's not a dismissal of it; it's a clarification of what it actually is.

The strategy of "live to avoid future regret" has a hidden flaw: you don't know what your 85-year-old self will regret. You might cut back on work to avoid work-regret and discover a different regret in its place. You might sacrifice today for a future that never satisfies you because you brought your dissatisfaction with you.

"Your problems are not created by the outside world. They're created by what you carry and the way that you see it."

The person who leaves a stressful company to open a bakery is likely to find themselves, two years later, with a second location, dependent employees, and the same feeling of obligation they were escaping. The problem travels. The bakery doesn't fix it.

What does help is understanding the mechanics of regret itself — well enough that you can observe it happening in your mind ("this is just a thought about a past I can't change") without being hijacked by it.

Living in Three-Day Segments Instead of Deferring Everything

The Majora's Mask analogy that closes the conversation is worth sitting with. The game is built on three-day loops. You don't beat it by staring at the moon falling toward you. You beat it by doing what needs doing in days one, two, and three — and then resetting.

Most burnout doesn't come from working hard. It comes from working hard in a terrible present while betting everything on a future day when things will finally be good. That day doesn't arrive on schedule, and if it does, you discover it's still just another day one, two, and three.

The sustainable approach is to make days one, two, and three livable — not perfect, not maximally optimized, just genuinely okay. Small pleasures matter: a bubble tea run with your kid, a DOTA patch dropping, an hour of reading something you actually care about. These aren't indulgences that steal from productivity. They're what makes the loop sustainable enough to keep running.

Treating vacations as breaks when they're actually debt. For anyone whose work has no substitute, "taking time off" often means doubling up before and after. Calling it a vacation while working twice as hard to create the appearance of one is a different problem from actually resting. Recognizing this clearly is the first step toward actually solving it.

Using "one day I'll..." as a motivating thought. One day is not a calendar date. It functions as a pressure-release valve that lets you avoid the discomfort of doing the thing now, while creating the illusion of intention. If it matters, it has a date. If it doesn't have a date, notice that.
