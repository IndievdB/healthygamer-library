Specific Phobias: Common, Treatable, and Not What You Think

Specific phobias — a fear of flying, heights, blood, snakes, small spaces — affect close to 10% of the population. That makes them one of the more common anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → presentations there is. The diagnostic criteria are straightforward: the presence (or even the anticipation) of a particular object or situation triggers immediate, intense fear, and the fear is out of proportion to the actual danger. People go to significant lengths to avoid whatever the phobia is centered on.

The treatment with the strongest evidence behind it is exposure therapy. And that phrase alone is enough to send a lot of people running in the other direction.

The mental image most people have of exposure therapy goes something like: you walk into a therapist's office, they pull the snake out of a bag, and they drop it in your lap. That's not what it is.

Here's how a real graduated exposure actually works, using a fear of elevators as the example:

  1. Think about an elevator — sitting in the office, no elevator anywhere nearby, just holding the concept in mind until it stops producing acute distress
  2. Think about going into an elevator — still purely mental, one step up in intensity
  3. Walk past an elevator — no stopping, no staring, just proximity
  4. Watch the elevator doors open and close — getting used to the sight and sound of it
  5. Step inside for one or two seconds, then step right back out — there's enormous safety in knowing you're leaving immediately
  6. Let the doors close, then hit the open button yourself — you're in control of when it ends
  7. Ride one floor — then a few more — then up and down freely

The goal at every step is manageability, not overwhelm.
