Summary

TL;DR — Comparison feels addictive because your brain evolved it for a real purpose, and fighting the comparison directly doesn't work. To stop comparing, you need to attack the two things that actually drive it: the unfairness hidden inside every comparison, and the negative emotion that triggered it in the first place.

Comparison is everywhere and it's getting worse. Most advice tells you to spend less time on social media — good luck with that. A more useful approach is understanding why your brain compares at all, how social media has broken that system, and what you can actually do to short-circuit it.

Why Your Brain Compares in the First Place

Comparison isn't a flaw or a character weakness. According to social comparison theory, it's a built-in cognitive tool with two specific jobs:

  • Upward comparisons (looking at someone doing better than you) are meant to serve as a road map for improvement. If your classmate scored higher on the exam, what study strategies are they using? If someone at the gym is more fit, what are they doing differently? The purpose is to spot a gap and find a way forward.

  • Downward comparisons (looking at someone doing worse than you) are meant to serve as a short-term emotional coping strategy. When you feel bad about yourself, noticing someone worse off provides temporary relief.

Both of these are genuinely useful in the right doses. The problem is that both can go off the rails.

How Comparison Goes Wrong

Upward comparisons backfire when they destroy motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → instead of generating direction. You walk into a gym, see people who are incredibly ripped, and instead of thinking "what can I learn from this?" you think "there's no point — I'll never get there." The comparison flips from a road map into a wall.

Downward comparisons backfire when they become a substitute for actually changing anything. If the only way you know how to feel better is by putting others down — finding noobs to feel superior to, criticizing people around you online — you get short-term emotional relief but your situation stays completely stuck.

The other major trap is that you don't compare yourself to one person holistically. You unconsciously take the best attribute from five different people and stack all of those against yourself:

  • That person's relationship vs. your relationship
  • This person's finances vs. your finances
  • That other person's fitness vs. your fitness

You're not making a one-to-one comparison. You're making a composite of everyone's highlights against your whole, unfiltered life. That's a competition you will lose every single time.

Why Social Media Made This So Much Worse

Your brain doesn't compare itself to elephants or ants. It doesn't compare itself to heads of state. It compares itself to people it perceives to be in the same league — historically, classmates, siblings, colleagues, neighbors.

Social media has quietly broken this filter. By giving you intimate access to a content creator's daily life — what they eat, what they wear, how they spend their time — your brain starts to process them as someone in your league. The parasocial closeness tricks the comparison circuit into firing.

Once that happens, you're comparing yourself to a carefully curated highlight reel presented as a real life. There's no realistic path from your current situation to what they're showing you, so the upward comparison can't do its job. It can't generate a road map. It can only make you feel inferior.

The Ego's Role (and Why Negative Emotion Is the Real Fuel)

Here's where yogic psychology offers something the science alone doesn't: a way to work on yourself rather than just understand the mechanism.

In the yogic model of the mind, the ahamkar (the ego) is the faculty that makes comparisons. This isn't "ego" in the casual sense of arrogance — it's the part of the mind responsible for your sense of self. And importantly, your sense of self requires comparison to exist. You can't be tall without someone being short. You can't feel successful without a reference point for failure. The deeper insight is this: the ego activates in response to negative emotion. When you feel bad, the ego fires up to protect you — and one of its tools is comparison. Downward comparisons temporarily relieve the bad feeling. Certain upward comparisons, paradoxically, confirm the bad feeling, which the ego also finds useful because it creates consistency.

Think about the post-breakup social media spiral. You feel terrible, so you go look at your ex's profile, see them looking happy and surrounded by attractive people, and feel even worse — but you can't stop looking. This isn't masochism. It's the ego seeking validation for the way you already feel. Feeling like a failure and then finding evidence that you're a failure is, in a strange way, satisfying to the ego because it makes the internal world feel coherent.

"If you want to stop making these comparisons, what you really need to do is focus on that negative emotion... because if I let the emotion activate my ego, then that'll change my behavior and cause me to make a lot of comparisons."

The comparison is downstream of the emotion. If you only fight the comparison, you're fighting the symptom.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to rationalize your way out of a comparison. When you or a friend argues "this is apples and oranges" or "your situation is different," it rarely sticks. You already know intellectually the comparison isn't perfectly fair. Thinking the solution is just less social media. It's the right direction but extremely hard to execute on willpower alone, and it doesn't address what happens in the moments when you are online, or when comparisons come from people you know in real life.

Confusing downward comparison with confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →. Being consistently dismissive of others — staying stuck blaming teammates, dismissing peers — feels like self-assurance but is actually a coping loop. People in this pattern often stay the most stuck, because the emotional need is being met without any real change happening.

Only targeting the comparison itself. The yogic insight is that the ego fires because of an underlying emotional state. Trying to suppress comparisons directly, without addressing what's stirring the ego up, is like trying to hold a lid on a boiling pot. The pressure will find another outlet.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Interrogate the comparison, not the outcome. The next time you catch yourself comparing, don't ask "is this comparison making me feel bad?" Ask: "Why did my brain pick this specific person as a fair comparison?" Trace the logic. Usually you'll find it doesn't fully hold — they have resources, circumstances, or a team you don't, or you're only seeing a curated slice of their life.

  2. Audit who you're actually comparing against. Notice when a comparison is actually a composite — when you're stacking one person's relationship, another person's finances, and a third person's fitness all against yourself simultaneously. Name each piece separately. The stack becomes much harder to take seriously once it's visible.

  3. When a comparison loop starts, turn toward the emotion underneath it. Ask: what am I actually feeling right now, before the comparison showed up? Lonely? Anxious? Ashamed? That feeling is the fuel. Sitting with it — even briefly acknowledging it without acting on it — takes pressure off the ego's need to go looking for comparisons.

  4. Use upward comparisons deliberately and narrowly. If you're going to compare upward, do it on purpose and with a specific goal: "What is this person doing differently that I could actually learn from or act on?" If there's no realistic action step available, the comparison isn't productive — notice that and let it go.

  5. Track your comparison patterns relative to your mood. For one week, whenever you catch yourself in a comparison spiral, briefly note what your emotional state was just before it started. Most people discover they compare significantly more when they're already feeling low. That pattern alone makes the ego's role much harder to ignore.
