Summary

TL;DR — Feeling overwhelmed isn't about having too much to do; it's about having too little that you chose to do. The fix is often to take on more, not less.

Most people assume that feeling overwhelmed means life has piled on too much, and the solution is to cut back. That's one valid response — but there's a second, counterintuitive solution that most people miss entirely: deliberately adding more active challenges to your life. Understanding the difference between the two types of challenges you face is the key to making sense of why this works.

Two Kinds of Challenges: Passive vs. Active

Every challenge you face falls into one of two categories:

  • Passive challenges are things life throws at you — an insurance company denying a prescription, a parking ticket that needs paying, a surprise deadline, bureaucratic friction. You didn't choose these. They just land on you.
  • Active challenges are things you choose to take on — starting a creative project, learning a skill, building something, committing to a new habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition →. You opted in.

The same amount of total effort can feel completely different depending on which category it falls into. Grinding through insurance phone calls for hours feels draining in a way that spending the same hours building something you care about simply doesn't — even if the second one is objectively harder work.

The Real Cause of Feeling Overwhelmed

Overwhelm isn't just "too much stuff." It's a specific imbalance: your passive challenges are outpacing your active challenges.

When life is throwing more at you than you are throwing at yourself, that's when the feeling of being out of control kicks in — and being overwhelmed is feeling out of control. They're essentially the same thing.

"The feeling of overwhelmed comes from a discrepancy between active challenges and passive challenges. When life is throwing more at you — not more than you can handle, but more at you than what you throw at yourself — is when you feel overwhelmed."

This reframes the problem. It's not that the total load is too heavy. It's that the ratio is off. You're a passenger in your own life rather than a driver.

The Counterintuitive Fix: Add, Don't Just Subtract

The obvious response to overwhelm is to reduce what life demands of you. That works, but it's often not fully within your control — the passive challenges keep coming regardless. The second solution, the one most people overlook, is to deliberately add active challenges.

When you choose something — anything — and start working on it, several things happen at once:

  1. You reclaim a sense of agency. You're no longer just reacting; you're initiating. That shift alone starts to dissolve the feeling of being overwhelmed.
  2. You spend energy productively. Active challenges are a legitimate outlet for mental energy. Without them, that energy doesn't disappear — it circulates in your head as anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →.
  3. You build momentum. Small wins from active challenges create a sense of accomplishment that carries over into how you handle the passive stuff.

The practical implication is real: even if your passive load hasn't changed at all, picking up an active challenge — cleaning your room, starting a project, committing to a workout — can meaningfully reduce how overwhelmed you feel. You're doing more total work, but you feel better.

Anxiety as Unused Mental Energy

There's a useful way to think about anxiety in this framework: anxiety is unused energy.

It isn't a mysterious force that arrives from nowhere. It's mental energy that has nowhere useful to go. When you're not directing your energy into chosen tasks, it stays in motion as looping thoughts and low-grade dread.

"All anxiety is unused energy — it's just thoughts bouncing around in your head that never go away. Anxiety is a disorder of excess mental energy."

Active challenges act as a drain on that excess. When you take on something you've chosen, you direct that mental energy outward into action. The pool of energy available to sustain anxious spiraling shrinks. You don't have to "fight" anxiety directly — you just give that energy somewhere better to go.

This also explains why busy people who love their work often report low anxiety even under heavy workloads, while someone doing relatively little but stuck in passive-challenge mode can feel constantly on edge.

The Upward Spiral

There's a well-known concept of the downward spiral — where overwhelm leads to withdrawalThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, which leads to more overwhelm, which leads to more withdrawal. The same logic runs in reverse.

When you take on an active challenge and complete it — even a small one — you feel more in control. Feeling more in control makes it easier to take on the next active challenge. That one builds more energy and confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →, which makes the passive challenges feel more manageable.

The entry point doesn't have to be dramatic. Cleaning a room, finishing a single task you've been avoiding, starting a small project you care about — these are sufficient. The size of the active challenge matters less than the fact that you chose it and you did it. That's what starts the cycle moving in the right direction.

Common Pitfalls

Waiting until you feel ready to add more. The instinct is to clear the passive backlog first, then choose something active. But the passive backlog rarely clears on its own. Waiting for calm before taking action inverts the actual sequence — the action is what produces the calm.

Confusing "doing more" with "piling on obligations." The active challenges that help are ones you genuinely choose. Taking on more things you feel forced to do just adds to the passive side of the ledger. The key ingredient is volition. If it doesn't feel like a choice, it probably won't have the same effect.

Treating all busyness as equivalent. Being occupied isn't the same as taking on active challenges. Scrolling through tasks reactively, staying busy in a purely responsive mode — that's still passive-challenge territory even if you're technically "doing things." The distinction is whether you initiated it.

Dismissing small active challenges. People often wait for a project worthy of their energy — something meaningful enough to "count." But any chosen task works. The mechanism doesn't require the challenge to be impressive. It just has to be yours.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your current challenge ratio. Write two lists side by side: everything life is currently demanding of you (passive challenges), and everything you are actively choosing to pursue (active challenges). If the first list dwarfs the second, that imbalance is likely the source of your overwhelm.

  2. Choose one small active challenge right now. It doesn't have to be important or impressive — clean a surface, start a document, begin something you've put off because you wanted to do it, not because you had to. The act of choosing and completing it is what matters.

  3. When you feel overwhelmed, ask: "What can I choose to do right now?" Not "what do I have to do" — but what do you want to work on, even briefly. Redirect your energy into that before returning to the passive demands.

  4. Use physical tasks as an entry point. Cleaning a room, organizing a space, doing a workout — these are reliable active challenges because the feedback is immediate and visible. They're good starting points when mental tasks feel too difficult to initiate.

  5. Notice when anxiety spikes and treat it as a signal. Instead of trying to suppress anxious thoughts, ask what active challenge you could direct that energy toward. Anxiety indicates unused energy — find it a productive outlet rather than fighting it directly.
