What Drug Dealing Actually Gave Him

The escalation from using to selling weed is usually explained as "the next step" or "economics." Doug's account goes deeper.

Yes, there was a financial logic — sell part of what you buy and smoke for free. But the real engine was something more fundamental: selling drugs was the first time people genuinely needed him.

His phone rang. People reached out. They wanted to spend time with him. He had something valuable that others lacked, and that gave him a form of power he had never experienced. For a kid whose home was a war zone between two parents who communicated only through lawyers, who was kicked out at sixteen, who was sleeping on hardwood floors at friends' houses — being needed was not a small thing. It was a basic human hunger being fed for the first time.

"I finally had some sense of power because people were now needing me for something that I had control over."

Dr. K makes the point that no medical training he received ever captured this. This is why "just stop using" misses the target so badly. You're not asking someone to put down a substance. You're asking them to demolish the only world in which they feel like a real person.

It's also why substitution often fails without deeper work. When Doug eventually got ripped and successful as a personal trainer, he hit many of the external markers of a transformed life. But the underlying mechanism — external validation as the source of self-worth — hadn't changed. He still needed people's approval of his physique to feel okay. The drug changed; the dependence didn't.

"I was basing my self-worth on people's approval of me. I had to keep maintaining that physique because I thought if I didn't have that physique nobody would like me."

What Rock Bottom Actually Is

The standard narrative of recovery has a clear structure: things got bad, the person hit rock bottom, then they turned around. Doug hit rock bottom in jail. He met a cellmate who challenged him, he did one push-up, and the upward arc began.

But Dr. K pushes on this, and it leads somewhere important.

Doug had a thousand potential rock bottoms before jail. He went to multiple friends' funerals as a teenager. He was hospitalized twice for panic attacks he thought were heart attacks. He was homeless. He was kicked out of two homes. Any one of those could have been the turning point. None of them were — because he wasn't receptive.

The cellmate didn't give Doug anything his mother, his friends' parents, or the girls who begged him to stop hadn't already tried to give him. What was different in that jail cell was Doug's state of mind: sober for the first time in years, stripped of his usual environment and coping mechanisms, face-to-face with someone who had no stake in flattering him.

"Rock bottom is defined by the moment you make a change, not the circumstance."

That reframe matters enormously for people who haven't hit some dramatic external low point. You don't need the jail cell. You don't need the ambulance. Rock bottom is wherever you are when you become willing to stop moving in the wrong direction. It's defined retroactively — it's the lowest point you reach before turning around. Which means the lowest point you ever need to reach is the moment you choose to turn around right now.

What rock bottom does, functionally, is remove excuses. Extreme circumstances do this automatically and brutally. But excuses can be removed deliberately, through honesty, through the kind of self-examination that doesn't require a crisis to trigger it.

Self-Worth and the Work That Actually Matters

One of the most quietly devastating moments in the conversation is when Doug describes what he would have wished for if his parents could have granted him anything as a teenager:

"I want attention from pretty girls, I want a six-pack and big biceps, and I want to make really good money."

He eventually got all three. And he was still miserable.

The reason is that none of those things addressed the actual question, which wasn't what do I want? but am I worth wanting? As long as self-worth is contingent on external evidence — approval, appearance, income — it's permanently unstable. The evidence can always be withdrawn. And so the person is always, at some level, auditioning for their own right to exist.

The real work is learning to validate yourself internally. This sounds like therapy-speak until you watch someone who hasn't done it keep reaching for the next external thing — a better physique, a bigger platform, a more impressive résumé — and noticing that the feeling of being enough never quite arrives.

Doug connects this to a pattern he recognizes in himself from his drug years: morphing into whatever version of yourself a given group seemed to want, until you no longer know who you actually are.

Small Wins and the Direction of Confidence

Doug couldn't do a push-up from his feet. He got down and tried in front of a room full of incarcerated men and collapsed. Nobody laughed. People encouraged him.

That single push-up from his knees felt, in his words, like climbing Everest. Not because it was athletically significant, but because it shattered a belief he had carried for years: that he was someone who couldn't do hard things.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing events with experiences. The bullet points of someone's recovery story — jail, cellmate, push-up, transformation — can make it look like circumstances drive change. They don't. Circumstances create conditions. Receptivity creates change. Waiting for the right dramatic circumstance is a way of deferring the choice indefinitely.

Replacing one external source with another. Getting sober and then becoming obsessed with fitness, money, or status isn't recovery from external validation dependency — it's a quieter version of the same thing. The question to keep asking is: would I be okay if this were taken away?

Hearing "take responsibility" as "it's your fault." These are different things. Difficult childhoods, divorced parents, chaotic environments — these are real, they have measurable effects, and they aren't your fault. But responsibility for change can't be outsourced. Nobody is coming to fix this for you, and that's not a moral judgment; it's just how change works.

Assuming people know how to do the "simple" things. Walk up and talk to someone at the gym. Reach out to a friend. Ask for help. These actions are described as obvious, but for someone who grew up with severe social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, whose socialization happened primarily through substances, or who has spent years in digital-only relationships — these are not simple. Treating them as obvious is a reliable way to make people feel more broken than they are.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Ask yourself the actual question. Not "what do I want to have or achieve?" but "what feeling am I trying to get, and what am I currently using to get it?" Write it down. Be specific. Doug wanted to feel normal and needed. That's the thing to name — not the substance or behavior, but the need underneath it.

  2. Identify one small, concrete thing you've been avoiding and do it once today. Not a lifestyle change — a single action. One email, one walk, one push-up. The goal isn't the action; it's the evidence that you can do something you thought you couldn't.

  3. Write down your deep emotional why. Not a goal ("I want to be healthier") but a specific image of a version of yourself you refuse to return to. The more visceral and personal, the more durable it will be when motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → runs low.

  4. Audit your environment. List the five people you spend the most time with. For each one, ask: does this person share where I want to go, or where I've been? This isn't about cutting people off — it's about being honest about what your environment is normalizing.

  5. Find a group activity. A fitness class, a club, a regular meetup — anything that puts you in the same room with the same people repeatedly and makes conversation contextually natural. You don't have to be good at socializing first. The structure does most of the work.

  6. When someone you care about is struggling, ask why before asking what. Not "what are you doing, why won't you stop, what do you need to change" — but a genuine, patient "why?" And then actually listen to the answer without immediately redirecting toward solutions. The person speaking is often the one who most needs to hear what comes out.

  7. Redefine your rock bottom. Write down the moment — not necessarily a crisis, just the clearest moment — when you knew something needed to change. That was your rock bottom. You don't need a more dramatic one. The question now is what you do from here.
