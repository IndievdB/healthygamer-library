Summary

TL;DR — Resilience can become a trap when it lets you ignore warning signs until you completely break down. The solution isn't toughing it out harder; it's consistently emptying the emotional trash before it overflows.

This is a conversation with LS, a League of Legends analyst and caster who had just finished four weeks averaging 110-hour work weeks — casting LCK five days a week, then coming back to the hotel to watch EU matches or stream North American ones. By week two, insomnia, anxiety, psychosomatic symptoms, and what he called emotional "snaps" had become routine. The conversation moves from triage to root cause: why does someone this intelligent and self-aware keep running themselves into the ground, and what's actually happening underneath all the separate symptoms?

Resilience Is a Double-Edged Sword

Most people think resilience is purely good. But there's a specific failure mode for highly resilient people: you can tolerate dysfunction at levels that would force a less resilient person to stop and fix the problem. You function at a high level until a hard breaking point, then suddenly snap — rather than bending gradually like most people do.

The gym analogy LS uses is apt: the goal is to push through discomfort without injuring yourself. The problem is he doesn't know where that line is anymore — or more accurately, he's so used to crossing it that he stopped feeling it.

"You've gotten used to injuries and that's gotta stop."

This creates a dangerous feedback loop. Because you can keep going, you assume you should keep going. The warning signals — insomnia, body aches, anxiety, emotional outbursts — get treated as noise rather than as data.

The Overflowing Trash Can

Think of unprocessed emotions as trash. Everyone generates emotional trash throughout the day: frustration, disappointment, fear, anger. Most people have a small trash can and feel the full smell quickly, so they deal with it. Resilient, high-functioning people often have a much larger capacity — they can pack the trash down and stuff in more, so they only notice the problem when the can is overflowing or the bag finally bursts.

"The only time you notice there's trash to be emptied is when your trashcan is overflowing."

LS described his breaking point clearly: a snap roughly every nine weeks where everything collapses, he yells, asks his roommates to leave, has a full emotional breakdown, and then resets. After the reset, he gets a new (metaphorical) paper trash bag and starts filling it up again.

That breakdown is actually adaptive — it works, in the sense that it allows him to keep functioning. But "working" and "healthy" aren't the same thing. The reset doesn't clean the basement. Years of accumulated, unprocessed emotional material sit underneath the everyday trash. The basement fills up even when the kitchen trash can looks empty.

When Multiple Symptoms Are Actually One Problem

A long list of diagnoses is often a sign that something deeper is being missed. Insomnia, anxiety, anger, psychosomatic illness (in LS's case, worsening asthma), identity confusion, imposter syndrome, and relational friction can all appear to be separate problems. They usually aren't.

The body and mind are linked. Autoimmune and inflammatory conditions — asthma, IBS, rheumatoid arthritis — are heavily influenced by psychological stress. LS noticed his breathing improved dramatically mid-conversation and initially assumed his albuterol had kicked in. The more accurate explanation: talking through what was weighing on him removed some of the stress load that was constricting his breathing in the first place.

"Psychosomatic doesn't mean it's all in your head. It means your body is processing what your mind can't."

Being Called a Liar Is Still Happening

LS talks about how much it bothers him when he makes a well-reasoned argument about game strategy — something quantifiable, something he can back up with video evidence — and gets dismissed or accused of being contrarian. He described it as talking to flat-earthers: you can see the problem with their logic, but you can't demonstrate it verbally, and they're not open to the evidence.

The deeper pattern: LS grew up in an environment where he lied frequently as a survival mechanism (to defuse volatile situations at home), got labeled a liar, and felt fundamentally misunderstood by the adults around him — teachers, family — who treated his behavior as the problem rather than asking why the behavior was happening in the first place.

When someone dismisses his strategic analysis today, something older than the argument activates. He isn't just defending a freeze position in a League match. He's fighting every moment where he was written off, mislabeled, or not given the benefit of the doubt.

"You're not just dealing with that interaction. Your history and emotional weight from the past actually play into it."

This is why the emotional charge feels so disproportionate to the surface-level argument. It isn't disproportionate — it's carrying decades of weight.

Identity: The Missing Foundation

Throughout the conversation, LS keeps circling something he can't quite name: a feeling he describes as homelessness. Despite being at the peak of his career, financially stable, doing work he genuinely loves — there's an emptiness underneath.

The explanation isn't complicated, even if fixing it takes time: he doesn't have a stable, secure sense of who he is. Identity is the internal anchor that makes external criticism manageable. When you know who you are, someone calling you wrong or stupid or contrarian is just noise — you already have the answer. When you don't have that anchor, the same criticism destabilizes you, which is why you need to keep convincing them. You're not trying to win the argument. You're trying to feel real.

The irony is that LS actually does know his purpose: he loves strategy, he finds it beautiful, he wants to advance how people think about it. That's not a vague aspiration — it's unusually clear. The gap is between knowing your purpose and trusting yourself as the person who holds it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the overflow as the problem. Getting a banana bag, booking a spa day, taking one afternoon off — these address the spill, not the full can. Relief feels like resolution.
  • Banking on future rest. "I'll take a month off when this season ends" sounds reasonable, but you're still you during that month. The anxiety about missed opportunities, the compulsive phone-checking, the unprocessed emotions — they all travel with you. The break is partly an illusion.
  • Confusing resilience with health. Being able to function under extreme stress is not evidence that the stress isn't hurting you. It just means the damage is delayed.
  • Thinking psychosomatic means imaginary. Physical symptoms driven by psychological stress are physically real. Dismissing them as "just stress" while continuing the behavior that causes them solves nothing.
  • Starting the day in the deep end. Checking your phone the moment you wake up floods your nervous system with stress signals before you've had a chance to exist as a person. You begin the day already reactive.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the "unwind" technique before bed. Starting from the last thing you did before lying down, mentally trace backwards through your day event by event, noting what you felt at each point without diving into the emotions. Acknowledge and move back. The goal is to unwind what accumulated during the day so you don't wake up still carrying it. This should take five to ten minutes. If you find yourself getting absorbed in a particular memory, that's a signal there's more trash there — note it and keep moving backward.

  2. No phone for the first 30 minutes after waking up. This is not complicated but it's non-negotiable if you want to stop starting your day already tilted. Watch Netflix, eat something, shower, sit outside — anything that doesn't involve social media, Discord, or news. You are giving your nervous system a window to exist at baseline before it gets kicked.

  3. Find a regular outlet for emotional processing. This can be a therapist, a structured conversation with someone you trust, or even journaling — but it has to be regular, not crisis-driven. The goal is weekly trash removal, not a biannual basement cleaning. You don't need to wait until you're welling up before a broadcast.

  4. Notice when you're fighting old battles in new arguments. The next time you feel disproportionately activated by someone dismissing your analysis or calling you wrong, pause and ask: am I actually defending this argument, or am I defending myself against something older? You don't have to resolve it in the moment — just recognize it's happening.

  5. Identify one constraint you can actually remove. LS stopped the EU co-streams as a first step. Look at your current load and find one thing that is truly optional — not "important to do" but optional — and remove it for two weeks. Notice what that does to your baseline.

  6. Don't wait for week two. The problem didn't start in week two when symptoms appeared. It started in week one when the conditions that produce symptoms were put in place. Begin managing stress in week one, when nothing feels wrong yet.
