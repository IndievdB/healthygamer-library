Summary

TL;DR — Personality isn't fixed; it's a collection of habitual reactions to situations, and you can deliberately reshape it by studying those reactions rather than trying to fix your behavior directly.

Most people treat their personality as a given — "I'm just lazy," "I'm not a morning person," "I can't handle parties." That framing is the problem. Personality is actually built from thousands of small, repeated reactions to everyday situations, and because it's built, it can be rebuilt. The catch is that almost nobody looks at how they're building it.

What Personality Actually Is

Personality isn't some fixed core you're born with. It's the sum total of how you respond to specific situations: where you sit in a classroom, how you react when someone is late, what you do after you lose a game, how you feel when you walk into a crowded room.

Two people handed the same situation will respond differently — and that pattern of differences, taken across hundreds of situations, is what we call personality. It isn't static. The evidence on this is overwhelming.

The Eastern Model: What Your Mind Is Actually Made Of

Western personality science (like the five-factor model — openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism) is great for describing populations and general tendencies. It can't tell you what you specifically should do. The yogic framework is different because it was built from the inside out — one person studying their own mind in exquisite detail. That makes it far more actionable at the individual level.

The Video Game Analogy That Explains Why Nothing Changes

Think about competitive gaming. There are players with thousands of hours in a game who never improve their rank. Meanwhile, a player with a fraction of those hours reaches the top. The hours aren't the variable. What separates them is whether they study their own play.

The low-MMR player queues up, loses, and immediately queues again. No replay analysis. No reflection. Just autopilot. Life works exactly the same way. Most people accumulate experience without ever examining how they responded to it. They stay at the same emotional and behavioral MMR for decades — and then conclude that this is just who they are.

The biohacking and life-hacking industry thrives on this exact mistake. People label themselves ("I'm lazy," "I'm not a morning person") and then look for supplements, hacks, or systems to work around that label instead of asking whether the label itself is a samskar.

How Samskars Are Actually Formed (and Dissolved)

Formation is simple: something happens → it produces an emotional reaction → you distract yourself rather than process it → the undigested emotion goes dormant → it reactivates later and shapes your behavior without your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

Dissolution works in reverse: you surface the reaction → you observe it without judgment → you follow it to its root → and as you genuinely understand it, it loses its grip.

This is also why clinical psychotherapy for personality disorders actually works. When a therapist helps a narcissistic patient examine a reaction — say, genuine hurt that parents didn't ask about a recently-adopted vegan diet — and they follow that reaction until it becomes clear how disproportionate it is and where it came from, the personality shifts. Not because someone told them to change, but because the underlying program got seen clearly enough that it stopped running automatically.

"Once you kind of internalize and truly realize that this is not working well — they stop being narcissistic."

The Three-Step Process for Shaping Your Own Personality

Step 1: Notice your reaction. In a situation that triggers you, don't immediately act on the impulse. Pay attention to your body. Notice the heartbeat, the tension, the urge to retreat. The manas fires automatically — you can't stop that. What you can do is observe it rather than immediately obey it.

Step 2: Play the tape through to the end. Fears don't hold up well under scrutiny. If your mind says "don't sit at the front," ask: What's the worst that actually happens? You get called on. Maybe you don't know the answer. You feel momentarily stupid. Your grade doesn't drop. You survive. Following the fear to its logical conclusion usually reveals it's been running on an assumption ("I definitely won't know the answer") rather than a fact.

Step 3: Run an intentional experiment. Go into the scary situation with the explicit goal of learning, not winning. Try sitting at the front. Buy more regen in lane. Have the difficult conversation. Notice what your reaction actually is — not what you predicted it would be. Most of the time, you'll discover the fear in your head was significantly worse than the reality.

"What you realize is that our fears are way worse in our mind than usually what reality is."

Over time, as you keep doing this, your default responses literally rewire. The samskar weakens. A new one forms in its place.

A Note on Observation vs. Self-Criticism

This process fails when people confuse observing their reactions with judging them. Self-criticism ("I'm so stupid, why did I do that?") is itself a samskar activating. It's not observation — it's just more reactivity in a different direction, and it breeds anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →.

Real observation is neutral data collection. You're not concluding anything about your worth. You're just noticing: what happened, what did I feel, what did I do, what followed?

A useful calibration: if you feel emotionally activated or numb while doing your analysis, the analysis is probably distorted. Numbness especially trips people up — it feels like calm logic, but it's often suppressed emotion quietly shaping every conclusion. Genuine clarity feels balanced and calm, not flat.

Common Pitfalls

  • Focusing on behavior instead of reaction. Trying to "fix" the problem directly (find a better partner, pick a stronger hero, take a supplement) leaves the underlying samskar intact and the pattern repeats.
  • Treating your personality label as permanent. "I'm just lazy" or "I'm not a morning person" are conclusions, not facts. Treating them as facts makes the biohack industry rich and keeps you stuck.
  • Mistaking self-criticism for self-reflection. Beating yourself up is not the same as studying yourself. One activates shame; the other dissolves it.
  • Expecting transformation to be sudden. Personality changes incrementally. A 3.0, then a 3.2, then a 3.4 — not an overnight 4.0. Expecting dramatic change and not seeing it is its own trap.
  • Trying to control the initial emotional response. You can't stop the manas from firing. You can't decide not to feel anxious walking into the front row. What you can influence is what you do after that impulse fires.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick one recurring avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → behavior. Something small — where you sit, whether you speak up in a meeting, how you respond to a notification. Notice it the next time it comes up without acting on it immediately.

  2. Play the tape through to the end. When you feel the pull to avoid, write out (or mentally trace) the actual worst-case scenario step by step. Follow it all the way. Most fear chains end at something survivable — "I'll feel embarrassed" — not catastrophic.

  3. Run a single intentional experiment. Choose one situation where you'd normally retreat. Go in with the explicit goal of observation, not success. Sit at the front. Say the thing. Before you go in, decide: I'm here to see what happens, not to win. Afterward, notice your actual reaction versus your predicted one.

  4. After any significant emotional event, give yourself 10–15 minutes of quiet. Not distraction — just sitting with whatever you feel. You don't need to analyze it yet. Just let it be present instead of suppressing it. This is the basic practice of digesting experience rather than letting it go dormant as a samskar.

  5. Check your analysis temperature. Before drawing conclusions about yourself or a situation, ask: am I calm right now, or am I activated or numb? If either of the latter, delay the analysis. Come back when the emotional charge has settled.

  6. Ask "where does this reaction come from?" rather than "what's wrong with me?" When you notice a strong automatic reaction, trace it back. When did you first learn to respond this way? What experience taught you that this situation was dangerous? This is the replay-analysis version of real life.
