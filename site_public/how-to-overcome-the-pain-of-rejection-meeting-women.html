Summary

TL;DR — You can't eliminate the fear of rejection, and trying to is what keeps you stuck. The real move is shifting your emotional landscape so that fear is one small feeling among many, and dropping the heavy expectations that make every interaction feel like a high-stakes test.

A lot of people approach social anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → the same way: how do I get rid of this fear before I act? The trap is that the question itself has no good answer. Fear and embarrassment are not bugs you can patch out. But that doesn't mean you're helpless — it means you've been focused on the wrong problem entirely.

Why You Can't (and Shouldn't) Try to Kill the Fear

The goal of eliminating panic before you approach someone is a dead end. Fear of embarrassment is a normal human signal; it doesn't switch off on command. Waiting until it disappears means waiting forever.

What actually changes over time isn't that the fear goes away — it's that other emotions show up alongside it. Excitement. Curiosity. Warmth. When fear is the only thing you're feeling in a social moment, it fills the whole frame and causes paralysis. When it becomes one of many things you're feeling, it loses its power to freeze you.

"If all there is is fear and embarrassment, that's the only emotion you're feeling. But if you're also feeling excitement and confidenceSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → and all these other things, panic and fear is just one of many things — it's not the overwhelming thing that causes the paralysis."

So the question isn't how do I delete the fear? It's how do I build up the other emotions so fear stops dominating?

The Expectation Problem

A big part of what makes the fear so intense is expectation. When you walk up to introduce yourself to someone, if you have a heavy outcome attached to that moment — she has to like me, this has to go somewhere, I need to not screw this up — then of course it feels terrifying. The stakes you've placed on the interaction are enormous.

There are a thousand reasons to introduce yourself to someone that have nothing to do with dating. You could meet a great friend. You could get a good recipe. You could have a twenty-minute conversation that brightens both your days and never goes further. When you approach a person carrying the full weight of romantic hope, every stumble feels catastrophic. When you approach with genuine curiosity about another human being, stumbling is just... part of talking to people.

The fix isn't a clever script or a psychological trick. It's letting go of the expectation that something specific has to come out of the interaction.

Meeting People, Not Targets

One subtle shift that helps: stop filtering your social world to only the people you're attracted to. If you only practice talking to people you want to date, every conversation carries maximum pressure. You're always performing. You're never just being.

Try meeting people across the board — not as a strategy to expand your dating pool, but because human beings are genuinely interesting and each one has something worth knowing. Low-stakes conversations with people you're not trying to impress are where you actually build social fluency. The ease and warmth you develop there will carry over naturally into the moments that feel higher-stakes.

"Just go out and meet people. Do some low-stakes flirting — learn to appreciate human beings for what they are, and the love will come."

This also reframes what "success" looks like. A conversation that doesn't lead anywhere romantic isn't a failure. It's a win in itself, because you showed up, you connected, you practiced being present.

It's Not Always About You

Another thing anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → does is make everything about you. Someone's unavailable — what's wrong with me? Someone seems uninterested — I must have done something wrong. But people's responses are filtered through everything going on in their own lives, preferences, moods, and histories. Most of the time, when an interaction doesn't go the way you hoped, it has far more to do with the other person's circumstances than with your worth.

This isn't just a consolation prize — it's accurate. The person who doesn't want to play frisbee might just find frisbees physically confusing. That's not a verdict on you. Holding that perspective loosens the grip that each individual outcome has on your sense of self.

The trajectory matters more than any single moment. Each interaction — awkward, smooth, forgettable, or funny — builds the kind of comfort that no amount of mental preparation can replicate.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting to feel ready. Readiness in the sense of "no more fear" never arrives. Acting in the presence of fear is the only path through it.
  • Treating every interaction as a referendum on your worth. One conversation, one rejection, one awkward moment tells you almost nothing meaningful about you as a person.
  • Only approaching people you're attracted to. This keeps the pressure artificially high and prevents you from building the relaxed social habits that make connection natural.
  • Collecting strategies instead of getting reps. Clever mental frameworks and scripts feel productive but don't substitute for actual experience. The discomfort of showing up is the practice.
  • Interpreting someone else's response as entirely about you. People are complex, busy, and preoccupied with their own stuff. A lukewarm response is usually about their world, not your inadequacy.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Start having conversations with no outcome in mind. This week, introduce yourself to at least one person you have zero romantic interest in — a neighbor, a coworker, someone in line. Practice curiosity without stakes.

  2. Notice all the emotions present, not just the fear. Before or during a social situation, do a quick internal scan: is there any excitement? Any interest? Any amusement? Naming those feelings helps them take up more space alongside the fear.

  3. Drop the script. Before an interaction, instead of rehearsing what to say, remind yourself of one genuinely curious question you have about this person as a human being. Lead with that instead.

  4. Try low-stakes flirting or playful banter with people across the board. Not as a manipulation tactic, but as a way of practicing warmth and lightness in everyday interactions. The goal is to make playful connection feel normal and not loaded.

  5. After an interaction that didn't go how you wanted, write down one alternative explanation that has nothing to do with your worth.
