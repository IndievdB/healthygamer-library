Summary

TL;DR — Therapy language is increasingly being used as a weapon to manipulate, exclude, and extract sympathy. Knowing the pattern protects you.

A disturbing social pattern is spreading: people combine therapeutic vocabulary ("I need to keep myself safe," "I hope you heal") with classic manipulation tactics to paint themselves as victims while placing all blame on you. This isn't random rudeness — it maps onto a documented psychological framework called DARVO, and it correlates strongly with dark triad personality traits. Understanding the mechanics lets you recognize it early and respond without getting outgunned.

What DARVO Actually Looks Like

DARVO stands for Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender. It's a three-step pattern:

  1. Deny — the person refuses to acknowledge that they did anything wrong.
  2. Attack — they go on offense, blaming you for trying to hold them accountable.
  3. Reverse victim and offender — instead of owning their behavior, they reframe the situation so that they are the one who has been harmed, and you are the aggressor.

This isn't a new invention. It's a tactic people have used for centuries. What is new is that therapeutic language gives it a modern, credible-sounding costume.

Consider this DM that was shared publicly online:

"Until you get your [stuff] together, I unfortunately will be cutting ties with you just to keep myself safe. I hope you heal and stop trying to be someone you aren't."

In a dozen sentences, that message hits several textbook DARVO items: attributing complaints to a vague, unnamed group ("some of my friends"), universalizing the condemnation ("nobody likes you," "nobody wants to support you"), and framing exclusion as a protective, even compassionate act. The sender positions themselves as reluctant, virtuous, and wounded — all while delivering a brutal social punishment.

Why Victimhood Became a Power Move

Something shifted culturally around the Me Too era. Society — rightly — started believing victims more. People who had been ignored for years finally got heard. That's genuinely good.

But any system that rewards a particular signal will eventually attract people who fake that signal. This happens throughout nature: some species mimic the appearance of more dangerous ones to gain protection. The same logic applies here.

Being a victim enables what researchers call asymmetric resource extraction: you receive resources — material ones like money, and social ones like compassion, sympathy, and protection — without having to give anything back. Once society reliably delivers those resources to victims, people with certain personality traits will learn to perform victimhood strategically.

The Dark Triad Connection

Research shows a meaningful correlation between dark triad personality traits and this kind of signaling. The dark triad consists of:

  • Narcissism — extreme self-centeredness
  • Psychopathy / Sociopathy — lack of empathy for others
  • Machiavellianism — willingness to use any means necessary to advance one's own interests

Here's what makes the pattern distinctive depending on which traits dominate:

  • Pure psychopaths will play the victim but won't virtue signal. They lack the empathy required to convincingly perform moral goodness.
  • Pure narcissists will virtue signal but won't play the victim. Being victimized implies weakness, and a narcissist doesn't see themselves as someone who gets taken advantage of.
  • Full dark triad individuals — narcissistic, psychopathic, and Machiavellian — will do both. They virtue signal to build credibility, and then leverage that credibility when they claim to be the victim.

This combination is what makes them so hard to counter. When someone has spent years building a reputation as a kind, moral person, and then claims you hurt them, the people around you are far more likely to believe their victim signal than yours.

"Portraying oneself as a moral person can provide supporting evidence for the credibility and legitimacy of one's victim signal."

Surveys suggest 24–34% of people have consciously faked being hurt to get something they wanted, and around 80% of people have witnessed this behavior. There's likely another 25–35% doing it without full conscious awareness. This is not rare.

How to Recognize It in Real Time

The clearest signal is the combination of virtue signaling and victim signaling happening at the same time. Either one alone is common and often innocent. Both together, especially in the context of a conflict where they're the ones in the wrong, is the red flag.

Specific language patterns to watch for:

  • Attributing grievances to unnamed third parties ("my friends think," "everyone agrees") rather than owning the complaint personally
  • Universal condemnations ("nobody likes you," "no one in their right mind")
  • Framing cruelty as self-protection ("I'm cutting you off to keep myself safe")
  • Offering hollow therapeutic wishes ("I hope you heal") after delivering an attack
  • Denying personal responsibility while claiming emotional injury

The DARVO questionnaire items from the 2017 research paper can serve as a practical checklist. If someone's words are hitting multiple items on that list, you're not imagining things.

Common Pitfalls

Trying to out-victim them. It's tempting to respond by explaining why you're actually the one who was wronged. The problem is that dark triad individuals have usually been doing this far longer than you have. They're better at it. Competing on their terrain almost always backfires.

Going public too fast. Immediately broadcasting the conflict to the broader friend group or forwarding evidence to everyone tends to escalate rather than resolve. It also gives the other person more material to work with — they can now play the victim about the fact that you're "spreading things around."

Assuming it's always conscious. A meaningful portion of people who do this aren't fully aware they're doing it. That doesn't make it less harmful to you, but it does mean "this person is an evil manipulator" isn't always the right frame. Sometimes it's just a deeply ingrained pattern.

Trying to hold them accountable directly. Confronting someone with dark triad traits about their behavior almost always triggers more DARVO. The denial, attack, and reversal escalates in response to accountability attempts, not in response to kindness.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Learn the DARVO questionnaire items. Look up the 2017 DARVO paper and read through the specific questionnaire items. When you're in a conflict, use them as a concrete checklist rather than relying on gut feeling alone.

  2. Do a language audit on recent difficult interactions. Think of a relationship where you've felt confused, blamed, or vaguely guilty without knowing why. Go back through messages or recall what was said. How many DARVO items appear? This exercise can cut through the gaslighting and give you an objective anchor.

  3. Stop engaging meaningfully with people who fit this pattern. You don't have to blow up the relationship or announce what you're doing. Just quietly stop loaning them money, stop trying to hold them accountable for things, and reduce the depth of what you share with them. Step back without making it a confrontation.

  4. Build one-on-one relationships with others in your shared social circle. Don't ask people to take sides. Just invest in individual connections. If the person is genuinely working this pattern, they will eventually burn bridges with others in the group. When that happens, those people will need someone to reach out to — and you'll have already built the trust to be that person.

  5. Do a reality check with someone you trust. If you're not sure whether you're being gaslit or whether you're actually in the wrong, ask a neutral person privately. Share the facts, share the messages if relevant, and ask: "Am I crazy here?" An outside perspective from someone with no stake in the outcome is one of the most reliable ways to orient yourself when someone has been systematically distorting your sense of reality.
