Summary

TL;DR — The reason your best mental states feel random isn't because they're random; it's because you haven't identified which conditions produce them. ADHD performance isn't primarily a trait problem — it's a state problem, and states can be cultivated.

Many people with ADHD have experienced at least a handful of days where everything clicks: sharp thinking, easy conversation, confidence, productivity, good mood. Then it vanishes, seemingly without explanation, and they're back to brain fog and frustration. The cruelty of this pattern isn't the bad days — it's knowing the good days are possible and having no reliable way to reproduce them.

This talk reframes that problem. Instead of asking "what's wrong with me?" it asks: what conditions have to exist in your body and environment for your brain to produce that state? And once you understand what those conditions are, you can start engineering them.

The Real Reason You Can't "Just Be Disciplined"

Most people explain performance — their own and others' — through one of two lenses:

  • Traits: You're motivated or lazy, disciplined or undisciplined, smart or not. Traits are fixed, inborn, and deterministic. If you frame your ADHD as a trait problem, you're stuck.
  • Experience: The more you do something, the better you get. But a professional gamer's game knowledge doesn't meaningfully change across 10 consecutive games, and yet their performance varies wildly. A doctor doesn't forget medicine on a bad week. Experience isn't what's fluctuating.

What's actually fluctuating is state — the temporary, alterable condition of your mind and body in a given moment. Having the flu is a state. Being sleep-deprived is a state. Being in flow is a state. States are not fixed. They respond to inputs.

"It's not about traits. It's not about discipline. It's not about intelligence. It's not about talent. It's about states."

If your brain has produced that ideal state even once, it is physically capable of producing it again. The goal isn't to become a different person — it's to recreate the conditions under which your brain already proved it could work.

The Seven Factors That Shape Your State

These are the inputs most people ignore when trying to explain why some days work and most don't.

1. Your thoughts and self-talk The internal narrative running in your head directly shapes your emotional and mental state. Negative thought patterns — especially ones rooted in past experiences — create pressure, kill confidence, and make presence impossible. Emotional processing work is required here; you can't just willpower your way past a belief that you're going to fail.

2. Your internal emotional state Research on people with addictions shows that relapse often follows a subsurface emotion the person wasn't consciously aware of. If you can't read your own emotional state accurately, you can't manage it. Awareness of what you're actually feeling — not what you think you should be feeling — is a foundation for everything else.

3. Diet Food is psychoactive. You already accept that caffeine, alcohol, and marijuana affect your mind. The mechanism is the same for what you eat. A systematic review of dietary interventions for ADHD in children found that elimination diets and fish oil supplementation showed promise for reducing symptoms. Omega-3 (found in fish oil) matters directly for neuronal health. Yogic traditions have understood this for thousands of years — serious meditators don't eat randomly, because they know certain foods make deep mental states inaccessible.

The practical question isn't whether to overhaul your diet overnight. It's: what did you eat in the 24–72 hours before your last great day? Start tracking.

4. Low-grade inflammation A mildly inflamed brain functions worse — less focus, lower motivation, more cognitive drag. Inflammatory food, gut issues, food sensitivities, and constipation all contribute. Think about how you feel the day after eating very poorly. That drag is physiological.

5. Rest quality Sleep duration is not the same as sleep quality. Alcohol helps you fall asleep but reduces REM sleep, which appears important for brain restoration. Regular drinking or smoking can mean you're sleeping eight or nine hours and still waking up in a cognitively compromised state, not knowing why.

6. Gut health Gut bacteria produce neurotransmitter precursors including tryptophan, which your brain converts to serotonin. Stool transplant studies have shown that healthy gut bacteria can improve depression in animal models. Two bacterial strains are consistently associated with high anxiety; two others are associated with low anxiety. What determines which bacteria dominate your gut isn't probiotic pills — it's what you feed the bacteria you already have. Eating processed food feeds bacteria that thrive on it. Eating complex fibers feeds different bacteria. The pill doesn't fix the diet.

7. Environment and permissive influences A permissive influence is something that allows a particular state to emerge — or prevents it. Certain people in your life make you want to work; others make procrastination feel inevitable. The four walls of your room create one kind of mental state; a professional meetup or a walk outside creates another. This isn't motivational fluff — your environment is a direct input to your cognitive state.

"What are the permissive influences for those moments? When you hang out with a particular group of friends, do they make you more interested in working or less?"

The Hyperactive vs. Hypoactive Mind — Why Fatigue Sometimes "Works"

Some people notice they think more clearly when tired, or that a drink or two helps them focus. This is worth understanding rather than dismissing.

Focus can be destroyed by two opposite things: - A hyperactive mind — too many thoughts, overwhelm, everything firing at once - A hypoactive mind — dulled, understimulated, craving adrenaline

If your mind normally runs at 120% and fatigue drops it to 80%, that might actually land you in a functional range. The same logic explains why some people use marijuana for anxiety — it slows a racing mind down to where it's manageable.

But the goal is to achieve that slower, clearer mind through methods that don't cost you the next day. Breathing is one of them. The speed of your thoughts correlates with the speed of your exhalation. Slow, deep breathing (which increases CO2 and forces you to hypoventilate) is physiologically calming. Yogic breathing traditions arrived at this long before neuroscience did.

The hypoactive-mind version of the problem looks different: technology use — especially fast-paced video games and short-form content — takes a dulled baseline and artificially spikes it to normal. When the screen goes off, you crash back to baseline. Over time, the brain downregulates, you need more stimulation to feel okay, and the baseline gets duller. This is part of why ADHD symptoms often worsen with heavy screen use.

Why You Keep Failing Even When You Know What to Do

The problem isn't information. Most people already know they should sleep better, eat better, and exercise. What's missing is the felt connection between those choices and the moments of brilliance.

"They don't realize that the cost is losing out on these moments of brilliance. What you're costing yourself is going into super saiyan mode. We just don't appreciate the cost because it never clicks that these two things are related."

When the cost feels abstract ("poor health long-term"), the behavior won't change. When the cost is visceral ("I missed one of my rare great days because I drank last night"), it starts to matter.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating motivation as a trait. Calling yourself "lazy" or "undisciplined" frames the problem as fixed. It isn't.
  • Trying to use willpower in the moment instead of making decisions the day before. The state you wake up in tomorrow is largely determined by choices you make today and tonight.
  • Relying on supplements instead of dietary change. Probiotics don't override a poor diet. 50,000 colony-forming units is negligible against the trillions of bacteria already in your gut. The bacteria that thrive are the ones you feed.
  • Mistaking a workaround for a solution. If fatigue, alcohol, or a stimulant gets you to a functional state, you've identified what state you need — but not how to get there sustainably. Use the workaround as a diagnostic, not a strategy.
  • Waiting for a complete overhaul. This isn't an all-or-nothing change. Eating an apple before the pizza is easier than skipping the pizza. Smaller shifts compound.
  • Ignoring environment. The people around you and the physical space you're in are not neutral. They're either permissive influences for the state you want or against it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your last great day. The next time you have one of those rare high-functioning days, don't just enjoy it — document it. What did you eat in the 48–72 hours before? How did you sleep? Who did you see? What was your stress level? Look for the pattern.

  2. Track sleep quality, not just duration. If you drink alcohol or use marijuana regularly, experiment with a one- to two-week break and note any changes in cognitive clarity the following day. Quantity of sleep ≠ quality of sleep.

  3. Add before you subtract. Instead of eliminating foods you enjoy, add one anti-inflammatory item first — a handful of vegetables, a portion of oily fish — before your usual meal. Lower the activation energy.

  4. Identify your permissive influences. List three people or environments that reliably put you in a better headspace, and three that reliably don't. Deliberately increase contact with the first group. Restructure or limit exposure to the second.

  5. Try a slow breathing practice for 5 minutes when your mind feels scattered. Focus specifically on lengthening the exhale. This is physiologically different from just "taking a deep breath" — the exhalation length is what slows the mind.

  6. Observe your diet–mood connection for two weeks. You don't need a perfect diet. You need data on your own body. Keep a rough log of what you ate and how your focus felt 24 hours later. You're looking for your personal signal, not following a universal plan.

  7. Change one environmental variable. If you do most of your work in the same space where you consume entertainment, try working somewhere different — a library, a coffee shop, outside — and note whether your mental state shifts. Environment is a faster lever than willpower.
