Summary

TL;DR — Gay conversion therapy (formally called "sexual orientation change efforts") is not supported by credible evidence, and multiple studies link it to serious psychological harm, including a dramatic rise in suicidal planning.

This piece walks through what the scientific literature actually shows about sexual orientation change efforts (SOCE): how well they work, what harms they cause, why they probably can't work in the first place, and what the biology of sexual orientation tells us. The goal is to give you enough information to make a genuinely informed decision — whether you're a parent, someone personally wrestling with these questions, or just curious about what the evidence says.

What the Research Actually Shows About Efficacy

The short answer: gay conversion therapy doesn't work for the vast majority of people who undergo it.

One large study of roughly 1,600 members of the Church of Latter-day Saints found that only 3.1% of participants reported any reduction in homosexual thoughts, feelings, or behaviors. That means for about 97 out of 100 people, it produced no meaningful change in sexual orientation.

The studies that do claim to show benefits are generally weak by scientific standards:

  • Small sample sizes
  • Retrospective surveys rather than prospective measurements
  • Recruitment from populations already committed to the outcome (e.g., recruiting from ex-gay ministries to study whether gay conversion therapy "works" introduces the same bias as surveying only meditation devotees about whether meditation is helpful)
  • No randomized controlled trials with appropriate control groups

No solid prospective study has been found that reliably supports positive outcomes from SOCE.

The Harm Data Is Far More Consistent

While the efficacy data is sparse and methodologically shaky, the evidence of harm is considerably stronger. Studies examining negative outcomes from SOCE have found associations with:

  • Increased depression and anxiety
  • Lower self-esteem
  • Damaged family and social relationships
  • Increased homonegativity (internalized negative attitudes toward gay people, including toward oneself)
  • Elevated suicidality — and more specifically, a roughly 75% increase in suicidal planning

That last point matters clinically. Passive thoughts like not wanting to wake up are qualitatively different from forming an actual plan. Suicidal planning is one of the strongest known risk factors for a completed suicide attempt. The fact that SOCE is associated with increased planning — not just ideation — is a serious red flag.

Any benefits people do describe from these programs tend to be social: a sense of community, mentorship, platonic same-sex bonding at retreats. What people don't report are medical-grade improvements — reductions in depression or anxiety, better self-esteem, or lower suicide risk. The benefits that do appear have nothing specifically to do with changing sexual orientation.

Why the Research Looks the Way It Does

Several factors help explain both why we don't see efficacy and why we keep seeing harm.

Lack of standardization. "Gay conversion therapy" isn't one thing. Practitioners include licensed mental health clinicians, pastors, and laypersons. Methods range from cognitive behavioral techniques and emotional regulation exercises to Bible study and weekend retreats. When asked what they actually do, some practitioners describe their work simply as helping clients "develop a clearer relationship with Christ." Without any standardized protocol, you cannot study the intervention reliably or reproduce results.

No baseline pathology to treat. This is the more fundamental problem. Medical treatments work by addressing something that's broken. A kidney transplant works because there's a failing kidney to replace. If there's no pathology to begin with, no treatment will fix it — because there's nothing to fix. Both the absence of strong efficacy data and the presence of harm data make sense if homosexuality is not a disorder.

This isn't just a modern progressive position. Freud himself did not consider homosexuality an illness, describing gay people as "normal variants of the population." Psychoanalytic theories from figures like Rado in the 1940s — which framed homosexuality as stemming from a phobia of heterosexual attraction — have since been largely discredited and aren't taken seriously in contemporary clinical settings.

What Biology Tells Us About Sexual Orientation

Stepping back to the level of evolutionary biology adds important context to why SOCE is unlikely to work.

Homosexual behavior has been documented in more than 1,500 animal species. What's striking is the pattern: the more primitive the organism, the more common same-sex behavior tends to be. Sea urchins and starfish, for instance, which lack clear sexual dimorphism (visible physical differences between males and females), engage in same-sex mating frequently — because they simply can't reliably distinguish between males and females of their species.

Sexual dimorphism itself emerged later in the evolutionary timeline. The further back you go, the less distinct the sexes look, and the more same-sex behavior you find. Some species have even developed signals to manage this — male common toads, for example, produce a specific release call to communicate to other mounting males that they are not female. Same-sex behavior is not evolutionary noise. It appears to be a persistent feature woven into the biology of sexually reproducing animals.

There are competing hypotheses about why homosexuality persists in humans specifically. The kin selection hypothesis (sometimes called the "gay uncle hypothesis") suggests that a homosexual relative who doesn't reproduce may still improve the survival odds of siblings' children by contributing care and resources to the broader family unit. The evolutionary math can work out even without direct reproduction.

But the deeper biological point is simpler: if same-sex behavior is present across 1,500+ species and appears to increase in frequency the further back you go in evolutionary time, it becomes very difficult to frame it as a defect unique to humans that medical intervention should correct.

Common Pitfalls

Treating weak positive studies as equivalent to strong negative ones. Some studies do report benefits from SOCE. But study quality matters enormously. A retrospective survey of people recruited from ex-gay ministries is not equivalent to a well-designed clinical trial. When weighing evidence, methodology counts.

Conflating religious or moral beliefs with medical claims. Whether homosexuality is a sin is a theological question. Whether gay conversion therapy is an effective and safe medical intervention is an empirical one. These are different questions answered by different kinds of authority. The medical evidence doesn't require you to abandon your religious convictions — it just tells you what a clinical intervention does and doesn't do to a person's health.

Assuming harm only comes from extreme or coercive versions. The studies finding elevated depression, anxiety, and suicidal planning are not limited to the most sensational forms of conversion therapy. The harm appears in the broader category of sexual orientation change efforts even when delivered by credentialed professionals using established psychological techniques.

Expecting community benefits to translate into therapeutic ones. Some people do report feeling less alone after joining SOCE-affiliated groups. That social support is real. But social support from a community of shared experience is not the same as a treatment working. You can find community without undergoing an intervention that the data links to harm.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you are a parent concerned about your child's sexual orientation, read at least one review-level summary of SOCE research before making any decisions about treatment — particularly the data on suicidality and suicidal planning. Your child's safety is a concrete, measurable thing the evidence speaks to directly.

  2. If you are personally struggling with feelings about your sexual orientation — especially if religion is part of that struggle — seek out a licensed therapist who will work with your values without pushing a predetermined outcome. A good therapist helps you reach your own conclusions, not theirs.

  3. Practice separating empirical questions from moral ones. Ask yourself: "Is this a question about what is true in the world, or a question about what is right?" Medical evidence answers the first kind. It doesn't answer the second. Being clear about which question you're actually asking helps you evaluate information more honestly.

  4. If you are evaluating any therapeutic intervention — for any issue, not just this one — ask two questions before anything else: (a) What does the best available evidence say about whether it works? and (b) What does the evidence say about harm? Efficacy without safety data is not enough to justify a treatment.

  5. Learn the difference between study types. Randomized controlled trials, prospective studies, and retrospective surveys are not equally reliable. When someone cites a study to you, a useful follow-up question is: "How were participants recruited, and what was the comparison group?" These two details alone will often tell you how much weight to give the findings.
