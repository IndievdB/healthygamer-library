Summary

TL;DR — The real story behind "Collargate" isn't about one streamer and his dog. It's a case study in how broken our collective thinking has become — and why that brokenness is genuinely dangerous.

A streamer clip goes viral. Tens of thousands of people form strong opinions within hours. People who disagree talk past each other for weeks without actually exchanging a single real thought. This is Collargate, but it's also Gaza, and gender politics, and every political debate you've ever watched go nowhere. The same psychological machinery is running underneath all of it. Understanding that machinery is what might actually help.

Why Our Brains Jump to Conclusions from a 37-Second Clip

Human brains are wired to detect change. A 1938 paper in the Journal of Physiology showed that our sensory systems are far more sensitive to shifts — from dark to light, from loud to quiet — than to steady states. The same principle governs our social attention: when someone known for progressive, pro-underdog politics is accused of hurting a small dog, that delta is enormous. The brain lights up. It pays attention.

The problem is what happens next. When your emotions activate, your brain produces thoughts — and you experience those thoughts as true. It doesn't feel like a guess. It feels like a conclusion. What actually determines which conclusion you reach isn't careful reasoning; it's the emotional framing you absorbed before you even started thinking. Memes shape belief. Tone of voice shapes belief. Whether someone makes you laugh — like Johnny Depp on the witness stand — shapes belief. The emotional wrapper around a piece of information can move you toward believing it or away from it, and there is no neutral zone.

Once a narrative forms, every new piece of evidence gets sorted into it. A friend defending Hassan reads as either "a credible witness who knows him" or "someone he's pressured into covering for him" — and which reading you choose is almost entirely predicted by which narrative you already hold. The evidence isn't driving the conclusion. The conclusion is driving the evidence.

The Failure of Mentalization

Mentalization is the practice of asking: when a person does something, what are the actual psychological and emotional drivers behind that behavior? It sounds simple. We are catastrophically bad at it.

The bridesmaid example makes this vivid: an insecure person sees her old friend has lost weight before the wedding and jumps to "she's trying to make me look bad." She never pauses to ask why else someone might lose weight — health, a breakup, a personal goal, a hundred other reasons that have nothing to do with her. Her mind produces a hypothesis that fits her emotional state, and she treats it as fact.

Collargate is wall-to-wall mentalization failure. When Hassan's friends come out and say "I've spent hundreds of hours with this guy and his dog, this isn't what it looks like," two groups hear two different things: - Believers hear: credible testimony from people with actual information. - Disbelievers hear: he's gotten to his people, they're covering for him.

The same action, the same words, interpreted in opposite directions. Neither group pauses to ask: how does this person arrive at their conclusion, and what might they know that I don't?

A genuine mentalization exercise for Collargate: assume for a moment that Hassan did not use a shock collar. What could he possibly do? Show the collar — it gets called doctored. Have friends vouch for him — they're called pressured. Respond emotionally — proof of guilt. Respond calmly — suspicious detachment. There may genuinely be no action available to him that would satisfy the people who've already decided. That is worth sitting with, regardless of what you believe about the underlying facts.

The Differential Diagnosis You're Not Doing

In medicine, when a patient presents with symptoms, the whole point of training is to resist the first explanation and work through a list of possibilities. Maybe it's COVID. Maybe it's cancer. Maybe it's an allergy. You don't collapse that list until you have real evidence.

On the internet, nobody does this. Depending on your narrative, you skip straight to the answer.

Two people give contradictory accounts of the same event. Online reaction: they're lying, there's a cover-up. But think about the last time you and two roommates were asked who does the most dishes. You'd get three different answers. That's not a conspiracy. That's ordinary human memory and perception being ordinary and flawed.

Mixed messages are often just how imperfect people recall imperfect situations under stress. When someone is a week into a prolonged public attack, their thinking is going to be worse, not better. Making a factual error under that kind of pressure doesn't prove the original lie — it proves that humans think badly under sustained stress.

Displacement and the Mob That Was Already Angry

Here's what's actually strange about Collargate: the reaction is disproportionate to the event. There is animal abuse happening everywhere, constantly. Why this case? Why this intensity?

The chat in this discussion actually answered it in real time: he always escapes responsibility. Exposes his true colors. Double standards. Gaslighting. Deflection. No accountability.

Read that list carefully. Does it describe one streamer, or does it describe a feeling a lot of people carry about the world? About people in power who never face consequences? About hypocrites who say one thing and do another? About systems that treat some people differently from others?

When you have pent-up anger at 15 different things you can't touch, and then someone shows up who fits the description of all of them and also happens to be vulnerable — the mob doesn't deliver proportional justice. It delivers everything that was already loaded.

Displacement is when emotional energy gets redirected from its actual source (which feels untouchable) to a substitute target (which feels available). Projection is attributing your own internal state to someone else. Neither process requires conscious intent. Neither is about the facts of the specific case. Both produce reactions that look and feel righteous from the inside.

This is not a defense of any individual. It's a structural observation: if you can't distinguish between a measured, evidence-based response and a displaced emotional flood, you will routinely deliver the wrong amount of force to the wrong target — and feel justified doing it.

We Have Stopped Talking to Each Other

The Collargate dynamic isn't unusual. It's the default.

Political debates give each side 3 minutes and a 90-second rebuttal. Nobody answers questions; they address their base. Communities on social media become progressively more insular. The algorithm rewards emotional engagement. People go down radicalization rabbit holes — in both directions — and come out more certain and less curious than when they went in.

"The whole point here is that the reality that you are observing is not the same as the reality that the other people are observing. And until you understand the reality that other people are observing, how do you know that your reality is more correct than their reality?"

Dialogue is how we calibrate our picture of reality against someone else's. Without it, both sides are just reinforcing their own model. The person who's been watching Hassan for 40 months and comes away suspicious has information. The person who knows Hassan personally has different information. Neither of them is listening to the other. Neither is asking the question that might actually matter: how did you get there?

A useful test: What would it take for you to change your mind? Ask yourself this about any strong belief you hold about a person or group. If the answer is "essentially nothing," that's the degree to which you've become radicalized — by your own echo chamber, one step at a time.

The Psychological Cost of Being a Creator

There's a structural reason why streamers often develop narcissistic patterns, and it isn't that they started out as bad people.

When 100,000 people hate you, your brain doesn't do the math and conclude that 99% of your audience still likes you. It just perceives 100,000 people who hate you. Human beings are terrible at probabilistic thinking — it's why people play the lottery, it's why the Monty Hall problem trips everyone up. The emotional reality of criticism doesn't scale with percentages.

To survive that environment, creators often develop defense mechanisms: deflect, dismiss, don't let it in. The narcissistic defense exists because if you actually absorbed every piece of hatred directed at you, you wouldn't get out of bed. Is it healthy? No. Is it adaptive? Yes. Does it tend to shade into patterns of gaslighting, avoiding accountability, and distorted self-perception? Absolutely.

That's the profession. The audiences that make creators powerful are also the audiences that make them fragile, and the psychological adaptations that protect them also create the very behaviors that audiences most resent.

Common Pitfalls

  • Thinking you're immune to emotional reasoning. Everyone does this. The belief that "I'm just looking at the evidence" is itself often produced by emotion.
  • Treating mixed accounts as proof of conspiracy. People give contradictory accounts of ordinary events all the time, in good faith, because memory and perception are unreliable.
  • Confusing proportionality with endorsement. Pointing out that a reaction is disproportionate is not the same as saying the person is innocent or that accountability is wrong.
  • Asking "what did they do?" without asking "what are the psychological drivers?" Behavior without context produces judgment. Behavior with context produces understanding.
  • Letting outrage stand in for action. Piling on in a viral moment feels like doing something. It usually isn't, and it often misses the actual problem entirely.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the differential diagnosis. Next time you form a strong opinion about something you encountered online, force yourself to write down at least three alternative explanations for the behavior you observed — including ones that don't fit your current narrative.

  2. Ask "what would change my mind?" For any strong belief you hold about a person, community, or public figure, answer that question honestly. If nothing comes to mind, treat that as a signal — not a sign of righteousness, but of radicalization.

  3. Mentalize before you react. Before responding to something that makes you angry, spend two minutes genuinely asking: what are the psychological or emotional drivers that might explain this person's behavior? Not to excuse it — to understand it.

  4. Track displacement in yourself. The next time you feel unusually angry about something relatively minor, ask whether the anger is really about this or about something you can't get to. The feeling of "this person can't keep getting away with it" is a good diagnostic signal.

  5. Seek out the contrary account. Actively look for the most credible version of the opposing view on something you feel certain about. Not to be fair in the abstract — because your picture of reality is genuinely incomplete without it.

  6. Notice what emotions a piece of content is producing in you before evaluating whether you believe it. Humor, outrage, and validation all bias your assessment of truth. Name the emotion first.
