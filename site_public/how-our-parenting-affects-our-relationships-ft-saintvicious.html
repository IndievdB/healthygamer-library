Summary

TL;DR — The way we were parented shapes our capacity for connection, and the logical frameworks we build to "fix" ourselves can actually keep us stuck if they're rooted in a misplaced sense of deficiency.

Brandon (SaintVicious), a former pro League of Legends player and streamer, came in wanting to talk about focus. The conversation went somewhere much deeper: a childhood shaped by parental absence, a lifelong struggle with empathy and friendship, substance use as escape, and a persistent feeling of being fundamentally deficient as a person. What emerges is a portrait of someone who has done enormous work on himself — and yet keeps hitting the same wall, because the foundation he's building on contains a flaw he hasn't fully examined.

Growing Up Without a Floor

Brandon's parents divorced when he was eight or nine. After that, both worked full-time, came home exhausted, and were constantly fighting legal battles over custody and finances. There were no birds-and-the-bees talks, no "life conversations," no one asking what he wanted to do after high school. His summary: "It was just, well, we fed you — go have at it."

This is not a small thing. Children don't just need food and school supplies. They need adults who notice them, who have genuine conversations with them, who demonstrate what caring about another person looks like. When that's absent, kids don't conclude "my parents were overwhelmed." They absorb something more personal: I am not worth noticing.

Brandon's response was a common one — he retreated into video games. World of Warcraft. StarCraft. The logic was elegant even if unconscious: in games, your presence is registered. Miss a raid pull, and people care. Disappear from school, and no one calls. Online, he had a place where his value was legible and his absence was felt.

What an Addictive Personality Is Actually About

Brandon describes himself as having a "very addictive personality" — when he gets into something, it's all he can think about. But when pushed to describe what happens when he doesn't have something to consume his attention, the answer is telling: existential dread, rumination about the future, replaying past interactions. His brain, if not numbed, would "just explode."

The substances — years of heavy drinking, then heavy marijuana use — were doing a job. They were turning off an unbearable mental noise. The problem is that marijuana in particular creates a paradox: it relieves anxiety in the short term while building a larger anxiety beneath the surface. Eventually the thing you're using to escape becomes the thing trapping you.

What actually helped him stop wasn't willpower. It was a gradual, deliberate shift in how he understood time. Reading philosophy, he landed on the insight that the past and future don't actually exist — only the present does. Most of his anxiety was about one of those two non-existent places. Shifting his attention to what was literally in front of him, again and again, loosened the grip.

Three Things That Are Not the Same Thing

One of the more useful conceptual moments in the conversation: the yogic model distinguishes between awareness, identity, and mind.

  • Mind processes the past and future — it's the machinery of thought
  • Identity is longitudinal — your sense of who you are, constructed from history and anticipated future
  • Awareness can only exist in the present — it's the part of you that watches the other two

Brandon had been trying to "chisel away at his identity" through self-reflection and philosophy. The observation here is that the part doing the chiseling is awareness — a distinct faculty from both mind and identity. This is why mindfulness and mantra practices help: they're not just relaxation techniques, they're training awareness to sit in the present rather than being dragged around by mind and identity.

When you're in a flow state — hyper-focused in a game — you are, by definition, sitting in awareness and the present. That's why it feels so clean. And it's why that state becomes harder to access as life expands: more things compete for your mind's attention, pulling you out of the now.

Why Pro Gamers Struggle Socially

Brandon's observation — and one worth taking seriously — is that most pro gamers he's known have some version of the same cluster: difficulty with empathy, missing social cues, trouble processing in-person relationships. His theory is that it might be learned behavior as much as anything innate.

The mechanism: online relationships don't require the same circuitry as in-person ones. You can't see faces or read body language. Games give you clean, legible feedback about your value. Over years of this, the circuits that process human emotional signals get rusty from disuse. Then, when you're in the same house as your teammates in Korea, or trying to date someone, those circuits fail. The gap gets filled with logic — hyperanalyzing every interaction, trying to solve the puzzle of what this person's behavior means — which is a poor substitute for the empathic circuitry it's replacing.

This is also why a little bit of instruction goes a surprisingly long way. Teaching people how to communicate and recognize each other's patterns — even briefly — produces real improvements in team performance and relationships.

The Logic Trap

Brandon has learned, through enormous effort, to logically empathize. He can reason his way to "I understand why my dad felt trapped." He can construct a fair-minded account of almost any situation. This is genuinely impressive progress from where he started.

But there's a catch. When asked how he feels about his dad leaving, he says "well, doesn't everyone feel trapped at some point?" — which sounds like understanding but lands as dismissal. What's actually happening underneath is closer to: everyone deals with this, so he should have dealt with it too. The logical framing is overwriting the emotional response before it can be examined.

"All of your lessons of empathy are actually reshaping some of your instinctive responses — and blinding you to your own experience."

The work of understanding other people's perspectives, done prematurely or too thoroughly, can become a way of skipping past your own legitimate feelings. Forgiveness and emotional processing are not the same thing. You can forgive someone and still carry unprocessed grief about what their actions cost you.

Why He Keeps People at Arm's Length

Brandon wants closer friendships. He can see what they look like in other people's lives. He doesn't have them. He says this is because he's "used to being alone" and doesn't put in the effort — and then immediately tries to explain why (the because reflex).

The deeper structure: he views himself as deficient, overwhelming, and too intense. He believes that letting people see who he actually is would burden them. So he protects them from himself by keeping them at a comfortable distance.

This gets named directly and without softening: that's not generosity, it's control. By deciding in advance that people won't want to handle what's inside you, you're taking away their choice. You're never giving them the chance to actually be a friend, because you've already answered the question on their behalf.

"You can't have a friendship where one person has all the power."

The armoring makes sense historically. Growing up, when Brandon failed out of his senior year, no one asked what was wrong. No one showed up. He learned, repeatedly, that people don't give a damn. If that's your data, you don't go around opening up to people — because the one thing worse than not knowing if people care is finding out they don't.

So the distance is protective. The deficiency narrative is, in a strange way, also protective: if everything is my fault, then I have the power to fix it. The alternative — that you're in this situation not because of your failures but because the adults around you failed you — is harder to hold, because it means the world is less controllable than you'd like.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking logical empathy for emotional processing. Understanding why someone did something hurtful is not the same as grieving what it cost you. You can do the first without doing the second, and the second is what actually moves things.

Blaming yourself for things that aren't your fault. Self-blame feels empowering because it keeps agency in your hands. But if the diagnosis is wrong, the solution can never work. Accepting more responsibility than is actually yours is not humility — it's a dead end.

Using the "because" reflex to escape feelings. Explaining why you feel something is not the same as feeling it. The explanation moves you into the logical mind and out of the experience you need to sit with.

Confusing solitude-as-habit with solitude-as-preference. "I'm used to being alone" is not the same as "I want to be alone." Habits can masquerade as identities.

Protecting people from yourself. This feels like consideration; it functions as control. The person being "protected" never gets to choose.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reach out to one person today. Think of something that happened recently that you wanted to share but didn't. Send the message, make the call, start the conversation.

  2. Practice the "share what you're hiding" meditation. Pick something you're uncomfortable sharing — it doesn't have to be profound, just something your internal voice says they don't want to hear about that. Before you say it, notice what that internal voice feels like in your body. Don't explain it, don't justify it. Just sit with the feeling of deformity or deficiency for a moment. Then share the thing anyway. Watch what happens to the feeling as you do.

  3. Drop the "because" for a week. When you notice yourself explaining why you feel something, stop at the feeling. Write it down if that helps. The point is not to eliminate reasoning but to stop using reasoning as a trapdoor out of your emotional experience.

  4. Audit your self-blame. When you feel deficient or responsible for something going wrong in a relationship, ask: is this actually mine to own? Some of it is. Some of it is inherited — from absent parents, from circumstances that shaped you before you could choose differently. Try to sort which is which, and be honest when you catch yourself claiming fault that belongs elsewhere.

  5. Let someone have it. The next time a friend reaches out or comes closer than you're comfortable with, resist the impulse to manage the distance. Let them see a little more than you usually would. Notice what you're afraid will happen — and notice whether it does.
