Summary

TL;DR — The formulas for success are fine. You are the variable that breaks them.

Most people already know what it takes to get good at something — put in the hours, be consistent, stay motivated. The problem isn't the formula. The problem is a set of internal traps that quietly drain your effort before it can compound into real progress. Understanding these traps and routing around them is the actual skill that separates people who follow through from people who don't.

Why You Keep Failing to Apply Things That Should Work

There's no shortage of good advice. Malcolm Gladwell's 10,000-hour rule is basically correct — that's roughly how long serious training takes to produce expertise. But knowing that doesn't help you put in the hours. You can optimize your sleep, buy a supplement stack, read productivity books, and still end up with nothing to show for a year of effort.

The reason isn't a lack of information. It's that every piece of advice has to pass through a human being — you — before it becomes action. And human beings come loaded with internal patterns that can silently sabotage even the most sensible plan. These aren't character flaws you're stuck with; they're recognizable traps you can learn to spot and sidestep.

The Problem With Wanting to Be "The Best"

This one is counterintuitive because almost every motivational message tells you to aim high and want to be the best. But wanting to be the best is actually rooted in insecurity — and it reliably kills follow-through.

Here's why: the desire to be the best usually activates when you feel like you're behind or have failed. If you've been getting Fs, your brain overcompensates and decides the only acceptable outcome is straight As. That's not ambition — that's shame trying to find a shortcut to relief. And the moment you get a single B, the logic collapses: well, I'm not going to be the best now, so what's the point? You quit not because you failed, but because you didn't achieve an impossible standard you set to cope with a prior failure.

"The crazy thing about being the best is that it actually sabotages your motivation. It actually pushes you to give up."

The fix is simpler than it sounds: focus on learning the material, improving the skill, making progress — not on where you rank relative to some abstract external standard. Letting go of "best" isn't settling. It's removing the trap that was going to make you quit anyway.

Why Your Reasons for Doing Something Matter More Than You Think

Not all motivation is equal. There are three distinct sources, and two of them will burn out on you.

Desires are impulses triggered by your senses — you see someone shredding on guitar, your brain fires, and suddenly you "want" to learn guitar. The problem with desire as a driver is that it's a lose-lose: - If things go well and you satisfy the desire, the motivation disappears (you got what you wanted). - If things are hard and the desire isn't satisfied quickly enough, your brain calculates that the effort isn't worth the reward and cancels the motivation anyway.

Either you succeed and stop caring, or you struggle and give up. Desire can get you started but it cannot sustain you.

Emotion is equally unreliable. Using loneliness to push yourself to socialize, or using anger to fuel training — these feel powerful in the moment, but emotions fluctuate. Your brain has a homeostatic mechanism that actively tones down strong feelings over time. The anger fades. The loneliness dulls. And when the emotion goes, so does the behavior it was driving. Worse, a competing emotion (like social anxiety showing up the moment you try to connect with someone) can hijack the whole thing.

Values are different. A value isn't a feeling or a craving — it's something you've decided matters to you as a person. And the critical scientific distinction is this: when you're operating from a value, you can tolerate the negativity that comes with hard work without abandoning it.

"If you want to get good at something, instead of just getting excited because you saw someone else do it, the first thing you should do is really ask yourself: what about this is important to me?"

When you hit a hard moment — the fingers hurt, the rejection stings, the material is confusing — ask yourself two questions:

  1. What is more important to me right now: moving toward what I care about, or avoiding this discomfort?
  2. Tomorrow, which choice will I feel proud of, and which will I regret?

Those two questions don't manufacture willpower out of nothing. They connect the present moment of difficulty to something you actually care about, which is the only fuel that lasts.

Use Your Beginner Knowledge Immediately

One of the quietest ways people stall out is by waiting until they're "good enough" to do anything with what they've learned. The logic feels sensible — you don't want to embarrass yourself — but it has a serious cost.

When you invest a year learning something but never apply it, all your brain registers is one year of effort with zero return. Add another year of the same, and you're burning through motivation reserves with nothing reinforcing the behavior. Eventually you burn out, start skipping, lose focus, and the whole project collapses.

The fix is to use your infantile, pathetic, beginner knowledge as quickly as humanly possible.

That means: if you're a first-year student, start tutoring people newer than you. If you've been playing guitar for two months, play 30 seconds of something recognizable for a friend and ask them to guess the song. Nobody expects you to be good — you're new, and everyone knows it. But small moments of applying what you've learned open the door for positive reinforcement. Your brain gets a signal that the effort is producing something real, and that signal keeps you going.

"We actually don't open ourselves up to the chance for behavioral reinforcement to kick in... we feel so embarrassed that we don't give ourselves the opportunity for positive reinforcement, and then we wonder why we give up."

You don't need to be 80% of the way there to start. Use 20% of what you know as soon as you have it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "be the best" as a goal instead of a red flag. That thought is almost always a signal that shame is driving the bus, not genuine motivation. Notice it, step back from it, and redirect toward concrete progress instead.

  • Confusing a strong feeling with a durable reason. Excitement, loneliness, frustration — these can spark action but they cannot sustain it. If your only reason to do something is "I felt fired up when I started," you're one bad week away from quitting.

  • Waiting until you're "ready" to get any return on your effort. Perfectionism and embarrassment keep you from applying early knowledge, which keeps your brain from ever getting the positive feedback it needs to stay motivated. Delay is not humility — it's a slow motivation leak.

  • Believing the obstacle is informational. If you've already read the books and know the advice and still aren't doing the thing, the problem is not that you need more information. The problem is internal, and more research won't fix it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Catch the "I want to be the best" thought. When you notice it — before starting something new, after a setback — don't act on it. Write it down if you need to. Ask yourself: is this about genuine interest, or am I trying to compensate for feeling behind? Then reframe your goal as "get better at this" rather than "beat everyone else."

  2. Write out your values around a goal you're struggling to pursue. Sit with the question: what about this actually matters to me as a person, independent of how I feel today? Write at least three to five sentences. This is not a brainstorm — it's an excavation. Take your time.

  3. Use the two-question check when resistance hits. Next time you're about to skip a session, avoid a social situation, or put off the work, stop and ask: What's more important to me right now? and Which choice will I be proud of tomorrow? Don't rush the answer — take a breath and actually think it through.

  4. Apply your current knowledge within the next two weeks. Whatever you're learning, find the smallest possible way to use it in front of another person. Tutor someone one level below you. Play the 30 seconds you've practiced. Share one thing you've learned with a friend. The quality doesn't matter. The point is to let your brain register that the effort produced something real.

  5. Audit your motivation source. For any goal you're currently struggling with, ask: Am I doing this because of a sensory desire, an emotion, or a genuine value? If it's the first two, you don't have to abandon the goal — but you need to find the value underneath it, or you'll eventually stop.
