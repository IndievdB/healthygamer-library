The Career-Happiness Trap

Almost everyone was raised with the assumption that the right career will make them happy. That assumption is worth examining before you pick a major, a job, or a field.

"Where did we get the idea that happiness comes from a career? That's what screwed our priorities from day one."

Happiness doesn't live inside a job title. What actually tends to produce both fulfillment and success is finding the overlap between what you love, what you're willing to get genuinely good at, and what offers something of value to the world. Those three things don't have to be perfectly aligned on day one — they grow into alignment over years.

The career you want is less a destination to pick at 18 and more something you find by paying attention to what you're actually drawn to, being patient with a slow process, and staying willing to pivot. A degree gives you credentials; it rarely gives you direction.

This doesn't mean waiting passively. It means doing the inner work while continuing to take the next reasonable step forward in your external life.

The Problem of Escalating Impossibility

One of the most practically useful ideas in the session: the longer you wait to start something, the more impossible it becomes — not because the task grows, but because your shame does.

If you needed to study 30 hours for a final and you keep putting it off, eventually you'd need 25 hours in 2 days. The task itself is the same. But the shame of having waited makes it feel unbearable, so you freeze, cram badly, and then beat yourself up for both the waiting and the poor result.

The same pattern plays out with jobs, friendships, exercise — anything meaningful. The solution isn't discipline. It's catching the shame early, doing the smallest possible thing today, and letting a win build on a win.

"Start with the lowest thing you can possibly do and let go of your ego. Because your ego is compensating for your shame — and the shame is still there."

"Full potential" is a version of this trap. The higher your shame, the more heroic your achievement has to be before it feels like enough. So nothing you actually do qualifies, and you stop trying. Drop the concept of potential entirely and replace it with: what can I do today?

Comparing Yourself to Others

The urge to feel superior to others is a symptom, not a character flaw. When you don't have a stable internal sense of your own worth, you derive it from comparison — the same way you'd figure out what a $5 bill is worth by holding it next to a $1 and a $10.

The fix isn't to try harder to stop comparing. It's to ask: why do I need to feel better than others? That question leads back to wherever the shame started — often in a childhood where your value was only acknowledged in relation to someone else's performance.

Social media makes this worse by creating a public, real-time ranking system for human approval. If you notice you care a lot about likes or follower counts, that's useful data about your sense of intrinsic worth, not a judgment about your character.

Making Friends Gets Harder — That's Not Just You

If you find it difficult to connect with people or feel like you run out of things to say, you're not uniquely broken. The average human's friend-making ability has measurably declined because most people now spend more time interacting with screens than with other people.

One counterintuitive fix: stop doing all the conversational heavy lifting. Ask a question, then go quiet. Let the silence sit for 15–30 uncomfortable seconds. The other person will feel the need to fill it, start talking, and when you respond with genuine warmth and interest, they'll feel liked — which relaxes them. Connection follows relaxation, not effort.

Finding your people also takes longer than most people expect. Sometimes you're in the right room but the timing is off. Forcing it rarely works. Showing up consistently and staying open tends to work eventually.

Here's the rough process:

  1. Pick a real job posting at a company you genuinely want to work for — not a dream so abstract you can't touch it.
  2. Read every requirement and ask: what does this actually mean? What would demonstrate this?
  3. Start on the easiest ones today.   4. Google each unfamiliar concept + "PDF." You'll find conference talks, research papers, and frameworks. Read them. Take notes. Synthesize them into 1–2-page appendices you can attach to applications.
  5. Target small and medium companies first. Find indie developers on Steam Early Access. Email 50 of them. Most won't respond — that's fine. You're looking for one internship, not 50 yeses.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting for the "right" career to reveal itself from the outside. Direction comes from doing and observing, not from thinking harder or waiting longer.
  • Treating potential as a compliment. "I'm capable of scoring very high" is a fantasy version of yourself that sets up real you to fail. Start from where you actually are.
  • Conflating planning with starting. Some people mistake elaborate planning for progress. The actual skill gap is usually starting, not following through. Get good at starting and abandoning — it's better than planning and never beginning.
  • Sending generic outreach. Asking someone "how can I help you?" without having done any homework wastes their time and yours. Offer something specific or don't reach out yet.
  • Dissing your last employer in interviews. Reframe negatives as learning about what environment you thrive in.
  • Learning something just to show off on Reddit. That's ego spending your energy budget. Save it for actually doing the work.
  • Using a therapist or partner as a substitute for professional help when the problem is clinical. You can be a supportive partner without being someone's depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → treatment. Love is not a medical degree.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Stop asking "what career will make me happy?" and start asking "what problem do I want to help the world with?" Write down every interest you have — not to pick one, but to look for overlaps.

  2. Find one real job posting for a role you'd actually want. Read every bullet point. For each one you don't understand, Google the exact phrase + "PDF." Read the first result you find.

  3. Start doing it. Post in a subreddit. Write a community guide. Engage somewhere public.

  4. Notice the next time you feel "not good enough" or superior to someone. Don't fight it. Ask: when did I first start to feel like my worth depended on how I compared to others? Write down what comes up.

  5. If you're procrastinating something important, don't ask "why can't I just do it?" Ask instead: "what is the smallest possible first action?" Pack the bag and walk out the door. Sit down with the book in front of you. Get started — then see what happens.

  6. Start an outreach campaign: identify 30–50 small companies in your field of interest. Write one short, specific email offering a concrete skill and asking about internship or junior opportunities. Send it to all of them over the course of a week.

  7. If you're in therapy (or looking for a therapist): tell your therapist explicitly what's working and what isn't. You are 50% of the room. A good therapy relationship is collaborative, not something that happens to you.
