Summary

TL;DR — Impulsivity usually isn't about willpower or bad habits; it's about identity, control, and an unexamined relationship with your own mind. Understanding the roots of a behavior matters far more than trying to suppress the behavior itself.

This is a conversation with Rich Campbell, a streamer and esports host, who came in wanting to talk about impulsive spending and FOMO-driven microtransaction purchases. What unfolds is a much deeper excavation: a childhood in financial instability near Atlantic City, a period of homelessness, a fierce self-made identity, authority issues, and a complicated relationship with control — all of it woven together into a pattern that keeps repeating.

The most important lesson is structural: if you only try to fix the surface behavior, you'll keep failing, because the behavior is the symptom. The root is something about who you believe you are.

The Real Problem Isn't the Spending

Rich frames his issue as impulsive buying — microtransactions, sneakers, binge shopping. But when you follow the thread, the spending turns out to be a mechanism for something else.

Every time Rich makes a purchase, his mind gets a specific kind of relief. It isn't pleasure exactly — it's more like dominance.

"I'm fucking the world back."

Spending money is how his mind asserts that it's winning. And that need to win isn't random; it has an origin story. Growing up with very little, watching his family lose their house in 2008, showing up at NYU and discovering that some people had private jets and kitchen artwork he'd only ever read about — these experiences quietly installed a scoreboard in his mind. The world has screwed Rich. Rich screws back. The score resets temporarily when he wins.

The problem: this works. The scrappiness, the defiance, the drive to outlast adversity — these are genuinely adaptive traits that have taken him far. That's precisely what makes them hard to question.

Being a Slave to Your Own Mind

The central diagnosis here isn't impulsivity in the clinical sense. It's something more specific:

"You are a slave to your mind."

When Rich's mind says jump, Rich says how high. When a FOMO notification appears, his mind says you need those Overwatch skins in case you ever play — and he buys them. When his mind says you can't call your parents today, a list of rationalizations immediately follows to justify it.

The key observation is about the order of operations: the feeling comes first, and the reasoning comes after. The "I can't" arrives before the four reasons why. His intellect isn't steering; it's being deputized by his emotions to construct post-hoc justifications.

This is why authority issues, impulsive spending, avoidance of his parents, and his love of chaos all share the same root. They're all moments where his mind is in the driver's seat and he's along for the ride — even though he experiences himself as a fiercely independent, self-directed person.

Thriving on Chaos as an Identity Problem

Rich is genuinely exceptional under pressure. When an earpiece failed mid-show in front of a sold-out venue, he felt no stress — he felt alive. He seeks out roles where everything could explode if he doesn't execute perfectly, because those are the moments he feels most like himself.

"Those are the moments I live for. If I don't have them, I'm so bored — what's the fucking point?"

This is admirable and real. But it creates a trap: if your sense of self depends on shining in moments of chaos, you become dependent on chaos to feel like yourself.

When there's no crisis, his mind manufactures one — through spending, through gambling, through burning down a stable job right when it's going well. Like patients who keep entering abusive relationships because survival is the only mode of being they trust, Rich keeps recreating the same conditions because those are the only conditions in which he knows he exists.

The insight here isn't that chaos-thriving is bad. It's that carrying your identity externally — in performance, in crisis, in proving the world wrong — means your sense of self is always one disaster away from collapse. You can thrive in chaos and know who you are when things are quiet. Those don't have to be mutually exclusive. But to discover that, you have to stop recreating the same scenario long enough to find out what's underneath.

The Control Paradox

Rich believes that when he's not in control, outcomes suffer. He has real evidence for this — projects where he called the shots did well; situations where he didn't, often didn't.

But there's a flaw in the data collection: our minds take credit for successes and assign blame for failures elsewhere. We never have data on the road not taken.

More importantly, when Rich does surrender control — with his OTK colleagues, people he deeply trusts — things go well. He holds this up as an exception. But look closer: he chose to trust those people. He chose to build a company structured around his strengths. That's still him being in control.

"You're not actually surrendering anything. It's still your choice."

The real issue is what happens internally when control feels out of reach. That's where the sickening feeling comes from — the pit in the stomach — and the response to that feeling is always the same: buy more, do more, take more control, chase that sense of winning until the feeling goes away temporarily.

The Family Thread: Shame, Pride, and a Closed Circle

Rich doesn't call his parents as often as he wants to. When asked to call them today, his immediate inner response was I can't — before he could explain why.

His parents are fiercely independent, won't accept financial help, and work a flea market every weekend. Rich grew up lying to them about his struggles because he didn't want them to feel they'd failed him.

"I didn't want them to know. I didn't want them to feel like they failed me."

Here's what that reveals: Rich has almost no accessible experience of shame. He reframes his homelessness as a chapter he's proud of, not something to feel diminished by. He grew up in a family that converted shame into pride — heads every flip, even though the coin has two sides.

Pride and shame are two sides of the same coin. The conspicuous absence of shame in someone who has faced genuine hardship usually means the shame is still there, just armored over. And that armor — the need to win, to prove, to not let anyone see the struggle — quietly shapes everything.

The people inside Rich's inner circle (Asmon, Tips, his old roommate) get to see the real him. His parents, paradoxically, are kept outside it — ostensibly to protect them, but also because letting them see him fully would require sitting with a kind of tenderness and vulnerability that doesn't fit the identity.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to fix the behavior directly. If you're impulsive, the temptation is to impose rules, spending limits, or willpower. These work temporarily but don't address why the impulse exists or what it's doing for you.

  • Confusing insight with change. Rich is unusually self-aware — he can describe his own patterns in real time. But he also admits he often understands a problem clearly and still doesn't try to fix it, sometimes because he believes the chaos makes him more interesting. Understanding is not the same as changing.

  • Treating your origin story as your identity. The scrappy kid who survived homelessness is a real and meaningful part of who Rich is. But making it the core of your identity means you need to keep surviving adversity to feel like yourself — which means unconsciously seeking adversity out.

  • Mistaking rationalization for reasoning. When your gut says "I can't" and your brain immediately produces four reasons why, that's not logic. That's your ego handing a brief to your intellect. Catching the order of operations — feeling first, reasons second — is the entry point to actually having a choice.

  • Thinking surrender of control is weakness. Choosing to trust someone is still an act of agency. You're not giving up control; you're exercising it differently.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the order of operations. The next time you feel an impulse — to buy something, avoid a call, chase a distraction — pause and ask: did the feeling come first, or did the reasoning? You're not trying to stop the impulse yet. Just observe whether the "I can't" or "I need this" arrived before the justification.

  2. Make the one call you've been putting off. Not because you've figured out why you've been avoiding it. Just to practice being the one in control of your mind, rather than the other way around. Notice the inner resistance without obeying it.

  3. When someone resists your help, ask instead of insisting. Specifically: if a parent or someone you love deflects your support, try asking "Can you help me understand why you need to handle this alone?" — not to argue, but to open a real conversation about what family means to each of you.

  4. Start a simple daily meditation practice — 3 minutes, twice a day. The technique described: hover a finger over the point between your eyebrows, close your eyes, and try to concentrate your attention on that spot. Each time your mind wanders and you bring it back, you're doing a mental push-up. Lots of wandering means lots of push-ups. Dawn and dusk are good times.

  5. Ask yourself: who are you when there's no crisis? Not to answer it fully, but to sit with the question. If your identity is entirely constructed around performing under pressure, try identifying one or two things that are true about you independent of any external situation — things no outcome can take away.

  6. Track the emotional payoff of impulsive decisions. Before and after a purchase or an impulsive move, briefly note how you feel. Are you buying because you want the thing, or because your mind needs the hit? The goal isn't to stop — it's to see the pattern clearly enough that it stops being automatic.
