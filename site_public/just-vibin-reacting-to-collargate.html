The Machinery Behind Internet Outrage

The Collargate controversy wasn't really about a dog collar. What made it worth examining is a pattern that repeats everywhere — in politics, in workplace conflicts, in marriages falling apart — and understanding that pattern is more useful than reaching a verdict on any particular person.

The first mechanism is sensitivity to change (delta). Human brains are wired to notice novelty and contrast. Research going back to 1938 confirms that the eye (and by extension, the whole perceptual system) is far more sensitive to changes in its environment than to steady states. On the internet, this translates directly: when someone who is perceived as a champion of the underdog appears to harm a vulnerable creature, that contrast is electrifying. The gap between the expected persona and the apparent behavior is what creates the viral energy — not the act itself.

The second mechanism is emotion-driven belief formation. When a clip activates your emotions, whatever thoughts your brain produces in that moment feel true. This isn't a character flaw; it's a consistent feature of how cognition works under arousal. The research on this is extensive. In the context of Collargate: funny, entertaining defendants feel more credible (see: Johnny Depp's demeanor during his trial). Memes depicting a person in a villainous role — even ones you "know are just jokes" — measurably shift your beliefs about that person. There is no neutral consumption.

The third mechanism is online radicalization and the collapse of dialogue. The more time you spend inside any ideological bubble, the more your thinking is shaped by that bubble. Two communities can start at similar points, stop talking to each other, and end up so far apart that they appear to be living in literally different realities. The tragedy is that each side then frames the other's divergent reality as evidence of dishonesty rather than as evidence of a different epistemic environment. This is why even direct conversations between critics and defenders of the same person talk past each other rather than to each other.

What Mentalization Actually Is — And Why We're Bad at It

MentalizationThe ability to understand actions — your own and other people's — in terms of the thoughts, feelings, wishes, and desires driving them.Full definition → is the practice of asking, when a human being does something: what are the psychological drivers actually producing that behavior? This includes understanding your own behavior from the inside.

The failure mode looks like this: someone's BFF loses weight before the wedding. The insecure bride's mind immediately produces the hypothesis "she did this to make me look bad." That hypothesis feels true. The bride never pauses to consider the hundreds of other reasons a person might lose weight, most of which have nothing to do with her. The mind produces a narrative, emotions confirm it, and then the narrative drives behavior — all without any genuine inquiry.

In the Collargate case, mentalizationThe ability to understand actions — your own and other people's — in terms of the thoughts, feelings, wishes, and desires driving them.Full definition → means being willing to sit with a genuinely uncomfortable question: if the accused person is actually innocent, what exactly could they do? Is there any conceivable action they could take that disbelievers would accept as exculpatory? If the answer is "no," that tells you something important — not necessarily about the accused person, but about the state of your own reasoning.

"What would it take for you to change your mind? We do not ask ourselves this question enough."

This question matters because we rationalize incrementally. You don't end up at a radicalized position in one step. You start at zero, rationalize a small thing, rationalize the next small thing, and end up at a hundred without ever experiencing a moment where you consciously decided to go that far. Asking "what would change my mind?" ahead of time is the only real protection against that drift.

Selection bias compounds everything. When a negative narrative forms, positive evidence gets buried and negative clips surface. This doesn't mean the negative clips are false — it means the sample of evidence reaching you is no longer representative. The people inside a situation who have spent years observing it hold information that a 37-second clip cannot convey, and discounting their testimony as coordinated cover-up is the lazy default, not the critical-thinking response.

Displaced Resentment and the Psychology of Mob Anger

There's something else underneath the viral outrage, something harder to name. When you sit with the question "why does this particular incident generate this particular level of intensity?" you start to notice a list of grievances that extends well beyond one streamer and one dog:

  • People escaping accountability for far larger harms
  • Hypocrisy in people with large platforms
  • Double standards in who gets punished for what
  • Gaslighting, deflection, and "whataboutism"
  • A general sense that the systems that are supposed to provide justice… don't

None of these grievances have a clear, actionable target. You can't personally hold a corporation accountable. You can't personally expose every hypocrite. The resentment accumulates with nowhere to go.

And then someone fitting the description shows up in a context where, for once, the crowd can act. The mob doesn't distinguish between the resentment that is legitimately about this person and the resentment that has been building from a hundred other sources. It all lands in the same place.

"We've got one guy and when the mob gets angry and it's angry at fifteen people and one person fitting the description of the other fourteen shows up, they tear him apart."

This is displacement and projectionProjection is the psychological process of taking something you dislike or can't accept in yourself and unconsciously attributing it to someone else instead.Full definition → operating at scale. Calling it out doesn't mean the person at the center is innocent — it means the intensity of the reaction is partially a function of how much pent-up emotional energy has accumulated and how few other outlets exist. An appropriate level of accountability applied to what actually happened is different from a mob dumping years of generalized frustration onto a single available target.

What Emotional Processing Actually Means

The brain is not like cement — it's like clay. Every time you activate an emotional circuit without processing it, you change the architecture of the brain in whatever direction the raw emotion points. Suppression doesn't stop this; it just makes you unaware of it while the rewiring continues underground.

Emotional processing is the act of allowing an emotion to exist and then dissipating it without letting it permanently reshape your neural wiring. The goal is not to feel nothing — it's to move through an emotion and come out the other side essentially the same person you were before it hit.

When emotions are not processed, resentment builds. The person who has spent years accumulating unprocessed emotional responses becomes genuinely different from who they were — not metaphorically, but neurologically. Their triggers are more sensitive (a phenomenon called kindling in neurology), their reactions more extreme, and their baseline further from neutral.
