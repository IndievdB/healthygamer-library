Stress, Vasopressin, and Why "Make Him Work for It" Is Half Right

One clip claimed that men fall in love through vasopressin — a hormone associated with stress — and therefore women should intentionally stress men out to bond them. The hormone part is real; the advice is a misuse of it.

Vasopressin is better known in medicine for regulating kidney water reabsorption. But it does play a genuine role in social behavior and pair bonding, and males of many species are more responsive to it than females. More importantly, vasopressin spikes during distress-based pair bonding — the kind of bond that forms when two people go through something hard together. Think of medical residents logging hundred-hour weeks together, or patients in a rehab unit all going through withdrawalSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition → simultaneously. That shared suffering produces real neurochemical bonding.

So the mechanism is legitimate. The problem is what happens after the crisis ends.

"A distressed pair bonded relationship can change drastically when the distressed period is over."

More than half of these relationships fall apart when normal life resumes, because the bond was built on circumstances rather than compatibility. The ones that survive are the ones where both people successfully transition out of the distress together — which is possible, but it's not something you can engineer by playing games.

More fundamentally: one of the three major factors that builds bonds, according to relationship science, is that another person makes your life less stressful and easier. Deliberately making someone's life worse works against this. The clip confuses "stress can sometimes bond people" with "you should be a source of stress." These are not the same thing.

The Doormat-to-Asshole Pendulum

A deeper pattern explains where advice like "stress him out" comes from. Many people have been genuinely hurt by being too accommodating — doing everything for a partner, never setting limits, letting their own value get eroded. Then, burned by that experience, they swing hard in the opposite direction. The lesson they learn isn't quite right.

"The problem is not that you're really nice to people. The problem is that you're a doormat. That you devalue yourself. That you let other people get away with devaluing you."

The correction isn't to become difficult. It's to understand your own worth, hold it consistently, and give appropriate feedback when someone violates it. There is something in the middle of the doormat–asshole spectrum. The way to find it:

  • A doormat never asks anyone to move toward greater responsibility
  • An asshole doesn't meet people where they are
  • The right approach is to meet people where they are and then ask them to move — hold their hand, but don't stay stuck

In practical terms, this means when someone offers a reason for not taking on responsibility ("I'm too tired," "I don't know how"), that reason becomes a problem to solve, not a justification to absorb their share of the work. "Okay, you're tired — what's your plan for being less tired?" The moment excuses become reasons for inaction rather than obstacles to address, nothing changes.

When they get emotionally reactive to being asked to take responsibility, the critical move is: don't match their emotional energy. Engaging their emotion pulls you into a fight, gives them ammo to dismiss you, and lets them off the hook. Acknowledge the feeling calmly and return to the question: "I'm sorry you feel that way. Which of these six things do you want to start with?"

Pay attention to the language: "You'll never catch me texting a guy without my husband in the group chat." We only catch criminals. The word choice reveals an implicit belief that cross-gender friendship is inherently transgressive. The conclusion that "one of you will always catch feelings" isn't a universal truth about human psychology; it's a description of how this person functions.

The real question isn't whether feelings might arise. It's whether you're emotionally mature enough to handle them when they do. Feelings aren't permanent. Feelings don't require action. If catching feelings automatically means the friendship is over, then yes — you can't have opposite-sex friends. Playing Hard to Get — Who It Actually Works On

The research on playing hard to get is mixed for an important reason: it works on specific attachmentA framework for understanding how early caregiving experiences shape the way people form bonds, relate to others, and navigate the world throughout life.Full definition → styles and not others.

There's real psychological support for the scarcity principle — things that are harder to get are valued more, and effort invested increases perceived value (this is also why hazing works, mechanistically). Studies from the 1970s established these as general psychological principles.

But more recent work shows that playing hard to get can also register as emotional rejection, making you less likable — not more. The determining factor is who you're playing it with:

  • Anxiously attached people will chase harder when you're less available
  • Avoidantly attached people may stay engaged as long as it stays casual, but will leave once emotional depth develops
  • Securely attached people (about 50% of the population) will recognize the game, interpret it as emotional immaturity, and leave

The broader principle: the strategy you use in dating selects for a certain kind of person. If you play games, you attract people who like games, people who lack self-worth, or people who are insecurely attached. That's not random — it's the natural filter your behavior creates. Negging (insulting someone to create attraction) works, but only on people with low self-esteemSelf-esteem is the internal sense of accepting yourself as you are — not a score you earn by comparing favorably against others.Full definition → who don't believe they can do better. The mechanism is sociopathic even when it's effective.

If you want a partner who is emotionally available, confident in themselves, and capable of a secure relationship — that strategy will screen them out.

Why So Much Relationship Advice Is Projection

This is the single most useful meta-lesson from all the clips reviewed:

"Anytime you see a soothsayer giving you 'these signals mean this' — that's not what the signals mean. That shows you how their mind works."

If someone blocks you, it means they're in love with you — according to one clip. What this actually means is: when this person blocks someone, it's because they're in love with them. It reveals nothing about the person who blocked you.

Almost all of the bad advice shared a structural feature: the advisor was generalizing outward from their own experience rather than examining the data. Why You Can't Break Up Even When You Know You Should

This comes down to one thing: you haven't accepted the price.

Every reason you haven't broken up yet is a reason you're staying — having to move, losing social connection, lonelinessA painful emotional state in which you feel disconnected from other people — distinct from simply being physically alone.Full definition →, avoiding guilt, fear of their reaction. Those aren't obstacles to breaking up. They are the relationship. The moment you mentally commit to paying those costs, breaking up becomes possible.

The practical move isn't to try to make the breakup feel better. It's to remove the practical obstacles in advance — find the sublet, plan the logistics, build back the social infrastructure. Do the work first. Then the breakup can actually happen.

This sits inside a broader pattern: as comfort has become more available in modern life, the field of action narrows to only things that don't feel bad. That's a kind of self-imposed paralysis. The path forward through almost any stuck situation — leaving a relationship, forming new ones, building an offline social life — runs through a period of feeling worse before feeling better. The brain doesn't naturally move toward disequilibrium. That's exactly why it has to be a choice.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing a real mechanism with good advice. Vasopressin and distress bonding are real. That doesn't mean deliberately stressing your partner is a good idea. Knowing that scarcity increases perceived value doesn't mean manufacturing scarcity is a healthy foundation. Real mechanisms get misapplied constantly.

Swinging from doormat to asshole. When being too accommodating has hurt you, the instinct is to become the opposite. The actual correction is much more specific: understand your own worth and hold it, without needing to make someone else's life harder to do it.

Treating population averages as individual predictions. A study showing men on average prefer X tells you nothing reliable about a specific man in front of you. Research averages wash out individual variation. The person you end up loving is usually not the person your survey answers would have predicted.

Engaging emotional reactivity during boundary-holding. When a partner gets upset at being asked to take on more responsibility, matching their emotional energy ends the conversation and undermines the point. Staying calm and returning to the practical question is what actually moves things forward.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit one piece of relationship advice you currently believe. Ask: is this describing a universal truth, or is it describing how this particular person (or I) experience the world? What evidence would it take to prove it wrong?

  2. Map your own doormat–asshole range. Think of a current relationship (romantic, friendship, work) where you feel undervalued. Write down: what is your actual responsibility in this dynamic? What is theirs? Where does the 51/49 split land? What's the one specific thing you could ask them to start doing?

  3. Try the "what would make you feel like it?" question. The next time you or your partner isn't in the mood for something — not just sex, but any area of friction — replace the push to just comply with this question. Listen to what comes back without immediately solving it.

  4. When someone gets emotionally reactive, practice the calm return. The next time someone responds to a reasonable request with emotional whining or defensiveness, don't engage the emotion. Acknowledge it briefly ("I hear you, that's hard") and return to the question. Notice what happens.

  5. Identify the price you haven't accepted. If there's a decision you've been avoiding — ending something, starting something, having a hard conversation — write down every reason you haven't done it yet. Those are the prices. Pick the smallest one and decide, explicitly, whether you're willing to pay it.

  6. Decrease one form of social empty calories for two weeks. Pick one platform or behavior (doom-scrolling, parasocial content, Tinder without follow-through) and reduce it with the explicit understanding that you will feel worse before you feel better. The dissatisfaction that follows is the signal working correctly.

  7. If a feeling arises that you'd normally bury, name it specifically. Jealousy of a partner's past? Attraction to a friend? Instead of acting on it or suppressing it, write it down and sit with it for 48 hours. Most feelings, examined and named, lose significant force on their own.
