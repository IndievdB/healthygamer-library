Summary

TL;DR — A wide-ranging conversation between psychiatrist Dr. K and streamer Destiny (Steven) that moves from the sociology of the internet age into deep personal territory: how we perceive the world, how we improve through deliberate practice, and why an emotionally independent person can still be quietly tangled up in everyone else's feelings.

The talk covers several distinct themes: the underappreciated role of phones and the internet in shaping young people's minds; why awareness alone can dissolve mental habits; how deliberate reflection drives performance better than raw practice hours; and a candid exploration of Steven's avoidant tendencies around asking for help, accepting compliments, and setting limits — tracing those patterns back to early formative experiences.

The Internet Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About (But Is Actually Everywhere)

There's a frustration that keeps surfacing in conversations about what's wrong with society: political commentators blame capitalism, others blame the loss of religion, still others point to changing gender dynamics. What gets less airtime — at least in popular culture — is something more mundane and more powerful: smartphones and the internet have fundamentally restructured how human beings relate to each other.

The concept of "warm places" captures the loss well. These are spaces where simply being around other humans allows friendships, collaboration, and relationships to grow organically. Third spaces — gyms, churches, local bars, community centers — are disappearing, and the online spaces replacing them are engineered differently. They are designed to maximize engagement, and maximizing engagement means hijacking the same reward systems that make substances addictive. The result is that relationship with technology is now arguably the most common presenting problem in mental health contexts: gaming, pornography, and social media use all share the same underlying mechanic of engineered compulsion.

One reason this story doesn't spread: it isn't emotionally activating. Content spreads on the internet through emotional arousal. A nuanced argument about phone design and adolescent social development doesn't go viral the way a hot political take does. The discussion is happening — in academic psychiatry conferences, in books like Jonathan Haidt's work — but the people most affected are on the very platforms that make that content hard to distribute.

Why Your Perception Is the Thing That Needs Training

Most personal development focuses on two targets: emotions (therapy, emotional intelligence) and logic (critical thinking, debate). What gets almost no attention is perception itself — the layer of interpretation your mind adds between what happens and what you think happened.

The yogic concept here is klisha (also rendered as "coloring"). The world produces events; your mind immediately coats those events in significance, history, and emotion. Someone doesn't text back in ten minutes — that's the fact. Everything after that is klisha. The shorter the content you're reacting to (a tweet, a text fragment), the more your mind has to fill in, and the more klisha dominates your experience of it.

What's counterintuitive is the remedy. Most people assume you fix a bias by struggling against it — applying willpower until you overcome it. The yogic tradition, and interestingly also a good chunk of clinical psychology, says otherwise:

"The way to rid yourself of biases is not to actually remove them but just to notice them. Noticing and awareness is sufficient for digestion, metabolism, or removal."

This is alien to people who are action-oriented, because it looks like doing nothing. But awareness itself metabolizes the bias. This is why in trauma therapy the work is largely about making the automatic additions of a traumatized mind visible — not fighting them, just seeing them.

Perfect Practice vs. Practice

A recurring theme throughout the conversation is the difference between logging hours and actually improving. The claim that experience automatically produces expertise is wrong. Studies of physicians show that most doctors establish their competency rank within the first two to five years of finishing training and stay relatively static after that. A highly specialized clinic in Germany that produces exceptional surgical outcomes does so partly because even senior surgeons are held to measurable metrics and required to undergo remedial training when they fall short — regardless of seniority.

The mechanism that actually produces improvement is deliberate reflection after the fact. There's a neuroscience concept called intentional binding — the finding that reflecting on an action after completing it increases the probability that you'll repeat it, independent of whether the action was rewarded. This means even sitting down after a bad gym session and noting "that was awful" increases the chance you'll go back. The act of reviewing wires the behavior in.

This maps onto what skilled performers already know intuitively: musicians record themselves and listen back, athletes watch game tape, competitive gamers review replays and make granular adjustments. What's rare is applying this same systematic loop to interpersonal behavior, emotional patterns, or professional decisions. Most people just show up, do the thing, and assume that accumulated experience is teaching them. It often isn't.

"Practice makes perfect is absolutely not true. Perfect practice makes perfect. The way that you practice and the way that you train is far more important than the hours you spend just grinding away."

Separating Results from Decisions

A related trap: using outcomes to evaluate whether a decision was good. Driving drunk and getting home safely teaches your brain that drunk driving is fine. Making a good poker decision and losing anyway teaches your brain to abandon that line. Outcomes and decision quality are genuinely separate, but the brain treats them as the same thing.

In medicine this shows up as over-correction: a patient dies after a reasonable treatment decision, and the physician starts avoiding that treatment even when it's the right call. The formal version of addressing this is the morbidity and mortality conference — a structured review where you separate "what happened" from "was this the right decision given what we knew." The goal is to evaluate the decision process, not the result.

This discipline — divorcing yourself from results when evaluating your reasoning — feels deeply inhuman because we are wired to learn from outcomes. Doing it well requires building a formal practice around it.

The Hidden Architecture of Emotional Independence

The most personal section of the conversation surfaces something that looks paradoxical: a person who appears supremely confident in public debates, who doesn't feel much sting from mass criticism, who has spent years deliberately developing resilience — and yet struggles enormously to say no to a kayaking invitation, to set a limit with a partner, or to simply ask someone for a favor.

The resolution is the difference between trait and state. Insecurity isn't a static number on a character sheet. It's a dormant pattern that arises in specific circumstances and disappears in others. Someone can be genuinely centered and confident in most of life and still have a particular configuration of triggers that activates an older version of themselves.

The origin of this particular pattern traced back through the conversation: a child who learned that needing people's approval was dangerous stopped cultivating the need for it. If you don't feel good when they cheer, you don't feel bad when they don't show up. The independence is real — and genuinely valuable. But the protection is also real, and it has costs.

"If I don't feel good when you say yay, I don't feel bad when you say nay."

What this produces in practice is ghosting as a coping mechanism — not malicious, just functional avoidance. Rather than saying "I'm tired from flying, I'll pass on kayaking," the response is to not respond, let time pass, and let the situation dissolve. It avoids the discomfort of explicitly disappointing someone, but at the cost of honesty and often the relationship itself.

Compliments as a Diagnostic Tool

A specific and teachable pattern emerged around compliments. When you give compliments calibrated to what the other person wants to hear rather than what you authentically appreciate, you train your brain to interpret incoming compliments through the same lens — i.e., as something the other person calculated to please you, rather than a genuine expression of their perception. A compliment becomes a transactional deposit rather than information.

The intervention is straightforward: start complimenting people for what you actually notice and value, not what you think they want to receive. When you give authentically, you receive without the filter of suspicion. As a side effect, the people who respond positively to your authentic observations tend to be more genuinely aligned with you.

"If you change what you give, your brain will automatically change what you receive."

The deeper principle: the boundary between you and another person matters. Your emotional state should not be a function of managing theirs. When someone compliments you and it lands as a burden — something you now owe them — that is a sign the self-other boundary has dissolved in that moment. Restoring that separation isn't coldness; it's the precondition for genuine connection.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking awareness for passivity. The idea that noticing a bias is enough to dissolve it sounds like an excuse not to do anything. It isn't passive — it requires sustained, honest attention. The doing is the noticing.

Applying "emotional independence" as a license for dismissiveness. Separating your feelings from someone else's does not mean their feelings don't matter. The goal is a stable internal ground, not indifference. A doctor who becomes completely numb to patient suffering is less effective, not more. Compassion (caring about their state) and empathy (feeling their state as your own) are different things.

Evaluating decisions by outcomes. This is cognitively normal and practically harmful. If you can only assess your reasoning when things go well, your feedback loop is broken.

Confusing high average confidence with no insecure states. Telling yourself "I'm not an insecure person" and therefore dismissing evidence of insecure patterns is a logical category error. Both can be true simultaneously. The question is always: in what state, in what circumstances?

Using logic to get what the addicted or anxious brain wants. Logic is not a neutral tool. It can be recruited by the part of the brain that wants to avoid discomfort, rationalize a bad choice, or protect a threatened ego. Selective logic feels identical to genuine reasoning from the inside.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. After your next significant action, reflect on it deliberately. Write a note — even a sentence — about what you did and how it went. The content matters less than the act of reviewing. This alone, per the research on intentional binding, increases the probability you'll repeat the behavior.

  2. Separate the decision from the result. The next time something goes badly, ask: "Was this the wrong decision, or did I make a reasonable decision that didn't work out?" Write both answers down. Get in the habit of evaluating the process, not just the outcome.

  3. Notice klisha in real time. The next time you react to a short piece of content — a text, a tweet, a brief comment — pause and list: (a) what actually happened (the fact), and (b) what your mind added (the significance, the implication, the story). You don't have to change anything. Just see the seam between fact and interpretation.

  4. Give one authentic compliment this week. Pick something you genuinely noticed and appreciated about someone — something you value, not something you think they want to hear. Observe how it lands, and how you feel giving it compared to the usual social-lubrication version.

  5. Track the moments your internal state becomes dependent on someone else's reaction. You don't need to change the behavior immediately. Just notice when it happens: "Right now I feel [X] because they seem to feel [Y]." That noticing is the intervention.

  6. Build a simple review loop for a skill you're working on. Record yourself, take notes after sessions, or find a coach. Identify what specifically went wrong, make one adjustment, and repeat. The goal is to stop relying on accumulated time as a proxy for improvement.

  7. When you catch yourself labeling your own thought as "dumb" or "cold" or "stupid," pause. Ask whether that's a logical judgment or an emotional one. You don't have to agree with the thought — just notice whether the self-criticism is dispassionate analysis or something with more charge behind it.
