Understanding how that hijacking happens is the first step to reclaiming it.

This talk covers how self-esteem forms, how childhood dynamics and systemic pressures erode it, and what it actually looks like to rebuild it — not through positive affirmations or achievement, but through a specific kind of inner work. The examples skew toward women's experiences, but the mechanisms apply across genders: everyone internalizes messages they never chose, and everyone has the option (often unknown to them) to question those messages.

What Self-Esteem Actually Is

The technical definition is deceptively simple: self-esteem is your perceived value and perceived worth. The word "perceived" is doing enormous work. It means self-esteem isn't fixed or objective — it's the story you tell about how much you matter.

That story has two main characters:

  • Who you think you should be — the standard you've absorbed from family, culture, and society

Everything in the gap between those two — the shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition →, disappointment, sadness — is the territory where most people quietly suffer. And when you live in that gap long enough, you stop noticing it's a gap. It starts to feel like the truth.

Three predictable coping strategies emerge from that gap:

  1. Assume the negative story is true. Act in ways that unconsciously confirm it (not noticing when people are friendly, assuming bad intent, withdrawing).
  2. Relentlessly try to disprove it.   3. Make yourself small. Fly under the radar, avoid any situation that might confirm the fear. This protects you from the pain but also from any evidence that could correct the belief.

What all three share: they reinforce the very belief you're trying to escape. It's a self-fulfilling prophecy regardless of the route you take.

How the Story Gets Written in Childhood

The roots of self-esteem reach back further than most people realize. Children have a small set of core developmental needs: to feel connected to caregivers, autonomous in their explorations, safe enough to take risks, and competent when they succeed. When those needs are met consistently, a child builds a quiet internal confidence — a felt sense that their experience matters and that they can handle things.

When those needs aren't met, the story starts going wrong.

Consider a simple scenario: a child's ice cream falls. She's sad. The response from her parents can go two ways. One route: her brother is brought over to acknowledge what happened, her feelings are validated, and there's a small repair. The message received: my experience matters; people adjust their behavior because of how I feel.

The other route — far more common — is some version of "it's not a big deal," "stop crying," or "you should have been watching where you were going." The message received: my feelings are an overreaction; there's something wrong with me for having them. The brother, meanwhile, learns he doesn't need to take responsibility for how he affects others. Two children, one moment, two completely different lessons about whose experience counts.

These aren't dramatic traumas. They're small, repeated, unremarkable interactions. But stacked over years, they become the bedrock of core beliefsDeep, largely unconscious conclusions about yourself — such as "I am unlovable" or "I will always be alone" — that your brain forms by abstracting patterns across many repeated experiences.Full definition → — the operating assumptions about yourself that run in the background of every decision you make.

There's a second wound layered on top of this: the learning wound. When children are labeled rather than encouraged — "you're so smart" instead of "look how hard you worked" — they start to treat their identity as fixed. If you're "the smart one," then anything you can't do immediately becomes a threat to your entire sense of self. So you avoid it. You never get to discover that you can learn things. You never develop a growth mindset not because you're incapable of it, but because no one showed you it was an option.

"The number of times that has been a revelation in a therapy session — that you have the option to be okay with how you're performing right now, that it doesn't have to be associated with self-hate or shame — I can't even count."

How the Outside World Takes Over

Here's the central tension: technically, self-esteem has very little to do with things outside you. But in practice, it ends up being almost entirely determined by them.

In Western cultures especially, self-esteem gets anchored to productivity, appearance, and how others perceive you. This isn't random — it's the water you swim in from birth. The problem isn't just that outside voices are loud; it's that you don't know you have the option not to take them at face value.

For women specifically, there's an additional layer: a persistent double standard that attaches shame to normal life processes. Aging is bad. Weight gain is bad. Body hair is bad. Taking maternity leave is bad. Assertiveness — praised as leadership in men — is read as aggression or being difficult. The double standard isn't subtle: female actors get aged out of the industry while their male counterparts become "silver foxes." Women who try to climb professionally get labeled in ways their male peers never would be.

The insidious part is that these systemic patterns — misogyny, racism, economic gatekeeping — get personalized. If you watch your father be passed over for promotions without anyone naming it as racism, and then you get bullied at school for your race without anyone naming it as racism, the most available conclusion your child-brain draws is: there must be something wrong with me. Systemic failures become personal ones. Self-esteem absorbs damage that was never actually about you.

This is how shame becomes internalized, and it is also how it travels across generations. A parent carrying unprocessed shame from their own childhood passes it forward — not through cruelty but through the ordinary ways that unexamined pain shapes behavior. Breaking that cycle requires awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → first, and awareness requires someone, somewhere, naming what's actually happening.

The Space Between You and What Others Think

One of the most transferable ideas here comes from a real conversation — a cousin who had always felt confident evaluating others' advice versus his daughter who was paralyzed by it. He described hearing people's perspectives and then picking and choosing what applied to him, discarding the rest. His daughter felt overwhelmed when different people pointed in different directions.

The difference: psychological distance. He had a stable sense of self that opinions landed near but didn't fuse with. She had no space between herself and the input — someone's view of what she should do would completely obscure her own. Neither option was a character flaw. The difference was that she had never been shown that distance was even possible, and he had never explained it to her. Instead, he shamed her for being confused. Which only deepened the belief that her own thoughts weren't worth trusting — the very belief that had caused her to overweight everyone else's opinions in the first place.

"What other people think of you, how people outside of you treat you, only matters as much as how what they're doing makes you think of yourself."

This reframe matters because it shifts the locus of work. You can't control what others say. You can — with practice — learn to notice what happens inside you when they say it, and choose not to equate that reaction with truth.

She had built her entire sense of self around being "the smart one" since childhood. After therapy, after naming both the message and the messenger, she rebuilt. She found that she had more to offer than the single identity she'd been permitted — and had been quietly denying herself.

What Recovery Actually Looks Like

Rebuilding self-esteem isn't a mindset shift. It's a daily practice with concrete structure. The framework that tends to work is acceptanceAcceptance is the honest, non-judgmental acknowledgment of where you actually are right now — not where you wish you were, and not where you fear you'll always be.Full definition → plus commitment to action, drawn from ACTAn evidence-based psychotherapy, rooted in cognitive behavioral therapy but distinct from it, that pairs radical acceptance of what can't be changed with active commitment to personal values.Full definition → (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy).

Acceptance doesn't mean agreeing that the negative thing is true. It means acknowledging, without judgment, that you're having a thought or feeling.

The specific reframe: instead of saying "I am a failure," say "I am having the thought that I am a failure."

That one grammatical shift creates distance. "I am a failure" is an identity statement — it's who you are. "I am having the thought that I am a failure" is an observation — it's weather passing through. The failure is no longer you; it's something you're noticing.

Commitment to action is then paired directly with that acceptance. Despite having the thought that you're a failure, you commit to one small act that aligns with your actual values — not a grand gesture, not an attempt to prove the thought wrong, just a small, deliberate step in the direction you care about. Call your mom. Go for a walk. Put the skates back on and watch a tutorial.

The point isn't to feel better before acting. It's to act in spite of the feeling, which — over time — gives you evidence that gradually loosens the grip of the old story.

Psychological flexibility is the broader capacity being built here: the ability to take a hit — criticism, rejection, discrimination, failure — and return to your own center rather than rewriting your identity around the blow. Think of it as being rooted enough to bend without uprooting.

"It is essentially your ability to see your intrinsic value despite, and in spite of, less than ideal circumstances or people that are trying to convince you otherwise."

On Masculinity, Femininity, and the Trap of Rigid Labels

Feminine and masculine are not synonyms for woman and man. They describe energy states that exist in all people — sensitivity, tenderness, emotional attunement on one end; assertiveness, decisiveness, boundary-setting on the other. Every functioning human being needs access to both, in different proportions at different moments.

The cultural segregation of these energies by sex creates a double bind for everyone:

  • Men who are naturally empathetic get trained to suppress it. Some then project that suppression outward — "stop crying" — because they've learned to find their own emotional sensitivity unacceptable.
  • Women who are naturally assertive get trained to edit themselves, which can mean allowing things to happen to them that they don't want, failing to protect their own interests, staying silent when they shouldn't.

The path out isn't to perform masculinity or femininity more confidently. It's to strip the labels entirely and ask: who am I when I'm not performing anything? When you're afraid, what do you actually need? When you're angry, what are you protecting? When you want to reach out, what's driving that?

The person who knows that about themselves can then code-switch with awareness rather than losing themselves in the adjustment. Code-switching is a real survival skill in many contexts — but there's a crucial difference between adjusting your presentation because you understand the situation and have chosen to, versus adjusting it because you believe your real self is unacceptable. The former keeps your self-esteem intact. The latter quietly erodes it.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing acceptance with resignation. Acceptance in this framework means acknowledging your current thoughts and feelings without judgment — not concluding that the negative belief is true. "I don't have a partner" is acceptance. "I don't have a partner because I'm unlovable" is a judgment masquerading as acceptance.

Trying to prove the negative voice wrong. This is the most seductive trap. It feels like agency — you're fighting back! But if you're organizing your behavior around disproving a belief, you're still giving that belief authority over your life. The goal is to render it irrelevant, not to defeat it in argument.

Tying self-esteem to outcomes. There are real consequences to aging in a society that penalizes it. There are real consequences to being assertive as a woman. Acknowledging those consequences is honest. But outcomes and self-worth are not the same thing, and there is no law that says they have to be. That decoupling is the actual work.

Expecting the work to be linear or finished. Self-esteem isn't a destination. The world will test it every day. You'll get knocked back. The practice is returning to center, not never getting knocked.

Mistaking arrogance for self-esteem. High self-esteem isn't thinking you're better than others. It's knowing you have value regardless of how you compare to others. The quarter doesn't become worth more by being next to a dime.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Practice the reframe. Take a belief you hold about yourself — "I'm not likable," "I'm a failure," "I'm not attractive enough" — and add the prefix: I am having the thought that... Say it out loud or write it down. Notice what shifts. Do this consistently whenever the self-critical voice appears.

  2. Find the gap. Write down who you think you are in a specific domain (friendships, work, physical appearance). Write down who you think you should be. Look at what's in between. Name the emotions there — shame, anxiety, disappointment. This gap is where your self-esteem is being spent.

  3. Identify your Velcro. Think of someone whose opinion stings the most — a parent, a boss, a specific type of person. Ask yourself: what does their opinion represent to me? What story am I telling myself about why their view matters? Trace it back to what you were taught to value.

  4. Pair acceptance with one small action. Pick one thing you've been avoiding because you fear you'll fail or be judged. Write the sentence: "I am having the thought that [negative belief]. In spite of that, today I am going to [small, specific action aligned with a value I hold]." Do the action. Keep it small enough that you can actually do it.

  5. Strip the labels experiment. The next time you feel pressure to be more "masculine" or more "feminine," pause and rephrase the question. Instead of "should I be more assertive (masculine)?" ask "do I want to set a boundary here, and why?" Get to know what you actually want separate from what the label tells you you're supposed to want.

  6. Notice code-switching. The next time you adjust your presentation in a social or professional context, check in: are you doing it from awareness of the situation, or from a belief that your real self is unacceptable? You don't need to stop code-switching — you need to stop letting it tell you something is wrong with you.

  7. Start small, build evidence. Choose one area where you've been telling yourself you can't improve. Commit to the most modest possible entry point — not to prove anyone wrong, but to show yourself that learning is possible. A recipe before a meal. Five minutes before an hour. The point is to collect small, concrete evidence that the fixed story isn't the whole story.
