The Shame That Gets Taught in Childhood

Some of the most formative psychological lessons aren't explicitly taught — they're demonstrated repeatedly until the body believes them.

Being placed a year or two early into a Korean elementary school that still used corporal punishment meant being publicly called out, hit in front of peers, and then watched as those same peers joined in. That's not just humiliating. It's a complete social education: other children are not safe. Adults will expose your failures. Trying your hardest and still getting it wrong means you deserve punishment.

What makes this particularly sticky is the compound message. It wasn't just "you got the answer wrong." It was: your teachers tell your parents you're not smart, your parents redirect you toward art because "you're too dumb for a real job," the kids pile on — and through all of it, you never tell anyone what's happening. You just quietly conclude that everything you're doing is wrong.

That conclusion doesn't stay in elementary school. It travels with you.

Why Your Friends "Always Leave"

A pattern that looks like bad luck is often something more structured.

In this case: a best friend moved to Japan and never wrote back despite four years of letters. Another close friend moved out of state. A high school best friend's parents became religious extremists and essentially banned the friendship. Every close relationship ended through circumstances that felt entirely outside anyone's control.

"It seems like life conspires to take people away from you."

When loss is that consistent, the rational mind doesn't conclude "I've been unlucky." It concludes "this is what happens." You adapt to needing only one close friend at a time — not because one is enough, but because you've learned to survive on it.

There's a subtler mechanism too. Once you have that one friend, you stop doing the things that would create more friendships. You feel "full," in a sense — not because your need for connection is actually met, but because the anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → of pursuing more connection outweighs the discomfort of having less. One person who understands you, who lets you keep working while you hang out — that's the minimum viable friendship. And getting used to the bare minimum of what you actually deserve is one of the quietest forms of suffering.

Work as a Nervous System Regulation Tool

Here's the core psychological mechanism, stated plainly:

When you were a child, you could see the moment coming — being called on, knowing you didn't have the answer, knowing what was going to happen next. That specific feeling of dread — the tight stomach, the anticipatory fear — got wired deeply.

Now, any time you step away from work to be with friends or family, a version of that same feeling activates. It's not as intense as it was in first grade, but the quality is identical. And the one thing that reliably makes it go away? Going back to work.

Work is safe for several reasons: - It doesn't abandon you - You can control it (unlike people) - Progress is visible and measurable - It can't leave for Japan or get taken away by religious parents

"Your work will be there for you."

This isn't a character flaw. It's a completely logical adaptation to an environment that repeatedly punished connection and rewarded performance. The problem is that it costs you your actual life — because every time you go be with the people you love, the feeling comes up, you can't be present, and you retreat. So you get neither the peace of full focus on work nor the genuine nourishment of real connection.

The Stick You Picked Up

One of the most striking moments in this conversation is an apparently random memory:

Yoona found a big wooden stick — the same kind used to hit students — and proudly brought it to school, intending to defend herself. Her teacher took it and used it as her preferred punishment tool for the rest of the year.

This isn't a digression. It's a precise metaphor for what happens every time you try to rise up, take control, and tell your life "things are going to be different now."

"Every time you try to take control, it gets turned around."

When you tell yourself I did a good job, I should be proud of this — that's you picking up the stick. And somewhere inside, there's a part of you that has learned, viscerally, that picking up that stick does not end well. The Self-Judgment Loop (And Why Trying to Stop It Doesn't Work)

There's a meta-problem that makes all of this harder to resolve: you judge yourself for judging yourself.

You notice you're too hard on yourself → you decide you shouldn't be → you feel bad that you are → you judge yourself for feeling bad → repeat.

"You can judge yourself for not judging yourself for not judging yourself. It just keeps going on forever."

The exit isn't to try harder to accept yourself. That's just another layer of judgment — "I should be more accepting of myself," which is functionally the same voice saying "you're not doing this right either."

The actual exit is stranger and simpler: accept that you judge yourself. Don't judge the judging. When you notice the cycle, you don't fight it or try to change it. You just... see it. And often, when you genuinely see the absurdity of it, the only honest response is something close to laughter.

There's a reason Zen tradition holds that enlightenment is often followed by laughing — the recognition that all the effort you were putting into fixing yourself was never actually required.

You can tell yourself "it's okay to cry, it's okay to hang out with friends, I should be proud of my work" completely sincerely — and still feel nothing change. Knowing something is fine and feeling it are operated by different systems. The head can say one thing while the stomach says another, and the stomach usually wins behaviorally.

Thinking the answer is "make more friends" or "work less." These are behavioral goals that don't address the underlying feeling. If you try to hang out with people while that anxious dread is still running in the background, you won't actually enjoy it — you'll just white-knuckle through it and run back to work, having confirmed that connection feels bad.

Using self-improvement as another form of self-punishment. Deciding you need to be less of a workaholic, more socially present, less of an imposter — and then feeling terrible when you fall short — is just the old cycle in new clothing.

Dismissing real distress as silly. The fact that something sounds objectively minor (dreams about pizza flyers, fear of showing your feet on stream) does not mean the distress is minor. Hypervigilance and intrusive dreams around a specific fear are worth taking seriously regardless of how absurd the content sounds.
