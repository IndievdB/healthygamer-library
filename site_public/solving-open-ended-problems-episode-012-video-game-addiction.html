Summary

TL;DR — Struggling with open-ended problems like finding a job or a relationship isn't a laziness or intelligence problem. It's a skill gap — and one that can be fixed.

Most people who feel stuck in life assume the problem is their work ethic or their personality. But there's a more accurate explanation: open-ended problem solving is a cognitive skill that develops through repeated practice, and if your life has been structured in a way that never required it, your brain simply hasn't built that muscle. This applies to gamers, but also — perhaps surprisingly — to doctors.

Closed-Ended vs. Open-Ended Problems

A closed-ended problem has a defined start point and a defined end point. Games are almost entirely built from these. Level one unlocks level two, which unlocks level three. You know what winning looks like, you know what tools you have, and you work within a fixed sandbox. Gamers are genuinely excellent at this kind of analytical thinking.

An open-ended problem has no clear finish line. Finding a job — how do you know when you've found the right one? Could you have found one that paid more? Had a better manager? How do you beat that game? You can't, because there's no defined endpoint. That ambiguity is what makes these problems hard for people who've been trained on closed-ended ones.

Why Doctors Struggle With This Too

Here's the counterintuitive case: highly successful, highly intelligent, deeply not lazy people can also be terrible at open-ended problems — because their life path never demanded otherwise.

Consider the trajectory of someone who decides at 15 they want to be a surgeon:

  • A school counselor gives them a checklist: AP classes, SAT prep, good GPA.
  • College applications give them a script: write 500 words on a challenge, apply to these schools.
  • Medical school provides another script: take these courses, volunteer, do research, take the MCAT.
  • Residency tells them where to show up, which unit to work on, and when to be on call.

Every single step of the way, someone hands them the next instruction. They board the train and ride the tracks — high school → college → medical school → residency — and then one day in their early thirties, they get off the train. And that is the first time in their life they have to actually find a job.

The result: many doctors get chronically underpaid in their first positions because they genuinely don't know how to negotiate, don't know what they're worth, and feel embarrassed asking for more. It's not stupidity. It's not laziness. It's a skill they never had to develop.

"It's just a skill. It's a part of your brain that you've never had to use."

How the Skill Actually Develops

The person who doesn't follow a pre-scripted path builds open-ended problem-solving ability gradually, through a widening set of real decisions:

  • At 16, they find a first job — maybe 10 realistic options to navigate.
  • Applying to college means weighing in-state vs. out-of-state, scholarships, proximity to home — maybe 20 variables.
  • Choosing a major requires actual self-reflection about what they want from life.
  • Finding a first job out of college opens up geography, industry, and salary considerations.
  • Deciding whether and when to pursue graduate school adds another layer of complexity.

Each stage, the number of variables grows. The brain is forced to practice the skill of taking an abstract mess and breaking it into workable pieces. By 30, that person looks "motivated" or "driven" to someone on the outside — but what they really have is reps. They've been solving progressively harder open-ended problems for fifteen years.

The gamer, meanwhile, spent those same years in environments where all the problem-structuring was done for them. It's not a moral failure. It's a training gap.

"It's not that they're lazy or not lazy — it's that they have learned how to manage a broader and broader set of abstract problems."

The Core Skill: Chunking Down

The practical move is learning to operationalize an open-ended problem — to consciously break it into its component parts until each piece becomes concrete enough to act on.

Take something like "find a girlfriend." That's completely open-ended as stated. But it can be chunked into:

  • Physical fitness
  • Hygiene and grooming
  • Fashion
  • Social skills (e.g., taking an improv class to get comfortable interacting with people)
  • Living situation

Each of those is now a closed-ended problem with real steps. And once a gamer has a concrete problem with a defined set of tools, they're genuinely good at solving it. The difficulty isn't execution — it's the translation from abstract to concrete.

The same applies to financial independence, career advancement, or any other life goal that feels overwhelming and vague. The goal isn't just to solve the problem once — it's to train the brain to do this kind of translation as a habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition →.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking the symptom for the cause. The most common trap is interpreting paralysis on open-ended problems as evidence of laziness or low intelligence. That story becomes self-reinforcing: you feel bad about yourself, which makes it harder to engage, which confirms the story. The actual problem is a skill gap, and skill gaps are fixable.

Stopping at the first layer of abstraction. If you sit down and try to write out the steps to reach a goal, you'll probably produce six or seven bullet points — and most of them will still be abstract. That's the point where most people stop and conclude they don't know what to do. The real work is pushing each of those bullets down another level until they become concrete actions.

Assuming the successful person is just wired differently. Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick one open-ended goal you've been avoiding — a job search, a health goal, a relationship goal — and write it at the top of a blank page.

  2. Write down every step you think is involved in achieving it. Don't filter; just list everything that comes to mind. You'll probably get 5–8 items.

  3. Look at each item and ask: is this concrete enough to act on today? If the answer is no, it's still abstract. Push it down one more level. Keep breaking each step down until you have something you could do in the next 24–48 hours.

  4. Identify the first single action — not the whole plan, just the first move — and do it. Momentum on open-ended problems comes from small concrete wins, not from figuring out the whole path in advance.

  5. Practice on low-stakes open-ended decisions to build the muscle. Planning a weekend trip with no fixed itinerary, cooking a meal without a recipe, exploring a part of your city with no destination — small exercises in tolerating ambiguity and making decisions with incomplete information.

  6. Notice when you're waiting for someone to give you a script. When you feel stuck and find yourself wishing someone would just tell you what to do, that's the skill gap showing up. Recognize it for what it is, and treat the discomfort as a cue to practice rather than a reason to disengage.
