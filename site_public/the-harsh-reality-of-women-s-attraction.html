Summary

TL;DR — Men and women evaluate male attractiveness using completely different criteria, and the science backs women's preferences up.

A Twitter poll showing a man's 12-week fitness transformation revealed a striking gap: men overwhelmingly preferred the leaner, more muscular "after" photo, while women preferred the "before." The male reaction — assuming women were lying — is itself revealing. Breaking down the evolutionary biology, psychology, and research behind this disagreement shows that the gap is real, predictable, and has nothing to do with dishonesty.

Intramale Competition vs. Female Mate Selection

A lot of male thinking about attractiveness is unconsciously borrowed from a model that doesn't apply to humans.

In species like lions, males fight each other for dominance. The winner gets mating access, and females essentially accept whoever the males have already selected. This is intramale competition: men evaluate men, and women follow. No female input required.

Human mating doesn't work this way. The best data on human pair-bonding points toward serial monogamy — long-term relationships with one person at a time, not dominance hierarchies where the strongest male wins a harem. If you beat up every guy in your school, women aren't lining up at your door. You'd likely come across as unstable and threatening.

The problem is that many men are still implicitly running the lion model. They evaluate male bodies the way they'd evaluate a competitor: who could beat whom? Women are running a different calculation entirely.

What Women Are Actually Selecting For

The evidence on female mate selection consistently points to three criteria:

  1. Safety — Does this person feel physically and emotionally safe to be around?
  2. Kindness — Is this person warm, accepting, non-judgmental?
  3. Financial/material security — Does this person have stable resources?

Run those three filters against an extremely muscular physique and see what happens. Which man seems more likely to be critical of your food choices? More likely to be rigid or judgmental? More likely to be physically dangerous? Which body type reads as "CEO" or "doctor," and which reads as "personal trainer"? None of this is a knock on personal trainers — but the inference women draw from extreme muscularity is about where a man's time and energy is going, and what his priorities reveal about his character.

The women in the Twitter thread weren't being irrational. Their gut reactions — "he gives off warmth and protection" versus "he seems self-absorbed and emotionally unavailable" — were tracking these three criteria from a single image, with no other information available.

What the Research Actually Shows

Three studies are worth understanding here.

Drive for muscularity and relationship outcomes. A study of male weightlifters found that single men and men dissatisfied with their sex lives showed a significantly higher drive for muscularity (DFM) than men in relationships. Longer relationship duration was associated with less dissatisfaction with muscularity and a weaker drive to become more muscular. The more a man is chasing peak physical cut-ness, the shorter and less satisfying his relationships tend to be.

Fitness and marital transitions. A separate study found that men's fitness levels tend to increase after divorce and decrease after marriage or remarriage. This doesn't prove anything on its own — it's correlational — but it forms part of a consistent pattern: the men in stable long-term relationships are not the maximally shredded men.

The face manipulation study. This is the most precise of the three. Researchers digitally altered the same male faces — adjusting muscularity and body fat independently — and asked women to optimize those faces for either a short-term or a long-term relationship. Women consistently preferred higher muscle mass for short-term relationships, but not for long-term ones. When choosing a long-term partner, women dialed the muscularity back.

Why? Prior research links high testosterone and high muscle mass to a larger number of sexual partners, lower likelihood of marriage, and higher likelihood of divorce. The researchers' conclusion: male faces that reflect high androgen-driven traits may be less preferred for long-term relationships because the behavioral traits those features signal are inconsistent with paternal investment and commitment.

"Male faces that reflect high levels of androgen-mediated traits may be less preferred by women in a long-term relationship because of the associated behavioral traits that are inconsistent with paternal investment."

In short: extreme muscularity is a short-term mating signal, not a long-term one. Women's nervous systems appear to know this.

The Mismatch in Plain Terms

Men evaluating male bodies are asking: who would win? It's an intramale competition lens.

Women evaluating male bodies for a long-term partner are asking: who would stay, contribute, and be safe to be vulnerable with? That's a completely different question with a different answer.

Extreme physical development implies high testosterone — which correlates with rigidity, judgment, and less investment in parenting or partnership. A moderately fit man who clearly spends most of his time on career, relationships, and other commitments reads, to female pattern recognition, as a better long-term bet.

"The more manly you get — there's kind of like a bell curve — if you get on the extreme end of physical fitness, that implies a high degree of testosterone, a high degree of judgment, a high degree of rigidity. And women won't actually prefer that."

Common Pitfalls

Assuming women are lying or in denial. The immediate male reaction to the poll — "they must be lying," "this wouldn't actually happen in the real world" — is itself evidence of the mismatch. Women aren't confused about their own preferences. Men are confused about what women's preferences actually are.

Conflating short-term and long-term attraction. The research doesn't say women are never attracted to highly muscular men. They are — for short-term contexts. The distinction is long-term partnership, where the calculus shifts significantly. Treating short-term attraction and long-term mate selection as the same thing causes a lot of confusion.

Misreading the fitness-marriage correlation. The finding that married men tend to be less fit than divorced men doesn't mean "getting fat will help you get married." It likely reflects where people's energy is being directed, not a direct causal mechanism. Don't flip the arrow.

Thinking any single study is a smoking gun. These studies are largely cross-sectional and correlational. The face study is the most experimental and the most direct evidence. Taken together they tell a consistent story, but none of them is definitive on its own.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what you're actually optimizing for. If you're investing heavily in physical appearance, ask yourself honestly: is this for your own health and wellbeing, for intramale status, or because you believe it will attract long-term partners? The answer changes what's actually worth your time.

  2. Test your own lens. Next time you evaluate another man's appearance, notice whether you're running an intramale competition filter ("could he beat me?") rather than asking what a woman seeking a long-term partner would actually want to know about him.

  3. Look at the signals you're sending, not just the signals you intend. Extreme physical development communicates things — about priorities, rigidity, emotional availability — independent of your actual character. If long-term partnership is a goal, consider what the full picture of your life signals, not just one dimension of it.

  4. Read the responses you get more honestly. If you share something — a poll, a photo, an opinion — and the responses don't match your expectation, sit with the data before concluding the other people are wrong or lying. That discomfort is information.

  5. If you're interested in the research, the most useful paper to read is the face composition study on muscularity and short-term vs. long-term preference. It's the most controlled and direct test of the question these Twitter debates are actually about.
