The Relapse Isn't the Problem You Think It Is

Ten days of not watching porn, then a slip. The instinct is to call that failure — especially when you've made your progress a public commitment and other people are watching. But that framing is wrong, and it does real damage.

Think about it like a meditationA practice of focused attention aimed at reaching a state of consciousness outside the thinking mind — where anxiety, desire, hope, attachment, and sadness simply cannot exist.Full definition → session. The goal isn't to have a perfectly still mind; it's to notice when your mind wanders and bring it back. Every time you bring it back, you're doing a mental push-up. The same logic applies here. The goal isn't a perfect, unbroken streak. The goal is to shorten the gap between relapses, reduce how long they last, and reduce how severe they are.

Zooming out to a year or two of someone's chart — even one with relapses — often looks like real progress. The problem is that most people only zoom in to the moment of the slip and declare themselves back to zero.

"If you can go ten days without fapping, you can go ten days again. If you've done it once, you can do it again — that's a statement of fact."

The Superhero Complex and Its Shadow

Almost everyone in this conversation independently describes the same internal oscillation: swinging between feeling like a genius who could do anything if they just got serious, and feeling like absolute worthless scum. What looks like two opposite states is actually one system.

You're Playing a Solo Character in a Party Game

AddictionSubstance use refers to the consumption of a substance — alcohol, opioids, caffeine, cannabis, and others — that triggers a neurological response your brain then adapts to over time.Full definition → recovery has an unavoidable single-player quality — no one else can control your behavior for you. But many people take this further, applying a lone-wolf philosophy to every aspect of their struggle. Accepting help feels like admitting something is broken that should have been fixable alone.

Notice the double standard: you'd readily help a friend who was struggling, and you wouldn't think less of them for needing it. But when it comes to yourself, you expect superhuman self-sufficiency.

There's a useful reframe here. You're not multiplayer. But you're also not literally alone — you're each playing your own single-player game alongside other people in the same dungeon. You can share notes, cheer each other on, and serve as proof of concept to each other. Seeing someone who is genuinely human — not a superhero — stumble and keep going is actually more useful to other people than watching someone perform a flawless victory.

"If Richard wakes up one day as Superman, anything he discovers about his playbook doesn't apply to anyone else — because they're not Superman. Exceptional means everyone else isn't."

The Escalating Impossibility Trap

There's a pattern worth naming: the problem of escalating impossibility. The further behind you fall, the bigger the comeback you feel you need to justify yourself. Then a year passes and it has to be even bigger. Five years pass. Now the gap between where you are and where you feel you'd need to be to hold your head up has become so wide that only a Messiah-level transformation would do.

This is a trap with no exit — because the fantasy keeps expanding to match the shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition →.

The practical way out is to redefine the game. Not evasion tanking (never getting hit), but damage control (accepting you'll take hits and learning to recover quickly). The relevant metric isn't whether you're perfect; it's how fast you can course-correct.

"A lot of my success comes from the basic idea that I'm just gonna kind of suck at life — and that's okay."

Relationships: Why "Wait Until I'm Ready" Backfires

Nearly everyone in this conversation is avoiding relationships for one of these reasons: - "I don't want to hurt anyone while I'm still a mess" - "I need to be successful/whole first, so the other person will respect me" - "I want to be free of shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → before I let anyone in" - "I've been badly burned before and I can't trust the process"

These all feel reasonable — and the underlying goals are legitimate. But there's a hidden assumption woven through all of them: I need to be perfect so I can have a perfect relationship.

That's evasion tanking applied to love. You're trying to engineer a relationship where neither person ever gets hurt, by becoming someone who can't be hurt and can't hurt anyone. That's not a relationship — that's a fantasy, and it's one you'll never qualify for.

"Good relationships are not the ones people enter into when they're perfect. A good relationship is one where the person you're with helps you become the starting point you're looking for."

Real relationships involve getting hurt and hurting each other. What distinguishes a good one from a bad one is what happens after: Do they gaslight you, or do they take responsibility? Do they stick around while you figure yourself out, or do they punish you for not already being whole?

Starting a relationship when you're imperfect isn't bad advertising — it's just honesty. Presenting yourself as level 100 when you're not is the actual problem, because it sets an expectation you can never maintain.

You Can Only Be Where You Are

This is the deepest idea in the conversation, and the hardest one to actually sit with:

Right now, in this moment, it is literally impossible for you to be anywhere except where you are. You cannot undo past decisions. You cannot be further along than your actual history has brought you. Every moment, you have the exact hand of cards that your previous choices and circumstances have dealt you — and you have to play that hand.

This isn't nihilism. It's the only place where you actually have agency. You can't act in the past or the future — only now. And from now, you can choose what to do next. But you can't choose to be someone who didn't make the mistakes you made.

Accepting this removes the two things that fuel the cycle most: the shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → of being behind (you couldn't have been anywhere else) and the fantasy of the superhero comeback (you don't need it — you just need to play the hand you have).

"Since you do not have the power to go into the past and change your decisions, you actually have no power to be except where you are."

External accountability helps, but when your entire reason for quitting is to be an inspiration, a single slip feels like betraying everyone who was watching. Your recovery has to be rooted in something internal.

  • Treating a streak as the goal. Ten days without relapsing isn't success followed by failure on day eleven. It's just the practice. The streak number is much less important than the trend over months.

  • Oscillating between Messiah and zero without questioning the framework. The oscillation itself is the problem, not which end you're on. Both poles keep you trapped in the same system.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Track trend, not streaks. Instead of counting days and resetting to zero after a slip, keep a simple log of: how long each relapse lasted, how quickly you returned to your baseline, and whether that recovery time is getting shorter over months. This shows real progress that a streak counter hides.

  2. Notice the oscillation in real time. When you catch yourself fantasizing about a superhuman comeback or an ideal future relationship, pause and ask: Is this the shameA painful emotion triggered by the gap between where you are and where you think you should be — essentially, I am not what I should be.Full definition → talking? What am I trying to compensate for right now? You don't need to stop the thought — just label it.

  3. Change your self-talk to match how you'd talk to someone you care about. When you relapse or fall short of a goal, literally write out what you would say to a friend in the same situation. Then re-read it addressed to yourself. The gap between those two voices is where most of the damage lives.

  4. Do one present-tense action instead of planning a transformation. Richard's ten days worked because he filled his days with things to do until he was tired at night — no grand strategy, just engagement with the present moment. Pick one concrete activity for today, not a plan for who you'll be in three months.

  5. Go outside and look at a plant. This is the contemplative exercise from the talk: look at it and ask yourself whether it's perfect and whether it could be anything other than what it is. Use the sensory experience to practice accepting that what is simply is — not as resignation, but as the only honest starting point.

  6. Practice mula bandha (kegels). Contract the perineum for three to five seconds, release, repeat. Beyond the physical benefit, this is a grounding practice — something small, concrete, and doable right now, which is exactly the point.

  7. Find one person to be an accountability partner — not a cheerleader. Not someone who tells you you're great after a slip, but someone who has been through similar things and can say "I get it, here's what I learned." Shared experience from someone in the same dungeon lands differently than encouragement from outside it.
