Why Intolerance Is Rising Across Every Group

The first instinct when talking about online racism is to make it a competition — which group has it worst. But that framing misses something important: intolerance on the whole is increasing. Anti-South Asian hate is up. So is anti-Jewish hate, anti-white-male sentiment, anti-Christian rhetoric, anti-conservative and anti-liberal hostility. Every group currently feels persecuted.

That doesn't mean the suffering is equal — it almost certainly isn't, and measuring it precisely is genuinely hard. But the underlying driver is the same regardless of target: internet platforms are algorithmically optimized not for truth or equality, but for emotional engagement. And the single most engaging emotion is righteousness — the feeling that something is wrong and you are right. When an algorithm selects for that feeling, it selects for conflict, and conflict cuts in every direction.

On top of that, people are increasingly living inside different information realities. You and someone you disagree with are not looking at the same set of facts. Cognitive bias was always present, but the sensory input feeding that bias used to be shared — the same neighborhood, the same newspaper, the same physical world. Now even the raw data is personalized. When you can't agree on what's real, making progress on what's right becomes nearly impossible.

"The world that you perceive is different from the world that other people perceive. You may not be privy to all of the anti-white hate. You won't be, by definition."

The Problem With Calling People Out

There's a sincere impulse behind wanting to publicly condemn hateful speech — someone should say something, and silence can feel like complicity. But examine what actually happens when you do:

  • People who already agree with you feel validated
  • People who disagree with you dig in harder
  • The exchange gets framed as a contest to be won, not a problem to be solved

Calling someone out galvanizes your own side and entrenches the other. This isn't an argument for silence — it's an argument for being precise about what the goal actually is. Validation and improvement are not the same thing. Feeling seen isn't the same as getting better. A security professional put it plainly: "My job is not to make people feel safe. It's to make people safe." The gap between those two things is where a lot of well-intentioned activism stalls.

There's also the distinction between being offended and being injured. When someone says something that conflicts with your worldview, that experience is real and can be genuinely painful. But treating it as equivalent to material harm — and responding with the social equivalent of force — is what drives cancel culture. The logic of cancellation is: I feel bad, therefore you must be silenced. That's a new form of bullying wearing the clothes of justice.

"We've started confusing being offended with being injured, and we've started emphasizing validation over improvement."

Where Hatred Actually Comes From

Most people who say racist or hateful things are not twirling a mustache. They are usually:

  1. Ignorant — they genuinely don't understand why what they're saying is harmful, because they grew up without encountering the people they're talking about
  2. Hurting — hurt people hurt people; toxicity is almost always downstream of unaddressed pain or unacknowledged grievance
  3. Pattern-matching poorly — the human brain builds patterns from available data, but it also sees bunnies in clouds; a white American whose doctors have been 50% South Asian will conclude Indians are smart doctors, even though that's a selection effect, not a fact about Indians

This doesn't make the harm less real. It does mean the right response is different from what feels satisfying in the moment.

A useful test: when you push someone to explain a racist belief — when you genuinely try to understand it rather than combat it — it usually falls apart on its own. Strongly held positions that are factually wrong tend to crumble under sincere inquiry. Strongly held positions that are shouted at tend to calcify.

"Most people are doing the best that they know how to do. I think most people who are racist — this is not a problem of hatred, it's a problem of ignorance."

Positive Representation vs. Adversarial Pushback

There's a meaningful difference between two possible responses to South Asian hate online:

  • Adversarial pushback: calling out bad actors, arguing against hateful statements, publicly labeling people as wrong
  • Positive representation: celebrating the culture, showing up proudly, being visible proof that the stereotype is incomplete or false

The second approach doesn't require conceding that the hate is acceptable. It just operates on a different level. For a young Brown kid watching a streamer openly love Bollywood, share Indian food, and exist successfully on a platform — that does real work that no counter-argument can do. It doesn't validate anyone's cruelty, and it doesn't entrench the people doing the hating. It offers a different version of reality.

Squeex describes this as the thing he actually wants to do — not to punish the haters, but to be a voice for the kid who feels isolated and unseen.

"I want to be the voice of people being like — you can be awesome. Dal chaval is better than Christmas."

Fighting fire with fire can work if you have a bigger fire. But most people on the internet don't, and even when it does work, you've won a battle inside a system that selected for the fight in the first place.

Why Solving Problems Is Boring (and Why That Matters)

The self-help industry is enormous partly because it doesn't work — if it worked, you'd be done with it. The same logic applies to political commentary. What keeps people engaged is not solutions but the ongoing feeling that something is wrong and their side is right.

"People are not interested in solving problems. People are interested in propagating their belief that they're right."

When a problem gets solved, the engagement disappears. So platforms, commentators, and content creators who depend on engagement are structurally incentivized to keep problems alive. This is why compassionate, curious dialogue almost never rises to the top — it's not failing because it's wrong, it's failing by the metric of views and clicks, which measures something else entirely.

Success on a platform and solving the underlying problem are two completely different outcomes. Conflating them is a trap.

But optimism and pessimism are both psychological states that interfere with action. Optimism leads to complacency ("it'll work out"). Pessimism leads to despair ("nothing matters"). Neither helps you do the thing in front of you.

The useful orientation is something closer to: there are problems I can affect today, and I'm going to work on those, regardless of whether I think we're all going to make it. The trajectory of the future is shaped by what you do now, not by how you feel about the odds.

A Note on Meditation (and Bad Teachers)

The conversation opens with a long meditation thread that connects to everything else. The short version: most meditation teaching is not great, and most people who found meditation temporarily helpful but ultimately unsatisfying probably had bad teachers rather than bad practices.

Specifically, the common instruction to "meditate without any goal in mind" is not universal wisdom — it's a teaching that applies to certain students at certain stages. Someone who doesn't yet know why they should meditate, or what it's supposed to do, will eventually lose motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → and stop. The practice needs to connect to something real in your life — a problem you're trying to solve, a quality you want to develop.

What meditation actually helps with, at least for someone like Squeex, is grounding your sense of self firmly enough that other people's perception of you doesn't hijack your attention. When someone walks into the room and you immediately start seeing yourself through their eyes, that's self-referential processing — a documented cognitive cascade that pulls your attention off your actual experience and onto how you're being judged. A meditation practice that helps you maintain your internal sense of self while still playing the necessary social games reduces that hijacking.

The problem isn't that meditation doesn't work. It's that without a teacher who can tailor the practice to what you actually need, you end up doing reps that produce a 30-minute glow and then wear off — and you conclude meditation isn't for you.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating validation as progress. Calling out a racist comment and getting 10,000 likes feels like winning. It is not the same as making anything better.
  • Assuming you can accurately measure who has it worst. The internet you see is personalized. Your feed is not a representative sample of human suffering, and your intuitions about relative inequality are probably wrong in ways you can't easily detect.
  • Conflating platform success with impact. The things that go viral are not the things that heal. Optimizing for one often means sacrificing the other.
  • Stopping meditation because the benefits wore off. The practice isn't broken; the application is probably too generic. Finding what you actually want from it, and finding someone who can adapt the method to that goal, changes the experience entirely.
  • Believing most haters are malicious. Most people who say harmful things are ignorant, not evil. This isn't a reason to excuse them; it's a reason to engage differently.
  • Making the world's problems your emotional North Star. Whether you're optimistic or pessimistic about civilization's future has very little to do with whether you can do something useful today.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit what "speaking out" actually accomplishes. Next time you feel the urge to call someone out online, ask: what do I expect to happen? Who am I helping? If the honest answer is "I want to feel like I said something," that's a valid human impulse — but name it accurately rather than framing it as activism.

  2. Practice genuine curiosity with a hostile view. Find one online opinion you find offensive or obviously wrong. Instead of arguing against it, try to trace where it comes from: what experience of reality would make that belief feel true? You don't have to agree. Just see if you can understand it from the inside.

  3. Shift from adversarial pushback to positive representation. If there's a group you care about that's being unfairly characterized, find one concrete way to represent something true and good about that group — without referencing the people saying bad things. Celebrate rather than counter.

  4. Reconnect meditation to a real goal. Before your next meditation session, spend five minutes writing down one specific thing you want to change about how you move through the world. Use that as the purpose for your practice, and notice whether having a direction changes the experience.

  5. Try the "empty cup" exercise. Pick a topic you have strong opinions about. For one conversation, commit to asking only questions — no statements of your own view. See what you learn that you couldn't have gotten by defending your position.

  6. Drop the optimism/pessimism frame entirely. When you catch yourself worrying about whether things will get better or worse, redirect: what problem can I affect in the next 24 hours? Write it down. Do it. Repeat.

  7. When you feel offended, check whether you've been injured. These feel similar but are different things. Offense is a signal worth noticing; injury is something that requires a response. Practicing the distinction helps you choose your energy more deliberately.
