Adrenaline: The First-Responder Hormone

Adrenaline activates in under a second. It raises your heart rate, spikes blood pressure, and causes sweating. It's your body's way of preparing for immediate action.

Two effects are especially worth knowing about:

  1. Tunnel vision — physical and cognitive. Adrenaline literally collapses your visual field from roughly 180 degrees down to about 30 degrees. Your pupils dilate and your focus narrows to whatever is directly in front of you. The same narrowing happens in your thinking. When anxious, you lose the ability to hold a wide, balanced perspective; you lock onto one thought or fear and can't let go.

  2. Hypothesis becomes reality. Adrenaline blurs the line between what is possible and what is real. If you encounter a tiger in the woods, you treat it as lethal even if it hasn't moved toward you — even if it's sick, scared, or uninterested. That response makes evolutionary sense. The problem is that anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → applies the same switch to things like a difficult conversation or a bill you can't pay. A hypothetical bad outcome starts to feel like a certainty, and that's not a flaw in your character — it's a hormone doing exactly what it was designed to do.
