Growing Up as the "Wrong" Version of Yourself

Michael grew up in a small rural town in Louisiana as the only Black kid deep in anime and Yu-Gi-Oh at a time when neither was mainstream. He got it from both sides. The popular kids ostracized him for being a nerd. Other Black kids told him he was "acting white" and questioned whether his interests were racially legitimate. He internalized both.

"Maybe their logic is correct. Maybe I'm not being Black enough. Maybe I'm not being valid in my identity."

This is what it feels like to be gaslit about who you are during the years when identity is still forming. The result wasn't just social pain — it was a chronic overthinking loop, especially around romance. Every potential interaction with a girl became a multi-layered strategic calculation: What do I open with? If I say this, she'll think that, so I need to pre-plan my next three moves. He describes it as a "pyramid of confusion" that consumed all available processing power.

The key insight here is that overthinking in social situations is often not a personality flaw — it's a protection mechanism born from repeated rejection. When being yourself has gotten you burned enough times, the mind starts treating every social interaction like a chess match.

Trihex as Escape — and What That Costs

The online tag Trihex came from a Yu-Gi-Oh tournament win at age 13. When Yu-Gi-Oh became too pay-to-win and Michael's local communityA Sanskrit term meaning community, sangha refers to the group of people — or beings — whose shared orientation supports your spiritual or personal growth.Full definition → rejected the Japanese ban list he'd printed out and carried in by hand, he pivoted to speedrunning. He was recording runs on his grandmother's VCR and mailing VHS tapes to websites to be digitized — this in 2004, before YouTube existed.

The speedrunning identity gave him something Michael never had: a legacy, purpose, and validation untethered from race, dress, or hometown social politics.

"Trihex had a legacy. Michael had nothing."

This split matters. When an online identity becomes the primary place where self-worth lives, the offline self starts to feel like a liability. Everything good about you exists as the avatar. Everything painful exists as the person.

Imposter Syndrome and the Need to Be Number One

When streaming took off and more runners joined the Yoshi's Island community, Michael found himself the most popular speedrunner but not the fastest. He was top eight, not number one.

"I did not allow myself to enjoy streaming until I got world record."

Getting world record wasn't about the time. It was about closing the gap between what people perceived him to be and what he actually was. As long as that gap existed, he felt like he'd "debated people into believing something that wasn't true." The terror underneath wasn't failure — it was exposure. That he couldn't actually be what Trihex was supposed to be.

This is the architecture of imposter syndrome: an external identity that outpaces internal belief, and the exhausting work of keeping the two from colliding in public.

The Loneliness of Viral Success

When Mario Maker launched in 2015, Michael went from ~400 viewers to 8,000 almost overnight. By most measures, this was the dream. In practice, it was disorienting and isolating.

Every DM felt like a scam. Every compliment felt transactional. He went three days without speaking to another human being — all communication ran through a monitor. His skin became "paper thin." A single joke that landed wrong with an Xbox fan could ruin a day because he had nothing to ground himself.

The lesson: scale amplifies everything, including vulnerability. More viewers means more feedback, more criticism, more projectionProjection is when something inside you — a feeling, impulse, or trait you can't consciously accept — gets attributed to someone else instead.Full definition →. If you don't have a stable enough sense of self to absorb that, success becomes its own kind of exposure.

Rejection, Catfishing, and the Fear of Being "Thirsty"

Michael had never had a girlfriend and was terrified of being perceived as "thirsty" — desperate for attention or attraction. He watched other streamers get mocked online for showing interest in women and internalized the lesson: showing genuine desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → is weakness, something to hide.

What's worth unpacking: that association didn't come from nowhere. Growing up around boys whose entire strategy with girls was manipulation and performance taught him that expressing attraction meant playing a role he didn't want to play. So he suppressed it entirely.

He was catfished — twice, possibly by the same person — during a period when he was already emotionally raw. Someone presented fake photos, built an emotional connection, and when he pushed for any kind of real contact, they deflected until he figured it out. No closure. No admission.

"You can't even seek closure. You have to just conclude on your own that you're done being on this merry-go-round."

The need for closure is real, but some situations are structurally designed to deny it. The more you reach for an admission of guilt, the more smoke screen goes up. At some point the only move is to get off the ride.

The Relationship That Actually Worked

Michael's current long-term relationship started because he snapped at a donation from someone he misread, called her a "basement-dwelling neckbeard," and then his Twitch community found her profile, verified she was real, and did damage control on his behalf. They've been together seven years.

When the relationship moved from long-distance to living together, he was deliberate about it: radical transparency from day one, named communication and trust as the foundation, planned every visit around building memories, and chose to drive her back to Louisiana rather than have her fly — so the transition felt gradual rather than abrupt.

Burnout, "Unconquerable Task Syndrome," and the Case of the Don'ts

After years of never taking a real break — no PTO, no vacations, streaming through events, always treating time off as "cross-pollination of opportunity" — Michael hit a wall. He describes sitting in front of his computer for nine hours and producing one hour of actual work, then feeling crushing guilt about it. He thought he might have ADHDA neurodevelopmental condition defined not by an inability to focus, but by a disorder of attention — meaning you lack control over where your attention goes, in both directions.Full definition →. He wondered if he needed Adderall.

What he actually had was burnout he hadn't named. The pattern he describes is common: easy work (streaming) always wins against hard work (emails, admin, creative output) because the brain optimizes for efficiency. You can stream eight hours or do one hour of inbox management — and the brain will choose the eight hours every time, leaving the hard stuff permanently deferred.

The fix that actually worked: he took a full month off in May 2022. Away from his office and the constant accessibility of streaming, he could finally see his own patterns clearly enough to change them.

Guilt, Ego, and What You Actually Owe Your Community

Michael describes "streamer guilt" — the feeling that he's never made the content his community deserves, that taking days off is letting them down, that his competitors are always live and always grinding.

The reframe worth holding onto: guilt requires an ego. You can only feel guilty about not living up to an expectation you've set for yourself. That expectation comes from an identity — in this case, Trihex the content god who owes his community perfection.

But his community isn't asking for that. The people who've been subscribed for 100 months aren't there because he achieved world record. They're there for something harder to quantify.

"What your community deserves from you is not a particular outcome. What you owe them is service — you showing up and giving what you have."

The desire to do right by people is healthy. The insistence that what you do will never be enough is the ego twisting that healthy impulse into self-punishment. The insecurity that made him want to prove himself in the first place — the kid who got called White Mike, who couldn't be nerd enough or Black enough for anyone — is the same insecurity driving the guilt now.

Adaptations to insecurity can drive real achievement. But at some point you have to grow past the adaptation, or it starts eating you.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing the source of the guilt. Streamer guilt, creative guilt, relationship guilt — these feel like they're about other people, but they're almost always about a standard you've set for yourself that no one else is actually holding you to.

  • Believing private practice would fix everything. The desire to improve in private is real and valid (flow state genuinely requires reduced cognitive load), but it can also be a way to avoid the scrutiny that might reveal you can't perform under pressure. Both things can be true.

  • Treating burnout as laziness or ADHD. The "unconquerable task" pattern — planning fun, execution impossible — is often a symptom of a depleted system, not a broken one.

  • Waiting until you're ready before taking the break. Michael waited years to take a real vacation because rest felt like weakness. The break was what allowed him to see the problem clearly enough to change it.

  • Expecting closure from people who can't or won't give it. Some relationships end without acknowledgment. Waiting for the other person to admit what happened keeps you on the ride indefinitely.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name the split. If you have an online identity or a "performed" self that feels more legitimate than your offline self, write down three things that belong only to the offline version. Notice whether those things are things you're ashamed of or simply things you haven't let yourself integrate.

  2. Audit your guilt's origin. When you feel guilty about underperforming, ask: Who actually said I needed to do this much? Is anyone asking for this, or is this my expectation of myself? Write down the expectation, then write down what evidence you have that anyone else holds it.

  3. Try the two-arrow check. When you make a mistake, notice whether you're shooting a second arrow — self-loathing on top of the original pain. The first arrow you can't always avoid. The second one is optional. Just noticing it is often enough to slow it down.

  4. Protect your reflection time. Michael couldn't see his own patterns until he was physically away from his office. Build in time — even an hour per week — where you are not in the environment where your compulsive easy-work happens. A walk, a different room, anywhere that doesn't have immediate access to the default.

  5. For the "unconquerable task" problem: Don't start by executing. Start by asking what the smallest possible increment is — not 100 emails, but open the inbox and read three subject lines. Stop there if you need to. The goal is to break the approach-avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition → loop, not to finish the task in one session.

  6. Try a basic breath meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → when the self-loathing loop starts. Sit upright. Breathe in starting from the abdomen, moving up to the chest and shoulders. Hold. Release slowly. Repeat three times. The goal isn't emptying the mind — it's stepping outside of time-pressure long enough to interrupt the spiral. Five minutes is enough to start.

  7. Reframe the main quest. If you have a long-term creative or service goal, treat it as a main quest with a long time horizon — not a sprint with a deadline. Ask: What would this look like if I gave it five years instead of five months? Then identify one thing you can do today that points in that direction, however small.
