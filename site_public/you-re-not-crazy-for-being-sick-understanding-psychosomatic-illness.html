Summary

TL;DR — Psychosomatic illness is neither "all in your head" nor purely physical. It is a real, whole-body condition rooted in how your nervous system, immune system, upbringing, and mind are all tangled together — and Western medicine is poorly equipped to treat it because it insists on separating them.

Conditions like IBS, POTS, and fibromyalgia sit in a gap that Western medicine was not built to handle. Cardiologists treat the heart. Psychologists treat the mind. Neither is trained to treat the place where those two things break down together. If you have one of these conditions, you will likely have to become your own expert — because very few practitioners have the multidisciplinary view that this actually requires.

Your Mind Is Not Weak — It's Too Strong

The most important thing to get out of the way: people with psychosomatic illness are not faking it, not weak, and not doing it for attention.

If anything, the pattern runs the opposite direction. The mind in these cases is powerful enough to override the body's distress signals and keep pushing forward — past the point where the body can sustain it. Think of the mind as a taskmaster that whips the body onward even as the body sends up warning flares. The body eventually breaks down not from weakness but from relentless overriding.

The mind skips its own moderating role, and the body takes the full hit.

What's Actually Happening in the Body

IBS

People with IBS tend to have a hyperactive sympathetic nervous systemThe body's fight-or-flight activation system — one half of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes you for intense physical action.Full definition → — not too much adrenaline exactly, but slower reuptake of noradrenaline, meaning the stress signal lingers far longer than it should. This creates a prolonged state of low-level physiological alarm.

On top of that, the gut itself becomes immunologically overactive — mast cells and enterochromaffin cells behave like the gut is having a low-grade allergic reaction, causing non-specific bloating, diarrhea, constipation. There's also visceral hypersensitivity: the nerves inside the gut have their volume turned way up. A stomach stretch that a healthy person experiences as comfortable fullness becomes genuinely distressing.

Combine prolonged noradrenaline exposure (which makes hypothetical dangers feel more real) with bad things that genuinely have happened (like someone who has actually had GI accidents in public), and you get behavioral amplification — the moment a stomach rumble appears, the person leaves the restaurant, cancels plans, isolates. Which then feeds the cycle.

POTS

POTS involves a failure of the blood vessels to adapt properly when you stand up. Normally, standing triggers dilation of vessels going to the brain (to maintain blood flow upward) and constriction of vessels in the feet (to push blood up against gravity). In POTS, this doesn't happen. Blood flow to the brain actually drops when you stand — a paradoxical vasoconstriction — and the heart compensates by racing.

The underlying mechanism ties back to the same sympathetic nervous systemThe body's fight-or-flight activation system — one half of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes you for intense physical action.Full definition → dysregulation seen in IBS. This is why 40% of people with POTS also have GI problems — the two conditions share a common mechanism, not just a coincidence of comorbidity.

There's also a CO2 component: POTS patients tend to be hypocapnic (low CO2), and CO2 is one of the signals that tells blood vessels in the brain to dilate. Raising CO2 levels — as happens during certain breathing practices and meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → — can actually correct the paradoxical vasoconstriction.

Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia involves central sensitization: the pain-processing pathways in the brain get stuck in an "on" position, like a phantom limb but distributed throughout the body. There's some peripheral input — minor inflammation, real nerve signals — but the brain amplifies and perpetuates the pain signal far beyond what those inputs would normally produce.

Low-grade whole-body inflammation (driven in part by gut microbiome dysregulation and immune hyperactivity) keeps the pain receptors sensitized. The brain is locked in a loop, and breaking that loop requires changing the brain's plasticity — not just muting the pain signal.

Why Western Medicine Struggles Here

Western medicine is built on linear, idiopathic models: one cause, one disease, one specialist. This is extraordinary for conditions like bacterial infections or single-gene disorders. It struggles badly for conditions where the disease is the relationship between systems, not a malfunction in any one system.

When you have IBS, you get sent to a gastroenterologist and a therapist — separately. Psychotherapy does help — not because the illness is psychological, but because the mind is one intervention point in a larger cycle. Fixing one point in a cycle slows the whole cycle. But it doesn't cure the condition, because the inflammation, the nerve hypersensitivity, the cardiovascular dysregulation are all still running.

Common Pitfalls

"It's all in your head." This is not only wrong, it's backward. There is documented immune activation, measurable nervous system dysregulation, and real physiological changes in all of these conditions.

"If social support helps, that proves it's psychological." Social support also improves survival in stroke, heart attack, and cancer. It is a biological resource, not evidence that a disease is imaginary.

"Psychotherapy will fix it." Psychotherapy is one lever. It interrupts the cycle at the mind-body interface. It doesn't address the gut microbiome, the noradrenaline reuptake, the visceral hypersensitivity, or the paradoxical vasoconstriction. It helps. It is not a cure by itself.

"I just need to find the right medication." There are medications that help individual parts of this — beta blockers, certain GI agents — but because the disease is in the connections between systems, a single drug that fixes one node rarely resolves the whole picture.

"Attending to my body signals means I'm just reinforcing the illness."
