Everything Costs More — And That's Not a Character Flaw

The foundational problem isn't that people with ADHD can't do adult life. It's that every single component of it — sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition →, work, socializing, self-care, masking at the office — draws from the same limited energy pool, and the draws are larger than they are for neurotypical people.

Most people with ADHD are simultaneously: - Trying to execute the actual task (working, sleeping, exercising) - Fighting to fit into systems and schedules not built for their brains - Actively hiding the fact that they're different (masking) - Running an internal script that tells them they're failing

Each of those is its own full-time job. When you add them together and then wonder why the "bare minimum" feels impossible, you're not seeing the real math. You are fighting battles on every front, all at once — and calling yourself lazy for getting tired.

"We are constantly spread thin. And it feels very much like I have to do everything all at once or else I'm a failure."

The first move is simply recognizing that the energy cost is real and large. Dismissing it as weakness makes everything harder.

Why Habits Take Longer to Form — and Longer to Stick

Learning to drive is a useful model. The first time, there's a 47-item checklist. Eventually you're halfway home before you realize you've been driving. That transition — from laborious checklist to effortless execution — is what habit formation actually is.

For people with ADHD, two things are true that aren't true for neurotypical people:

  1. It takes more energy to get a habit started — the initial effort is higher.
  2. It takes longer to reach the "effortless" stage — the runway before automatic execution is extended.

This means the standard advice of "build the habit for 21 days" is especially misleading. Even neurotypical people rarely execute anything perfectly for 14–21 days in a row. Expecting that of an ADHD brain, while also fighting masking and self-criticism, is setting up a guaranteed collapse.

The practical implication: don't measure progress by whether you've crossed an arbitrary finish line. Measure it by whether the thing is getting slightly less effortful over time.

The One-or-Two-Things Rule

One of the most repeated ideas across every question in this talk: stop trying to fix everything at once.

It feels counterintuitive because life doesn't pause. Sleep, diet, exercise, work, relationships, social connection — they all matter, and they're all suffering. The temptation is to attack all of them simultaneously.

But many of these things are interconnected in ways that make them partially self-solving. Fix sleep, and the ruminationA pattern of repetitive, internally focused thinking that amplifies emotional pain rather than resolving it.Full definition → about work decreases, which helps work performance, which reduces anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → at bedtime, which helps sleep. You don't have to tackle the whole loop — you pick one entry point and let the ripple happen.

A practical example: if sleep is terrible, work suffers, which causes evening rumination, which destroys sleep — pick sleep only. Not sleep AND work performance AND social networking. Just sleep. Then wait, with patience (which is brutally hard for impulsive people), for the cascade.

The same logic applies to exercise: don't commit to five gym sessions per week because you can white-knuckle it for two weeks before collapsing. Start with walking around the block. Then the gym once a week. Then twice. Each step makes the next one less effortful because momentum compounds — but only if you don't blow it all on a sprint.

"You are making your first few steps the literal hardest they could possibly be. We can do that, but then we give up and we are totally fatigued."

Harnessing Neurodivergence Instead of Hiding It

A lot of energy goes into trying to become neurotypical. Some accommodation is unavoidable — if your job requires you to be somewhere at 9 a.m., you have to work with that reality. But a lot of accommodation is optional, and the cost of it is invisibly enormous.

Some places where you can stop forcing fit: - Sleep schedules: If you're a natural night owl, are there jobs or arrangements that don't require early mornings? Not always possible, but worth genuinely asking rather than assuming. - Social situations: If you need to attend a draining event, drive separately. Preserve the buffer before and after. Find the specific people you can be around without masking — where you can both exist without performing. - Communication style: There are people and contexts where your rambling, tangential, divergent storytelling is fine and even enjoyable. Protect those spaces. They're not indulgences — they're recovery.

The most successful people with ADHD, in clinical observation, are generally not the ones who hide their ADHD most effectively. They're the ones who have found environments where their particular brain is an asset and built their lives around that reality rather than spending all their energy pretending to be someone else.

"The most successful people I know with ADHD are harnessing and leveraging their ADHD, not trying to fit in as like, 'Oh no, nobody would know I had ADHD.'"

Communicating Clearly When Your Thoughts Are Nonlinear

Stammering, losing your thread, burying the main point in a wall of context — these are common and frustrating. Two things are usually happening:

  1. There are more thoughts trying to exit than your mouth can transmit, often because anxiety and judgment thoughts are competing for the same bandwidth.
  2. There's no internal filter telling you which piece of information is most important, so you try to transmit everything at once.

Practical approaches:

The elevator pitch drill: Pick one complex idea from your work or life. Set a timer for two minutes and explain it out loud. Do this repeatedly. The goal isn't to memorize a speech — it's to train your brain to quickly find the essential skeleton of an idea so you can retrieve it in real situations.

The grandmother/six-year-old test: After you get comfortable with a two-minute version, try explaining the same idea in two sentences to someone who either doesn't understand the field or doesn't have the cognitive capacity for nuance. This forces genuine prioritization.

The TLDR-after: When talking with people you're comfortable with, just say everything — and then at the end, say "okay, my TLDR is..." and give the one-sentence summary. It's much easier to summarize after you've emptied your brain than to pre-select what matters. Over time, this trains the pre-selection instinct.

Written story outlining: For storytelling specifically, physically write the story as numbered steps. You'll immediately notice where you want to insert 3a, 3b, 3c — ask yourself honestly: if I removed 3a, would the story still make sense? If yes, skip it. If no, it's not a sub-step, it's a main step. This makes the nonlinear structure of your mind visible so you can edit it deliberately.

Emotion Dysregulation and Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria

Emotion regulation is an executive function. It lives in the frontal lobeThe brain region responsible for planning, executing tasks, controlling impulses, and regulating other parts of the brain — essentially the hardware of self-regulation and mature behavior.Full definition → — which is the part of the brain that functions differently in ADHD. This is rarely discussed, but it means that emotional intensity and volatility aren't personality problems or immaturity. They're structural features of how the brain processes and manages emotional states.

Growing up with undiagnosed ADHD, or with ADHD in a world that doesn't accommodate it, adds another layer: you learn that your emotions are "too much." So you try to suppress them. But suppressed emotions still leak — sometimes through inappropriate expressions that confuse people, which then creates more anxiety about emotional expression, which leads to more suppression. It becomes a spiral.

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) fits into this. When someone declines an invitation, sets a boundary, or expresses mild irritation, it can land as a devastating rejection rather than a neutral event — because the brain hasn't reliably learned to discriminate between "this person is mildly inconvenienced" and "this person is rejecting me as a person."

The work here is explicit discrimination practice: consciously walking yourself through the question of what actually happened. Was this person setting a limit on their time, or were they rejecting me? Did I make a genuine mistake that warrants empathy and repair, or was I 8 minutes late to a meeting where the first 10 minutes were casual chatting?

"When you make this process explicit and you walk yourself through this understanding, you are literally training your brain to better understand when am I truly being rejected and when is it just a natural part of life."

DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy) is worth knowing about here. It was built around significant emotion dysregulation and offers structured, evidence-based tools for tolerating and managing intense emotional states — including the specific skill of bringing emotional intensity down before trying to solve the problem that triggered it.

Maintaining Relationships When Out of Sight Means Out of Mind

Many people with ADHD genuinely care about their relationships and genuinely forget to act on that care. This isn't indifference. It's a working memory and attention issue. The result — friends feeling abandoned, colleagues feeling ignored — is painful on both sides.

Concrete systems that help:

  • Put relationship maintenance in your calendar: birthdays are obvious, but also anniversaries of important events people have mentioned, recurring reminders to text specific people, and notes like "this week is hard for [person] every year because of [reason]."
  • Use your phone's memory features against themselves: when your phone generates a "memory" photo from years ago, send it to the person in it. It takes ten seconds and communicates that you're thinking of them.
  • Recurring alarms for specific people: a daily 12:30 alarm that just says "text a family member" is a low-friction prompt that keeps relationships warm without requiring spontaneous motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition →.

For the people around someone with ADHD: the bar isn't perfect reciprocityThe mutual exchange of care, effort, and goodwill between friends — not as an equal trade, but as each person contributing what they genuinely can.Full definition →. Many relationships can sustain one person doing more of the logistical maintenance if the other person is doing something — small, consistent signals that the connection is real. The goal is finding the version of showing up that's achievable rather than the version that feels socially correct but never happens.

Common Pitfalls

Treating "just thoughts" as harmless. Thoughts are how the brain constructs its understanding of reality and your place in it. Thoughts that say "you're worthless and hopeless" aren't neutral background noise — they're actively shaping the world you inhabit. Dismissing them instead of taking them seriously doesn't make them go away.

Going zero to one hundred. Starting a new habit at maximum intensity feels productive but almost always leads to collapse within two weeks — before any real benefit materializes, and before the habit becomes easier. The failure then gets internalized as proof that change is impossible, rather than feedback that the ramp-up was too steep.

Trying to build five habits simultaneously. Every habit you add is another draw on limited executive function. The one or two things at a time rule isn't a limitation — it's the strategy that actually works.

Expecting a system to be a perfect fit. No productivity method, app, or framework will be 100% right for your specific brain and context. The goal is to try something, identify what's working and what isn't, and keep the useful parts. Abandoning a system entirely because part of it isn't working throws away real value.

Confusing masking success with functioning. Some people with ADHD get categorized as having "grown out of it" because they've learned to mask well. The masking is real work, it costs real energy, and it's often what's causing the exhaustion rather than solving any underlying challenge.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick one thing. Identify the area of life that, if it improved, would most likely have a positive ripple effect on everything else. Work only on that for the next four to six weeks. Let the rest be imperfect.

  2. Build a ramp, not a wall. For whatever change you're making, identify the smallest possible starting version. If you want to exercise five days a week, start with one ten-minute walk. After two to three weeks, add one more session. Let momentum compound.

  3. Do the progressive muscle relaxation wake-up. Before getting out of bed, squeeze and release muscle groups starting from your feet and moving upward. This draws blood into the extremities and signals the brain to exit low-energy mode — without a screen.

  4. Try the TLDR-after practice. In casual conversation with people you trust, let yourself ramble — and then, when you've finished, give a one-sentence summary. Do this repeatedly until the summary comes more naturally at the start.

  5. Write your story as a numbered list. Take something you've been trying to explain — at work, to a friend, anywhere — and write it as numbered steps. Ask of each step: "If I removed this, would the rest still make sense?" If yes, cut it. This teaches the brain to find main steps versus sub-details.

  6. Build a relationship calendar. Open your calendar and add: every important person's birthday, one or two emotionally significant dates they've mentioned, and a recurring daily alarm to reach out to someone in your life. Keep a running note of small personal details people share so you can reference them later.

  7. Practice explicit discrimination for RSD. When you feel rejected or stung by someone's behavior, write out (or walk yourself through out loud): What actually happened? Was this person rejecting me as a person, or were they communicating a limit, a preference, or a mild frustration? What's the most neutral explanation for what they did? Do this enough times that it starts to happen automatically.

  8. Find your unmask contexts. Identify two or three people or situations where you don't have to perform neurotypicality — where you can exist as you are without energy drain. Prioritize protecting and increasing those contexts, especially around high-masking obligations.
