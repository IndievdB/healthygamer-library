Summary

TL;DR — Unprocessed emotion is at the root of most destructive behavior, and the path out runs through naming what you actually feel, building real relationships, and learning to sit with discomfort instead of running from it.

This conversation covers a lot of ground: the emotional roots of violent crime, how childhood invalidation shapes adult life, what makes friendships genuinely healing, why traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → keeps replaying itself, and how people recover — often without any formal help at all. What holds it all together is a simple through-line: most suffering comes from emotions that were never allowed to exist, and most healing comes from finally letting them.

The Emotional Roots of Violence and Rage

A pattern shows up repeatedly in true crime cases: someone raised in an environment that relentlessly invalidated their emotions — a narcissistic parent, constant humiliation, no safe outlet for anger — eventually hits a wall. They were never given tools to identify or process what they felt, so everything gets compressed. When enough pressure builds, it comes out in the most primitive way available: destruction.

"They have all this repressed and just stored anger that is never validated, never expressed — and they end up going to the most primal form of expressing that anger, which is violence."

Public humiliation deserves special attention here. There's a meaningful difference between embarrassment (something that arises from within) and humiliation (something done to you, often in front of others). When humiliation happens repeatedly — especially from a parent, especially publicly — it can metastasize into a worldview where the entire world owes you a reckoning. That particular combination of shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → and audience shows up with striking consistency in cases of mass violence.

One practical implication: media that glorifies perpetrators — giving them scary names, detailed profiles, front-page coverage — likely functions as an instruction manual for people who are in pain and want to be somebody. New Zealand's response to the Christchurch shooting (erasing the perpetrator's name and face from public record) offers a deliberate counterexample.

How Childhood Invalidation Becomes a Life Script

When a child repeatedly brings their pain to a caregiver and gets nothing back — dismissal, deflection, "just pray about it," or simple absence — the child doesn't conclude "my parents are bad at this." They conclude I don't matter. My needs are not real.

That conclusion gets tested against the world over and over. Every time reality seems to confirm it, the belief deepens. By the time someone is in their twenties, they're not just having a rough time — they're living inside a narrative that was written for them before they had any say in it.

"When that happens over and over, a child rationalizes with the best tools they have available — and it works backwards to: it happened again, I must not matter, so I should stop."

The cruel irony is that undoing this work is almost impossible alone. The belief was installed by other people, and it typically takes other people to dismantle it — which is part of why isolation makes mental health worse, and why one of the most powerful things therapy provides isn't technique but simply another human being who treats your wellbeing as partially their responsibility.

Triggers Are Emotional, Not Cinematic

Many people expect trauma triggers to look like flashbacks — a war veteran diving for cover at a car backfire. But for most people, being triggered doesn't look like a memory replay. It looks like an emotion arriving at the wrong time, with way too much intensity, for no apparent reason.

What's actually happening: the brain has tagged certain stimuli as connected to an old emotional experience. When one of those stimuli appears, you don't relive the scene — you relive the feeling. Full intensity, injected into a mundane Tuesday.

This makes everyday life confusing and exhausting. You react with disproportionate emotion, the person in front of you gets scared or annoyed, they tell you you're "too much," and you internalize that too. The cycle reinforces itself.

The brain keeps the trigger active because it's still trying to solve the original problem. Its logic: this was the most dangerous thing that happened to me, so I need to keep practicing until I know how to handle it. This is why people unconsciously recreate painful dynamics — not because they enjoy suffering, but because the brain is running a survival subroutine, trying to finally get it right.

The "Too Much" Wound and Other Named Injuries

Naming a wound matters. It makes something that felt formless and shameful into something recognizable and workable. A few worth knowing:

  • The "too much" wound: Learned when your natural self — your volume, your energy, your emotions — was treated as a problem. A tired or overwhelmed parent who couldn't meet you where you were taught you to make yourself small to stay in their presence. The result is a person who apologizes for taking up space, who interrupts their own good insights to say "sorry, I'm just rambling."

  • The abandonment wound: The hypervigilance that comes from being left, repeatedly, in ways that confirmed you weren't worth staying for. Often shows up as self-sabotage — leaving first, before you can be left, so you at least control the fall.

  • The collapse-and-submit response: A trauma response that looks like giving up but is actually sophisticated self-protection. If standing up means being hurt harder, the most efficient move is to go down on your own terms. It works — until it starts costing you respect, advocacy, and opportunities you never even tried for.

These coping strategies aren't flaws. They were the best tools available at the time. The hard truth is that replacing them isn't an upgrade — it's a downgrade. You're trading a sharp, well-oiled instrument that fits perfectly in your hand for something clunky and unfamiliar. That's why change is hard: not because the old behavior doesn't work, but because it works extremely well.

Most of us have been punished our whole lives for having negative emotions. Parents who shut it down. Peers who mocked it. A culture that posts videos of "Karens" losing it rather than asking what drove someone to that point. So we learn: feel something bad, get punished. The logical response is to suppress it.

The problem is that suppressed emotion doesn't disappear. It redirects. It shows up as rage at strangers online, or drinking until the pressure in your chest goes away, or gaming through a weekend because you can't face whatever's sitting underneath.

"Anger is really just a shield for our vulnerable emotions. If you dig deep enough through the anger, you find out what's underneath — and you find out so much about yourself."

What actually helps is sitting with the feeling long enough to name it, locate it in the body, and let it move through you. Not because that's pleasant, but because the emotion contains information about what you want — and if you can't feel it, you can't use it to make decisions. You just react.

Being a Good Friend Is a Skill

When someone tells you something genuinely painful, the instinct is to say "I'm so sorry that happened" and change the subject. That lands like almost nothing. What actually breaks through the isolation of trauma is having someone name the specific emotional experience you went through.

Instead of "I'm sorry": - "I bet in that moment you felt completely betrayed." - "That must have been terrifying — being ignored like that."

The difference is that you're not just acknowledging that something bad happened; you're confirming that the emotion made sense. For someone who has spent years being told their feelings were wrong, that confirmation can be the first real relief they've felt.

A practical framework for being the kind of friend who actually heals people: 1. Understand them first — really listen, without being scared of what they share 2. Name the emotion — reflect back specifically what they must have felt, not just what happened 3. Know their particular wound — if a friend has a history of neglect, just reliably responding to their texts matters more than you think 4. Keep showing up — especially through moments of discomfort that would normally make both people retreat

Relationships deepen through discomfort, not around it. The wall that keeps things comfortable is the same wall that keeps them shallow.

Recovery Rarely Looks Like the Official Version

Something like two-thirds of people with addictions achieve sobriety without ever seeing a medical professional. That's not an argument against getting help — it's a clue about what actually drives recovery.

What tends to work: building a life that's worth protecting. A relationship you don't want to blow up. Work you have to show up for. Days that feel valuable enough that you don't want to waste them. The value of a day is circular — the more you build into it, the more it costs to throw away. The less you build, the cheaper it gets to waste.

Harm reduction is real and evidence-based. You don't have to choose between full sobriety and chaos. But the deepest shift is when you start to genuinely care about yourself enough that the calculation changes — not because someone told you to care, but because experience slowly built a case for it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Comparing your pain to someone else's — "Other people have it worse" is probably the most corrosive thing you can say to yourself. A 10 is a 10. If it hurt that much, it hurt that much.

  • Mistaking the logical story for the real problem — Smart people are especially prone to building airtight therapeutic narratives around a decoy problem, one that feels appropriately serious but lets the actual wound stay hidden. If you've been in therapy for a while and nothing is shifting, it's worth asking whether you're pointing at the right thing.

  • Adopting a diagnosis as an identityA stable sense of who you are, built from the narrative integration of emotionally salient experiences — not a fixed trait you possess, but a story you construct by stringing together the moments in your life that…Full definition → — A label can be clarifying and relieving. It can also become a cage. The goal is to treat the symptoms, not to become the diagnosis. You're allowed to be more than the thing that hurts you.

  • Assuming discomfort means retreat — The moment in a relationship when both people want to pull back is usually the moment that matters most. Working through it is how shallow becomes deep.

  • Thinking recovery requires perfection — The pendulum doesn't have to swing all the way to the other side. You can still be working on something and be wise. Both are true at the same time.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Name your top three emotions. Identify the feelings that come up most often in your life — not in a crisis, just regularly. Then make a simple plan for what you'll do when each one shows up, before you're in the middle of it.

  2. Practice feeling emotions in your body. The next time a strong emotion arrives, pause and locate it physically. Where is it — chest, throat, stomach? Let it move. Take slow breaths. Name it out loud if that helps. You're not trying to make it go away; you're learning that it won't kill you.

  3. Audit your close friendships. Pick three to four people you genuinely care about. For each one, identify their wound — what did they never get enough of? Then do one small, specific thing that addresses it: a text back, a hug, telling them you love them, actually remembering what they told you last time.

  4. When someone trauma-dumps, try naming the emotion. Replace "I'm so sorry that happened" with a specific reflection: "I bet that made you feel completely invisible" or "That sounds like a real betrayal." Then notice how the conversation changes.

  5. Find a moment of discomfort in a relationship and stay. The next time you want to pull back from a friend or partner because something got awkward, pause. Ask yourself: do I want this relationship to continue? If yes, be the one who stays in the discomfort. Say something. That's how trust gets built.

  6. Look for the emotion underneath the anger. When you feel irritated, frustrated, or rageful, sit with it long enough to ask: what's underneath this? Fear?
