Summary

TL;DR — Growing up autistic without a diagnosis means spending decades trying to reverse-engineer social rules everyone else seems to have been born knowing. Des's story shows what that process actually looks like from the inside — and what it costs.

Des (Desmephisto), a World of Warcraft streamer and charity fundraiser, didn't receive an autism diagnosis until age 29. This conversation traces what it was like to grow up noticing something was off, to mask and adapt without any framework for why, and to eventually find belonging, a marriage, and a sense of purpose — all while managing the very real cognitive and sensory demands of being autistic in a neurotypical world.

What Masking Actually Looks Like From the Inside

Long before Des had a word for what he was, he had a model: his uncle, an undiagnosed autistic man who was yelled at, shamed, and had love withheld based on his behaviors — rocking, reacting to sounds, holding a fork with his fist.

"Every time I saw him walking back and forth, I did everything I could to avoid displaying those behaviors."

So Des ran his own informal behavior-modification program on himself. If a spoon scraping a ceramic bowl was painful to his ears, he'd grin and bear it. When he noticed excitement looked like his uncle's excitement — the kind that got punished — he muted it entirely. Christmas became just Christmas by the time he was a young child.

This is masking: suppressing or replacing authentic autistic behavior to avoid social penalty. Des did it constantly, largely unconsciously, and at significant cost. What he describes is essentially self-administered ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis) — the same framework many autistic children are put through formally — driven not by a therapist but by watching what happened to someone he loved when he acted like himself.

The masking held for years. It eventually collapsed after college, during what's called autistic burnout: a period where all the coping mechanisms you've built up stop working. For Des, this meant driving became harder, OCD symptoms surfaced, anxiety spiked, and the scaffolding he'd spent decades quietly constructing came down all at once. That collapse is what finally led to his diagnosis.

The Experience Nobody Talks About: Meltdowns and Overload

An autistic meltdown is not a tantrum. Des describes it as the brain going straight into fight-or-flight with no channel for the energy — a state where cognitive function basically shuts off.

"Everything ceases to function. I can't think, I can't function. It's like everything is at its absolute worst and you can't really process anything going on."

During meltdowns, Des has hit himself in the head. He's shared video of this publicly, knowing it could affect future employment, because he's seen what happens when people finally recognize themselves in someone else's experience.

The aftermath of a meltdown — or the alternative when he catches it in time — is a shutdown: a heavy, muted, depressive state that can last several hours and often ends in exhaustion.

Managing this requires catching the warning signs before the threshold is crossed. Des uses an analogy worth keeping:

"It's like we have a water balloon. Normal individuals have valves towards the bottom — it takes a lot for it to fill enough to burst. For those of us with autism, we have a much smaller balloon and the valves are at the very top. It fills up at a much more rapid rate, and it takes longer for water to come back out."

Workplace environments — open offices, ringing phones, mouse-clicking coworkers, no way to step away when you're nearing capacity — are structurally hostile to this reality. Streaming gave Des a setup where he controls his own off switch.

Learning Social Rules the Hard Way

Most people learn social interaction implicitly, by feel. Des had to do it explicitly, by observation and experiment.

At 21, he sat in bars alone — no phone — and just watched. He studied body language, topic transitions, how people held conversations. Then he built scripts. He noticed when someone got bored or annoyed and reverse-engineered what had gone wrong, tweaked one variable, and tried again.

"It's like mimicking the behaviors — you have to put all this thought into, okay, I put my arm here, this indicates this behavior. All those components into being able to start trying to develop friendships."

This process is exhausting, and it produced an unexpected side effect: Des spent years worried he was being manipulative. If every social interaction is calculated — if you're consciously engineering responses — doesn't that make you a bad person?

The answer is no. Calculation without malicious intent is not manipulation. It's adaptation. Plenty of people who act on instinct are manipulative. Plenty of people who think carefully about their words are not. Des's method was born from necessity, not predation — but the anxiety about it was real and worth naming.

Eventually the observation paid off in a friendship, which led to an introduction, which led to his wife.

Relationships: What Actually Makes Them Work

Des is honest: some of how his marriage came together was luck. But what's kept it together for eight years — through a belated autism diagnosis, through points where divorce seemed possible — is something more concrete.

"Communication is so incredibly important. If the other person doesn't know where you're at, they can't work with you."

What stands out about how Des describes his relationship is that the same hyper-awareness that made social life so exhausting turned out to be an asset. Because he'd spent years cataloguing every emotional signal and interpersonal variable, he was quick to name problems early — before they compounded. He didn't stew; he said things. His wife didn't know he was autistic when they married, but they'd built a communication pattern that could survive the revelation.

Being unable to work full-time, having sensory needs, having meltdowns — none of that disqualified him from a lasting relationship. What mattered was being on the same page, repeatedly, over time.

The Relief of a Diagnosis — and What Comes After

When Des finally got his autism diagnosis at 29, the dominant feeling was relief. Not because anything changed externally, but because there was finally a reason.

For years he had felt like everyone else was watching the same movie with the volume on, and he couldn't figure out what he was missing or why things that seemed effortless for others were so hard for him. A diagnosis doesn't fix any of that, but it reframes it: the difficulty isn't a moral failing, a low charisma stat, or a character defect. It's a different neurological architecture.

What the diagnosis also gave him was community — other autistic people who recognized his experiences, which meant no longer feeling like a broken outlier. That sense of belonging turned out to be as valuable as the clinical explanation.

One frustration: the mental health system has limited investment in diagnosing autistic adults. Des encountered the attitude that resources should go to autistic children, that adults were somehow less worth diagnosing. The practical result is that once you age out of childhood, the support network largely evaporates — one of the reasons he started doing charity work focused on autistic adults.

The Problem With ABA — and With Fixing People in General

ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis) is a therapeutic approach that replaces "undesirable" behaviors with "desirable" ones through reinforcement. It's widely used with autistic children.

Des's objection isn't abstract. ABA's logic is: autistic behavior is the problem, and we should condition it away. But many autistic behaviors are responses to genuine sensory pain or overload. If a certain sound causes real distress and someone grabs a professor's cup that was touched on the rim, ABA would treat that as a behavioral problem to extinguish — through repeated exposure to the trigger — rather than asking whether the environment could simply be adjusted.

"It puts the burden on the autistic to suffer, rather than creating a situation where no one is suffering."

The broader point applies beyond ABA: when we decide someone lacks autonomy because of a diagnosis or disability, we tend to do things to them rather than with them. That's how well-intentioned treatment becomes iatrogenic — causing the harm it was meant to prevent.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming autism means not wanting social connection. Des wanted friends desperately. The difficulty was the how, not the desire. Many autistic people are social; the circuitry for reading implicit signals is different, not absent.
  • Treating masking as success. Masking is effortful, cumulative, and eventually breaks down. Des's burnout after college is a textbook example: he'd maintained the mask so long that when conditions changed, all the coping mechanisms collapsed at once.
  • Conflating calculated social behavior with manipulation. Thinking carefully about how to communicate is not the same as trying to exploit someone. The anxiety Des felt about this was real, but the premise was wrong.
  • Thinking certain circumstances make relationships impossible. Unable to hold a full-time job, autistic, coming from a difficult background — none of this precluded a successful long-term relationship. The requirements for relationships are not the ones society advertises.
  • Assuming the expert knows more about your experience than you do. Des had to fight for his own diagnosis and for agency over his own medication. The knowledge a clinician has about a condition in general is different from knowledge about what it's like to be you. Both matter.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you suspect you might be autistic (or ADHD), take that feeling seriously. The clearest signal is: things seem so much harder for me than they appear to be for everyone else, and I don't know why. That's worth exploring with a professional — even if you're an adult.

  2. Learn to catch the warning signs before a meltdown or breakdown, not after. Build a personal list of early signals that your "balloon" is filling up — irritability, difficulty focusing, sensory sensitivity rising. Practice stepping away at that point, not after you've hit the wall.

  3. If you're autistic and struggle with social interaction, consider the observation method. Watch how people interact in low-stakes settings before trying to join. Note what topics come up, how transitions happen, what body language accompanies interest vs. boredom. Build from observation rather than throwing yourself into situations blind.

  4. In any close relationship, communicate your limits and your location explicitly. Don't assume your partner knows where you are emotionally. Say it. Des's marriage survived its hardest stretch because they named things before those things became crises.

  5. Try bellows breath as a tool for downregulating before you hit overload. Sit comfortably, close your eyes, and do 15–20 forceful exhalations through the nose, letting inhalation happen passively. After the final breath, let your breathing slow naturally — don't force it. Sit in the stillness for 60 seconds. The goal is to drop CO2 levels enough that your parasympathetic system kicks in. Practice this when you're calm first; don't debut it in a crisis.

  6. If you are a clinician, parent, or support person, ask before acting. The default move of overriding someone's expressed preferences "for their own good" tends to corrode trust and strip autonomy. Accommodating a need (moving the cup) is almost always better than conditioning someone to endure the pain of having that need.
