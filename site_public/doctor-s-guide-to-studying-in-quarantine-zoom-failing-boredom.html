Remote school hit students hard. GPAs dropped, mental health deteriorated, and the number of people quietly falling apart behind their webcams was staggering. If that sounds familiar, the first thing to understand is that the struggle is structural, not personal. Once you accept that, you can start making practical changes that actually help.

The System Was Designed to Trap Your Attention, Not Earn It

Traditional school is not an activity — it's a place. The word "school" itself refers to a physical location. The entire model is built on removing you from anything interesting for eight hours so that the only thing left to do is listen. That's not education; that's environmental coercion.

When that location disappears — when you're suddenly expected to absorb a boring lecture from your bedroom, two feet from your gaming setup, with no social pressure to look engaged — the system breaks. It was never robust enough to survive that. So when Zoom school started failing students, it wasn't because the students suddenly got lazy or dumb. It's because the scaffolding that held the whole thing up (a physical classroom with nowhere else to look) was gone.

"It is my job as a professor to restrain your attention. If I am not engaging or interesting enough, then you shouldn't be listening to me anyway."

A learning environment that depends entirely on students having nowhere better to be is, by definition, a poorly designed one. Recognizing this matters because it shifts the blame off you and onto the actual problem.

Stop Kicking Yourself While You're Down

Knowing you can do better but consistently not doing it is one of the most frustrating feelings in school. The natural response is self-criticism: Why am I so stupid? Why can't I just do it?

That response makes things worse, not better. Life knocks you down — a pandemic, a hard semester, a system working against you — and then your own mind steps in to kick you while you're already on the ground. It produces shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, avoidance, and more gaming.

What you actually need from yourself right now is support. You're playing the game on hard mode. You don't get to beat yourself up for struggling on hard mode.

"Be compassionate towards yourself. Help yourself up instead of kicking yourself while you're down."

This isn't soft advice. It's practical. Shame spirals eat the energy you need for actual work.

Build a Physical Anchor for Studying

Since the school environment is gone, you have to rebuild some version of it yourself. The core principle: your brain associates spaces with behaviors. A bedroom is for sleeping. A couch is for relaxing. When everything happens in one undifferentiated spot, your brain never gets the signal that it's time to work.

The fix is to create a dedicated workspace — even a minimal one:

  • If you have two desks, use one exclusively for work and one for everything else.
  • If you only have one desk, physically move your chair or shift your setup when switching between work and leisure. The gesture matters.
  • If you have a desktop PC, unplug it and move it to a different corner for study sessions, then move it back for gaming. Yes, this is slightly annoying. That friction is the point.
  • A separate user account on the same computer can work as a lighter version of the same idea.

The goal is that when you sit in the work spot, your brain already knows what's coming. Over time, the environment primes the behavior.

Scent is an underrated tool here. Smell is one of the most direct routes to memory and state. If you light a specific incense — Himalayan sage, whatever you actually like — only when you're studying, and never at any other time, your brain starts to associate that smell with focused work. Eventually, lighting it becomes a reliable on-ramp into a study mindset.

The Real Problem: Avoidance and Self-Doubt

One piece of this goes deeper than study habits.

"When I try to do homework or listen, I get doubts in myself and I'm playing games instead of facing things and dealing with them."

That framing is important. The games aren't the problem — they're the escape from something uncomfortable. The doubts, the fears, whatever is sitting unexamined underneath — those are driving the avoidance. Until you look at that stuff directly, it will keep pulling you away from work.

One surprisingly effective exercise: sit in front of a blank wall and do nothing. No phone, no music, no input. Just sit. It sounds trivial and turns out to be genuinely difficult, because there's nothing left to distract you from your own thoughts. That's exactly the point. Facing the discomfort directly — rather than endlessly routing around it with games — is how it loses its grip.

Therapy or coaching can help accelerate this. Outcomes Are Not in Your Control — Effort Is

Almost never submitting projects. Almost never getting a good score. These outcomes feel like the problem, but they're downstream of everything else. You cannot directly control your grade. You can only control whether you study.

"You can't ever get a good score on a test. The most you can do is study."

Fix the environment, deal with the avoidance, stop the self-punishment, and the grades take care of themselves. Trying to attack the outcome directly — obsessing over scores, catastrophizing about your GPA — just adds pressure that makes the avoidance worse.

Common Pitfalls

  • Blaming yourself for a system failure. The pandemic broke a model that was already fragile. Treating your struggle as a character flaw keeps you stuck in shame instead of solving the actual problem.
  • Thinking a "workspace" requires a whole separate room. It doesn't. A folding table, a shifted chair, a different corner — the signal your brain needs is much smaller than you think.
  • Using scent or other anchors inconsistently. If you light your study candle while gaming, you erase the association. The power of environmental anchoring comes entirely from consistency.
  • Trying to fix grades without fixing what causes them. Grades are an output. Studying is the input. Focusing on grades without changing your environment, self-talk, or avoidance habits is like staring at a thermometer to make the room warmer.
  • Treating gaming as the enemy. Gaming is an escape from something, not the root cause. If you take away the escape without addressing what you're escaping from, something else will fill the gap.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Designate a study-only zone. Pick a specific spot — a desk, a corner, a folding table — and commit to using it only for schoolwork. Never game there, never scroll there.
  2. Pick a study scent and use it exclusively. Buy a stick of incense or a candle you actually like. Light it only when you sit down to study. Never light it at any other time. Give it a few weeks to build the association.
  3. Physically separate work and play devices or setups. Move your laptop, shift your chair, or create a separate user account — whatever creates a clear, felt distinction between "work mode" and "off mode."
  4. Try the blank-wall exercise. Set a timer for 10–15 minutes. Sit in front of a blank wall with no devices and no input. Don't try to solve anything — just sit. Notice what comes up. Do this regularly if you want to start surfacing what you're actually avoiding.
  5. Write down one thing you're dreading about school and why. Don't try to fix it immediately. Just get it on paper. Avoidance thrives in the unexamined. Naming the thing is the first step to dealing with it.
  6. Reframe your daily goal from outcomes to inputs. Instead of "I need to get a good grade," make the goal "I will study for 45 minutes today." Focus entirely on the input. Let the output follow.
  7. If the self-doubt feels bigger than study habits, get support. A therapist if there's clinical anxiety or depression involved; a coach if it's more about patterns of avoidance and self-belief. Either way, don't try to logic your way through it alone.
