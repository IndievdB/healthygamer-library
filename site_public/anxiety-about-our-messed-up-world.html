Summary

TL;DR — The world-anxiety you're feeling isn't a character flaw or a meditation failure. It's your stress physiology being hijacked by a media ecosystem designed to keep it switched on — and there are three concrete ways to fight back.

Panic attacks triggered by news half a world away, chest tightness with no obvious cause, dread that breathwork can't touch — these are increasingly common. The reason isn't that you're weak or insufficiently mindful. It's that your body's stress system was built for brief, local threats, and modern information technology is running that system around the clock.

Your Stress System Was Never Built for This

The body's stress response — the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenocortical axis — was designed as a short-term survival tool. A predator appears, adrenaline floods your system within seconds, cortisol follows within minutes and stays elevated for roughly 24 hours, and your entire physiology pivots toward immediate survival: muscles break down for quick energy, digestion slows, focus narrows.

The key word is short-term. After the threat passed, the system was meant to quiet down. For most of human history, that's exactly what happened.

What technology does is prevent the system from ever quieting. Every news cycle introduces a new emotionally activating story. Ukraine. Roe v. Wade. Inflation. Climate. The editorial logic behind this isn't malicious in a conspiratorial sense — it's just business: emotionally engaging content gets clicks. The result is that your stress system gets activated Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and every day after, with no recovery window in between. Run that pattern long enough and you're moving physiologically in the direction of a panic attack — not because of any single event, but because your body is simply exhausted and overstimulated.

Panic attacks themselves are largely physiologic, not mental. People with panic disorder often experience them with no trigger at all. What chronic stress exposure does is push your baseline closer to the threshold where the body fires that response on its own.

The Information Diet Problem

Physical diet is a useful analogy. Eat junk food every day and your body degrades — lower energy, worse mood, poorer resilience. The same logic applies to what you consume mentally.

"Being well-adjusted to a sick society does not make you healthy." — Krishnamurti

The internet isn't just one source of stress input anymore — it's inside your phone, your TV, your laptop, your tablet, and increasingly your watch and your refrigerator. It has colonized every idle moment. And the platforms filling those moments are in an arms race to activate your stress system, because activation equals engagement.

This matters especially because of how the brain handles anxiety. Anxiety is essentially excess mental energy looking for an outlet. The brain generates anxiety to prompt action — fix the problem, discharge the energy. But global problems (war, climate change, political collapse) are not solvable by you individually. So the brain generates anxiety, finds no available action, generates more anxiety, and loops. Consuming more news feeds more energy into that loop. Meditation can help discharge some of it, but if the inputs remain constant, meditation alone will never be enough.

The Krishnamurti quote is worth sitting with. Adapting to a worsening environment isn't the same as being healthy. You can successfully cope and still be suffering.

Three Things You Can Actually Do

1. Control Your Information Inputs

Go on an information diet — not by pretending the world's problems don't exist, but by treating news consumption the way you'd treat time in a toxic household. The less time you spend there, the better you feel.

Practical steps: - Don't carry your phone room to room inside your home. Portability made sense for being out in the world. Inside the house, leaving it in one place forces intentionality. - Designate specific times for internet use rather than letting it fill every empty moment. The empty spaces of your life are not dead time to be filled — they're recovery time. Protect them. - Don't let the internet invade the quiet. If something is genuinely world-historical, you will hear about it without actively monitoring the news. The guilt some people feel about "not keeping up" is worth examining: it usually traces back to a value that was installed by someone else, not one you consciously chose.

On that guilt specifically — the feeling is valid (it reflects what you were taught) but that doesn't make it correct. There's a difference between a feeling being real and a feeling being accurate. Examining where the guilt came from is how you free yourself from it, not by suppressing it.

2. Target Your Stress Physiology Directly

Meditation helps, but not all meditation hits the same target. What you want are techniques that specifically act on the autonomic nervous system — the stress system itself.

The most evidence-backed option here is alternate nostril breathing (nadi shodhana pranayama), now also studied under the name "cardiac coherence breathing." The physiologically active ingredient is simple: lengthening the exhalation. An extended exhale activates the parasympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system and demonstrably reduces hyperactivation — it de-stresses you at the hardware level.

Qigong and pranayama more broadly share this mechanism. If you're already meditating but not getting relief, check whether your technique is actually targeting the autonomic system or just keeping you occupied.

3. Take Insufficient Action

This is the counterintuitive one, and probably the most important.

Anxiety's biological purpose is to push you toward action. When you take action, the energy discharges and the loop breaks. The problem is the thought that appears whenever you contemplate acting on a global problem: it won't be enough.

That thought is the reason young people don't vote. It's the reason people don't exercise when they're out of shape (one walk won't fix it). It's the reason people don't act on climate anxiety (planting a tree won't stop warming). The thought of insufficiency is what keeps the energy trapped and the anxiety spinning.

The reframe: start with insufficient actions anyway.

Worried about climate? Plant a seed. Pick up litter in a park. Worried about your community? Do one small thing. The action doesn't need to solve the problem. It needs to discharge the energy.

"No snowflake thinks it's responsible for the avalanche — because it's true."

Beyond the physiologic benefit, small consistent actions build something else over time: a relationship with your own integrity. I can't fix climate change, but I did my part. That's not resignation — it's a foundation for genuine equanimity rather than performed detachment.

Exercise works the same way. It doesn't fix the geopolitical problem you're anxious about. It doesn't need to. It discharges the energy that was keeping the loop alive.

Common Pitfalls

Meditating without changing inputs. Meditation is a decompression valve, not a filter. If you're still consuming anxiety-inducing content at the same rate, meditation will never fully compensate. Both sides of the equation matter.

Waiting for a sufficient action. The thought "this won't be enough" feels like reasonable cost-benefit analysis. It isn't — it's the anxiety talking, and acting on it keeps you stuck. Insufficient actions are the starting point, not the failure condition.

Mistaking valid feelings for correct ones. Guilt about not reading the news, dread about events that don't directly affect you, empathic distress about suffering on the other side of the world — these feelings are real and make sense given how you were raised and how your brain is wired. That doesn't mean the behavior they're pushing you toward (more news consumption, more dread, more paralysis) is actually good for you.

Assuming panic attacks are purely psychological. If you're having panic attacks, get a clinical evaluation. Panic disorder is a physiological diagnosis, and chronic stress exposure can push someone toward it regardless of how mentally resilient they are.

Treating "spending less time online" as simple. The internet is engineered to be difficult to leave and is colonizing more and more of your physical environment. Treating reduced usage as a willpower problem misses that you're up against systems specifically designed to overcome your willpower. You need structural changes — different logins, phones left in rooms, designated browsing times — not just good intentions.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Leave your phone in one room of the house. Pick a spot — a counter, a desk — and keep it there when you're home. Don't carry it to the kitchen, bedroom, or bathroom. Notice how often you reach for it anyway.

  2. Schedule your internet time like a meeting. Block one or two windows per day for news and social media. Outside those windows, don't browse. This isn't permanent — it's an experiment. Run it for a week.

  3. Practice alternate nostril breathing (nadi shodhana) daily. Ten minutes is enough to start. The key mechanism is extending your exhalation longer than your inhalation. Search for a guided version if you haven't done it before. Do this in addition to whatever meditation you already practice, not instead of it.

  4. Identify one insufficient action tied to something you're anxious about. Climate? Pick up litter on your next walk. Politics? Register one other person to vote. Community? Hold the door, send the text, show up somewhere small. Do the action this week. Notice what happens to the energy of the anxiety afterward.

  5. Examine the guilt. If you feel guilty for stepping back from the news, write down: Where did I learn that I'm obligated to follow the news? Who taught me this? What did they say would happen if I didn't? You don't have to abandon the value — but make sure it's actually yours before you let it run your nervous system.

  6. If you're having panic attacks, see a clinician. The framework above is for managing chronic stress and anxiety. Panic disorder is a medical condition and warrants a proper evaluation — both to rule out physical causes and to get targeted treatment if needed.
