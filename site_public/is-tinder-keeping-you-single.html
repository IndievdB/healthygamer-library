Summary

TL;DR — Dating apps are built around what can be measured, not what actually makes relationships work. The features that fill a profile — height, income, profession — have little to do with whether a relationship succeeds. Communication and interpersonal skill do.

Most people treat dating like a job application: optimize the résumé, get the interview, win the offer. Dating apps reinforce this completely. They can only represent what fits in a profile, so by design they train you to believe that profile-worthy attributes — your job title, your height, your physique — are the variables that determine romantic success. They're not. The app's architecture has quietly become your mental model of attraction, and that's a problem worth taking seriously.

The Profile Is Not the Relationship

When dating apps were built, the designers didn't start by researching what leads to lasting relationships. They started by asking: what can we capture in a profile? Those are two very different questions, and almost nobody notices the gap between them.

The result is a system that lets you optimize endlessly for things that don't actually predict success. The person who finishes medical school expecting dates to suddenly flood in, only to find nothing has changed — that's what this looks like in practice. The boxes got checked. The relationship still didn't materialize. The confusion that follows often turns into resentment, which makes things worse.

Meanwhile, someone who looks terrible on paper — broke, no impressive credentials, maybe actively struggling — can be genuinely good at being with another person. That skill doesn't fit in a profile field. So the app never surfaces it, and the mismatch between what the app measures and what actually matters keeps producing confused, frustrated people on both ends.

"Checking the boxes has nothing to do with the actual interaction."

What the Research on Matching Actually Shows

To make this concrete: there's a parallel situation in therapy. Researchers have spent decades trying to figure out how to match clients with therapists based on measurable characteristics — ethnicity, gender, language, religion. The intuition is obvious: an Indian therapist will better understand an Indian client; a gay therapist will better understand a gay client.

The data doesn't support it. Large meta-analyses find that demographic matching between client and therapist does not reliably predict better outcomes. What does predict outcomes is the quality of the therapeutic relationship itself — how well the therapist adapts to the client's worldview, how strong the alliance is, how well they communicate. The measurable surface-level variables turn out to be largely irrelevant.

This is directly analogous to dating. You can filter endlessly on religion, diet, income, height — and there are happy couples and divorced couples at every combination of those filters. The demographic overlap doesn't determine the outcome. The relationship does.

"We tend to act based on what we can measure. But it's not clear that what we measure actually correlates with success."

One striking example: a long commute — specifically, over 45 minutes — is associated with a dramatically increased likelihood of divorce. Distance and logistics, a variable almost no dating app prominently features, may matter more than religion, income, or education. Yet nobody builds their filters around it.

Where Everyone's Energy Is Going (and Where It Should Go)

The dominant cultural advice for single people is work on yourself. Go to the gym. Get a better job. Learn to cook. Become more successful. All of this is framed as preparation before the interaction — and there's nothing wrong with self-improvement in itself. The problem is when all the investment stops the moment you're actually sitting across from someone.

Nobody practices the interaction. Nobody develops the skills that relationships actually run on:

  • Flirting — genuine, playful connection in the moment
  • Articulating what you want — clearly, without expecting the other person to guess
  • Listening — actually hearing what the other person is communicating
  • Expressing discomfort — before it festers into resentment
  • Setting and respecting boundaries — including knowing which ones are negotiable
  • Emotional availability — being present, but also being able to pull back when the other person needs you to regulate yourself

That last one trips people up. Wearing your emotions on your sleeve constantly, never modulating, can be just as distancing as being emotionally closed off. A relationship requires a degree of calibration — showing up fully while also being capable of composure when the situation calls for it.

A relationship doesn't fail because one person had the wrong height or income. It fails because two people couldn't talk about what was building up between them. A post about a girlfriend breaking up with someone because he "needs more than her" illustrates this perfectly: that feeling didn't appear the day she left. It had been there for a long time, quietly growing, because neither person knew how to surface it and work through it together.

The Dehumanizing Shortcut That Backfires

Some corners of dating culture offer a workaround to the anxiety of real interaction: stop treating the other person as fully human. If they're an object, their judgment can't hurt you. Your insecurities get bypassed rather than addressed.

This shows up in pickup-artist frameworks that treat women as systems to be gamed, and it shows up on the other side too, in communities that use dehumanizing language for men. The pattern is the same regardless of direction: reduce the other person to make the approach feel safer.

It sometimes works in the short term — you get through the initial interaction. But it poisons everything downstream. The only partner who stays in a relationship built on dehumanization is someone with serious insecurities of their own. And then you're in exactly the kind of relationship that quietly destroys both people.

The actual solution to interaction anxiety is not dehumanization. It's building genuine interpersonal skill — which requires practicing real interactions with real people.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing preparation with practice. Grinding at self-improvement is not the same as practicing how to be with another person. You can be in peak physical condition, professionally successful, and still have no idea how to flirt, listen, or navigate a disagreement.

Taking the app's framework as ground truth. When you see "must be 6 feet" on a hundred profiles, it starts to feel like a law of nature. It's not. It's an artifact of what a profile can display. The majority of married people don't meet height thresholds listed on dating apps.

Assuming the perfect-on-paper person will be perfect in reality. Shared religion, shared diet, matching income levels — none of these guarantee chemistry, communication, or longevity. You'll find divorced couples in every demographic combination.

Treating red flags as dealbreakers before the relationship has even started. Some of the strongest relationships form around working through something difficult together. That process — showing up for each other, making mistakes, committing anyway — is often what builds real closeness.

Expecting the app to do work it structurally cannot do. Dating apps will not publish internal research on whether their matching actually leads to successful relationships, because the honest answer might be that very little of it does. Treating the app as a precision tool for finding your ideal partner is likely to produce frustration. Treating it as one imperfect way to meet people, and then doing the real work in person, is more realistic.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit where your energy is going. Make a rough estimate: what percentage of your effort around dating goes into pre-interaction things (appearance, career, self-improvement) versus in-interaction things (conversation, flirting, emotional attunement)? If it's heavily weighted toward the former, rebalance.

  2. Practice the interaction directly. Have more real conversations with people — not to date them, just to practice being present with another human. Learn what it feels like to flirt lightly, to listen without planning your next sentence, to express mild discomfort without making it a confrontation.

  3. Loosen at least one filter you've treated as non-negotiable. Pick a profile criterion you always filter on — height, income, education, religion — and give a few people who don't meet it an actual conversation. Notice whether your real-time sense of connection aligns with what the profile suggested.

  4. Work on articulating what you feel, in small doses. Practice naming what you're experiencing in low-stakes situations. The ability to say "I've been feeling a little disconnected from you lately, can we talk?" is a learnable skill, not a personality trait — but it requires practice before it becomes natural in high-stakes moments.

  5. After a date that didn't work, ask the right question. Instead of "what was wrong with them?" or "what's wrong with me?", ask: "How was the communication? Did I listen? Did I share something real? Did I make the other person feel heard?" This shifts your feedback loop toward the variables that actually matter.

  6. Notice resentment early. Resentment toward a partner — or toward the dating pool generally — is a signal, not a verdict. When it shows up, treat it as a cue to practice articulating what you actually want, rather than letting it calcify into a worldview about why dating is impossible.
