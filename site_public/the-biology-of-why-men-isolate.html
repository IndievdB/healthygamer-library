Why Men Are So Starved for Compliments

Research going back to the 1980s and running through at least 2008 consistently finds the same ratio: women receive about 75% of all compliments, men about 25%. It's not just quantity — the type differs too.

  • About 75% of compliments women receive are about their appearance, and the majority of those come from other women.
  • About 70% of compliments men receive are about their performance.
  • In a workplace setting, performance-based compliments dominate for everyone: 68% for women, and a striking 98% for men.

The practical upshot: men move through social life almost never hearing that something about them — not what they did, just who they are or how they look — is appreciated. When it does happen, it lands like a highlight reel moment. That's not weakness or neediness; it's just math.

Men's relationship to social bonding under stress works the same way. What feels instinctive and restorative to someone with high estrogen — reaching out, opening up, seeking connection — feels unnatural, even actively wrong, to someone whose hormonal environment is pushing the opposite direction. Telling men to "just form bonds" without acknowledging that biology is like telling a depressed person to just stand up.

This matters for how we assign responsibility. When a significant portion of a population struggles with the same thing, that's a systems-level issue, not an individual failure. Fixing it requires understanding the system.

Understanding the Other Side (Without False Equivalencies)

The compliment data creates a tempting illusion for men: women receive far more attention than men do, so surely they have it easier socially. But quantity of attention and quality of that experience are not the same thing.

Consider the frequency of unwanted sexual attention women report from strangers: - 32% experience catcalls, whistles, or stares at least once a month; 31% experience it every few days or more - Over a third hear offensive sexual jokes or sexist remarks from strangers at least monthly - 36% report being the victim of unwanted touching once a month

If you're a man, try this thought experiment: picture someone you are not attracted to. Now imagine that person stroked you, stared at you, or made unwanted sexual comments — not once, but every few days to once a month, indefinitely. That frequency is what shapes the experience.

The point here isn't to rank suffering or declare one group's experience worse than the other's. The point is that we consistently misread other people's lives through the lens of our own biology. Men, starved for any attention at all, look at women's situation and think it sounds great. Women, for whom bonding is biologically natural, look at men's isolation and think they just need to try harder. Both reads miss the actual subjective experience on the other side.

How to Actually Give and Receive Compliments

Since men receive so few compliments and are rarely taught to give them, some practical retraining is genuinely useful.

Giving compliments:

  1. In professional settings, stick to performance. Compliment what someone did, not what they look like. This applies across genders; for men it's nearly universal (98% of workplace compliments to men are performance-based).
  2. For appearance, the safest approach is to reference a specific attribute they chose — a hairstyle, a piece of clothing, an accessory — and connect it to them personally. "You're really rocking that jacket" works. "You're hot" is categorically different.
  3. Complimenting possessions is a low-friction, low-stakes entry point, especially between men. Commenting on someone's car, setup, gear, or tools communicates appreciation without any romantic ambiguity.

Receiving compliments:

The instinct when complimented is often to deflect — to reverse it immediately back onto the other person, or to minimize it. Both moves reject the positive energy being offered.

Instead, appreciate it. Let it land. Acknowledge that it worked.

  • "Thank you, I appreciate that."
  • "Thanks, I actually spent a while picking this out."
  • "Thanks, man."

If you feel a little embarrassed, that's fine — it just means the compliment connected. That's the whole point.

  • "Men just need to try harder." This treats a biological and systemic reality as an individual discipline problem.   • "Women have it easier because they get more attention." This conflates quantity of attention with positive experience. The frequency of unwanted sexual attention fundamentally changes what "receiving attention" feels like.
  • Drawing a false equivalency between male lonelinessA painful emotional state in which you feel disconnected from other people — distinct from simply being physically alone.Full definition → and female harassment. These are separate phenomena. Acknowledging that men face a real biological barrier to bonding does not minimize or compare to the reality of sexual harassment. Both things are true simultaneously.
  • Parrying compliments. When you immediately deflect a compliment back to the person who gave it, you're rejecting their gesture. It feels like humility; it actually closes a door.
  • Assuming your own experience of the world is universal. The entire problem — in both directions — comes down to projecting your hormonal and social reality onto people whose biology is running a completely different operating system.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Give one non-performance compliment this week. Pick someone in your personal life (not work) and compliment something specific they chose — clothing, a possession, a hairstyle. Use the framing: "You're really [verb]-ing that [thing]."

  2. Next time you receive a compliment, sit with it for two seconds before responding. Then say thank you — genuinely, without immediately deflecting or returning it. Notice what that feels like.

  3. Run the frequency thought experiment. Whether you're a man dismissing women's experience of unwanted attention, or a woman dismissing men's difficulty bonding — spend five minutes actually imagining the other person's experience at the frequency it occurs. Not once. Repeatedly, over months.

  4. When you're stressed, notice the pull to isolate. You don't have to override it immediately. Just notice it as a biological signal rather than a fact about what you need. Then ask: is there one person I could contact, even briefly?

  5. Audit your compliment habits. For one week, track the compliments you give. Are they all performance-based? Are they only directed at one gender? Are you giving any at all? The data on what you actually do is more useful than assumptions about what you think you do.
