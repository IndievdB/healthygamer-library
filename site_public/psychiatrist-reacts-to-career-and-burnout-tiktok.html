Summary

TL;DR — Burnout isn't just a company problem or a personal problem; it's the collision of both, and the fastest path out runs through the individual, not the corporation.

This talk works through several career and burnout topics: why companies pass over internal candidates, what "quiet quitting" actually reveals about workplace culture, how to read cortisol-and-burnout claims with appropriate skepticism, and — most importantly — how people-pleasing drives burnout from the inside out. The through-line is that understanding the system you're operating in, and your own role in it, gives you far more leverage than waiting for the system to change.

The Real Math Behind Promotions

When a company decides whether to promote you internally, the calculation is rarely just "who deserves it." Promoting you creates two vacancies to fill and onboard — your old role and the new one — plus potential friction if colleagues suddenly report to someone they used to work alongside. An outside hire starts with a clean slate and no inherited dynamics.

This is also where the Peter Principle becomes relevant. The idea, roughly, is that companies promote people to the level of their incompetence — not out of malice, but as a structural outcome. Here's the logic:

  • If you're excellent at your job, you get promoted out of it.
  • If you're excellent at the next job, you get promoted out of that too.
  • This continues until you reach a level where you're merely mediocre.
  • Once you're mediocre, you stop getting promoted and stay there.

The end result is that the people who remain in most roles are those who couldn't distinguish themselves at that level — which means, paradoxically, most companies are staffed largely by people who are not ideally suited to their current position. It's not a personal failing; it's a selection mechanism built into how advancement works.

If you're gunning for an internal promotion, the most useful thing you can do is make the case for fit — why your skills map onto the new role's demands — rather than simply pointing to your track record in your current job. Good sales numbers don't automatically predict good management, and the people doing the hiring already know that.

The "Quiet Quitting" Label Is the Problem

"Since when did doing your actual job become quitting? That's not quitting, that's doing your job."

The phrase quiet quitting — meaning employees who stop going above and beyond but don't actually leave — is framed so badly it makes honest conversation almost impossible. No employee is going to raise their hand and say "yes, I'm quiet quitting" when the phrase sounds like a fireable offense. The label has effectively silenced the conversation it's supposed to open up.

And the framing itself is revealing: calling "doing your job" a form of quitting implies that going above and beyond has quietly become the baseline expectation. When extra effort is treated as the floor rather than the ceiling, of course you can't talk about it openly.

Here's why people actually pull back, and it has less to do with burnout than most people assume: it's Pavlovian. When extra effort consistently goes unrecognized — no raise, no promotion, no acknowledgment — people stop putting it in. That's not disengagement; that's a rational response to an absent feedback loop.

There's also a blind spot worth noting. In coaching, a common pattern shows up: people believe they're going above and beyond when some of what they're doing either (a) is actually part of their job description, or (b) isn't something the company particularly values. Extra effort aimed at the wrong target doesn't register as a contribution, no matter how genuinely it's given.

What Cortisol TikToks Get Wrong (and Why It Still Matters)

Short-form content on burnout symptoms — high cortisol causing anxiety, irritability, carb cravings — may not be wrong exactly, but it tends to collapse too many distinct things into one. A few distinctions worth keeping:

  • Anxiety exists on a wide spectrum. Mild, occasional anxiety is a normal part of human experience, not necessarily a sign of a dysregulated cortisol system.
  • It isn't automatically established that burnout and elevated cortisol are always linked just because the logic sounds plausible.
  • Sixty seconds is genuinely not enough space to accurately explain the relationship between cortisol, burnout, and anxiety.

That said, practitioners who work closely with people sometimes identify effective interventions before the science catches up. Meditation is the clearest example: people swore by it for decades before rigorous research validated what they were observing. Clinical experience and scientific evidence don't always travel at the same speed. Take these kinds of claims seriously enough to investigate, skeptically enough not to over-pathologize.

Burnout Is Half Company, Half You

"Burnout is the intersection of job plus personal."

This is the part that tends to make people uncomfortable, especially when it feels like the company is clearly at fault. And companies can absolutely be at fault — some are genuinely predatory and will exploit anyone who can't set limits. But the science, and a lot of coaching experience, points to a more complicated picture.

A consistent finding is that people who burn out at one company often burn out at the next one too. The environment changes; the pattern doesn't. That's usually a sign that something internal is also operating.

The most common internal driver is people-pleasing — specifically, the inability to say no even when the person asking you is perfectly willing to hear it. The scenario plays out like this:

  1. A manager (who may be perfectly reasonable) asks if anyone has bandwidth for an additional project.
  2. The people-pleaser, afraid of how "no" will land, says yes — regardless of actual capacity.
  3. This happens repeatedly.
  4. The manager, getting affirmative answers, has no reason to think there's a problem.
  5. The employee burns out.

From the manager's side, if they were genuinely offering a choice, they never knew the employee was overwhelmed. The breakdown wasn't exploitation — it was a communication failure driven by the employee's internal rules about what they're allowed to say.

People-pleasing doesn't stay in one compartment of life. If you're doing it at work, you're almost certainly doing it in friendships, family, and romantic relationships too. That makes it a personal pattern to address directly, not just a workplace problem to manage around.

None of this absolves companies of structural responsibility. But efficacy and fairness are different questions. Waiting for corporate culture to shift before addressing your own patterns means waiting a very long time.

Having the Actual Conversation

The actionable alternative to quiet quitting — or to burning out silently — is learning to talk about it directly. Here's a sample structure that tends to work:

"I really enjoy working here and I want to contribute well. One thing I genuinely struggle with is saying no when someone asks for help, even when my plate is already full. I end up taking on too much and it's starting to affect the quality of my work. Can you help me figure out what a healthy workload actually looks like?"

This isn't complaining. It's not accusing anyone of being predatory. It puts you in a position of asking for help with something you identified as a problem — which is very different from implying your manager is the problem. Most people, including most managers, want to help when approached this way. The difficulty is that almost nobody is taught how to have this kind of conversation, so instead they either stay silent or assign blame outward.

And yes — your manager may be a people-pleaser too. If they are, coming to them with a problem they can solve is exactly what they want.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming your track record earns a promotion automatically. Past performance in role A is weak evidence for future performance in role B. Make the case for fit, not just results.
  • Taking "quiet quitting" at face value. The framing smuggles in the assumption that doing your job is insufficient. It also makes the real conversation impossible to have.
  • Treating burnout as purely a company problem. This is emotionally satisfying and sometimes partially true, but it leaves you dependent on external change that may never come.
  • Pathologizing normal stress responses. Not every bout of anxiety or irritability signals a cortisol disorder or clinical burnout. Over-labeling can create its own anxiety spiral.
  • Going above and beyond in the wrong direction. Extra effort toward things the company doesn't actually value doesn't build goodwill — it just drains you.
  • Assuming your boss knows you're overwhelmed. People-pleasers signal "I'm fine" clearly and consistently. You cannot expect someone to read capacity they can't see.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit where your extra effort actually goes. List what you do beyond your job description this week. Ask honestly: does the company (or your manager) actually notice or value each item? Stop investing in things that register as invisible.

  2. Reframe the promotion case you'd make. Instead of listing accomplishments, write a one-paragraph argument for why your specific skills fit the responsibilities of the role you want. If you can't write that paragraph, that's information worth having.

  3. Notice where else you people-please. For one week, track the moments you say yes when you wanted to say no — at work, at home, with friends. Look for the pattern across contexts before concluding the problem is only your job.

  4. Practice the manager conversation. Write out a version of the dialogue above adapted to your situation. Role-play it with someone you trust. The goal is to ask for help with your pattern, not to make an accusation.

  5. Before assuming bad faith, ask where the expectation comes from. If you're working late, identify whether that's temporary (learning curve), structural (the industry standard), or avoidable (no one asked for it and you're generating the expectation yourself). The answer changes what you do next.

  6. Apply the Pavlovian audit. When you feel the pull to go above and beyond, ask: what has going above and beyond actually returned in this specific environment? If the honest answer is nothing, that's useful data for how much discretionary energy to invest — and where to put it instead.
