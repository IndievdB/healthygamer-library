Suicidality Isn't Proof of a Broken Brain

There's a common assumption baked into how we talk about suicide: if someone wants to die, they must be mentally ill, and since mental illness impairs decision-making, no one who is suicidal can truly be of sound mind. That framing is worth questioning. About half of people who present as suicidal don't meet the criteria for a clinical diagnosis. They aren't necessarily cognitively impaired — they've looked at their lives, don't see a path forward, and want to stop. Dismissing this as a symptom of disease is deeply invalidating. It tells someone: "Your perception of your own life doesn't count because your brain is broken."

A more useful starting point is to treat their perspective as understandable, even if you don't think suicide is the right answer.

"If someone says I'm 35 years old, I've never been on a date, I'm going to be alone for the rest of my life, I want to kill myself — it's reasonable, I think, for them to feel that way. And also I don't think they should do that. And that's what you have to work on."

If someone you care about is still intent on dying after you've had those conversations, the moral obligation is to get them evaluated by a professional. Not because they're definitely impaired, but because you're not trained to assess that, and erring toward preserving life is the right call when you can't know for certain.

Why Material Success Doesn't Get You There

The reason so many people orient their lives around income, titles, and possessions isn't stupidity or shallowness — it's cultural programming that begins in childhood. The first question adults ask a 14-year-old is what they want to be, meaning what job. The milestones society celebrates — promotions, acceptances, salary jumps — are all professional and material. No one runs a ticker-tape parade for someone who built three close friendships this year.

This creates a logical, if flawed, formula: happiness is downstream of achievement. Check enough boxes and you'll arrive. The problem is what happens when you reach the end of the checklist. The Buddha story captures this exactly — a prince who had everything, who woke up unhappy with no remaining boxes to check. That moment of desperation, when external success has been exhausted, is actually the entry point for something more durable. True spirituality — meaning the pursuit of inner life — tends to be born not from privilege and ease but from that specific kind of exhausted desperation.

"Spiritual, true spirituality is born of abject desperation and terror. It's when nothing else in the world will work for you, you only have one option left."

You can't skip to that step from comfort. The ancient saying "you can't meditate on an empty stomach" exists for a reason — Maslow's hierarchy makes the same point. The conditions for deeper happiness need to be met before the pursuit of it becomes real.

How to Actually Support a Friend Who Is Struggling

Two common mistakes people make when a friend has mental health problems:

Underestimating their own impact. If you keep inviting a socially anxious friend to things and they keep declining, you might conclude your invitations don't matter. They probably matter enormously, even when nothing visible changes. Mental illness often trends downward — your consistent presence may be what's keeping someone level, even when it doesn't look like it's "working."

Trying to fix rather than befriend. A mental health professional has a different role than a friend. It's actually inappropriate for a therapist to show up at a patient's door. But a friend can do that, and sometimes should. If you know from experience that getting a depressed friend out of the house leads to a good time they'll later acknowledge — use that information. How to Catch Yourself Lying to Yourself

Eastern psychology describes the mind in three layers that are worth understanding practically:

  • ManasThe reactive, emotional layer of the mind — gut-level, visceral, and immediate.Full definition → — the reactive, emotional mind. It stamps everything immediately with like or dislike, often faster than you consciously register it.
  • BuddhiYour intellect and analytical capability — the part of the mind that does logic, reasoning, and objective thought.Full definition → — the analytical, logical mind.
  • Ahamkar — the ego, which sits between the two.

When you experience a negative emotion, the ego activates to protect you from feeling it fully. It then bullies the analytical mind into producing justifications rather than genuine analysis. The result is post-hoc rationalization: you've already decided based on emotion, and the logical mind is now constructing reasons after the fact.

The tell is comparison. Any time your reasoning involves comparing yourself favorably to others, the ego is present and your logic is compromised.

The practical implication: you can't trust your rational mind when your ego is active, and your ego becomes active in the presence of negative emotion. So the precondition for actually thinking straight about yourself is the ability to notice when you're feeling something — even mild annoyance, embarrassment, or threat — and name it.

"I would start by assuming that if you don't detect emotion, some emotion is there. Start by assuming you're not actually logical, because chances are you aren't."

Most people believe they're the exception — that they think critically about themselves. The emotionally numbed person who doesn't cry at movies often reads this as self-control. It's usually the opposite. Suppression looks like equanimity from the outside.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming suicidality equals mental illness. Starting from this premise invalidates the person's real concerns and closes off the most useful therapeutic avenue: understanding what they actually need.
  • Thinking diagnosis is the same as understanding. Labeling someone with a diagnosis can give the people around them a false sense that they now know what's wrong — and stop them from actually listening.
  • Pathologizing non-conformity. A mind that struggles in a classroom may be perfectly adaptive in different environments. "Disorder" is sometimes just a mismatch between a mind-type and a social structure.
  • Mistaking emotional numbness for emotional control. The absence of visible emotional reaction is not the same as regulation. It's often suppression, which makes self-understanding harder, not easier.
  • Quitting on a friend because you don't see results. The absence of visible improvement doesn't mean your presence isn't helping. With mental illness, maintaining someone's baseline is real and meaningful work.
  • Treating therapy as a magic fit that either exists or doesn't. The fit itself is something you work on with a therapist. If something isn't working, saying so in the session is the intervention — not quietly shopping for someone else.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. If you're supporting someone who is suicidal: Before assuming impairment, try to understand their specific reasons. Ask what they'd need to see change. Validate that their despair makes sense given their situation — then work on the actual problem underneath. If they remain intent on self-harm, help them access a professional evaluation, not as a way to override their choices, but because you're not trained to assess decision-making capacity.

  2. Test your actual self-control: Pick one arbitrary behavior — a food you eat out of habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition →, a late-night routine, a reflex response in arguments. Try removing it for 60 days with no substitution. How hard it is tells you more about what's actually driving your behavior than any amount of introspection.

  3. Start emotion detection practice: For one week, try to notice and name what you're feeling before you respond to anything that irritates, embarrasses, or challenges you. Don't try to change the feeling — just observe and label it. This is the foundational skill that makes genuine self-analysis possible.

  4. Watch for the comparison signal: Whenever you notice yourself comparing your situation, choices, or quality favorably to someone else in an argument or internal debate, treat it as a flag that ego is active. Pause. That's not your analytical mind speaking.

  5. Keep the invitations going: If someone you care about keeps saying no to social contact, keep asking anyway. Check in occasionally about whether they want you to keep asking — the meta-conversation itself is healthy. Don't let their non-responsiveness convince you to disengage.

  6. If you're shopping for a therapist: Make appointments with three different therapists in sequence, spaced about two weeks apart. Pick based on fit after the first session. And if you have a problem with a therapist — bring it to the session directly. That's not awkward; it's the work.

  7. Audit your success metrics: Write down what "progress in life" looks like to you over the next ten years. Notice how much of it is material versus relational. Ask whether the relational goals — quality of friendships, depth of relationships — were even on the list, and if not, why not.
