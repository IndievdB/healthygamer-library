Summary

TL;DR — Rationalization is a powerful coping tool, but it can quietly seal off emotions you haven't actually dealt with. Learning to notice what you're not feeling may matter more than analyzing what you are.

This conversation with HotshotGG (George, founder of the esports organization Counter Logic Gaming) covers a deceptively simple territory: what happens when someone who is genuinely self-aware, genuinely growth-oriented, and genuinely trying to help the world is still carrying a wound they haven't fully touched? George is articulate, reflective, and clearly on a real journey — but the conversation reveals a recurring pattern: he starts with a personal feeling, then rapidly extrapolates outward to the suffering of humanity, to systemic problems, to esports culture, to anything bigger than himself. That outward motion, while sincere, keeps him from sitting with what's actually his.

The core lessons here are about rationalization as a defense mechanism, buried emotion and its physical consequences, the difference between forgiving people and protecting yourself from the pain of what they did, and what a genuine apology — even an unnecessary one — can unlock.

When Your Best Coping Tool Becomes Your Blind Spot

George grew up applying game-theory logic to real life. When his father left, he rationalized it: Dad's just not a great person, I have my grandfather, I can move on. When he was hurt in esports, he rationalized: It's a corrupt industry, it's how everyone operates, either you survive or you fail. When describing his mission to fix the world's suffering, he rationalized outward from his own pain to a planetary diagnosis.

Rationalization isn't pathological. It's actually a high-functioning response — it lets you keep moving when other people would be stuck. But it has a cost:

"You're like you start with a feeling, you start with a personal experience, and then you extrapolate it — and by that extrapolation you move away from yourself and towards the outside world."

The pattern looks like this: 1. Something painful happens 2. You feel it briefly, or not at all 3. Logic is applied: here is why this makes sense, here is how the world works 4. You move outward — toward a cause, toward helping others, toward fixing systems 5. The original feeling is never fully processed

The trap is that this strategy works well enough that you never have a reason to question it. You remain functional. You make progress. You genuinely help people. So why would you ever look back?

What the Body Knows That the Mind Won't Say

George mentioned, almost as a joke, that he used to fake stomach aches to skip school and then actually developed IBS and acid reflux. This gets explored seriously — not as karma, but as physiology.

When emotion gets buried under rationalization, it doesn't disappear. The brain and the gut are deeply connected (there's an entire emerging field around gut-brain health), and serotonin — the neurochemical at the center of depression treatment — is most abundant in the gut. Children under psychological stress commonly develop stomach aches that are real in every physical sense even though their origin is emotional. The body carries what the mind won't acknowledge.

"When you bury emotion, it forces itself out through the body."

This isn't mysticism. It's the mechanism behind a whole class of conditions called functional diseases — conditions where the impairment is real but no clear structural cause can be identified. The body is expressing something the conscious mind has successfully suppressed.

The Problem with Premature Forgiveness

One of the more striking moments in the conversation is the distinction between genuine forgiveness and what might be called protective forgiveness — forgiving people not because you've actually processed what they did, but because holding them responsible would require you to feel the full weight of what happened.

George was betrayed badly inside CLG. A person with significant social capital ran a false narrative that millions of people believed, destroyed relationships, and sent George into over a year of isolation. His response, with time: "I've forgiven these people."

But when asked whether he ever felt hatred toward them, the answer was essentially no — and that's where things get interesting. Not because hatred is a virtue, but because its absence in this context is a signal.

"You can't let go of something unless you realize you're holding on to it."

The logic of premature forgiveness goes like this: If I see all people as complex, neither fully good nor fully bad, then what happened to me isn't a personal wound — it's just the nature of things. That worldview is philosophically defensible. But emotionally, it functions as armor. It means you never have to feel fully wronged, which means you never have to feel the depth of the pain, which means the pain stays exactly where it is, sealed under a rational framework, bleeding quietly every day.

"It blows my mind that you're not angry and hateful toward everything in the universe — and I get that you've done some work on that. But what did they do to you, man? And how is that fair?"

The "I'm Sorry" Moment

The most instructive moment in the whole conversation is simple and small. After a stretch where the conversation had become more clinical and analytical, George gently said he felt like he was being treated as a patient rather than as a person. He was asked: Should I apologize? His first answer was no.

He was then encouraged to really think about it. And then an apology was offered anyway — for judging, for not listening, for not walking the journey together rather than analyzing from above.

George's response: It was nice. We connected as human beings.

And then, immediately: I wouldn't have had hard feelings either way.

That last sentence is the tell. Because the apology clearly mattered — it landed, it created connection, it did something real. But rather than let it matter, the protective reflex kicked in: It's fine, no hard feelings. The armor went back up within seconds.

"You became an artificial Buddha and you protected yourself from the pain."

This is the live demonstration of the whole pattern. Someone acknowledges a wrong, you feel it briefly, and then you immediately rationalize away the significance of it — because if it doesn't matter, you don't have to feel hurt. And if you don't feel hurt, you don't have to want anything from that person, and if you don't want anything from them, they can never disappoint you again.

The insight that follows:

"If an unnecessary apology creates that kind of emotional response, maybe the apology is necessary. Maybe those people should have asked for your forgiveness. Maybe when other people wronged you, you should hold them responsible for it."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking intellectual self-awareness for emotional processing. George had thought deeply about his life. He could articulate his patterns, his history, his grandfather's influence, his blind spots at a conceptual level. But intellectual understanding of your emotional life is not the same as actually feeling it. You can know exactly why you rationalize and still be rationalizing.

Confusing altruism with healing. Wanting to help others who suffer is real and good. But when that desire is rooted in your own unprocessed pain — when fixing the world is how you avoid fixing yourself — it keeps you perpetually oriented outward. The mission is real, but it's also doing double duty as avoidance.

Treating forgiveness as a final destination. Forgiving someone before you've actually felt the full weight of what they did skips a step. Real forgiveness usually comes after anger, not instead of it.

Interpreting the absence of hatred as spiritual progress. If you've been genuinely wronged and you feel no hatred whatsoever, that's worth examining. Not because you should cultivate hatred, but because its complete absence may mean it's been buried, not transcended.

Functioning as a measure of okayness. Just because you can keep going doesn't mean the wound isn't there. High-functioning people can carry enormous pain for decades precisely because they're good at compensating for it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the full range of what you actually feel today. Not what you think you feel, or what makes sense to feel — what you actually feel. At some point during the day, stop and run through the emotional spectrum deliberately. Can you find irritation? Sadness? Resentment? Contempt? If certain emotions (especially anger or hatred) seem completely absent from your inner life, that's the starting point, not proof you've resolved them.

  2. Try the "I'm sorry" exercise. Pay attention the next time someone apologizes to you — even for something minor. Notice your first instinct. Is it to immediately minimize? "It's fine, no worries." If so, slow that down. Let the apology land. Notice what it does in your body before you respond. That gap between the apology and your response is where the real information is.

  3. Map where your outward focus begins. The next time you find yourself talking or thinking about the world's problems, the suffering of others, or systemic issues, pause and ask: What was I just feeling before I went here? Trace it back. The outward move often starts with a personal feeling that got redirected.

  4. Bring the hypothesis to your therapist. Not as a conclusion — as a question. Something like: Is there a possibility that I've been rationalizing my way out of emotions I haven't actually felt? And specifically, is there any anger or hatred in me that I've been filing under "just the nature of things"? Let that be explored over time rather than resolved in one session.

  5. Allow confusion to be a signal of progress, not failure. When you hit a moment of genuine confusion — where your previous framework doesn't quite account for something — resist the urge to immediately resolve it with an explanation. That confusion is the gap between old knowledge and new knowledge. Sit in it. The insight usually comes from staying there, not from thinking your way out quickly.
