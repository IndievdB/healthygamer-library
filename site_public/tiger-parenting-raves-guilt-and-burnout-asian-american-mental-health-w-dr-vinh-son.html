Summary

TL;DR — The pressures of tiger parenting don't disappear when Asian-American kids succeed; they go underground, shaping self-esteem, emotional range, and the unlikely communities people seek out for relief.

This conversation between two psychiatrists — one a child and adolescent fellow, the other a practicing clinician and parent — moves across several connected territories: what tiger parenting actually looks like from the inside, why shame works as a behavior-shaping tool and what it costs, why disproportionate numbers of high-achieving Asian-Americans show up at raves, how the four classic parenting styles map onto adult outcomes, and what it takes to parent a neurodivergent child without defaulting to reward-and-punishment mechanics.

What Tiger Parenting Actually Feels Like From the Inside

Tiger parenting, popularized after Amy Chua's Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother, sits on a spectrum. Even at the moderate end — all A's required, an instrument mandated, a single acceptable career path — the internal experience is markedly different from the external presentation.

On the outside: compliant, high-performing, doing everything right.
On the inside: never feeling good enough, low self-esteem, identity confusion, and a sense of living a script written before you were born.

The mechanism is simple and brutal: there is no positive reinforcement for meeting the standard. Getting an A produces relief, not celebration. The emotional operating system runs entirely between shame and relief. Praise doesn't exist as a tool because the baseline expectation is excellence — why reward someone for doing what they're supposed to do?

"If you get an A, that's to be expected. There's no praise, no positive. And then the comparison to others is a constant."

Comparisons to other kids — cousins, classmates, strangers who graduated as valedictorian at 16 — aren't delivered with malicious intent. They're offered as encouragement. But the effect is accumulative pressure: you are always behind someone else's benchmark, even when you're objectively succeeding.

Why Parents Don't Remember (And Why Kids Do)

A recurring puzzle in these conversations: a child carries a core memory — "you can be a doctor or you can work at McDonald's" — and the parent either doesn't remember it or denies it happened.

The explanation isn't necessarily gaslighting. A parent who has lived decades of war, immigration, financial struggle, and family upheaval has an enormous backlog of significant events. A single passing comment is easily buried. For a child with a relatively sparse archive of formative memories, that same comment can be the defining moment of a decade.

Denial, when it does happen, often connects to a norm about acceptable feelings. In collectivistic cultures that use shame to regulate behavior, acknowledging that you caused shame carries its own weight. It's easier to say "I never said that" than to sit with having caused harm.

The Collectivist Foundation Underneath It All

The tiger-parent pressure doesn't arise in a vacuum. It's rooted in a coherent value system that just happens to collide hard with the individualistic assumptions of Western culture.

Collectivism emphasizes: - Family over individual preference - Social harmony and obedience - Duty and self-sacrifice for the group

Individualism emphasizes: - Personal autonomy and self-expression - "Do what makes you happy" - Independence as a virtue

For first-generation immigrants who sacrificed an entire previous life to create opportunity, the child's success isn't just nice to have — it's the point. The retirement plan is a child who becomes a doctor. The 401(k) is the kid. That context reframes what looks like unreasonable pressure: it is pressure, but it emerges from an enormous and real sacrifice.

"You're standing on the shoulders of giants. They've overcome war-torn countries, communism — and then afforded you the opportunity to do well for them."

The flip side of this: Asian parents will often brag about their children to other people but struggle to say "I'm proud of you" directly to the child. The pride exists. The emotional language to deliver it doesn't.

Why High-Achieving Asian-Americans Overrepresent at Raves

Walk into a rave and the proportion of Asian attendees — nurses, doctors, finance professionals — is striking relative to the general population. The explanation isn't random.

The rave community operates under PLUR: Peace, Love, Unity, Respect. It's an ethos of unconditional acceptance — you are welcome here simply by showing up. That is the precise inverse of the conditional love structure many Asian-Americans were raised in (acceptance contingent on grades, career, and behavior).

When you've spent your whole life in an environment where love and belonging have to be earned, finding a space where belonging is simply granted is genuinely novel. The music, the lights, the crowd — these are real draws. But community is the core mechanism. Being seen, heard, and accepted without performing anything.

MDMA's role in this is pharmacological: it floods serotonin and dopamine, amplifies sensory experience, and — critically — dampens the fear response by reducing amygdala activity. For someone carrying chronic anxiety and emotional suppression, that temporary neurochemical shift is profound. (It's also why MDMA is under active research as a PTSD treatment.) The costs are real — bad trips, jaw clenching, dehydration, and a post-rave crash lasting several days as depleted neurotransmitters replenish — but for many people the community benefit outweighs them.

The mental health parallel is direct: Asian-Americans are the most underutilized demographic for formal psychiatric care. Stigma, cost, and family cultures that respond to distress with "just pray about it" all create barriers. Raves aren't a replacement for therapy, but they may be providing something real — specifically, the protective factor of genuine community — that reduces the functional burden of unaddressed mental health struggles.

The Four Parenting Styles and What They Produce

Diana Baumrind's framework from the 1960s identifies three original styles (a fourth is commonly added):

Style Warmth Involvement If the kid breaks a rule...
Permissive High Low "Try not to do it again"
Authoritarian (tiger) Low High "Because I said so. End of story."
Authoritative High High "Let's talk about why that rule exists"
Absent/Neglectful Low Low —

Tiger parenting is authoritarian: high control, low warmth, no questioning of authority. The outcome for kids raised this way isn't just compliance — it's a narrowed self. They often don't know what they actually want because wanting things that fall outside the approved script was never a real option.

Permissive parenting produces different deficits: kids may lack discipline structures, struggle to delay gratification, and face a steep learning curve when the real world requires self-regulation nobody taught them.

Authoritative parenting — involved and warm — is what the research consistently supports. It includes expectations and limits, but those limits come with explanations, reasoning, and genuine attunement to the child's inner life.

What Growing Up Tiger-Parented Does to Adults

The long-term costs are less visible than the short-term benefits. High-achieving adults from tiger-parent households often:

  • Can't identify what they want — they've been executing someone else's script for so long that their own preferences feel foreign or even threatening
  • Struggle with emotional vocabulary — alexithymia (difficulty identifying one's own feelings) is common; the emotional circuitry was never trained
  • Experience life as a willpower battle — when there's no internal motivation driving a path, every day requires forcing yourself through it. The trajectory is upward, but it belongs to someone else.
  • Are vulnerable to burnout — especially the "bright burnout" of high achievers who are succeeding by every external metric and feel nothing, or feel resentment

"If they feel like it's not their life they're living, but rather someone else's life, it has long-term ramifications. It leads to resentment toward your parents."

Emotions themselves become alien. All emotions are sources of information and motivation. Anger, in particular, is a compass — it points at what is missing, what is unacceptable, what actually matters to you. When you suppress anger (or any emotion), you lose navigational data. Life becomes purely reactive.

A Framework for Undoing Tiger-Parent Conditioning

The book Permission to Come Home by Jenny T. Wang offers a sequence worth working through:

  1. Permission to question — examine the values and expectations that were handed to you. Not to automatically discard them, but to ask: is this mine? Would I choose this? Would I pass this on to my own children, and why?
  2. Permission to feel — reconnect with the emotional experience that suppression blocked. Logic and feeling have likely been running on separate tracks.
  3. Permission to rage — anger is a compass. Use it to understand what in your upbringing made you feel violated, diminished, or unseen.
  4. Permission to say no, take space, choose, fail, play, grieve — each is a reclamation of autonomy that was systematically foreclosed
  5. Permission to come home — integration; making peace with your history without being defined or imprisoned by it

The most important early question isn't "was my upbringing bad?" It's: what is the cost of not examining it?

Common Pitfalls

Assuming your parents were malicious. Most tiger parents were using the only behavioral tools they had — tools passed down from their own parents, who used shame and punishment because they knew nothing else. The harm is real; the intent usually wasn't cruelty.

Thinking success means the damage was worth it. "I turned out fine" is the most common way people avoid examining the price they paid. Turning out successful and turning out whole are different things.

Mistaking emotional suppression for stoicism. Real stoicism is about not being controlled by emotion, not about pretending emotion doesn't exist. What gets called stoicism online is often just dissociation — and it destroys your internal compass.

Overindexing on reward and punishment as parenting tools. Incentivizing behavior without teaching understanding produces kids who require constant external motivation and develop a performance mindset. The behavior is induced, not grown.

Assuming neurodivergent kids just need more effort. Telling a kid with ADHD to focus harder, or a kid with sensory processing differences to just eat the food, assumes neurotypical hardware. It doesn't fix anything and generates enormous friction.

Waiting until you're a parent to examine your upbringing. The conditioning shows up in your relationships, your emotional vocabulary, your ability to feel satisfied, and your sense of self — long before you have children of your own.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your parenting style. Draw two axes: warmth (cold → warm) and involvement (hands-off → hands-on). Place your own parents. Where would you place yourself, or how you want to parent? Notice the gaps.

  2. Ask the propagation question. For any belief or practice from your upbringing — about success, emotions, relationships, money — ask: If I had kids, would I want to pass this on? Why or why not? The answer tells you whether you've actually chosen it or are just carrying it.

  3. Use anger as a compass. The next time you feel angry — at your parents, your job, a relationship — don't manage the anger away. Sit with it and ask: What does this anger tell me is missing or unacceptable in my life? Write it down.

  4. Practice the permission sequence. Take one week per "permission" from the framework above. Start with permission to question. Journal what you find. Don't rush to the next step.

  5. If you lose things constantly (ADHD or otherwise), practice scanning vs. spotting. When you can't find something, don't just open your eyes and wait to spot it. Systematically scan: top shelf left to right, second shelf left to right, and so on. Practice this in one room deliberately until it becomes a habit.

  6. Build habits with neurodivergent kids (or yourself) through repetition, not reward. For a sequence you want to automate — morning routine, bedtime routine — do it the same way, in the same order, every single time for at least a week. Be present for it. Don't incentivize it. The habit itself becomes the mechanism.

  7. If you have a neurodivergent child, ask "what don't you like about it?" before problem-solving. When they resist food, tasks, or social situations, ask whether the obstacle is flavor or texture, the concept or the execution, the feeling or the logic. The answer tells you which kind of adapter to build.

  8. Teach distraction resistance as a game. Give a child (or yourself) a task, then intentionally introduce a tempting distraction. Make not getting distracted the objective of the game. Level up the distractions gradually. The resistance itself becomes intrinsically rewarding.
