Summary

TL;DR — Accepting death as a certainty isn't morbid; it's one of the most practical tools for getting unstuck in life. This conversation ranges across mortality, perfectionism, toxic family dynamics, the creative grind, and the psychology of envy — pulling out concrete mental tricks that help some people move through life with unusual ease.

Stand-up comic Che, who spent seven years doing ten to twenty spots a week before the pandemic hit, turns out to be a surprisingly grounded thinker about mental health. The conversation moves from his use of death as a calming device, through lessons learned from a manipulative mother and a father who got Alzheimer's before he could enjoy retirement, to a surprisingly useful framework for turning procrastination into a trigger for action. The session closes with a short but dense breakdown of envy — what it actually is and how to dissolve it.

Death as a Grounding Device, Not a Horror

Most people hold a quiet, irrational belief that death might not apply to them — that technology, spirituality, or sheer luck will create an exception. Che's insight is simply to collapse that fiction.

"Everyone has died very successfully. Knowing that this experience is finite gives me a sense of peace and calm."

This isn't nihilism. It's closer to what the Tibetan Book of Living and Dying or any serious psychotherapeutic tradition calls coming to terms with impermanence. The practical payoff is real: when you genuinely absorb that the clock is running, two things happen simultaneously.

  1. AnxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → about outcomes shrinks. A lot of worry is about unknown futures. Knowing the final outcome — you die — actually resolves one massive unknown and makes the smaller ones feel less catastrophic.
  2. Procrastination loses its logic. Procrastination is, at its core, waiting for a better moment. Death makes clear that time is being spent whether or not you act. There is no hoarding time for later.

Research on this tracks: roughly 20–25% of people who struggle with procrastination have an underlying fear of death, and when that fear resolves, the procrastination often goes with it. The people most affected tend to be already capable — they can feel every year slipping past — but they haven't connected that feeling to the finitude that causes it.

Che's father is the clearest illustration. He worked two jobs, sacrificed his marriage, grinded through graveyard shifts, and deferred everything enjoyable to retirement. He got about four years of freedom before Alzheimer's — the non-genetic kind, likely exacerbated by chronic sleepSleep is the period during which the brain converts short-term memories stored in the hippocampus into stable long-term memories — a process called memory consolidation.Full definition → deprivation — took his capacity to live the life he'd been saving up for. Watching that happen in real time was what turned abstract mortality into something Che could actually feel and act on.

"Capture these things now a little bit more. Don't get caught up in the work-now-save-for-later lifestyle, because you might not get the later."

The Sister and the Twenty-Year Dream

Che's sister wanted to go to Greece at twenty. She's now turning forty. Every year brought a plausible reason not to go — honeymoon plans shifted, kids arrived, schedules filled. Che's response when she hedged again: he bought her a plane ticket.

The pattern she fell into is worth naming precisely. It isn't laziness. It's waiting for conditions to be perfect before acting — a kind of perfectionism dressed up as responsibility. The problem is that perfect conditions are a moving target. They generate endless pre-conditions, each of which generates its own pre-conditions.

The antidote isn't recklessness. It's the recognition that you learn more in the process of doing than in any amount of preparation for doing. Che learned more stand-up in a year of actually bombing at open mics than he would have in a year of interviewing comedians and reading about craft. The doing is the learning environment. Preparation outside the doing is a simulation of learning that rarely transfers.

This is why the ten-year Muay Thai procrastination is instructive. Once Che actually signed up, the scheduling problem — which had seemed like a genuine obstacle — resolved itself within a month. The obstacle existed primarily as a mental object, not a practical one.

Recognizing a Toxic Parent Without Being Told

Not all parents are good. Some are actively harmful. The difficulty is that children raised inside these dynamics are often the last to see it clearly, because toxic parents typically redirect responsibility onto the child — leaving the child perpetually trying to fix a problem that isn't theirs to fix.

A few signals worth watching for:

  • You feel like a chess piece. Someone else is positioning you, using you to achieve goals you weren't told about, sending you into situations where you're set up to fail and then blamed for failing.
  • Information is gatekept. You find out later that things you were told — about what other people said, wanted, or felt — weren't true. The parent controlled your relationships by controlling what you knew.
  • Leaving interactions feeling worse than when you arrived. This is a useful comparison tool: contrast how you feel after time with one person versus another. One makes you feel energized and equal; the other makes you feel guilty, diminished, or obligated.

Che's mother fabricated a message from a sick family member to prevent other siblings from visiting, deepening a division in the family for no clear reason other than to maintain control. That's the pattern — not dramatic abuse but quiet manipulation of information and relationship access.

What broke the spell wasn't a revelation or a confrontation. It was physical distance. Moving away gave him the space to build relationships outside her influence, which in turn gave him comparison points. This aligns with traumaTrauma is the experience of an overwhelming emotional event — one where there is simply too much emotion to process in a single moment, forcing the mind to handle the aftermath in waves.Full definition → research: complex PTSD — the kind that comes from chronic relational harm rather than a single event — requires safety and space as a first condition of healing. You cannot think your way out while still inside the system.

"When you have the relationships outside of that person you can compare the relationships. One makes you feel bad or less than — they implement negative emotions on you. When you can see those side by side, you can start to see that one is clearly better."

Cutting the relationship entirely is, for many people, genuinely the healthiest move. Quitting the wrong relationship often has a greater positive impact on your life than finding the right one. The cultural bias against quitting — treating loyalty as a virtue regardless of what you're loyal to — keeps a lot of people chained to dynamics that are slowly corroding them.

The Procrastination Flip

Most motivational advice says: push through the resistance. Feel the discomfort and do the thing anyway. Che does something structurally different.

His approach in practice: 1. He notices the procrastination thought — "maybe I'll just scroll a little before my walk." 2. He doesn't fight it by forcing himself to do the target behavior. 3. He does something tiny and adjacent instead — puts on flip-flops (not sneakers), walks out the door, just walks. 4. The walk warms up his body. Going to the gym after a walk becomes easy.

The key insight here is that procrastination is a trigger — an identifiable cognitive event that points toward a specific sink behavior. Once you catch it as a trigger rather than living inside it unconsciously, you have a choice. And the choice doesn't have to be the full target behavior. It just has to be anything except sinking into the avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →.

This is actually sound behavioral psychology. Trying to go from zero to full effort is hard because the activation energy is too high. But breaking the behavioral chain at the point of the procrastination trigger, and substituting a lower-resistance version of the desired behavior, preserves momentum without demanding willpower you may not have.

Additional tactics that reduce friction: - Make the environment easier. Che's gym is two blocks away. He brings no bag. He walks in and goes directly to the equipment. - Pair the activity with something you're actively looking forward to. A podcast you only let yourself listen to at the gym turns the gym into the place you get the thing you want. - Do the rewarding task last. He saves content creation — scrolling his phone — for the end of his dailies. That means checking his phone becomes something he earns, not something he escapes into. - Fake it until it's real. When the thought "I don't want to do this" appears, consciously replace it with "I love the gym, I love how I feel when I'm done." It sounds too simple, but it redirects the narrative before it can build momentum.

The Craft of Stand-Up as a Model for Any Skill

Che's writing process is worth understanding in detail, not because you're going to do stand-up, but because it's an unusually refined example of deliberate creative practice.

  • He begins every writing session by spending ten minutes watching videos that have made him laugh — seeding a mental state of genuine amusement before he tries to create.
  • He writes for one focused hour with his phone on airplane mode, walking around his apartment speaking out loud as if performing, embodying the joke physically.
  • He lets jokes go on ice — stops performing them — and returns months later with fresh eyes and no investment in a particular direction.
  • He separates tinkering from writing. The best feeling in stand-up, he says, is when a rough new ten minutes gradually congeals into a working set through iteration.

The therapist parallel he draws is sharp: a comedian who bombs and blames the audience for not getting it hasn't done the work of translation. Neither has a therapist who says something that lands badly and concludes the patient is "in denial." The job — in both cases — is to meet people where they are, not to demand they receive whatever you've decided to deliver.

Envy: What It Actually Is and How to Dissolve It

Envy = appreciation + ego.

You can't envy something you don't value. So envy has a positive core — genuine appreciation of something real. What corrupts it is the ego layer, which shows up the moment you make a comparison: this person has it, I don't.

The diagnostic shift: when you feel envy, your eyes are pointed at another person. When you feel inspiration, your eyes are pointed at yourself. Same underlying appreciation, completely different direction of attention.

Envy is also always person-centric. You can want a physique. You can't envy a physique. You envy the person who has it, because comparison requires two subjects.

And comparisons only happen around insecurities. If you're secure about something, you don't notice it in others in that charged way. Bullies figure this out instinctively — they try five different angles and watch what you react to. The thing you react to is the insecuritySelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition →. Over time, the ego develops precisely to protect those insecurities: "I'm not stupid, you're stupid." The defense is real, but it's downstream of the wound.

How to work with envy practically:

  1. Notice the envy. Don't suppress it.
  2. Ask: what do I actually appreciate here? Name the attribute, not the person.
  3. Ask: why am I comparing? What insecurity is this comparison defending?
  4. Separate the appreciation from the ego. The appreciation is real and useful. The ego is noise.
  5. Redirect: what is one small thing I can do to move toward what I genuinely appreciate?

The energy that goes into envy — all that ego cycling, all that comparison — is energy that doesn't go toward the thing you actually want. Remove the ego layer and the motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → that was powering the envy is suddenly available for movement instead of spin.

A connected point on pain and motivation: pain exists as a signal to change. In the past, psychic pain — shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →, social rejection, unfulfilled desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → — pushed people toward behavioral change. Now there are dozens of ways to relieve that pain without changing anything. You can numb shame, lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition →, envy, and emptiness without building or fixing anything. This is why so many people feel stuck: the signal is being silenced before it can point anywhere useful. The pain isn't the problem. The problem is short-circuiting the signal.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking "accept death" for nihilism. The point is not that nothing matters because you'll die. It's the opposite — that because you'll die, the time you're spending right now is the only time you have, which makes it matter enormously.

Thinking you have to do the whole thing. Che didn't go from "I've been avoiding Muay Thai for ten years" to "I'll go five days a week." He went once. Then figured out where it fit. People trip up by trying to solve the problem from the outside — designing the perfect schedule before starting — rather than starting small and letting the schedule emerge.

Confusing inspiration with envy. They feel similar because they share the same positive core. The difference is where your eyes are pointing. Inspiration is self-directed. Envy is other-directed and comparative. Getting this wrong means trying to "use envy as motivation" — which works in a polluting, exhausting way — rather than extracting the appreciation and letting the comparison drop.

Treating toxic relationships as fixable through effort. If you're in a dynamic where blame is always redirected onto you, working harder at fixing it is part of the trap. The system is designed to absorb your effort without changing. The exit is often the only real intervention available.

Waiting to feel ready. Readiness is almost always a feeling that follows action, not one that precedes it. You don't feel ready for the walk, then put on your shoes, then go. You put on your shoes, then walk out the door, then the readiness feeling arrives.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do the death meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →. Sit quietly and actually let yourself absorb the fact that you will die. Not as a concept — as a reality. Notice what shifts in how you're thinking about tomorrow, next year, the thing you've been putting off. Repeat whenever you notice yourself waiting for the "right moment."

  2. Name the twenty-year dream. What is the thing you've been planning to do for years that hasn't happened yet? Write it down. Write the actual first step — not the plan, just the first physical action. Do that action this week, regardless of conditions.

  3. Use the procrastination trigger. For one week, every time you notice the pull to avoid something — pick up your phone, open another tab, do something easier — treat that feeling as a signal to do a reduced version of the target behavior. Not the whole workout. Just put on your shoes. Not the whole essay. Just open the document.

  4. Audit one relationship for the chess-piece feeling. Think of a relationship (family, friend, colleague) that consistently leaves you feeling guilty, blamed, or set up to fail. Write down the last three interactions. In each one, who held the information? Who got blamed? Who decided what counted as success? The pattern will be visible on paper in a way it isn't in the moment.

  5. Dissect your next envy. The next time you feel envy, write three things: (a) what specific attribute you're appreciating, (b) what insecurity is driving the comparison, (c) one concrete action you could take toward what you actually want. Skip directly from (a) to (c) and see what happens.

  6. Build dailies, not a to-do list. Identify five things that, if done every day, would make you healthier and more functional. These are not goals — they're maintenance. Treat them like brushing your teeth. Do them in order before touching anything on the to-do list.

  7. Remove one barrier. Pick the habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → you're most inconsistent with and identify the smallest friction point preventing it. Is the gym far? Try the one that's closer even if it's less good. Is writing hard to start? Pre-load one topic the night before so you're never staring at a blank. Friction reduction is not laziness — it's behavioral engineering.
