Summary

TL;DR — Bipolar disorder medication isn't just for when you're sick; some of it is the reason you feel well. Stopping it when you feel fine is often the exact wrong move.

Bipolar disorder is widely misunderstood — by patients, and sometimes by the clinicians treating it. The most dangerous misconception isn't dramatic; it's quiet and logical-sounding: "I feel great now, so I don't need the medication anymore." Understanding why that reasoning can backfire requires understanding what bipolar medications actually do — and there are four distinct jobs, not one.

What Bipolar Disorder Actually Looks Like

Before getting into medication, it helps to be clear on what bipolar disorder is and isn't.

It is not day-to-day mood swings or emotional reactions — blowing up when a video game goes badly, for instance, is better described as emotional lability, not bipolar disorder.

Bipolar disorder is characterized by extended mood episodes — we're talking weeks to months — that then resolve and sometimes flip in the other direction:

  • Manic or hypomanic episodes: reduced need for sleep, elevated energy, irritability, grandiosity, racing thoughts, disorganized behavior. These can feel exhilarating, which is part of why they're hard to treat.
  • Depressive episodes: low energy, difficulty concentrating, hypersomnia or fatigue, anhedonia (the inability to enjoy things that normally feel pleasurable), negative self-attitude that feels refractory — meaning encouragement from others tends to "bounce off" rather than land.

Hypomania — a milder form of mania — is worth flagging specifically. It can function as an adaptive advantage: needing only four hours of sleep, feeling highly energized and focused. Students at elite universities are overrepresented among people with bipolar II or hypomanic tendencies, which is part of why many people are deeply reluctant to medicate it away. That reluctance is understandable. It also makes treating the full condition harder.

The Four Jobs of Bipolar Medication

This is the framework most patients — and unfortunately some providers — don't have. Bipolar medication isn't one thing doing one job. It does four distinct things:

  1. Acute treatment of mania — bringing someone down from a manic episode back to baseline
  2. Acute treatment of depression — lifting someone out of a depressive episode back to baseline
  3. Prevention of future manic episodes
  4. Prevention of future depressive episodes

Think of jobs 1 and 2 like taking ibuprofen for a fever — you're correcting an abnormal state. Jobs 3 and 4 are something different entirely: they're what keeps the abnormal states from coming back.

Some medications, like lithium, can check all four boxes. If lithium works for you and you tolerate it, staying on it long-term is a reasonable single-medication approach.

Other medications are better suited to specific roles. Antipsychotics like olanzapine are often started during acute mania — including cases where mania tips into psychosis — and work well for that acute phase. But olanzapine carries meaningful long-term risks (weight gain of around 20 lbs, worsened cardiovascular outcomes, risk of pre-diabetes), so it isn't always the ideal choice for maintenance. The problem is that medications started for acute episodes frequently just... stay on the list.

SSRIs work reasonably well for preventing depression, and in some cases for treating acute depression, but they take around eight weeks to kick in and — importantly — may increase the risk of triggering mania in some people. In the US, standard practice is to pair an SSRI with a mood stabilizer if you have a bipolar diagnosis. Lamotrigine (Lamictal) is another example: useful for preventing depression and managing emotional lability, but less effective for preventing or treating acute mania.

The takeaway isn't to memorize which drug does what. It's to understand that your prescription may contain medications serving different functions, and you should know which function each one is serving.

Why "I Feel Fine, I'll Stop" Is the Trap

This is the central problem. The logic feels airtight: I've been stable for a year, no hospitalizations, no lost jobs, healthy relationships — clearly I have this under control. Why keep taking something with side effects if I don't need it?

The answer: the reason you feel fine may be the medication itself.

"The key thing to remember about bipolar disorder is that some of the medication is responsible for you feeling normal. And what people want to do is they want to stop the medication when they're feeling normal — 'I'm feeling fine now, why do I need the medication?' That's why you feel fine — because you're getting the medication."

A useful parallel: someone who gets sober, feels great after six months, decides they no longer need AA meetings or therapy because they "don't even think about alcohol anymore" — and then relapses three months later. The support structure was part of what made the not-thinking-about-alcohol possible.

"If you're feeling too good today, don't stop the stuff that got you here. You don't go to the gym for two years, look at yourself in the mirror, and think 'I'm really fit — time to stop going to the gym because I've got it under control now.'"

Maintenance medication is not like antibiotics for an infection. It's ongoing infrastructure.

How Medication Lists Become a Mess

A common and frustrating pattern: someone is started on an antipsychotic during an acute hospitalization. They're discharged with a two-week prescription. It never gets reviewed. Two months later they're still on it. They come back reporting irritability and sleep trouble — symptoms that may actually be side effects of the original medication — and a new provider, not wanting to touch someone else's prescription, adds a mood stabilizer instead of removing the antipsychotic. This cycle repeats.

The result is patients on four, five, or six medications for bipolar disorder, where no single prescriber can clearly articulate why each one is there.

Fragmented care makes this worse. When coverage rotates between providers, each new clinician may be reluctant to discontinue something a previous one started — there's a perceived legal or liability risk in stopping a medication, even if continuing it no longer makes clinical sense.

If you don't know why you're taking a medication, that's a problem worth solving — not by stopping it on your own, but by getting a clear answer from whoever prescribed it.

Non-Medication Foundations

The single most important treatment for bipolar disorder isn't pharmacological — it's sleep.

Sleep deprivation is a known trigger for manic episodes, and it also destabilizes depression. Patients who come off medication and manage well tend to have more disciplined lifestyles overall — regular sleep schedules, structured routines, consistent self-monitoring.

This doesn't mean medication-free management is impossible. It means it usually requires more active effort, not less. People who go off medication expecting life to get easier often find the opposite.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing acute and maintenance treatment. Stopping a medication that was keeping you stable because you feel stable is like concluding the bridge is unnecessary because the river hasn't flooded.

  • Stopping everything at once out of frustration. When people are on five medications and feel bad, the temptation is to quit everything. Without knowing which medications are doing which jobs, this almost always leads back to acute episodes.

  • Assuming your therapist and psychiatrist are coordinating. They often aren't. If they're giving you contradictory advice, they should be in contact with each other. Therapists typically aren't trained in psychopharmacology; psychiatrists may not know the full picture of how you're functioning day-to-day. Both perspectives matter, and someone needs to synthesize them.

  • Treating "trust your doctor" as a substitute for understanding your treatment. Trust and clinical explanation aren't in competition. You're entitled to know why you're taking what you're taking.

  • Not questioning a growing medication list. More medications isn't always more treatment. Sometimes it means symptoms from one drug are being treated with another, compounding the problem.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. List every medication you're on and ask, for each one: what is this for? Specifically: Is it for acute mania, acute depression, prevention of mania, or prevention of depression? If you don't know, that's your first question for your prescriber.

  2. Ask your prescriber directly: "Can you walk me through the clinical reasoning for each of these medications? What would happen if we stopped this one?" Listen carefully. A thoughtful clinician should be able to answer this without defaulting to "because you have bipolar disorder."

  3. If your medication list has grown over time across multiple providers, consider requesting a full medication review with a single psychiatrist who can evaluate the whole picture — not add to it, but rationalize it.

  4. Before stopping any medication, distinguish whether it's an acute treatment (which may be reasonable to taper once the acute episode has resolved) or a maintenance medication (which requires much more careful consideration and shouldn't be stopped simply because you feel well).

  5. Prioritize sleep as a non-negotiable. Regardless of your medication status, treat sleep as a primary intervention. Track it, protect it, and treat disruptions as early warning signs worth taking seriously.

  6. If your therapist and psychiatrist are giving you different guidance, ask both of them: "Have you spoken with my other provider about this?" If they haven't, facilitate that conversation or ask for a coordinated care plan.

  7. If you're thinking about coming off maintenance medication, discuss with your prescriber what a very gradual taper might look like — and build in clear criteria for what would signal it's not working (sleep changes, energy shifts, mood instability). Don't make it a unilateral decision.
