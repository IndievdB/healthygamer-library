Summary

TL;DR — Avoiding appreciation isn't always low self-esteem; sometimes it's not knowing who you are because you've never existed outside of relationship to others. And staying silent about harm isn't always fear — it can be love.

This is a wide-ranging conversation that starts with impostor syndrome and quickly becomes something much deeper: a young woman working through a difficult breakup, a lifelong pattern of not knowing herself independently, and two experiences of sexual assault she has minimized for a long time. The conversation surfaces several ideas that go well beyond Yvonne's individual situation — about selfishness, regret, the physiology of freezing, and why victims protect their perpetrators.

Dodging Appreciation: What It Actually Means

There's a difference between low self-esteem and not knowing yourself. Yvonne describes instantly deflecting any compliment — her channel's success must be the people around her, her work ethic is just what anyone would do. The reflex is automatic: someone tries to hand her credit and she returns it before she's even registered it.

But when pressed about how she sees herself, the answer isn't "badly" — it's "I don't know."

That distinction matters. The dodge isn't necessarily "I think I'm worthless." It may be something more specific: you can't accept credit for a self you haven't fully met yet. If you've always existed in relation to other people — always duo-queuing, never solo-queuing — you genuinely don't have a stable individual benchmark to measure yourself against. The appreciation slides off because there's no anchor for it to stick to.

"You're duo-queuing for everything, so you never really know what your skill is. You hit a particular rank but how much of that is you being carried versus you doing the carrying?"

The Breakup and When Selfishness Is Okay

Yvonne ended a seven-year relationship with someone she describes as nearly perfect — supportive, funny, loved by her friends. The reason wasn't anything he did wrong. It was a persistent, growing awareness that she had never been alone. She had been in relationships since she was 16 and had no idea who she was outside of one.

She feels guilty. It feels selfish. It is selfish — and that's the point.

There's a reflexive assumption that selfishness is always unacceptable, and that something unfair must also be wrong. But relationships are frequently unfair, even healthy ones. The relevant question isn't is this fair to him? It's am I being honest with myself and with him?

"Anytime you break up with someone when the other person doesn't want to break up because you're not happy, that's selfish. And I think it's fine."

What's genuinely impressive — and rare — is accepting that you did something unfair without immediately constructing a story to make it fair. Many people work hard to find flaws in their partner, rationalize the exit, or manufacture justifications. Holding both things at once — he did everything right and I still had to leave — is harder and more honest.

The alternative she was avoiding was a life of quiet regret: not regret about him specifically, but regret about never knowing what she was like on her own. That's a different kind of cost — one that compounds invisibly over decades.

The Freeze Response: Why You Couldn't Say No

Yvonne shares two experiences of sexual assault. In both, she froze. She couldn't speak. She already tried pushing one man's hand away, and when he came back, she had nothing left.

People routinely say: you could have said no at any point. And technically, that's true. But it misses something important about what actually happens in the body.

There is a mammalian stress response called catatonia — a neurological lockup, not just a psychological hesitation. Some animals freeze and play dead when in danger. Human beings do something similar under acute threat. It is not a choice. It is not weakness. It is biology. The brain stops functioning the way it does in calm conditions, and the body locks up.

"People need to understand there's physiological evidence that when human beings are in danger, literally what they do is lock up. It's not a psychological lockup — it's a neurological lockup."

Blaming a victim for not speaking — or blaming yourself — assumes the calm-state toolkit is available in the threat state. It isn't.

One of the most important things Yvonne says is that she wasn't prepared. She didn't expect it from either person. That's where much of the vulnerability lives: we don't prepare people for the reality that someone who seems friendly, who is a friend, can cross a line. Because no one told them it was possible, they have no script for it when it happens.

Why Victims Protect Perpetrators: It's Not Always Fear

After the freeze, there's a second silence — the long-term silence of not naming names, not speaking publicly, minimizing what happened so thoroughly that it becomes hard to acknowledge even to yourself.

The common assumption is that this silence comes from fear. Fear of not being believed, fear of retaliation, fear of chaos. Fear is real and does play a role. But it doesn't fully account for the amount of suffering people willingly carry when they choose silence.

What emerges in this conversation is something different: love. Yvonne is protecting people she cares about. She is playing support, absorbing damage so the team doesn't fall apart. This isn't delusion — the people she loves would probably support her if she spoke. But she's making a sacrifice on their behalf that they haven't asked for and might not want.

"I wonder if it's actually fear that keeps your mouth shut. I'm beginning to realize it's not fear — I think what you're trying to do is preserve and protect."

This reframe matters enormously. If silence is only about fear, the solution is courage — just speak up. But if silence is also about love and protection, the conversation becomes much more complicated. You're not just asking someone to be brave. You're asking them to risk something they love.

The Regret Equation: When to Change and When to Stay

There's a genuinely hard philosophical question underneath all of this, one without a clean answer: when is giving into the fear of regret the right call, and when is it the wrong one?

Acting on the fear of regret can be wisdom — it's how Yvonne made a decision that was costly in the short term but true to who she is. It can also be destructive — the 45-year-old who blows up a family because he never got to ride a motorcycle across Asia.

The difference isn't always obvious in the moment. What seems to matter is whether the thing you're pursuing is reasonable and has been building over a long time rather than arriving on a whim, and whether staying means ongoing dishonesty — with yourself or your partner. That's not a formula. It's a judgment call that requires genuine self-knowledge, which is exactly what Yvonne was trying to develop.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating unfairness and wrongness as the same thing. A relationship ending can be genuinely unfair to one person and still be the right decision. Forcing false fairness usually means lying to yourself or your partner.

  • Assuming the "freeze" means consent or passivity. The freeze is a physiological response, not a decision. Judging someone for not saying no more forcefully misunderstands how the nervous system works under threat.

  • Assuming silence from victims is purely fear-based. That framing makes courage the only solution. When silence comes from love and protection, "just be brave" misses what's actually happening and why it's so hard.

  • Thinking you can know yourself from inside a relationship. If you've always defined yourself in relation to someone else, you don't have an independent baseline. The appreciation people offer you may not stick because you genuinely don't know what to attribute it to.

  • Confusing "not blaming yourself" with "having processed it." Yvonne doesn't blame herself for the assaults, which is healthy and rare. But minimizing what happened — actively working to suppress it — is a separate thing that can persist even when self-blame is absent.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Test whether you're deflecting or genuinely disagreeing. The next time someone compliments you, pause before responding. Ask yourself: do I actually think this is wrong, or does it just feel uncomfortable to accept? Discomfort isn't the same as inaccuracy.

  2. Solo-queue something. If you suspect you don't know who you are outside of other people's influence, find one area — a project, a hobby, a decision — where you set the terms entirely without input. Notice what you choose when no one else is watching.

  3. If you've experienced a freeze, explain it to yourself as biology first. Before analyzing what you "should have done," name what happened physically. Your nervous system locked up. That is a documented response to danger. Start there before doing any other work.

  4. Examine the logic behind your silences. If you're protecting someone by staying quiet, ask: have they actually asked you to make that sacrifice? Would they want you to? Sometimes the protection is a story we're telling ourselves rather than something the other person needs.

  5. When facing a major change you fear you'll regret, ask two questions: Has this feeling been building over a long time, or did it arrive suddenly? And does staying require ongoing dishonesty — with yourself or someone else? Those two factors don't resolve the decision, but they clarify what kind of regret you're actually weighing.

  6. After a hard conversation or emotional session, don't force processing. Give yourself time to return to your natural shape. Sunlight, water, an animal, something comforting — let your nervous system decompress before you try to make sense of what just happened.
