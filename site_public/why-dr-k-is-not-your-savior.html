Summary

TL;DR — You don't need a specific person to save you. The real source of your healing is you.

There's a seductive belief that can quietly take hold when you're struggling: that there's one specific person — one therapist, one teacher, one creator — who truly gets you, and without them you're stuck. This talk dismantles that belief directly, and replaces it with something more useful and more honest.

The "Only One Who Can Help Me" Trap

It feels real. When someone's words finally land after years of feeling misunderstood, the relief is intense, and it's easy to attach that relief to the person delivering it rather than to the process you're both participating in.

But consider what actually makes a conversation, a session, or a stream impactful. If the helper were the sole source of value, everything they did would land with equal force. That's not how it works. Some sessions are transformative; others fall flat — with the same helper, using the same skills, on the same platform. The variable isn't the helper. It's you: what you brought in, how ready you were, how honestly you engaged.

"I'm the constant at Healthy Gamer, but what makes something impactful is the person who comes on. The real value comes from you — you just think it comes from me because you always see my face."

The helper's face is visible. Your internal work is invisible. So you credit the face.

What a Group Member Learned in 16 Weeks

After 16 weeks in a coaching group — having entered convinced that no one else could possibly understand her — one participant came out with a different conclusion:

"I came to this group because I thought Dr. K was the only one that could help me. After 16 weeks, I realized I never needed him. The only person I needed was myself."

That's not a feel-good platitude. It's a description of something that actually happened through sustained effort. She didn't need a specific person. She needed herself to show up consistently, and a structure that gave her the space to do it.

This is also the best possible outcome of any good therapeutic relationship: you stop needing the therapist. Dependency on a helper is a phase, not a destination. If you leave therapy still convinced only that person can help you, something important didn't get transferred.

The Chef Analogy

Here's a useful reframe. Imagine a five-star chef whose cooking is genuinely extraordinary. Watching them is thrilling. Their food is delicious. But delicious food exists all over the place — in other restaurants, in home kitchens, in cities you've never visited. Why Therapists Succeed or Fail

No helper is equally effective with every person. What determines success isn't the helper's skill in isolation — it's the combination of that skill with what the person across from them brings.

"I fail too. And what causes me to fail or succeed depends on the person sitting across from me — because I can't fix them. They have to fix themselves. I'm just going to help them."

This is a real constraint, not false modesty. A therapist cannot do your internal work. They can ask the right questions, hold space, offer frameworks, reflect things back — but the actual movement happens inside you. That means the quality of what you put in determines the quality of what you get out, regardless of who is sitting across from you.

This cuts both ways. A less celebrated therapist who you work with honestly will often outperform a highly credentialed one you're waiting to be fixed by.

The Work Is Yours

The practical implication of all this is that "finding the right therapist" — while it matters somewhat — is far less important than most people treat it. The search for the perfect guide can become its own form of avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition →. You keep looking for the person who will make this easy, who will finally understand you so completely that the healing happens without much effort on your part.

It won't. That's not a pessimistic claim — it's a liberating one. Because if your growth depends primarily on you, then it's already within reach. You don't have to wait for a specific person to have an opening, or to go viral, or to notice you. You need to show up, honestly, to whatever support is available, and do the work.

"If you show up and give it your best effort with a therapist, I think you're going to do a good job."

That's the whole formula. Show up. Engage honestly. The specific person matters less than you think.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking resonance for uniqueness. When someone's framing clicks for you, it can feel like proof that only they understand you. More often, it means you're finally ready to receive that kind of help — and that readiness will travel with you to other helpers.

Waiting for the perfect fit before starting. Holding out for a specific guide, or endlessly vetting therapists without committing, keeps you in motion without getting anywhere. Imperfect engagement beats perfect inaction.

Crediting outcomes to the helper, not yourself. When a session or coaching group genuinely helps you, notice what you did: you showed up, you were honest, you sat with discomfort, you applied something. The helper provided a container. You did the work inside it.

Treating dependency as connection. Feeling like you desperately need a specific person can feel like a meaningful bond. It can also be a way of offloading responsibility. Real growth moves toward self-sufficiency, not deeper reliance on any single outside source.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your attribution. Think of a moment when therapy, coaching, or a conversation genuinely helped you. Write down specifically what you did in that interaction — how honestly you engaged, what you were willing to say, what you applied afterward. Notice how much of the outcome traces back to you.

  2. Start with what's available. If you've been waiting for a specific person or the "right" therapist before engaging with support, pick someone reasonably qualified and commit to at least six to eight honest sessions. Show up fully. Evaluate after that, not before.

  3. Track your consistency, not the helper's quality. For one month, keep a simple log: Did I show up? Did I engage honestly? Did I apply anything between sessions? This shifts your attention from rating the helper to measuring your own input.

  4. Notice the urge to seek a savior. The next time you feel convinced that a specific person is the only one who can help you, sit with that feeling for a moment before acting on it. Ask: is this about their unique ability, or about my readiness to receive help right now?

  5. Define what "getting better" looks like for you. A helper can reflect you back to yourself, but they can't set your destination. Writing down, in concrete terms, what improvement looks like in your life gives you something to move toward — and makes your progress visible to you, independent of who's guiding you.
