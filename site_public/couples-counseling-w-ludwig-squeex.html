Summary

TL;DR — "Actually" kills relationships, and truth matters far less than understanding. Two people in conflict need to get on the same page before they can move forward, and that requires listening to understand rather than listening to respond.

What starts as a lighthearted streaming collab turns into a genuinely revealing session on how relationship dynamics form, calcify, and can be shifted. Ludwig and Squeex are four days into a Dark Souls marathon when the real tensions surface: one partner feels like the perpetual "little brother," the other can't figure out why his attempts to help keep backfiring, and both are unconsciously cycling each other into worse and worse positions. The patterns they fall into — weaponizing "actually," trading resentment like a hot potato, engineering the environment instead of addressing feelings — are the same ones that quietly destroy real relationships over years.

Why "Actually" Is the Most Dangerous Word in Any Relationship

The moment one person says "actually" in a conflict, they've shifted the conversation from feelings to a courtroom. "Actually, here's what really happened." "Actually, you started it." "Actually, I was just trying to help."

The problem isn't that one person is right and the other is wrong. The problem is that even if you could prove it, winning the argument damages the relationship. If you bring in all the evidence, identify the exact person who cast the first stone, and get a verdict — one person is vindicated, and one person is humiliated. The relationship pays the price.

"You'd probably lose the relationship in the search for truth."

Both Ludwig and Squeex are certain they didn't start the negative cycle. Both are almost certainly right — from inside their own perspective. That's how cycles work. There is no first domino you can identify without the other person having a reason they did what they did before that.

The fix is not to stop noticing when something seems factually wrong. It's to catch yourself making an "actually" move out loud. If you do slip into an "actually" and then call it out — "Hey, I just did the actually thing, my bad" — you don't just neutralize the damage. You actually gain ground. It signals to the other person that you're genuinely working on the relationship, which is worth more than being right.

Truth Is Third on the List — Feelings Come First

This is counterintuitive enough that it's worth sitting with. When two people are in conflict, the instinct is to establish what actually happened, and then figure out how to fix it. That approach fails because the truth our minds perceive is more influenced by feeling than by fact.

What each person experienced is real to them. Their perception of events is shaped by their emotional state, their history, and what they were primed to notice. You can argue facts forever and never touch the actual problem, which is that one person feels dismissed and the other feels unappreciated.

"If you want to have a successful relationship, truth is like third or fourth on the list."

The goal in working through a conflict is not to arrive at the objectively correct account of events. It is to understand the other person's experience — including when that experience seems completely wrong to you. You don't have to agree with their perception. You just have to genuinely get it. That's what creates the conditions where both people can actually move.

The Cycle Nobody Thinks They Started

Ludwig puts Squeex down when he's frustrated. Squeex, who has been generous and patient, retaliates by being a little superior about his own competence. Ludwig feels more inferior, so he's more likely to take a jab next time. Squeex gets more fed up, so he's more likely to counter-stoke.

Neither of them is wrong about what the other person did. Both of them are wrong about who started it. This is the nature of cycles — they don't have a clean origin point because each behavior is a response to something that was itself a response.

The more important question is not who started it but what maintains it. What's maintaining this one is insecurity. Ludwig feels like the little brother — literally, the mod they're using makes him player two with a structurally inferior role. When Squeex tries to help from that position, it reinforces the dynamic rather than relieving it. "Big brother extending a hand down from on high" is still an asymmetric gesture, even when it's genuinely kind.

"Slap me in the face then give me a cookie. Like what are we doing here?"

When someone is deep in self-loathing, offers of help tend to sting. Not because the other person is doing it wrong, but because receiving help confirms the inferiority they're already feeling.

The Wallow/Positivity Trap

In many relationships, one person becomes the designated wallower and the other becomes the designated upbeat presence. This feels like balance — one person holds the sad, the other holds the happy — but it's actually a trap for both of them.

If Squeex is always positive, Ludwig can't join him there. The gap is too large. If Ludwig is always down, Squeex can't go there either without losing his role. The positions become rigid, then uncomfortable, then resentful.

The paradoxical solution is to let each person access both registers. If Squeex allowed himself to show frustration or self-doubt, Ludwig would likely step up and support him. That's not a bug — it's healthy. Relationships where one person always gives care and the other always receives it are not sustainable. The goal is oscillation: sometimes you're the one who's struggling, sometimes your partner is, and you trade those roles fluidly.

"If you're the one who's the caregiver, it's very important to become the care receiver."

There's even evidence that receiving help when you're struggling has a similar effect on depression as giving help to someone else. Human beings are wired to need both directions of that exchange.

Don't Fix It — Dump It in Their Lap

When someone is in the self-loathing seat and you try to fix their problem for them, you reinforce their position as the one who needs fixing. You become the competent one and they become the one who couldn't handle it.

The smarter move, especially when there's no good option, is to name your own helplessness honestly and hand the decision back to them.

"Ludwig, I don't know what to do. If I beat the boss, I do it without you. If I wipe myself, you feel infantilized. There's no way to win."

When you say "I don't know what to do" and genuinely mean it, you do a few things at once. You stop performing competence. You treat the other person as capable of solving the problem. And you implicitly invite them to become player one in that moment.

Watch for this: when the choice is handed back, the person who was wallowing often brightens. Not because the problem is solved, but because they've been trusted with it.

Engineering the Environment Instead of Addressing the Need

The SL1 challenge — playing the game at the lowest possible level — seems like a gaming decision. It's actually a psychological one.

Ludwig, who feels inferior at the current skill levels, is proposing a reset to an even playing field. If both players are stripped of their accumulated advantages, the person who learns faster wins. And who learns faster? Ludwig. He's not consciously strategizing this. His mind is doing it for him, hungry for a way to feel equal.

"We will engineer controlling behaviors to create what we psychologically want" instead of telling people what we actually need.

This pattern appears in every kind of relationship. Someone feels unattractive and, instead of saying "I need to feel like you still find me attractive," starts limiting their partner's contact with other people. Someone feels out of control at work and starts being controlling at home. The behavior looks like one thing and is actually another.

The fix is not to identify and suppress the behavior. It's to surface the underlying need and address it directly. Once Ludwig understood and could hear that Squeex genuinely respects his ability to learn fast and stick with something, the pressure behind the SL1 push started to deflate. The psychological hunger was being fed a different way.

Reflective Listening: The Simplest Hard Thing

The technique that shifts the whole conversation is simple to describe and genuinely difficult to do.

Ask a "what" question: "What makes you think I'm good at games?" Then — and this is the part that requires effort — just listen. Don't redirect. Don't add your own hypothesis as a question ("But what about Counter-Strike?"). Don't start planning your response while they're still talking.

Then reflect back what you actually heard. Not what you wanted to hear, not what confirms your own story. What they actually said.

"You learn games fast, faster than me. And you stick with a game for a long time."

That's the moment Ludwig hears something about himself he didn't know Squeex thought. Not because Squeex hadn't felt it — he had — but because Ludwig's self-loathing had been routing every conversation away from receiving it. The "what" question creates an opening that a direct compliment can't, because it puts the listener in a receiving posture rather than a defensive one.

When you don't understand something your partner says, don't argue it and don't ignore it. Ask a "what" question. "What makes you feel that way?" "What are you most worried about?" It sounds simple. In the middle of a conflict, it's one of the harder things you can do.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing vindication with resolution. The moment Squeex acknowledged Ludwig's perspective, Ludwig's first thought was that he was "winning couples counseling." That instinct — to treat a partner's vulnerability as a point scored — is exactly what sends the cycle back to the beginning. When someone extends understanding, respond to the extension, not to the fact that they had to make it.

Thinking positivity is the job of the upbeat partner. If you're the one who's usually doing okay, it's easy to think your role is to keep spirits up. But relentless positivity actually prevents the other person from feeling met. Being willing to show your own uncertainty or frustration gives the other person somewhere to go.

Using reflective listening as a trick rather than genuine curiosity. If you ask "what makes you think I'm good at this?" and you're already preparing a counter-argument, it doesn't work. Squeex could tell when Ludwig's Counter-Strike question was a fishing expedition rather than real openness. The technique only functions if you're actually trying to understand.

Assuming the problem is the other person's behavior. The SL1 situation looks like a disagreement about a gaming choice. It's actually about feeling inferior. Addressing the behavior ("stop suggesting SL1") without addressing the underlying need doesn't resolve anything — it just moves the expression of that need somewhere else.

Conflating self-respect with external performance. Ludwig can't receive Squeex's respect because he hasn't given himself his own. No amount of Squeex's validation will fill that gap permanently. The internal work of self-respect is separate from, though it can be supported by, the relationship work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your "actuallys" in real time. For one week, pay attention to when you use "actually" in a disagreement — out loud or in your head. When you catch yourself doing it, name it: "I just went actually. My bad." Then try again without it.

  2. Practice the "what" question. The next time you're in a disagreement or feeling defensive, instead of stating your position or countering the other person's, ask one "what" question. "What made you feel that way?" "What would help right now?" Just one. Then stop and listen for the full answer.

  3. Reflect before you respond. After the other person finishes talking, try to repeat back the core of what they said before adding your own view. Not parroting, but genuinely checking: "So what I'm hearing is X. Is that right?" This does two things: it slows you down, and it shows the other person you were actually listening.

  4. Map the cycle, not the cause. Instead of trying to figure out who started the conflict, try to describe the pattern: "When I do X, you do Y, and then I do X more." Saying it out loud together — without assigning blame — often deflates more than an hour of argument.

  5. Trade seats deliberately. If you're usually the caregiver, let yourself be cared for this week. Ask for help with something you'd normally handle alone. If you're usually the one struggling, find one moment to show up for your partner instead. Notice how it feels for both of you.

  6. Surface the psychological need behind the behavior. If you find yourself insisting on a particular change to your environment or circumstances — or if your partner is insisting — ask: "What would this give me (or them) emotionally?" Try to address that need directly in words before engineering a solution around it.

  7. When you're stuck, dump it in their lap. The next time you genuinely don't know what to do in a conflict, say so honestly and hand it to them: "I can't figure out the right move here. What do you need from me?" Resist the urge to fill the silence with your own answer.
