Why Failure Needs to Be Safe

One of the most immediately practical ideas in this conversation is the design of environments where failure is expected and acceptable. When running a game jam, the challenge wasn't technical — it was emotional. People didn't believe they could make games, so they weren't willing to try. The solution was to sidestep the fear entirely: instead of asking people to build a game, ask them to describe what they would do if they built one. Create a game design document. Map the tools, the plan, the whole thing on paper.

This is what psychotherapy calls going meta — moving one layer of abstraction away from the thing that's activating someone's fear, so they can engage with it without triggering all the defenses. The game jam stumbled into this by intuition, not clinical training. The result: 98% of participants who completed the design document went on to build a prototype, because the real obstacle — I don't think I can — had already been dissolved.

This principle extends well beyond game design. The same mechanism explains why people stay stuck in relationships, jobs, and self-improvement efforts. The goal is never the immediate output; the goal is creating conditions in which a person feels safe enough to try.

The Surprising Science of Getting Things Done

There's a body of research on intentional bindingThe science of how to sustain and repeat behavior without relying on reward.Full definition → — what actually increases the likelihood that you'll repeat a behavior — and the findings run counter to common intuition.

Three things increase behavioral repetition, and crucially, this effect is independent of the outcome:

  1. Think through your options beforehand. Not necessarily pick the best one — just consider that multiple options exist. Simply going through the mental process of do I go to the gym or exercise at home? makes you more likely to exercise at all.
  2. Predict the consequences. Spend a little time imagining what you expect to happen. Again, it doesn't matter whether the prediction is correct — the act of predicting increases follow-through.
  3. Reflect afterward. After a task is complete, spend five to ten minutes doing nothing in particular — just sitting with it. No forced insights required. The reflection itself is the mechanism.

Most people skip all three. They either spiral in obligation (I need to do this, I don't want to do this) or they finish something and immediately move on to the next thing without processing what happened. Both patterns undermine the brain's natural reinforcement of chosen behavior.

The key word is chosen. The brain tracks the difference between things you signed up for and things that happened to you. When the majority of your life consists of passive challenges — problems that aggroed you without your consent — you burn out. The counterintuitive fix isn't to do less; it's to take on more intentional, chosen activity. Even two deliberately chosen projects can improve your ability to manage the five things you didn't choose.

What Homelessness Teaches That Comfort Cannot

Living out of a car for a year after a business partner open-sourced shared work without consent is a specific kind of catastrophic failure — one that was partly the partner's fault and partly the result of not having financial constraints in place. Both things were true simultaneously.

"I could blame them, but where's that going to get me? I'm going to get back in this exact same scenario."

The emotional arc moved from rage to pragmatics: I'm not getting back what was lost. That's already out of the bag. So what's the victory condition?

That reframe — what is the victory condition? — is a remarkably effective emotional tool. Instead of trying to suppress an emotion or logic it away, you redirect the underlying energy by changing the objective. The question isn't is this feeling valid? It's does acting on this feeling move me toward or away from what I actually want?

This isn't emotional suppression. The feelings remain present. What changes is whether they drive the steering wheel. Extreme emotions get noticed — I am very angry right now, wait a minute — and briefly held rather than immediately discharged. Normal emotional range flows freely. The filter is calibrated to intensity, not to emotional valence.

The homelessness experience also crystallized the practice of the three intentional bindingThe science of how to sustain and repeat behavior without relying on reward.Full definition → behaviors described above. With real stakes and real consequences, deliberate thinking about options, consequences, and reflection became survival tools rather than abstract advice.

Red Flags and the Value of Early Conflict

There's a counterintuitive observation about relationships: some of the healthiest long-term partnerships began with what looked like red flags. The reason is structural. Early conflict, awkwardness, or difficulty forces both people to develop conflict resolution skills before the stakes get enormous. A relationship that runs perfectly smooth for years and then hits its first real argument often can't survive it — not because the argument was catastrophic, but because neither person has any practice.

The absence of conflict in a relationship isn't a green flag. It's often a sign that conflict is being avoided rather than resolved, and that pressure is building rather than releasing. A relationship with some friction early on is like a boss fight with a save point nearby — you lose, you learn, you try again. Without that practice, the first real fight can become the last one.

The same logic applies across many domains: gaming skill, career resilience, emotional regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition →. The more you've failed and practiced recovering, the more potential energy you've built up. People who appear far behind can catch up with remarkable speed once conditions shift — not because circumstances changed, but because they've been accumulating capacity that wasn't visible from outside.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "work on yourself" as vague inspiration. The actual mechanism matters. Thinking through your options, predicting outcomes, and reflecting afterward are specific behaviors with documented effects — not just mindset shifts.
  • Mistaking the absence of conflict for relationship health. Smoothness early on can mean that neither person has developed the skills they'll need when things get hard.
  • Assuming that being overwhelmed means you're doing too much. The relevant variable isn't quantity — it's the ratio of chosen to unchosen challenges. Taking on more intentional work can actually reduce the feeling of overwhelm.
  • Using ego-driven logic to evaluate yourself. The ego isn't neutral. It selects information that confirms what it already believes. This is why the stories people tell about their own failures always tend to implicate everyone except themselves.
  • Trying to name or systematize the third layer. Every attempt to nail it down makes it smaller. The most honest description available is not this, not this — and the most productive relationship with it is to notice when it shows up and follow it, without needing to explain it first.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. When you're afraid to try something, go meta. Instead of confronting the scary thing directly, spend time thinking about why you're afraid of it, or what you'd need in order to try it. Often this dissolves the obstacle without requiring any force.

  2. Before any important action, run the three-step intentional bindingThe science of how to sustain and repeat behavior without relying on reward.Full definition → process: (a) List your options — even if some are silly. (b) Predict what you think will happen. (c) After the action is done, sit quietly for five to ten minutes without forcing any conclusion. Repeat this for tasks you want to build into habits.

  3. Ask "what is my victory condition?" when you feel a strong negative emotion. Not to suppress the emotion, but to redirect the energy behind it. If the feeling, when acted on, doesn't move you toward your actual goal, hold it rather than discharge it.

  4. Keep a running record of things you've chosen versus things that happened to you. If most of your to-do list is unchosen, pick one small intentional project and add it. Notice whether your sense of overwhelm changes.

  5. Practice radical transparency in one area of your life for 30 days. This doesn't mean saying everything you think; it means not actively constructing false impressions. Note how it changes your behavior, not just other people's reactions.

  6. When you catch yourself blaming teammates, employers, partners, or circumstances, ask: what would I see if I looked at the analytics on my own performance? What would an objective observer say about your contribution to the situation?

  7. Pay attention to the third layer. When something arrives as this is correct rather than I want this or I've reasoned my way to this — notice it. You don't have to name it or explain it. Just track when it shows up and whether you follow it.
