Summary

TL;DR — Insecurity, ego, and anger are often running side by side in your mind without you realizing it. Understanding how they interact gives you tools to handle difficult people and stop holding yourself back.

This is a wide-ranging conversation that touches on panic attacks, imposter syndrome, dealing with a manipulative colleague, and — most interestingly — the discovery that what looks like insecurity on the surface is often something more nuanced underneath. The most valuable ideas are about the ego as a protective mechanism, why we make excuses for people who hurt us, and a subtle but important distinction between confidence and arrogance.

The Ego Is a Bouncer, Not a Villain

The ego — called ahamkara in Sanskrit — isn't a flaw. It's your mind's way of protecting you from hurt. When something damages your sense of self-worth, the ego steps in and either pumps you up or puts someone else down.

"If I ask someone out on a date and they turn me down, my ego says, 'Oh, they're not actually that pretty — I was doing them a favor.'"

That thought is egotistical and, on some level, dumb. It's also completely natural. The ego fires automatically whenever you feel small.

The key insight is that ego and confidence are opposites, not synonyms. Most people assume they go together — that a confident person has a big ego. The reality is reversed:

  • A confident person, when called an idiot, shrugs it off.
  • An arrogant (ego-driven) person, when called an idiot, gets defensive or seethes quietly.
  • A confident person can easily admit they're wrong. An arrogant person almost never does.

So if someone in your life never admits they're wrong, they don't have confidence — they have ego. And when you run up against their ego with logic and good arguments, you'll lose every time, because their mind simply isn't processing the merit of ideas. It's protecting itself.

Why You Make Excuses for People Who Hurt You

When someone treats you poorly without a good reason, it hurts more than when they have a legitimate grievance. There's a spectrum:

  • You forget to pick someone up from the airport → they're angry → that's understandable, it stings less.
  • You did everything right → they're still cruel to you → that's personal, raw, much harder to absorb.

So the mind does something clever and sneaky: it moves the person from the "no good reason" camp into the "good reason" camp by manufacturing understanding. You put yourself in their shoes, decide maybe they know something you don't, maybe there's more to the story — and suddenly their behavior becomes explicable. Less personal. Easier to tolerate.

"The more you can justify them having a good reason to treat you like crap, the less it hurts."

This is protective. It is also, quietly, a way of making yourself smaller. You're not resolving the hurt — you're just making it easier to function alongside it.

The tell that this is happening: you hold two completely contradictory beliefs simultaneously. Part of you knows you're right and they're wrong. Another part is actively explaining why they might be right. Both processes run in parallel. Noticing that both are running is the first step to not being controlled by either.

The Difference Between Insecurity and Fear of Failure

These look identical from the outside but they're different animals.

True insecurity is a genuine belief that you're not capable or worthy. Fear of failure is actually a form of self-protection — you hold back just enough so that you're never fully exposed. If you never really try, you can never really fail. The comfort is that your potential stays intact and untested.

The giveaway is what happens when you imagine encouraging a younger version of yourself. The right answer isn't "go for it, you can do it!" — that's pressure, and it bounces off. The right answer is gentler:

"You don't have to do it all the time, but you can go for it once in a while."

That response offers safety, encouragement, and forgiveness for falling short — all at once, before anything has even been attempted. That's what actually works for someone who is anxious about trying. You're not pushing them. You're giving them room.

If that's the advice you'd give your eight-year-old self, that's also what you should be telling yourself now.

The Hidden Cost of "Always Wanting to Be Better"

The endless pursuit of self-improvement feels virtuous. It's also one of the quietest ways to make yourself miserable, because it encodes a permanent verdict: you're not good enough yet.

"If you can be better, it means you're not good enough."

There's a different motivational engine available. Instead of I need to be better, the alternative is: I'm going to show up and give what I have to the people who are here. The focus shifts from self-evaluation to service. You can still put your best foot forward — but "best foot" means what you actually have today, not a hypothetical improved version of yourself.

The pursuit of being better is, at its core, about yourself. Showing up for other people is about them. That shift in orientation also makes it easier to forgive yourself when you fall short, because falling short of a standard you set for yourself is ego — falling short in service of other people is just being human.

Dealing With an Ego-Driven Person at Work

When someone's behavior is driven by ego rather than logic, arguing with logic doesn't work. Their mind isn't evaluating the merits of your idea. It's defending their sense of self.

A practical approach for group settings:

  1. Ask for their opinion first. Being heard disarms the ego. The more they feel recognized, the less resistance you'll get.
  2. Reflect back what they said without judgment. "So it sounds like you're thinking X because of Y" — this signals that you heard them, not that you agree.
  3. Open the question to others. "What does everyone else think?" Let the room speak.
  4. State the group's view without using the word "but." But is a flag that triggers defensiveness. Replace it with "at the same time."
  5. Name the decision and move on. "It sounds like the group is leaning toward orange — we're going to go with that." Then stop.

This only works if you're not the one person's direct subordinate. The goal is to take control of the process of the conversation, not to win the argument on the merits.

Also worth internalizing: when someone makes you feel small, it is almost never actually about you. The ego pumps itself up by putting others down. Someone who needs to make you feel small is, underneath it, not confident in themselves. Remembering that doesn't erase the hurt, but it does change what the hurt means.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing ego with confidence. They are not the same. Ego is defensive and brittle. Confidence is relaxed and can absorb criticism.
  • Mistaking mental gymnastics for wisdom. Constantly expanding the "grey zone" to explain away someone who hurts you isn't being fair-minded — it's avoiding the reality that someone is treating you badly.
  • Thinking "I want to improve" is always healthy. The desire to be better is only healthy when it coexists with genuine acceptance of who you are right now. Without that acceptance, it's just a slow drip of self-rejection.
  • Ignoring your anger. Burying it doesn't make it go away. Unacknowledged anger drives a lot of behavior without you realizing it. You're better off knowing it's there.
  • Suppressing internal experience to maintain an external persona. Being meek and calm on the outside is fine. Lying to yourself on the inside about what you actually feel is not.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice your parallel processes. The next time someone disagrees with you or makes you feel small, check whether you're running two programs at once: one that knows you're right, and one that's making excuses for them. You don't have to act on either — just observe that both are there.

  2. Identify what you're doing when you "try to understand" someone who hurt you. Ask yourself: am I genuinely seeking understanding, or am I moving this person from "no good reason to treat me this way" into "good reason to treat me this way"? The distinction matters.

  3. Talk to your eight-year-old self. When fear of failure is holding you back, imagine a younger version of yourself who is hesitating. What would you actually say to them? Not "go for it, you can do it" — something gentler and more honest. Write that down and use it on yourself.

  4. Try the alternate nostril breathing practice (Nadi Shodhana). Block the right nostril, inhale through the left. Switch, exhale through the right. Inhale through the right. Switch, exhale through the left. That's one cycle. Work up to five minutes daily. The goal is to interrupt the "not good enough" feeling long enough to notice it losing intensity.

  5. Shift your pre-stream (or pre-work) framing. Before you show up for something that triggers self-evaluation, ask not "how can I be better today?" but "what can I give the people who are here today?" That question is answerable. The first one is a trap.

  6. Let yourself be angry — on purpose, briefly. If someone has been making you feel small, give yourself explicit permission to feel that anger rather than immediately explaining it away. Write it down if that helps. You don't have to act on it. You just have to stop pretending it isn't there.
