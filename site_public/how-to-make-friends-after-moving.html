Summary

TL;DR — Making friends as an adult comes down to two things: regular unplanned interaction and common cause. Everything else is secondary.

Most people treat friendship as a mysterious, personality-dependent process. It isn't. Like most things in life, you can apply a kind of engineering mindset to it — identify the key variables, then structure your life around them. Once you understand the actual mechanics of how friendship forms, you stop waiting for it to happen and start building conditions where it can.

The Two Variables That Actually Matter

Think about every friend you've made. School. Work. A club. A team. What do all of these have in common?

Regular unplanned interaction — you showed up, they showed up, neither of you was there specifically to befriend the other. There was no social pressure. You just kept appearing in the same place.

Common cause — you were both working toward something, or struggling through something together. A shared boss you both find insufferable. Studying for the same exam. Trying to stay sober. Common cause means shared goals and shared struggles.

That's the core recipe. Common interests help. Personality compatibility helps. But they're third-tier variables. The reason people make friends at school and work isn't because those environments filter for interesting people — it's because those environments create repeated, low-pressure exposure with a built-in shared purpose.

A train station has people in it. That doesn't make it a place to make friends. The difference is regularity and stakes. When you see someone three times a week for six weeks, by the time you strike up a real conversation there's already a foundation there. Nobody had to try.

How to Actually Structure Your Life for This

If you've moved somewhere new, or if years of gaming have left you without in-person relationships, the fix isn't to become more charming or more outgoing. It's to put yourself in environments that provide both variables automatically.

Some examples that work: - Take a recurring class — fitness, cooking, language, art. Other people will show up week after week. You don't have to force anything. - Find a board game store. If you're a gamer who just moved somewhere, this is almost tailor-made — intellectual, social, time-bounded (you can't play Agricola for 14 hours), and built around a shared activity. - Volunteer regularly. You're there for a cause, not to network. That removes pressure and adds common cause simultaneously. - AA and similar groups work for exactly this reason — regular attendance, shared struggle, people who need sober friends and are actively looking for them.

The key insight is that you're not attending these things to make friends. You're attending them to do the thing, and you let friendship emerge from the structure. That's what makes it feel natural instead of desperate.

Small Talk Isn't the Enemy

A common complaint about meeting people in real life is that it involves social posturing, small talk, and pretending to care about things that don't matter. And there's a real frustration in that.

But criticizing social norms for being arbitrary misses why they exist. Driving on the right side of the road has no inherent moral value. It only has value because everyone does it. Small talk is similar — the ritual of introducing yourself, asking easy questions, not immediately demanding someone's deepest fear, exists because it's how people gently signal they're not a threat and build enough trust to go deeper.

The move isn't to skip small talk. The move is to get through it efficiently with questions that are curious without being invasive. Instead of "what are your passions?" (weird), try "what are you excited about going into next year?" or "have you traveled anywhere interesting lately?" These are easy to answer, they open doors, and they let you play off whatever they give you.

"If you find people boring, you're probably not bothering to understand who they are or what's interesting about them."

Most people aren't boring. They just need a question that unlocks something they actually care about. Ask someone about fishing, about their hometown, about something they're proud of — and then actually listen. You'll learn something. They'll feel seen. That's the entire transaction.

On the flip side, let people talk about themselves when they want to — but don't drill them with questions as a tactic to get them to like you. Letting someone talk about themselves is different from forcing it. Pay attention to what kind of person they are and calibrate. Some people want to be asked questions. Introverts often prefer to listen.

Introverts Don't Have to Become Extroverts

The world isn't actually designed for extroverts — it just looks that way from the outside. If you're introverted, you don't have to learn to "fill the room." You have to identify where your strengths already are and work from there.

One-on-one conversations. Small lunches. Grabbing coffee with a single colleague. Deep, meaningful exchanges instead of loud group banter. These aren't lesser social skills — they often build stronger individual relationships than anything an extrovert does in a crowded room. When review time comes, the people you've had real conversations with will speak highly of you.

The goal isn't to play someone else's game. It's to recognize that solo queue and party queue are both valid ways to play.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "common interests" as the most important variable. You probably have friends you don't share hobbies with. What you share is proximity and shared struggle, not necessarily taste in movies.
  • Trying to manufacture a friendship through a single planned hangout. One coffee meeting where both people are consciously "trying to make a friend" is high-pressure and usually doesn't work. Repeated low-pressure exposure is what creates the conditions for it to happen naturally.
  • Dismissing online friendships entirely — they're real and valuable, just incomplete in specific ways.
  • Dismissing in-person interaction entirely — "people are boring" is usually a sign you haven't figured out how to open them up, not that they're actually dull.
  • Skipping small talk in pursuit of depth. Depth comes after trust. Trust comes after repeated, low-stakes interaction. Small talk is the on-ramp.
  • Forcing people to talk about themselves as a social strategy. It becomes interrogation. Let it happen, don't manufacture it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Identify one recurring activity you can join in your area — a class, a board game night, a volunteer shift, a running group — that meets at least weekly and will have consistent attendees. Commit to showing up for at least six weeks before evaluating whether it's "working."

  2. If you're a gamer new to a city, find a local board game store with open play nights. It provides the intellectual engagement you're used to, in a social and time-bounded format.

  3. When you meet someone new, lead with one low-pressure, open-ended question — something like "Is there anything you're looking forward to this year?" or "Have you been anywhere interesting lately?" Then actually listen to the answer and respond to it, rather than jumping to the next question.

  4. Notice when you're dismissing someone as boring and treat it as a signal to ask a better question, not a verdict on them as a person. Try to find one thing they're genuinely interested in and ask them to tell you about it.

  5. If you're an introvert trying to build professional or social relationships, stop trying to perform extroversion. Instead, intentionally seek out one-on-one time — coffee, lunch, a short walk. These interactions will build stronger bonds than any group setting.

  6. If you've been gaming heavily and feel socially rusty, start getting regular low-stakes real-world exposure — not to make friends immediately, but to wake up the parts of your brain that read body language and social cues. Think of it as physical therapy for your social sonar.
