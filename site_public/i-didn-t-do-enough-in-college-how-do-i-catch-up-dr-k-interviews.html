This is a live coaching session with a 25-year-old named Andrew who graduated with a game design degree, spent a year doing nothing, then settled into a retail job he doesn't care about. He feels like he's falling further and further behind some imaginary peer group, can't articulate what he wants, and can't sustain any momentum when he tries to change. The Gap That Keeps Growing

There's a pattern that explains why people in their mid-twenties suddenly feel a crushing sense of inadequacy they didn't feel at eighteen. In college, everyone is on the same track. Grades differ, but the structure is shared. Around 20 or 21, a quiet divergence starts: some people are getting internships, building portfolios, doing "extra" things while you're just attending class. You notice it, it unsettles you, but you keep going.

Then you graduate. The structure disappears. The people who were doing the extra things are now ahead, visibly. And every year you don't close that gap, it widens. By 25 or 26, what started as a quiet worry has become a loud, pervasive sense of being behind — not just professionally but as a person.

The trap is thinking this is a strategy problem. It isn't. It's a deeper problem about what you believe you're capable of.

You Don't Let Yourself Want Things

There's a subtle but devastating move the stuck mind makes: it conflates wanting something with believing you can do it. If you're not sure you can succeed at something, you preemptively stop yourself from wanting it. You don't even let the desireOne of the four classical Hindu goals of life, Kama is the pursuit of pleasure, desire, and sensory enjoyment — including but not limited to sexuality and lust.Full definition → form fully.

This is where the "lack of purpose" most people complain about actually comes from. It's not that you genuinely have no idea what you want. It's that the wanting feels too dangerous if you might fail. So the mind goes abstract — "I need a sense of purpose," "I need to get on track," "I need to figure out my career" — instead of landing on anything specific that could be attempted and potentially failed at.

"I don't think you have a problem with that — that's not where your problem is."

The analysis, the planning, the weighing of options: these feel like progress but they're a way of staying safe in the realm of ideas while never having to risk the realm of action.

Where the Belief Comes From

In Andrew's case, the core problem traced back to something his mother repeated throughout his childhood: you're only good at video games. Said enough times, that becomes a samskarA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from past experiences that gets stored in the mind and automatically activates in present situations, distorting your perception of what's actually happening.Full definition → — a deeply embedded impression formed by emotionally significant experience, especially in youth. Samskars aren't logical. You can't argue yourself out of them. They operate in the background automatically, quietly filtering every decision you make.

The samskar here told Andrew: whatever you try, it won't work out. So he hedged his bets by putting everything into the one domain he'd already been told he was limited to. He went to school for game design — not really because he was passionate about making games, but because games were the one acceptable basket for all his eggs. When that didn't pan out the way he imagined, it felt like confirmation.

"You have some fundamental sense that you're not able to accomplish, not able to succeed. And this is pervasive."

Willpower can temporarily override a samskar. You can force yourself to try new things, apply to jobs, wake up with resolve. But willpower runs out. The samskar keeps generating resistance — like an enemy spawn point that never stops producing enemies. You kill the enemies; more appear. The spawn point itself has to be addressed.

Experience Is the Only Actual Fix

The spawn point — the samskar — isn't dissolved by insight, therapy alone, or making better plans. It's dissolved by experience. Specifically, new positive experiences that write new impressions onto the mind.

This is why logic and planning fail to produce lasting change. Your brain isn't going to let you sustain six hours a day of painting if it doesn't believe you can be a painter. The motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → doesn't come from deciding; it comes from having had experiences that made you feel capable, engaged, alive in some new way.

Think of how automatic food preferences are. You eat something and get sick once — you don't need willpower to avoid that restaurant. You have an incredible meal — you don't need willpower to go back. Experience writes the code that runs automatically. Plans don't write that code.

"If you want to change the person that you are automatically, so that you don't have to use willpower, you just need to have different experiences."

The critical insight is that the experiences don't have to be grand, purposeful, or directly related to your career goals. They just have to be different from what you've been doing. The point isn't to find the right experience. The point is to break the sameness of your days, because sameness preserves the self you already are.

Andrew's life, if mapped over the last year versus the year before, would look essentially identical in terms of the kinds of things he was experiencing. That sameness is the problem in its most concrete form.

Dharma as a Tool for Getting Unstuck

One additional lever for people who can't get moving because their internal fear is too loud: DharmaDharma is your duty or responsibility in life — not a grand cosmic purpose, but the ongoing attitude of doing right by yourself and the people around you.Full definition →, the Sanskrit concept of duty or responsibility. Acting from a sense of duty to someone or something outside yourself creates a different relationship with your own fear.

When you're sitting with anxietyA normal physiological response to new, unfamiliar, or high-stakes situations — one that actually helps you perform.Full definition → or self-doubt, those feelings are in the foreground — they run the show. But when you're acting for someone else, or out of a genuine sense of responsibility, you become less central to the equation. The fear is still there but it no longer controls the decision. Dharma is less about suppressing fear and more about relocating your attention.

Crucially, the Dharma you most often fail is the one to yourself. Most stuck people will go to enormous lengths for friends, family, and coworkers — they'll make chicken soup for a sick friend but won't cook nourishing food for themselves. They'll stay at a job they hate out of imagined loyalty to coworkers (whose workload is actually the boss's responsibility, not theirs). The Dharma to yourself — to grow, to try new things, to take care of your own development — is the one that gets perpetually deferred.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking planning for progress. Running options in your head feels productive. It's the mind's way of appearing active while keeping you safe from actual risk. The longer you stay in the planning phase, the more you confirm to yourself that you don't know what to do — because plans without action produce no information.

  • Waiting to want something before trying it. Most people think they need a clear passion or purpose before they can act. Actually it works the other way: doing things creates preferences and desiresDesires are signals generated by the sense organs (the indriyas) that point toward something you currently lack — creating a gap between what you have and what you want.Full definition →. The motivation follows the experience, not the other way around.

  • Confusing a samskar with the truth. "I'm just not capable" feels like a factual observation about yourself. It isn't. It's an old impression laid down when you were young and didn't have the emotional equipment to process a painful situation. It's real in its effects, but it's not an accurate picture of what you're capable of.

  • Interpreting short bursts of effort as evidence of failure. When you force yourself to try something new and then stop after a week, it can feel like proof that you're a hopeless case. It's actually just what willpower looks like without the support of experience. The solution is more experience, not more willpower.

  • Accepting imagined obligations that aren't yours. Staying in situations out of loyalty to coworkers, or out of vague guilt about letting people down, when those situations are genuinely preventing your growth — this is confusing someone else's Dharma for your own.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Do something genuinely different today. Pull up Google Maps and look at your area. Find one thing within reasonable distance that you've never done — a yoga class, a shooting range, a volunteer opportunity, a type of food you've never tried, a trail you've never walked. The content matters less than the novelty. Do it this week, not "soon."

  2. Add variety deliberately and repeatedly. The one-off experience helps, but what changes a life is sustained novelty over time. Commit to doing at least one new thing per week for a month. Track it simply — a note in your phone is enough.

  3. Notice when you go abstract. When you catch yourself thinking in terms of "I need to figure out my purpose" or "I need to get on track," pause. Those are signs you've retreated to the safe territory of ideas. Ask yourself: what is one specific, concrete thing I could do in the next 24 hours instead of thinking about this?

  4. Trace your self-limiting beliefs backward. If you believe you're not capable of succeeding at something, try to identify when you first started believing that, and who or what was around when it formed. You don't need to fix it immediately — just seeing it as an old impression rather than a current fact creates some distance.

  5. Make a list of your actual Dharmas. Write down the people and roles you feel responsible to. For each one, ask: what does my real duty to this person require — and what am I doing that actually goes beyond that duty out of anxiety or guilt? The place where guilt and genuine duty blur is worth examining.

  6. If you're in a loop of low-grade depressionA disruption in mood and functioning that exists on a spectrum — not a single, uniform illness.Full definition → or hopelessness, treat finding a therapist like an errand, not a big decision. Call your insurance company, get a list, look up three names, meet with all three, pick the one you like most. Treat it like test-driving cars.

  7. After any new experience, message someone or write a line about it. Articulating what was different — even briefly — helps consolidate the impression. This is a small way of helping the experience actually stick.
