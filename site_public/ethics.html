Summary

TL;DR — What happens in a coaching conversation or public mental health talk is not therapy, and that distinction is legally and ethically precise, not a matter of opinion. This talk walks through exactly how medical ethics defines clinical practice, why public mental health education falls clearly outside it, and how to recognize when online criticism is driven by emotional manipulation rather than genuine evidence.

Medical ethics aren't philosophical preferences — they're codified in state, federal, and international law, and enforced by licensing boards. Understanding the actual definitions matters, because most people form their intuitions about "therapy" from TV and media, not from any formal training.

The Four Core Principles of Medical Ethics

Before anything else, it helps to know what medical ethics actually requires of practitioners:

  • Beneficence — maximize the good you do
  • Non-malfeasance — minimize the harm you cause
  • Autonomy — patients have the right to make their own decisions, including refusing treatment
  • Justice — deliver care fairly and without discrimination (a doctor treats a 50-year-lung cancer patient the same as a non-smoker)

These principles aren't ideals to aspire to — they're the operational framework clinicians are legally held to. Every prescription decision, for example, is essentially weighing beneficence against non-malfeasance: does the clinical benefit outweigh the side effects?

Why Empathic Listening Isn't Therapy

A common source of confusion: therapy uses skills like empathic listening, asking insightful questions, and building rapport. But those skills are not exclusive to therapy.

FBI hostage negotiators build rapid rapport and make empathic statements. Customer service representatives are trained to validate feelings. Executives receive interpersonal and communication training. A physical therapist teaches stretching — but that doesn't mean every personal trainer doing a warm-up is practicing physical therapy.

The same logic applies to mental health. Teaching someone to meditate can happen within a clinical relationship — but a Zen priest teaching meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition → on weekends is not practicing medicine. An empathic conversation between friends about a breakup is not therapy. The activity doesn't determine whether it's clinical — the presence of a formal doctor-patient relationship, diagnosis, and treatment plan does.

"Just because something happens within a therapeutic relationship does not mean that it is therapy when it happens outside of a therapeutic relationship."

There are things exclusive to clinical medicine — prescribing controlled substances, for instance. A psychiatrist cannot ethically prescribe medication to a spouse or friend. Those lines are real and hard. But empathic listening and asking good questions are not in that category.

What Informed Consent Looks Like in Practice

Even when an activity is clearly non-clinical, ethical standards still apply. In the context of public mental health conversations, a meaningful informed consent process means:

  • Explicitly telling participants that what's happening is not the practice of medicine
  • Making clear that no doctor-patient relationship is being formed
  • Giving participants the ability to set limits on what topics they discuss
  • Allowing participants to withdraw or redirect at any time
  • Resolving any confusion before proceeding — and stopping if it can't be resolved

This is where the principle of autonomy becomes active: an adult who understands what they're agreeing to and chooses to have a public conversation about their mental health has the right to do so.

How to Recognize Hate Farming

Online criticism ranges from genuinely useful to actively manipulative. The manipulative variety — sometimes called hate farming — follows a recognizable pattern worth understanding:

It works emotionally first. Strong thumbnails, charged language, and narrative framing designed to produce outrage before you've evaluated a single claim. The goal is to get you to pick up a pitchfork, not to help you think clearly.

It starts with a conclusion. This is the a priori problem: the judgment comes before the evidence is collected. Once someone has decided you're guilty, every search they conduct, every tweet they send asking for "information," is shaped by that prior conclusion. Confirmation bias doesn't have to be malicious to be distorting — it's just how information-seeking works when you already know the answer you want.

It creates selection bias at scale. If you tweet "I'm making a video about how X is harmful — message me if you've experienced this," you will receive an enormous volume of confirming responses. The internet is large enough that almost any claim can be supported by gathering anecdotes. Then a creator filters those responses for the most compelling ones and builds a video around them. The result looks like evidence.

It strings together expert opinions as a substitute for evidence. Tobacco companies found physicians who testified under oath that cigarettes didn't cause cancer. Finding credentialed people who agree with you is not the same as conducting a sound investigation. Notably, dissenting experts don't appear in these videos.

"A collection — stringing together a group of expert opinions — does not actually constitute evidence."

The difference between legitimate criticism and hate farming is often methodological: legitimate criticism cites sources, acknowledges counterarguments, and asks questions that could in principle be falsified. Hate farming asks loaded questions designed to return only confirming answers, without any equivalent of an independent review.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking familiarity for definition. Most people's mental model of "therapy" comes from television. Real clinical therapy involves formal diagnosis codes, treatment plans with measurable objectives, and a legally defined relationship. Conversational depth and emotional insight are not the distinguishing features.

Assuming that because a doctor is involved, it's medical. A psychiatrist can have a personal conversation, teach a friend to meditate, or discuss mental health publicly without practicing medicine. The doctor's presence doesn't automatically make something clinical — the three pillars (relationship, diagnosis, treatment) do.

Treating online criticism as neutral. Not all criticism is created equal. The presence of credentials, emotional intensity, or a long YouTube video doesn't mean the underlying methodology is sound. Ask whether the questions being asked could have returned a different answer — if not, the process was probably biased from the start.

Thinking ethics is a matter of personal belief. Whether something is ethical in a medical context isn't settled by what the practitioner believes or what viewers feel. It's settled by what licensing boards, professional associations, and law actually define. Strong conviction on either side of an argument doesn't change those definitions.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Learn the three pillars. The next time you encounter a mental health service, coaching offer, or online conversation, ask: Is there a formal doctor-patient agreement? Is a diagnosis being assigned? Is a treatment plan being developed? If none of those are present, it's not clinical practice — regardless of who's involved.

  2. Audit your information sources for a priori reasoning. Pick one topic you hold a strong opinion about. Search for credible evidence against your position. Notice how much harder that feels, and how the phrasing of your searches shapes what you find.

  3. Before reacting to a critical video, run the methodology check. Ask: What questions did this creator ask to gather information? Could those questions have returned different answers? Are dissenting voices included? Is the emotional intensity proportional to the quality of the evidence?

  4. Practice informed consent in everyday conversations. Before a difficult personal conversation, briefly establish what both parties are expecting from it — venting, advice, problem-solving, or just being heard. This is a lightweight version of what makes clinical interactions safe.

  5. When you notice outrage building while consuming content, pause. Strong emotional reactions while watching something are a signal to slow down, not speed up. Ask what specific factual claim is being made and whether it's been sourced — before you share it or act on it.
