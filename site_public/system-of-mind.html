TL;DR — Your thoughts don't appear randomly; they come from two specific sources, and once you understand those sources, you can actually shape what your mind produces.

Most people experience their thoughts as something that just happen to them — intrusive, automatic, and outside their control. Understanding this system is the foundation for changing your behavior, your self-image, and ultimately your life, because what you think determines what you do.

Where Thoughts Come From: The Two Sources

Every thought in your mind traces back to one of two origins: the outside world (your sense organs) or the inside world (your unconscious mind).

Source 1: The Sense Organs (Indriyas)

The Sanskrit word indriyas refers to the five sense organs. The first and most immediate source of thoughts is sensory input — what you see, hear, smell, taste, and touch.

This sounds obvious, but the implications are deeper than they first appear. You literally cannot think about something until you've been exposed to it. Before you heard about a particular video game, you had zero thoughts about it. Then you heard about it — and the thought appeared. Then you saw a trailer — and the thought grew. Then you played the game for hours, bombarding your senses with it — and the next day in class, that's all you could think about.

This is the engine behind video game addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition →, and behind any obsessive preoccupation: massive, repeated sensory exposure populates your mind with that thing. The flip side is equally true. Stop the exposure, and the thoughts gradually disappear. "Out of sight, out of mind" is not just a casual saying — it's a description of how the mind actually works.

The Victorian Wall vs. the Yogi's Window

Once you understand that sensory input shapes thought, two very different strategies emerge for managing it.

One approach — call it the Victorian approach — is to wall off the outside world entirely. Control the environment so rigorously that no "dangerous" input can get in. This works, to a point, but it's fragile and exhausting. You're entirely dependent on being able to control everything around you, which isn't always possible.

The other approach — the one developed through yogic practice — is to build a window instead of a wall. Rather than trying to sanitize the entire external world, you develop the internal capacity to regulate what actually passes from your senses into your mind. You don't need to control other people's behavior to control your own thoughts. You cultivate the ability to let things in selectively.

This internal skill is called pratyaharaThe fifth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed yoga, pratyahara is the deliberate withdrawal of attention away from external sensory objects and back inward.Full definition → — the practice of withdrawing or restraining sensory input at the point where it enters the mind.

The Bhagavad Gita uses a vivid image for this: the mind is a chariot pulled by five horses (the five sense organs). If the charioteer has no skill, the horses run wherever they please, and the chariot — and you — go with them. You've felt this: you smell something baking and suddenly you have to eat; you see a notification and you have to check it. Your senses drag you. PratyaharaThe fifth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed yoga, pratyahara is the deliberate withdrawal of attention away from external sensory objects and back inward.Full definition → is the training that lets you hold the reins — not to brutally restrain the horses, but to guide them with a practiced, light touch.

Why This Actually Matters

The point of all this isn't philosophical — it's practical. What you think determines what you do. Your mind's internal chatter is steering your behavior constantly, mostly without your awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →.

If your mind generates thoughts like there's no point in applying — you're not qualified, you won't apply. If your mind generates thoughts like this is worth a shot, why not try, you will. The content of your unconscious isn't just feelings; it's the engine of your behavior.

The same applies to what you find motivating. Some people wake up genuinely excited to learn something hard. That's not magic or personality — it's the result of their mind being populated (through exposure and practice) with thoughts that find learning rewarding. The mind can be cultivated in this direction deliberately.

"Wouldn't it be awesome if you could wake up one day and instead of your mind telling you 'I really wanna play a video game,' your mind told you 'I really wanna study biochemistry'?"

That's not a fantasy — it's a design problem. And the design starts with understanding these two sources.

Common Pitfalls

Thinking willpower is the answer. Most people try to change their behavior by gritting their teeth and forcing themselves. But if the underlying thought-generating system is still running the same programs, willpower will eventually lose. Lasting change starts upstream, at the level of what's populating your mind.

Underestimating passive exposure. It's not just the hours you spend directly doing something — it's watching others do it, talking about it, hanging around people who are immersed in it. All of that counts as sensory input and all of it shapes your thoughts.

Assuming samskarasA samskara is a ball of undigested emotional energy from past experiences that gets stored in the mind and automatically activates in present situations, distorting your perception of what's actually happening.Full definition → are fixed. People often treat their self-critical inner voice as just "who they are." It isn't. These are thought patterns deposited by past experience. They can be metabolized — worked through and dissolved — and new, more useful patterns can be intentionally planted in their place.

Choosing the Victorian wall as your only strategy. Trying to control your environment completely is sometimes useful (cutting a genuinely toxic person out of your life, for instance), but it's not a complete solution. You also need the internal capacity — pratyaharaThe fifth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed yoga, pratyahara is the deliberate withdrawal of attention away from external sensory objects and back inward.Full definition → — to manage what your mind does with what it encounters. Relying purely on environmental control leaves you vulnerable any time the environment changes.

Ignoring the positive version of the problem. It's easy to focus on removing bad thoughts, but planting genuinely good ones is equally important and often neglected. The goal isn't just a quieter mind — it's a mind that actively generates useful, constructive thoughts on its own.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your sensory diet for one week. Track what you're spending the most time seeing, hearing, and engaging with. Notice which topics dominate your thoughts the next day. This makes the indriya → thought connection concrete and personal.

  2. Practice the "sensory gate" pause. When you notice a strong pull — to check a notification, eat something, open an app — pause for ten seconds before acting. You're not suppressing the urge; you're practicing the moment of choice between sense input and response. This is the beginner's version of pratyaharaThe fifth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed yoga, pratyahara is the deliberate withdrawal of attention away from external sensory objects and back inward.Full definition →.

  3. Identify one recurring negative thought and trace it back. Ask yourself: where did this thought come from? Was it something you were told repeatedly? A pattern from childhood?   4. Deliberately increase exposure to what you want to think about more. If you want to be more interested in a skill, a subject, or a healthier habitAutomatic behavior that operates without motivation, willpower, or conscious thought — you simply do it.Full definition → — start surrounding yourself with it. Read about it, listen to people who do it, spend time around people who are immersed in it. You're seeding thoughts through the indriya pathway intentionally.

  5. Reduce passive exposure to what you want to think about less. This isn't about dramatic purges. Start small: remove one source of chronic passive exposure (an autoplay queue, a social feed, a group chat) and observe what happens to your thought patterns over the following week.
