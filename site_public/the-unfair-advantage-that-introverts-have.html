Summary

TL;DR — Every time you talk about what you want to do, you spend some of the energy you'd otherwise use to actually do it. Introverts' natural quietness is a structural advantage.

There's a principle from tantric yoga — and one that modern psychology quietly confirms — about how motivation works. Positive internal energy, like a goal or ambition, behaves a lot like fuel. When you keep it inside, it compounds. When you vent it through words, it dissipates. Most people treat talking about their goals as a warm-up for doing them. It isn't. It's a partial substitute for doing them.

The Therapy Insight, Run in Reverse

Evidence-based psychotherapy is built on a simple mechanism: you take something painful — trauma, shame, grief — store it inside a person, and you get them to talk about it in a safe space. As they speak, the emotional charge of that experience diminishes. The shame that was running their life quietly loses its grip. Venting negative energy through language is literally the treatment.

Now flip that around entirely. If talking reduces the power of what's stored inside you, then talking about your positive goals and intentions does the same thing to them. The energy you're carrying around a project — that restless, pressurized drive to build something — can be vented just as easily as trauma can. The mechanism is the same. It's just running in the wrong direction.

This is what the tantric tradition understood about mantra: you don't share it, you compound it. The secrecy isn't superstition. It's an instruction to stop venting the internal state you're trying to grow.

Why Talking About Goals Feels So Good (And Costs So Much)

When you tell someone you're going to become a doctor, start a company, or write a book, something real happens: they're impressed. You get a social reward. And when your brain receives a reward, it registers what behavior produced that reward — in this case, talking about the goal, not pursuing it.

Two things happen simultaneously: - You reinforce the habit of announcing intentions instead of executing them. - You receive the dopamine hit of the achievement before doing any of the work, which quietly drains the urgency to actually do it.

This is Freud's observation, carried forward in psychotherapy research: language and action draw from the same pool of energy and are, to the brain, partial substitutes for each other. Talking about going to the gym is not neutral with respect to going to the gym. It costs something.

You can see this everywhere once you notice it. The people online who are constantly posting about their grind, their passive income strategy, their sigma mindset — they are performing productivity, and the performance is eating the productivity. The people actually running companies mostly aren't posting about it. The player who can genuinely beat you at the game is playing it, not arguing about it on Reddit.

"The more that you vent what you're gonna do, the less motivated and successful you will become."

The Introvert's Structural Edge

Introverts don't naturally broadcast. They don't instinctively run to social media to announce a new ambition. They don't reflexively tell ten people about the book idea they had on the train. That silence, which feels like a social deficit in a culture that rewards loud self-promotion, is actually protective of motivational energy.

When an introvert has a goal, it stays inside by default. It builds pressure. That pressure has nowhere to go except into action. This is why introverts who channel their energy well can be remarkably effective builders, creators, and entrepreneurs — not despite their quietness, but because of it.

The extrovert's default, on the other hand, is to share — and sharing feels connective and energizing in the moment, while slowly bleeding out the drive to follow through.

The Rule for Journaling and Necessary Conversations

This doesn't mean total silence forever. There are two situations where communicating about a goal makes sense, and both require care.

Journaling: Journaling can vent positive energy just like speaking does. If you journal about your goals, you need to re-read what you wrote. The point is to externalize the thought, then pull it back in. Vent and re-internalize. If you write it out and close the notebook forever, you've just drained the energy without recovering it.

Talking to other people: Conversations about your work aren't always wasteful — but every word carries a cost, and that cost should be acknowledged explicitly. Before speaking, ask whether the benefit of this particular conversation clearly outweighs the motivational energy it will consume. If you're meeting with someone vague — brainstorming sessions that go in circles, "ideas meetings" where everyone's enthusiastic and nothing ever gets built — that is pure drain with no return.

When a conversation is necessary, keep it: - Scoped — a specific topic with a specific person for a specific reason - Brief — minimum words to accomplish the purpose - Intentional — you know exactly what outcome justifies the cost

"Every word I say in this conversation is going to deplete my motivation. So the conversation comes at a cost."

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking enthusiasm for momentum. A long conversation where everyone leaves excited is not progress. The excitement is the energy that would have driven progress. You've spent it early.

Thinking journaling is automatically safe. Writing about your goals without re-reading is just venting to paper. You still lose the energy.

Believing that "talking it through" helps you commit. Telling people about your goal doesn't make you accountable in a useful way — it gives your brain the social payoff of the goal before you've done the work, reducing the internal pressure that drives follow-through.

Dismissing this as "introvert cope." The underlying mechanism — that language and action are neurologically linked substitutes drawing on the same motivational resource — has real grounding in clinical psychology. It's not a personality preference, it's a description of how the system works.

Over-correcting into total isolation. The point isn't to never speak about your work. It's to be ruthlessly deliberate about when talking serves a real purpose versus when it's just leaking fuel.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick one active goal and tell no one. Choose something you're currently working on — or want to start — and commit to not mentioning it to anyone until you've made meaningful progress. Notice what happens to your drive over the following week.

  2. Audit your recent communications. Look back at the last few weeks. Where have you talked about things you wanted to do? What was the ratio of talking to doing? Be honest about whether the talking preceded action or replaced it.

  3. Before your next "ideas conversation," ask one question: What is the specific, concrete outcome I need from this conversation, and does it justify the cost? If you can't answer that clearly, skip the meeting.

  4. Adjust how you journal. If you journal about goals, aspirations, or things you want to build, add a step: read the entry back before you close the notebook. Re-internalize what you vented.

  5. Lean into introversion deliberately. If you're naturally quiet, stop treating that as a problem to fix. The next time you feel the pull to post about something you're working on, or announce a plan before it's real, recognize that impulse and let it pass. The energy it would have cost stays with you.

  6. Track the correlation in your own history. Think of two or three things you actually followed through on. How much did you talk about them beforehand? Now think of something you talked about extensively that never happened. The pattern is probably already there — you just haven't looked at it directly.
