Summary

TL;DR — The feeling that you're fundamentally unacceptable to others usually starts as a conclusion you drew about yourself in childhood, not an accurate read of reality. And the coping strategy you built around that belief — filtering yourself, making the case for your own value before anyone asks, pre-empting rejection — is quietly running your life.

This is a deep, wide-ranging conversation about where the feeling of being unacceptable comes from, how the mind tries to protect itself from rejection, and why the very strategies that let you survive that pain eventually trap you. It covers the "some scar" (a part of your mind frozen at the age a wound formed), the double bind of believing you're both good and worthless at the same time, and what it actually looks like to let go of a coping strategy that has — in a twisted way — been working.

The Core Wound: Deciding You Are Unacceptable

There's a specific, painful logic that forms in childhood. You notice that the world outside you seems fine — other people, circumstances, relationships — but the responses you get are consistently off. People look at each other when you talk. The adults who are supposed to love you can't find what you're saying interesting. The parent who should be your compass keeps saying this kid isn't going to make it.

From inside a child's mind, the only explanation that makes sense is: the problem is me. Not any one thing about me — everything about me. The unacceptability becomes generalized. It isn't "I'm bad at homework" or "I'm bad at reading social cues." It's "I am, at the core, unacceptable."

Once that belief is in place, it shapes everything. You stop letting people form a genuine judgment of you and make it on their behalf, in advance. You already know the verdict. You just need to figure out how to survive it.

The Some Scar: Why This Keeps Happening in the Present

When a wound like this forms early, it doesn't just become a memory — it becomes a mode. A "some scar" is a part of your mind that is essentially frozen at the age the wound was formed. When something in the present resembles the original situation — a person in authority, a moment of potential rejection, someone who seemed safe behaving unpredictably — your mind snaps back to that age.

"When a some scar activates, it returns your mind to the age at which it was formed."

This is why you can feel, in the middle of a work meeting, like you have no body, no internal structure, no sense of yourself as an adult. A four-year-old doesn't have internal structure. That's who is suddenly running the show.

The some scar isn't wrong, exactly. It's just a piece of your experience that hasn't been updated. The issue is that most people don't realize it's a separate part of them — they identify with it. They think the I-just-don't-want-to, or the I'm-not-good-enough, is them. It isn't. It's a scar.

Filtering: The Strategy That Seems Like Safety

Once the belief "I am unacceptable" is running in the background, the logical next step is damage control. If people see the real me, they'll reject me. So the task becomes: filter what gets out. Show only the defensible parts. Lead with the evidence, the documentation, the track record. Make the case before anyone asks. Preemptively acknowledge your own incompetence so the conversation can skip past it to the parts that might actually land.

"I feel like I've already made that decision on their behalf."

This feels like protection, but it has a serious problem: it means you are never actually letting people accept you. You're letting them accept a curated presentation. And on some level you know that, which means any acceptance you receive doesn't really count — because they didn't see the real thing.

There's also a subtler trap. When you're raised by someone who genuinely couldn't see your value, your internal compass gets calibrated to that. When someone does accept you — genuinely, wholeheartedly — it feels wrong. Confusing. Suspicious. You can't quite believe it.

"The safe people are the ones who can accept someone even like me."

So the group of people you trust becomes very small, and the belief that most people will reject you stays perfectly intact — not because it's being confirmed, but because you're filtering out the evidence that contradicts it.

The Double Bind

Here's where it gets genuinely painful. There are two things running at the same time:

  1. A deep, persistent sense that you are fundamentally unacceptable.
  2. An equally deep sense that you are good — that you have something real to offer, that your intentions are pure, that you genuinely want to help.

These two beliefs don't resolve each other. They just create a constant internal war. You want to share the good thing. You also know it's going to get rejected. You try to control how it comes out. The trying comes with panic. The panic narrows your awareness. And in the middle of all of it, the person in front of you experiences someone who is simultaneously desperate and abrasive and overwhelming and guarded.

"There's something pure that's just going to get kicked — like an innocent thing that for whatever reason I can't protect."

When someone finally does seem to accept your ideas, the dam breaks. Everything floods out at once. They get overwhelmed. They pull back. And the original belief is confirmed again.

Why You Don't Let Go of What's Hurting You

The coping strategy — the premise of unacceptability, the filtering, the case-building — is genuinely painful. But it also works, in a specific, limited sense. It has gotten you here. It has produced real results. And more importantly, it protects you from the most terrifying possibility of all.

"Growing up is the process of abandoning what works."

If you hold on to the premise that you're unacceptable, you can survive rejection. You already knew it was coming. You built for it. What you cannot survive — or so it feels — is letting yourself hope, genuinely, that you might be accepted, and then finding out you were wrong. That's the unbearable version. So you never fully commit. You hedge. You study for the test without studying as hard as you can, so if you fail, at least you're not actually dumb.

The same logic applies to love and relationships. An abusive person who grows up being told they're worthless will sometimes sabotage a loving relationship — not because they want to be hurt, but because the familiar game is survivable and the unfamiliar one (being genuinely loved) is not. You know the rules of rejection. You don't know the rules of acceptance.

Letting go of the premise means giving other people their agency back — letting them actually decide whether they accept you or not, without you rigging the outcome in advance. That's terrifying. But it's the only way the good thing inside you actually gets out.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming the feeling is accurate. The belief "I am unacceptable" feels like a conclusion drawn from evidence. It isn't. It's a conclusion drawn from the perspective of a small child trying to make sense of adult behavior that had nothing to do with them. A five-year-old cannot accurately judge an eight-year-old's intentions. A child cannot accurately interpret a stressed parent's explosions.

  • Thinking that filtering is working. You may believe you're successfully hiding the "bad" parts and presenting only the acceptable ones. But if you're abrasive, guarded, and overwhelming when you're anxious — which you likely are — people are already seeing past the filter. They're seeing more difficulty than what's actually inside you, not less. The filter is creating a negative impression, not a positive one.

  • Confusing the some scar with your whole self. The part of you that says "I'm not acceptable" is not a truth about who you are. It's a learned response that formed at a specific time under specific circumstances. It wants to dictate your actions. It doesn't have to.

  • Using the Dharma as a workaround. Having a real sense of purpose — genuinely wanting to help people, building things that matter — is not the same as being acceptable. The purpose is real. But using it as a reason why people should tolerate you conflates two separate things. You don't need a reason for people to accept you. You need to let them.

  • Interpreting selective evidence. When you discover that someone you trusted wasn't trustworthy, it confirms the belief that your judgment is bad and your hope was stupid. But this reasoning only runs in one direction. You don't apply the same scrutiny to the many times your judgment has been right, or the many people who have stayed.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Notice the trying. Throughout the day, catch the moments when you're trying to control how someone perceives you. You don't need to stop it immediately — just notice it. Notice the low-level panic that usually accompanies it. Name it: this is the some scar activating.

  2. Ask yourself how old you feel. When you're in a difficult interaction — at work, with someone in authority, in a moment of potential rejection — pause and ask: do I have a sense of my body right now? Do I feel like an adult? If the answer is no, recognize that you've temporarily returned to the age at which the wound formed. The thoughts and feelings happening right now are coming from the past, not the present.

  3. Stop making the case. Before sharing an idea, notice if you're leading with a defense — "I know you probably won't agree with this, but..." or "I understand this might seem wrong given my track record..." That's pre-emptive case-building. Try presenting the idea without the preamble. Put it in the display case. Let people choose.

  4. Give other people their agency. Pick one interaction where you would normally try to manage someone's perception of you. Instead, say what you actually think and let them respond however they respond. Don't flood them afterward. Don't interpret their reaction through the lens of "I knew this would happen." Just let it land and observe.

  5. Practice the nostril-switching breath observation. Lie down on the side of your currently more-open nostril. Breathe quietly and try to catch the exact moment your breath shifts to the other side — there will be one breath where both nostrils are briefly equally open. Sit in that space as long as you can. The goal isn't relaxation; it's learning to live in the in-between, in the tension of two opposing pulls, without immediately collapsing to one side. This is the same skill you need for the internal double bind.

  6. Let the four-year-old be there without letting them drive. When the some scar activates, you don't have to argue with it or suppress it. Acknowledge it: there's a part of me that believes I'm unacceptable right now. Then act anyway. The two can coexist. You don't have to resolve the belief before you're allowed to speak.
