Summary

TL;DR — If every job burns you out, the problem might not be the jobs. It might be the pace you're forcing on yourself.

This is a conversation with Nick, someone who spent years job-hopping because he was convinced every workplace was the problem. Through introspection and therapy, he discovered something simpler and harder to accept: he was the one burning himself out. The talk explores how understanding your personal motivational style — specifically through the Ayurvedic concept of doshas — helped him stop working against his own nature, and what that shift actually looked like in practice.

The Common-Denominator Question

When you hate every job you've ever had, there are two possible explanations. Either all jobs are bad — plausible, but bleak — or you're bringing something to each one that makes it painful. Nick noticed that he was the common thread and started asking what he was carrying with him.

What he found wasn't laziness or incompetence. It was the opposite: he was working at an "absurd pace," self-imposing a relentless quota that nobody else had asked for. The work wasn't burning him out. He was burning himself out.

This distinction matters because if the problem is external — a bad boss, a toxic culture, a boring industry — the only fix is to leave. But if the problem is something you bring with you, leaving just resets the clock on the same pattern.

Working Against Your Own Nature

The lens that helped Nick make sense of this came from Ayurveda, specifically the idea of doshas — three fundamental "forces" or motivational styles that the system uses to describe individual differences in how people function. It's worth being clear about the evidence here: while some Ayurvedic treatments have been studied scientifically, the core dosha framework has only very preliminary genetic support, with small effect sizes. These are heuristics — useful tools for self-understanding, not biopsy-confirmed biological categories.

With that caveat on the table, many people find the framework genuinely clarifying. The three doshas are associated with different elemental qualities and, practically speaking, different motivational styles:

The Quotas Nobody Gave You

One of the most practically useful things Nick discovered: most of the pressure crushing him wasn't assigned by anyone.

"I realized that this quota was not something given to me. It was something I was giving myself."

He was volunteering for a version of his job that was harder than the actual job. This is remarkably common. The anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → that you'll be left behind if you don't sprint, that you need to be the office star, that slowing down is the same as falling behind — these feelings are real, but they're often not accurate descriptions of what's actually being asked of you.

The mind's assessment of a work situation and the situation itself are not the same thing. Ego shows up in subtle ways at work: taking on unrequested responsibilities to feel indispensable, working harder when you perceive someone outshining you, needing credit in ways that quietly multiply your workload. Noticing where your mind is adding to the pile is different from noticing that the pile is too big.

What Slowing Down Actually Looks Like

Knowing you should slow down and actually doing it are different problems. Nick found two things that made it work:

1. Accepting that the experiment was worth running. His first fear was that slowing down would cause him to fall behind. But that fear was based on quotas he'd invented himself. The only way to test whether he was right was to actually try a different pace and see what happened. Quality increased significantly when he did.

2. Being transparent with the people around him. Rather than quietly hoping no one noticed he worked differently, he told his co-workers and supervisor directly: I do really good work, but I have to take my pace. This kind of honesty feels risky, but it removes the exhausting performance of pretending to be a different type of worker than you are.

Slowing down required constant self-reminding at first — it wasn't a switch he flipped once. The pull toward rushing felt like second nature. That's worth knowing in advance: adjusting to your actual nature takes practice when you've spent years overriding it.

Sharing Positivity Without Pushing It

Nick's second question was about being naturally optimistic in a way that doesn't alienate people who are struggling — the problem of toxic positivity.

The mechanics of it often come down to a single word: "but."

"It sucks, but things are temporary."

The word "but" erases everything before it. In therapy training, it's actively removed from the vocabulary when trying to be empathetic. Replace it with "and" — which holds both realities at once instead of canceling one out.

The deeper issue is directional. Positivity becomes toxic when it's something you do to people — an injection of hope, a correction of their perspective, a project to get them to see things your way. That's a spotlight. What actually helps is being a light bulb: diffuse, steady, present. People who are suffering aren't at the same place you are. You can't explain your way there for them, and trying to rush them mirrors exactly the mistake Nick was making with himself.

"If you want to be a positive person, it's not something you do to other people."

Lead by example rather than explanation. Let people sit in your vicinity. They'll come to you — they're probably already sensing something, which is why they bring their problems to you in the first place.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming the pattern is the job's fault until you've really sat with whether it's yours. Externalizing is easier and sometimes correct — but if the pattern repeats across very different environments, it's worth turning the lens inward.

  • Treating Ayurvedic types as scientific fact. The dosha framework is a useful heuristic, not a confirmed biological reality. Use it if it illuminates something; don't build a rigid identityA narrative you construct by stringing together emotionally salient experiences — the moments in your life that carried the most emotional charge — into a coherent story of who you are.Full definition → around it.

  • Confusing slowing down with falling behind. Many of the performance standards driving burnout are self-imposed. Before assuming the quota is real, check whether anyone actually set it.

  • Using "but" when you're trying to validate someone. It reads as dismissal even when the intent is encouragement.

  • Trying to transfer your hard-won perspective to someone else too quickly. You can't fast-forward someone's growth by explaining what you've learned. Patience with others is an extension of the same patience you're learning to have with yourself.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the common-denominator audit. List every job or role that burned you out. Write down what the experience felt like and what pace or standard you were holding yourself to. Look for what's consistent across all of them — that's what you brought with you.

  2. Identify your self-imposed quotas. For one week, each time you feel behind or overwhelmed at work, ask: Was this actually assigned to me, or did I assign it to myself? Keep a simple log. The pattern will surface quickly.

  3. Experiment with your pace deliberately. Pick one recurring task and do it at 70% of your normal speed. Evaluate the output honestly. You may find quality holds or improves — that data is more useful than the assumption.

  4. Explore the dosha framework as a self-reflection tool.   5. Remove "but" from empathetic conversations. For one week, every time you catch yourself about to say "I hear you, but..." replace it with "I hear you, and..." Notice how the conversation shifts.

  6. Practice being a light bulb, not a spotlight. If you're a naturally optimistic person, resist the urge to redirect struggling friends toward hope.
