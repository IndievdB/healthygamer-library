Why Life Feels Like Pointless Suffering — and Why Coping Won't Fix It

The instinct to ask "why should I live if life is just suffering?" is understandable, but the framing itself is the problem. Throwing someone who can't swim into a pool and telling them to cope is not a solution — the solution is to learn how to swim.

The world has changed faster than our education has. Without that existential pressure, something else has to give life meaning, and nobody told us what that something is.

Spirituality as the Study of the Self (Not as Religion)

Spirituality, properly understood, has nothing to do with God. It is the discipline of understanding yourself — the personal equivalent of what psychology does at the population level. Psychology is like economics: it describes how people in general behave. Spirituality is personal finance: it's about your inner life, your reactions, your source of suffering and satisfaction.

This is why even a committed atheist has every reason to engage in it.

First: all experience happens inside you. You cannot enjoy anything outside of yourself. The Epidemic of Male Loneliness — A Structural Problem, Not a Personal Failing

The framing that AI girlfriends are ruining a generation of men gets it backwards. A market product appears when a market need exists. The ruin already happened. The AI girlfriend is the end-stage symptom, not the cause.

What actually produced this situation is a stack of structural changes, none of which is the "fault" of any individual group:

  • The demonization of male physical and emotional contact. When homophobia peaked in the 80s and 90s, men stopped being able to touch each other, joke around physically, or be emotionally close without it being sexualized or stigmatized. Men became almost exclusively dependent on romantic relationships for physical and emotional connection — and when those relationships aren't available, they're completely isolated.
  • The disappearance of third spaces and positive male role models. Older men shaping younger men's behavior has always been the mechanism by which adolescent male aggression and aimlessness gets corrected — the data on this comes from studies of delinquent male elephant behavior as much as from human sociology. That mentoring relationship has become culturally difficult and legally fraught.
  • Expectations haven't adjusted to match a changed world. Women have rightly gained independence, careers, and the ability to set higher standards for partners. But the expectations placed on men have not updated in the other direction. Men are told they need emotional availability, financial security, and to be tall — two of those three they mostly can't control, and the emotional availability one is the cruelest demand, because we've never taught men how to access or express emotion, and then punish them socially when they try.

What Real Equality Looks Like Here

The lesson from the progress made on behalf of women over the last 50 years is that neglected suffering doesn't fix itself; it requires the active attention of everyone. Men supporting the civil rights movement. Allies showing up for causes that weren't personally their problem.

The same principle applies now. Men's lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → is not the fault of women. It is also not solely men's problem to solve in isolation. Any part of society that is suffering deserves the attention and help of everyone else — not because anyone is responsible for creating the problem, but because that is what functional communities do.

The one group we currently exempt from this social contract — the one group we say should pull itself up without assistance — is men. And that exemption is precisely what makes the problem intractable.

Common Pitfalls

Mistaking "look within" for "do whatever you feel like." The instruction to pay attention to yourself is not an instruction to satisfy every impulse. Following your impulses blindly produces the same suffering as ignoring yourself entirely — just in different flavors. The practice is to notice, distinguish between surface cravings and deeper valuesSomething you genuinely care about that, when you move toward it, produces a felt sense of fulfillment — not just a vague wish, but the force that actually gets you out of the chair and moving.Full definition →, and choose accordingly.

Thinking spirituality requires religion — or that religion automatically provides it. Spirituality in this framework is the study of the self. Religion, when disconnected from that practice, becomes a set of behavioral rules without understanding. You can be religious without being spiritual, atheist without missing out on spiritual practice, or anything in between.

Treating "coping" as the goal. Coping with the feeling of not being able to swim is not the same as learning to swim. If life feels like constant suffering, the answer is not a better coping mechanism — it's understanding what is generating the suffering in the first place.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Tolerate boredomA stimulus-seeking emotional state your brain manufactures to punish you for not engaging in dopamine-producing activities.Full definition → deliberately. Pick one daily window — a commute, a meal, the first ten minutes after waking — and do not fill it with your phone or external stimulation. Notice what thoughts arise. Write them down if that helps. Do this consistently for two weeks. The question you're trying to answer is: what do I actually care about?

  2. Track your end-of-day feeling, not your in-the-moment feeling. For the next two weeks, note how you feel at 10 PM relative to how you spent the day. Look for the pattern: which kinds of days leave you content, and which leave you hollow? This is empirical data about your own eudaimonic system.

  3. When you feel pulled to do something or avoid something, ask: does this want come from outside me (social pressure, advertising, fear of judgment) or from inside me? And if it comes from inside: is this a surface craving or something I'll feel good about at day's end? You don't need to always choose the harder thing — you need to make the distinction consciously.

  4. Practice cognitive reframingDeliberately shifting how you interpret an event to change its emotional impact — not by forcing positivity, but by questioning whether your automatic reading of the situation is actually accurate.Full definition → on one recurring frustration. Pick something that reliably irritates or discourages you. Write down the story your mind tells about it (e.g., "if I fail this test, my whole future is ruined"). Then write out, as specifically as possible, what is literally true versus what your mind has added. Notice the difference between the fact and the meaning you've attached to it.

  5. If you're a man who is isolated: find one in-person context with other men. Not online. A sport, a class, a recurring activity. The goal is not deep emotional conversation immediately — it's proximity and shared activity, which is how male friendship has always worked. Start there.

  6. If you're consuming content about why your life is hard, audit it. Ask: after I watch or read this, do I feel clearer about what to do, or do I feel more resentful and stuck? Echo chambers that validate suffering without pointing toward action are making the problem worse, not naming it helpfully.
