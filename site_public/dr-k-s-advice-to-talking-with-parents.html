Summary

TL;DR — When telling your parents about your mental health struggles, keep the opening simple, ask what they understand rather than explaining yourself, and let go of the expectation that they'll respond the way you need them to.

Telling the people who raised you that you've been depressed or suicidal is one of the heaviest conversations imaginable — especially when part of you suspects they contributed to the problem, and you're not sure they'll respond well. This piece walks through the principles behind having that conversation: how to open it, what to actually say, where the real pain comes from, and how to protect yourself emotionally going in.

Keep the Opening Short and Simple

The instinct before a hard conversation like this is to over-prepare — to explain everything, preempt every misunderstanding, and cushion every sharp edge before they even have a chance to react. That instinct tends to make things worse.

The actual opening doesn't need to be more than two or three sentences:

"Mom, Dad — there's a part of my life I haven't shared with you because I've been afraid of your reaction. I was suicidal when I was younger, and I've been struggling with depression for years — like my sister. I just wanted you to know."

That's it. No lengthy justifications. No "it's not your fault" disclaimers (which, paradoxically, just puts the idea of blame front and center). No full history of your therapy or diagnosis. One clear, honest statement. The goal of the first conversation isn't to resolve everything — it's simply to open the door.

Ask What They Understand Before You Explain Anything

After the opening, the most useful thing you can do is ask a question:

"What does that mean to you — when I say I was suicidal and that I've been depressed?"

Then listen. Really listen.

This does a few things at once. It tells you exactly where they are — what they actually understand about mental health, what assumptions they're bringing in, whether their reaction is about you or about their own confusion and guilt. It also slows the conversation down before it can spiral into defensiveness or blame.

People who have never engaged with mental health concepts often react badly not because they don't care, but because they genuinely don't know what depression means. That's a separate battle from any personal history between you and them. Figuring out which one you're dealing with early saves you from fighting the wrong fight.

Your Expectation Is a Large Part of Your Suffering

This is the piece that's easy to skip over and hard to sit with: a lot of the pain around this conversation happens before it even starts.

If you go in expecting your parents to finally get it — to respond with warmth, to say the right thing, to validate what you went through — and they don't, the gap between what you hoped for and what you got can feel like a wound on top of a wound. The conversation can even feel like proof that nothing will ever change.

The relief you're looking for here probably won't come from their reaction. It's more likely to come from the act of no longer carrying the secret — from detachment rather than validation. You can't control who they are. You can only take the first step.

That reframe isn't a consolation prize. It's actually more freeing, because it takes the outcome out of their hands.

Why Shame and Self-Blame Run So Deep

One of the more painful dynamics in families like this is the way a parent's beliefs become a child's internal voice. When a child is repeatedly made to feel like a burden — even subtly, even without anyone intending it — the brain learns to interpret its own existence as an inconvenience.

Freud described depression as anger turned inward. When the people around you constantly model blame and shame as the explanation for everything that goes wrong, you internalize that model and apply it to yourself. The self-critical voice in your head isn't an objective narrator. It's a parrot that learned what to say from the people around it.

"These thoughts are like a parrot that is just repeating what it's heard. They're your parents' thoughts — they're not actually a measure of your value."

Recognizing this doesn't make the voice go away immediately. But it changes your relationship to it. The thoughts stop being truth and start being echoes.

You Have to Blame Before You Can Forgive

There's a tendency, especially if you're a thoughtful or empathetic person, to jump straight to "I understand why they were the way they were" without ever letting yourself feel that they actually caused harm. That's not forgiveness — it's avoidance dressed up as maturity.

Real forgiveness requires naming the thing first. It requires being honest that yes, some of what happened to you was a result of how you were raised — that the way you were made to feel like a burden, the emotional unavailability, the stigma around seeking help — these things did a number on you.

You don't have to arrive at forgiveness right away. That can be a years-long process. But you can't skip the step of honest acknowledgment. Skipping it just buries the resentment where it's harder to deal with.

Two Separate Battles in One Conversation

When you tell a parent with no mental health literacy that you've been depressed, you're not fighting one battle — you're fighting two.

The first is the personal one: the family history, the emotional distance, the feelings of being a burden, the fear of judgment. This is the wound that's specific to your relationship.

The second is the cultural one: the stigma that says people who go to therapy are "crazy," that mental illness is weakness, that suffering is something you caused yourself. This has nothing to do with you personally and nothing to do with them personally — it's just a belief system they absorbed and never had reason to question.

Separating these two in your mind is useful. It means that a dismissive or confused reaction isn't necessarily a verdict on whether you matter to them. Sometimes it's just ignorance, and ignorance can change over time.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the first conversation like it has to solve everything. It doesn't. The first conversation is just an opening. Give it room to be incomplete.
  • Pre-loading the talk with reassurances like "it's not your fault." This often backfires, making the conversation about their guilt rather than your experience.
  • Going in hoping for validation. If your parents struggle with mental health stigma, they may not be capable of giving you what you need in that first moment. Hoping for it and not getting it will hurt more than going in with realistic expectations.
  • Confusing your inner critic with the truth. The voice that says you're a burden, that you're causing problems by bringing this up, that you're taking space that isn't yours — that voice was learned. It is not a reliable narrator.
  • Skipping blame on the way to forgiveness. Trying to be understanding before you've been honest about the hurt doesn't heal anything. It just postpones it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write out your two or three opening sentences before the conversation. Keep it factual and brief: what you've been going through, how long it's been, and that you wanted them to know. Read it back. If it's more than a short paragraph, cut it down.

  2. Prepare the question you'll ask after your opening. Something like: "When I say I've been depressed — what does that mean to you?" Have it ready so you don't fill the silence with more explaining.

  3. Notice your expectations going in. Before the conversation, write down what you're hoping they'll say. Then ask yourself: given who they are and what they understand, how likely is that response? Adjust your expectations accordingly — not to give up, but to protect yourself.

  4. Identify which battle you're dealing with. After the conversation, try to separate their response into two categories: personal (about your relationship and history) and cultural (about mental health stigma and ignorance). These require different responses from you.

  5. Catch the parrot. When your inner critic speaks up — especially around this topic — try naming it: "That's a thought I learned, not a fact about me." You don't have to argue with it or replace it with a positive affirmation. Just notice it came from somewhere outside you.

  6. Lean on your therapist before and after. This is exactly the kind of conversation worth processing with a professional — both to prepare for it and to make sense of how it actually went.
