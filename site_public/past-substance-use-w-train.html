The Trap Door After the Ego Dissolves

There's one more obstacle worth naming, because it's almost invisible: the ego of having no ego.

The moment you genuinely start losing your ego — when you begin to see yourself clearly, feel humbled, understand that you're not exceptional — that very experience can become the new pedestal. Look how much I've grown. Look how self-aware I am. Most people can't do what I just did.

Without noticing, you've used the loss of ego as new evidence that you're different. The justification returns in a more sophisticated disguise.

Common Pitfalls

Believing controlled use means safe use. Functioning during use — maintaining grades, a job, a social life — isn't evidence of safety. It's evidence of how good the denial is, and it masks the baseline shift happening underneath.

Treating the first high as representative. The first experience is neurologically exceptional and will never be reproduced. Every subsequent use is chasing something that no longer exists, with diminishing returns and escalating risk.

Mixing drug classes. Benzodiazepines (Xanax, etc.) and opiates together suppress breathing synergistically. This combination is frequently lethal. Returning to a previous dosage after a period of sobriety can also be fatal because toleranceThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → resets.

Thinking relapse means starting over. The internal growth doesn't zero out with a relapse. Treating it as a total loss produces shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition → that makes continued recovery harder, not easier.

Confusing "not judging yourself" with healing. Clarity about what you're actually doing is necessary. Softening everything into "it's fine, I'm on a journey" is just denial with better vocabulary.

Assuming someone else's recovery path works for you. Train's anchor was a specific dream he'd carried since childhood. That anchor worked for him because it was genuinely his. Borrowing someone else's reason to quit is information, not understanding — it won't hold under real pressure.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Audit your baseline coping mechanism. When you're stressed, sad, or bored, what's the first thing your mind reaches for? Write it down honestly. If it's a substance, identify when that became the default, and notice what it displaced.

  2. Map your selective evidence. If you're currently telling yourself "I have it under control," write down every piece of evidence your mind uses to support that. Then, separately, write down everything your mind has been quietly ignoring. Look at the second list.

  3. Find your anchor — something you love more than the escape. This doesn't have to be grand. It needs to be real and genuinely yours. If you can't identify one, that's important information about what work needs to happen before or alongside stopping.

  4. After any relapse or setback, ask: what is the real-me noticing? Train described waking up after a bender and thinking "what am I doing?" That moment of clarity is not a failure — it's the recovery happening. Write it down. Take it seriously. It's the part of you that's getting stronger.
