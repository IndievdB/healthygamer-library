The Fear Is a Mirage

AnxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition → about pooping in public feels overwhelming, but it has an interesting property: the closer you examine it, the weaker it gets.

A lot of fears are nebulous — meaning they exist as a vague, looming dread rather than a concrete, logical threat. Like a mirage, they look solid from a distance but start to dissolve as you approach them. This is especially true for social anxieties, where the feared outcome is something like humiliation or judgment from other people.

The way to test this is a technique called "play the tape through to the end." Instead of letting the fear sit as a fuzzy, unexamined dread, you follow it forward in your mind, step by step, and actually ask: then what?

Playing the Tape Through

Here's how the logic unfolds when you actually follow the fear:

  • Someone hears a sound from your stall. Then what?
  • They know someone is pooping. Then what?
  • They might think about it for a moment. Then what?
  • Are they going to bring it up to you? No.
  • But what if they know it was you specifically? How would they even know?
  • Maybe they'd look at your shoes under the stall door...

And right there, the logic starts to get weird. You're now imagining a coworker actively crouching down to peer under a bathroom stall to identify you by your shoes. When you actually follow the fear to its conclusion, the chain of events required for your worst-case scenario to come true becomes increasingly implausible.

This isn't about dismissing the fear or telling yourself it's stupid. It's about genuinely walking through it. By making the fear specific and concrete in your mind, you strip away the vague dread that gives it power.

The process looks like this:

  1. Identify the exact fear (someone will hear me)
  2. Ask what happens next (they'll know I was pooping)
  3. Keep asking what happens next (they'll think less of me / they'll know it was me)
  4. Follow each step until you hit something that doesn't actually hold up under scrutiny

In many cases, you'll find the fear simply runs out of road. The catastrophe at the end of the tape never materializes into anything real.

Why This Kind of Anxiety Develops

Working from home during an extended period — like during COVID lockdowns — essentially let people optimize entirely for comfort. Pooping at home, in private, with no time pressure and no audience, becomes the baseline. The body and nervous system adapt to that as normal.

Returning to an office environment doesn't automatically reset that baseline. The nervous system has learned that privacy and comfort are available, and now it's treating the absence of those things as a threat signal. This isn't a character flaw or excessive sensitivity — it's the nervous system doing exactly what it's designed to do: flag situations that deviate from the established norm.

Understanding this doesn't fix it immediately, but it reframes it. You're not broken. You're recalibrating to a context your nervous system temporarily forgot.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Play the tape through, on paper. Write down your specific fear in one sentence. Then write the next step, and the next, following the chain of consequences all the way to the end. Keep asking "and then what?" until you either reach a genuinely catastrophic outcome (unlikely) or the chain runs out (likely). Do this once when you're calm, not in the middle of the anxietyA normal physiological response in which the brain prepares you for high-stakes or unfamiliar situations — triggering the sympathetic nervous system, releasing adrenaline and cortisol, and narrowing your attention onto…Full definition →.

  2. Use the toilet paper trick. Before you sit down, lay a few sheets of toilet paper across the surface of the water. This absorbs the impact and eliminates splash noise.   3. Practice staying instead of leaving. When the urge to leave or wait kicks in, try staying for 30 more seconds before making a decision. You don't have to force yourself to finish — just practice not immediately fleeing. Gradual exposure, even at this small scale, reduces the reinforcement of avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious act of moving away from a negative thing — not making a deliberate choice, but fleeing a feeling.Full definition →.

  4. Notice when the fear gets specific and weird. As you play the tape through, pay attention to the moment the logic starts to require unlikely things — like a coworker inspecting shoe profiles under stall doors. That moment of "wait, this is kind of absurd" is useful information. Let yourself notice it without judgment.

  5. Give yourself a recalibration timeline. If you've been working from home for a long time, your nervous system genuinely adapted. Expect that readjustment takes time and repeated low-stakes exposures, not a single act of willpower.
