Summary

TL;DR — Psychedelics aren't one thing — each is a different "equalizer" setting that turns specific neurotransmitters and brain regions up or down, and the neuroscience determines the experience. Understanding which circuits a substance moves explains everything: why one heals trauma and another triggers panic, why set and setting are load-bearing, and why deep meditation reaches similar places by reshaping the brain rather than just switching on what's already there.

Stop asking "are psychedelics good or bad?" Start asking "what does this molecule do to these circuits?" That single shift reframes the danger, the therapeutic potential, and the relationship to spiritual practice.

The Equalizer: How to Think About Psychedelics

Picture an audio equalizer — sliders, gain, gating. A psychedelic doesn't add something foreign; it grabs the sliders and moves them, and each substance has its own signature pattern.

"Each of them is like an equalizer for our brain... Some things get amplified, some gates that are shut off will get turned on. Some gates that are on get shut off."

Two layers combine to produce the felt experience: neurotransmitters (which chemical signals get boosted or blocked) and brain regions (which circuits activate or suppress). The insula gives body-sense — some drugs ramp it up (embodiment), others damp it (disembodiment). In PTSD, an overactive amygdala and suppressed hippocampus make trauma arrive as overwhelming feeling; MDMA reverses this, opening memory access while calming the alarm.

We measure subjective effects on the 5D-ASC scale across five dimensions: oceanic boundlessness, visual restructuring, ego dissolution (including anxious ego dissolution), emotional amplification, and empathy. Each substance produces a different fingerprint — and which dimensions light up determines clinical benefit, sometimes independent of how intense the trip feels.

The Neurotransmitter Toolkit

  • 5-HT2A — workhorse of classic psychedelics. In the medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC) it reduces cognitive control — the neural basis of surrender (being "stuck in your head" is hyperactive MPFC). In the amygdala it slows threat reactivity.
  • 5-HT1 — produces the hallucinations (perception, not mood).
  • Dopamine (D2) — drives euphoria and paranoia/psychosis. This, not "lab-made vs natural," is why LSD risks bad trips and addiction more than psilocybin.
  • BDNF — grows neurons; the engine of neuroplasticity ("edit mode"). The difference between a temporary tune-up and a lasting rewrite: "I can give you an apple every day, or I can give you an apple tree."
  • Oxytocin — MDMA's ingredient; directly calms the amygdala.
  • NMDA — ketamine's door; produces dissociation, not merging.

Edit Mode: Why Psychedelics Heal And Harm

Many psychedelics put the brain into an editable, neuroplastic state — and editing cuts both ways. You can rewrite a traumatic association (apology = danger) into genuine healing. But enter edit mode while acutely unwell, with the wrong mindset, in the wrong place, and you rewire in the wrong direction — panic disorder, PTSD from the trip itself, HPPD, a permanently worsened baseline.

"If your sick brain enters into an edit mode and starts putting a bunch of stuff in there, it can actually mess you up even more."

This is why set (mindset) and setting (a tightly controlled environment with shepherds/guides) aren't optional. The clinical gold standard mirrors ancient traditions: preparation, supervised dosing with trained guides, and — critically — integration therapy afterward, the "Ctrl-S." Most people enter the neuroplastic state, close the file without saving, and snap back to square one.

Meditation vs. Psychedelics

There's real overlap — meditation reduces depression via the same 5-HT2A system, and practices like vairagya (non-attachment), shunya (disabling the default mode network), and slow breathing (high CO₂, whole-brain effect) reach similar destinations. But:

"Meditation changes the landscape of your brain. Psychedelics turn on an existing landscape."

Psychedelics bind existing receptors; meditation reshapes them over years — upregulating dopamine receptors so ordinary life becomes more joyful, yielding bliss (samadhi). And your brain makes its own DMT from serotonin; flood it from outside and endogenous production shuts down. So years of meditation and years of DMT are opposites, not equivalents — which is why traditions insist on abstinence.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "psychedelics" as one thing. LSD ≠ psilocybin ≠ MDMA ≠ ayahuasca ≠ ketamine.
  • "Natural is safe, synthetic is dangerous." LSD's risk is its D2 activity; ayahuasca's distinctiveness is its MAOI, not purity.
  • Using a psychedelic to fix yourself while acutely unwell — exactly when edit mode wires in new damage.
  • Believing the trip is the healing. Without preparation, shepherding, and consolidation, no change.
  • Misreading "low toxicity" as "safe." A huge therapeutic index says nothing about the dose that psychologically wrecks you.
  • Assuming meditation and psychedelics are interchangeable — or that doing both daily compounds benefit. Chronic psychedelic use is the opposite of contemplative training.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reframe the question: "Which neurotransmitters and regions does this specific compound move, and is that the change I actually need?"
  2. Audit your starting state honestly. Your current mental health is the biggest predictor of harm.
  3. If pursuing therapeutically, demand set, setting, shepherding — and integration therapy afterward. Anything less is recreation, not medicine.
  4. Choose the durable, endogenous path: cultivate vairagya, keep a clean low-stimulation lifestyle, and practice slow breathing/pranayama consistently over months and years.
  5. Protect your endogenous chemistry. Sustained abstinence keeps your serotonin-to-DMT machinery running; reducing stress keeps it fueled.
  6. Install the Ctrl-S habit. After any meaningful experience, ask: What did I realize? What change does it demand? What blocks living it tomorrow? Then implement it.

The Equalizer: How to Think About Psychedelics

Picture an audio equalizer — sliders for different frequencies, plus gain (amplification) and gating (the minimum signal needed to let sound through). Your brain normally runs at one configuration. A psychedelic doesn't add something foreign so much as it grabs the sliders and moves them.

Some channels get amplified. Some gates that are normally shut get forced open. Some gates that are normally open — like the amygdala's threat signal — get clamped shut. And each psychedelic has its own signature pattern of changes. That's why you cannot lump them together.

"Each of them is like an equalizer for our brain that gets modified. Some things get amplified, some gates that are shut off will get turned on. Some gates that are on get shut off. And each of the psychedelics has a unique effect on our brain."

To understand any psychedelic, look at two layers and combine them:

  1. Neurotransmitters — what chemical signals get boosted or blocked.
  2. Brain regions — what structures get activated or suppressed.

The combination produces the felt experience. Two examples:

  • The insula gives you a sense of your body. Some psychedelics ramp it up (intense embodiment); others turn it down (disembodiment).
  • In PTSD, the amygdala (emotion) is overactive while the hippocampus (memory) is suppressed — so trauma arrives as overwhelming feeling. MDMA does the reverse: it opens hippocampal memory access while damping the amygdala, letting you reach the memory without the emotional detonation.

The Five Dimensions We Measure

The subjective experience is measured with the 5-Dimensional Altered States of Consciousness scale (5D-ASC) — 94 questions ("I felt connected to a higher power," "I felt as if I no longer had a body") that collapse into roughly five dimensions:

  • Oceanic boundlessness — dissolving into something larger
  • Visual restructuring — hallucinations, patterns, distortions
  • Ego dissolution (with a subset, anxious ego dissolution)
  • Emotional amplification
  • Empathy / social warmth

Each substance produces a different fingerprint. Roughly: MDMA — high empathy, reduced anxious ego dissolution, almost no visuals. LSD — strong visuals, high anxiety risk, big emotional amplification, little social connection. Psilocybin — very high oceanic boundlessness and ego dissolution, modest empathy, mild visuals.

The deepest insight: which dimensions light up determines whether you get a clinical benefit — and a strong trip can produce no benefit at all.

The Neurotransmitter Toolkit

A few signals do most of the heavy lifting.

5-HT2A (a serotonin receptor) is the workhorse of classic psychedelics, but its effect depends on where it fires: - In general circuits it reduces anxiety and lifts mood (partly how SSRIs work). - In the medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC) — your seat of cognitive control — activating it reduces control. Being "stuck in your head" is hyperactive MPFC: the anxious mind frantically trying to control its own thoughts until the control spirals. Turning it down is the neural basis of surrender. - In the amygdala, it slows threat reactivity.

5-HT1 is a different serotonin receptor that produces hallucinations — the visual content. So 5-HT2A is mood/anxiety/identity; 5-HT1 is perception.

Dopamine (D1/D2 agonism) is where LSD diverges from psilocybin. LSD has a "messier" receptor profile including strong dopamine activity. D2 agonism drives euphoria, craving, reinforcement — and paranoia and psychosis. This is why LSD carries higher risk of bad trips and addiction — and it has nothing to do with "natural vs. lab-made."

BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is the difference between a temporary tune-up and a lasting rewrite:

"I can give you an apple every single day, or I can give you an apple tree... once we give you the apple tree, your body will automatically be plucking apples."

BDNF literally grows neurons. It's why a single supervised dose of psilocybin can shift depression for months, while ketamine drives less durable structural change and needs repeating. BDNF is the engine of neuroplasticity — the brain entering "edit mode."

Oxytocin is MDMA's special ingredient — it directly calms the amygdala (the way cuddling soothes a frightened child), enabling connection and trauma access without overwhelm. NMDA is ketamine's mechanism — a different door entirely, producing dissociation rather than oceanic merging.

Edit Mode: Why Psychedelics Heal And Harm

Many psychedelics put your brain into a neuroplastic, editable state — and editing cuts both ways.

In trauma, the brain has wired a brutal association (a parent saying "I'm sorry" right before hurting you, so apology now means danger). Psychedelics can let you enter "edit mode" and rewrite that wiring — reduce the amygdala alarm, re-engage hippocampal memory, reinterpret the narrative. Genuine healing.

But if you enter edit mode while acutely unwell, with the wrong mindset, in the wrong place, you can rewire in the wrong direction.

"If your sick brain enters into an edit mode and starts putting a bunch of stuff in there, it can actually mess you up even more."

The risk factors for a damaging experience are tied to your current mental health. So the intuitive plan — "I'm suffering, let me use this to heal me" — is exactly backwards for the unprepared. People develop panic disorder, PTSD from the trip itself, or HPPD (hallucinogen persisting perception disorder). Someone "merely" severely depressed can come out with depression plus PTSD. This is why the same substance can be medicine or wound — and why set and setting exist.

Set, Setting, and the Shepherd

Set is your mindset going in. Good outcomes correlate with already being fairly mentally healthy or being expertly guided. The "heroic dose" idea is misunderstood: it isn't just a big dose, it's a whole posture — "I'm at the peak of my health, resilient, I've done the therapy, and I'm choosing to grapple with the nature of what I am." Stripped of preparation, a heroic dose can harm as much as help.

Setting is the tightly controlled environment — what you can see, the comfort, the presence of guides. Across both clinical trials and ancient traditions you find the same thing: highly controlled settings, never someone alone in a basement.

"Using psychedelics in a healthy way is in a controlled setting. It's not you in your basement alone doing drugs. That's doing drugs."

The shepherd matters because the trip is genuinely two-directional — "profound inner peace" and "tormented, threatened, like a puppet" are both on the same questionnaire. DMT/ayahuasca frequently produce "extraplanar encounters." A skilled guide doesn't help you flee; they help you move through to the other side. The clinical gold standard mirrors the traditional one: psychotherapy before, supervised dosing with two clinicians for around eight hours, and crucial therapy afterward — costing $2,500–$5,000 for a reason.

That aftercare is the "Ctrl-S." The neuroplastic window opens, you have profound realizations — and then most people close the file without saving and snap back.

"People enter the neuroplastic state and then they close it without saving, and then they just go back to square one."

The Substances, One by One

Psilocybin — One of the few with durable single-dose effects; you can predict the response from the degree of oceanic boundlessness. Strong 5-HT2A, big BDNF increase, shuts down the default mode network and MPFC (so you stop ruminating), and produces positive lateralization in the amygdala — emotions amplified but skewed positive. It dissolves the toxic self-identity ("I'm a loser") while letting emotion flood in, so you feel sadness without concluding you are a failure. Best safety profile.

LSD — Synthetic and "messier": serotonin plus dopamine plus glutamate plus cardiovascular activity. Same DMN disruption and high oceanic boundlessness as psilocybin, but with two dangerous differences: no positive amygdala lateralization (fear is not dampened) and D2-driven paranoia/psychosis. Heavy rewiring around amplified negative emotion means more panic disorders and bad trips. It also activates the primary visual cortex (V1) independently of the eyes — which is why closing your eyes can intensify the visuals.

MDMA — An entactogen ("increase contact"). Boosts serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine, and oxytocin. You don't lose your sense of self — instead the barriers between you and others drop. Oxytocin calms the amygdala while the hippocampus opens, letting you revisit trauma without being overwhelmed — an optimal fear-extinction state that decouples the memory from the alarm. It also quiets the insula, reducing body-based panic.

Ayahuasca / DMT — Why "natural vs. synthetic" misses the point. Ayahuasca pairs DMT with a plant containing a monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI) that blocks the gut/liver from destroying ingested DMT, producing a sustained level rather than a synthetic spike-and-crash. It drives strong hippocampal and amygdala activation plus heavy insular/somatic involvement — you feel your memories in your body. The famous purging is reported as the felt experience of expelling trauma.

Ketamine — A different door (NMDA). Produces dissociation, not ego dissolution — you disappear (the "K-hole") rather than merging. Remarkably, it's the one psychedelic where the therapeutic effect is largely independent of the subjective experience. It drives some hippocampal remodeling that lifts depression, but without identity restructuring — which is why it's not the trauma tool the others are.

Identity Is a Constellation

Your sense of "I am" isn't stored in one place. It emerges from the synchronous activation of many structures — there is no "identity region." The temporoparietal junction (TPJ) integrates roughly three streams: your social self ("I'm a father"), your interoceptive self (the felt sense that "you're there"), and your decision-making self.

Normally identity = A + B + C. What psychedelics do is rewire which inputs feed the constellation. On LSD the TPJ hyperactivates and starts talking to the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex in ways it normally doesn't, while social and decision-making inputs drop out. So "I am" gets built from an entirely different set of signals — sometimes the whole alphabet of brain regions. That reshuffling is the fundamental psychedelic experience (and the root of synesthesia — "hearing a rose").

Meditation vs. Psychedelics: Overlap, Not Identity

There is real overlap. Meditation reduces depression and anxiety, which must be mediated through the serotonin (5-HT2A) system — the same family psychedelics hit. Advanced practice reaches some of the same destinations by other roads: vairagya (non-attachment) primes the 5-HT2A / oceanic-boundlessness machinery; shunya (emptiness) disables the default mode network; slow breathing raises CO₂, which has a whole-brain effect no psychedelic matches.

But here is the decisive difference:

"Meditation changes the landscape of your brain. Psychedelics turn on an existing landscape."

A psychedelic binds your existing receptors and switches on what's there. Meditation, over years, alters the number of receptors — your dopamine receptors upregulate so ordinary life becomes more joyful (the opposite of stimulants). When you reach oceanic boundlessness with hypersensitive dopamine circuitry, you get bliss — samadhi — precisely what's absent in psilocybin (and why psilocybin has low addictive potential).

And there's brutal chemistry: your brain makes its own DMT from serotonin. Flood the product from outside and the body stops producing it.

"If I ever take DMT, what will happen to my endogenous DMT production? It'll shut down."

So meditating for seven years and taking DMT for seven years aren't the same — they're opposites. This is likely why contemplative traditions insist on abstaining from psychoactive substances. The other difference is control: on a psychedelic you're strapped into the roller coaster; in meditation you're driving — you can reach the state and back off, allowing a depth of exploration that being dropped on the mountaintop by helicopter doesn't.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "psychedelics" as one thing. LSD ≠ psilocybin ≠ MDMA ≠ ayahuasca ≠ ketamine. Different receptors, circuits, and sometimes opposite effects.
  • "Natural is safe, synthetic is dangerous." LSD's higher risk comes from its dopamine (D2) activity, not from being lab-made. Ayahuasca's distinctiveness comes from an MAOI.
  • Using a psychedelic to fix yourself while acutely unwell. Edit mode plus a sick brain plus a bad mindset can carve in new damage.
  • Believing the trip itself is the healing. For most substances, the experience only helps when bracketed by preparation, shepherding, and consolidation. No Ctrl-S, no change.
  • Misreading "low toxicity" as "safe." Psilocybin's huge therapeutic index says nothing about the dose that psychologically wrecks you.
  • Thinking microdosing is harmless. The best meta-analysis suggests it's largely ineffective and possibly harmful — and a hyperactive default-mode mind can get psychologically hooked.
  • Assuming meditation and psychedelics are interchangeable. They overlap, but chronic psychedelic use is the opposite of contemplative training, not a shortcut.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reframe the question. Stop asking "are psychedelics good or bad?" Ask: "Which neurotransmitters and brain regions does this specific compound move, and is that the change I actually need?"
  2. Audit your starting state honestly. Your current mental health is the single biggest predictor of a harmful experience. Crisis is exactly when edit mode is most likely to wire in something worse.
  3. If you pursue this therapeutically, demand set, setting, and shepherding. A controlled environment, prepared mindset, trained guides, and — critically — integration therapy afterward to "save your changes." Anything less is recreation, not medicine.
  4. Choose the durable path through endogenous practice. Cultivate vairagya (non-attachment), keep a clean, low-stimulation lifestyle, and practice slow breathing / pranayama over months and years — this upregulates dopamine receptors so ordinary life becomes more joyful.
  5. Protect your endogenous chemistry. Your brain makes its own DMT from serotonin and shuts production down when flooded from outside, so abstinence is part of why deep practice works. Reducing stress and depression keeps that pathway healthy.
  6. Install the Ctrl-S habit. Insight without consolidation evaporates. After any meaningful experience, ask: What did I realize? What change does it demand? What gets in the way of living it tomorrow when we're arguing over the dishes? Then actually implement it.

Same DMN disruption and high oceanic boundlessness as psilocybin, but with two dangerous differences: no positive amygdala lateralization (so emotions amplify with no positive bias — fear is not pharmacologically dampened) and D2-driven paranoia/psychosis. Combine heavy rewiring with amplified negative emotion and you rewire around fear. Hence more panic disorders and bad trips than psilocybin. Also activates the primary visual cortex (V1) independently of the eyes — which is why closing your eyes can intensify the visuals (only the "radio" is playing) while opening them adds the competing real-world "piano," producing the muddled distortions.

MDMA — An entactogen ("increase contact"). Boosts serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine, and oxytocin. You don't lose your sense of self the way you do on LSD/psilocybin — instead the barriers between you and others drop. Oxytocin calms the amygdala while the hippocampus opens, letting you revisit trauma without being overwhelmed — an optimal fear-extinction state that decouples the memory from the alarm. It also quiets the insula, reducing the body-based panic signals that usually derail trauma processing.

Ayahuasca / DMT — A masterclass in why "natural vs. synthetic" misses the point. Ayahuasca pairs DMT with a plant containing a monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI). Normally your gut and liver destroy most ingested DMT; the MAOI blocks that breakdown. The pharmacokinetics differ sharply from a lab-made DMT pill: with the MAOI you get a sustained peripheral level alongside the brain effect, whereas a concentrated synthetic dose spikes and crashes. Ayahuasca has strong hippocampal and amygdala activation plus heavy insular/somatic involvement and visual restructuring — you feel your memories in your body. The famous purging/vomiting is reported as the felt experience of expelling the trauma — emotional release made physical.

Ketamine — Different door entirely (NMDA). Produces dissociation, not ego dissolution — you separate and disappear (the "K-hole") rather than merging with the universe. Remarkably, ketamine is the one psychedelic where the therapeutic effect is largely independent of the subjective experience — the depth of your trip barely predicts the benefit. It drives some hippocampal synaptic remodeling that lifts depression, but without identity restructuring, which is why it's not the trauma tool that the others are.

Identity Is a Constellation

The deepest single idea: your sense of "I am" isn't stored in one place. It emerges from the synchronous activation of many structures — there is no "identity region." The temporoparietal junction (TPJ) integrates roughly three streams into self-awareness: your social self ("I'm a father"), your interoceptive self (the felt sense that "you're there" when you close your eyes), and your decision-making self (the choices you make).

Normally identity = A + B + C. What psychedelics do is rewire which inputs feed the constellation. On LSD the TPJ hyperactivates but starts talking to the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex in ways it normally doesn't, while social and decision-making inputs drop out. So "I am" becomes built from an entirely different set of signals — sometimes the whole alphabet of brain regions rather than just A, B, C. That reshuffling is the fundamental psychedelic experience (and the root of phenomena like synesthesia — "hearing a rose" when the visual-association cortex fires alongside the auditory).

Meditation vs. Psychedelics: Overlap, Not Identity

Is deep meditation the same as taking psilocybin? The honest answer requires the neuroscience.

There is real overlap. Meditation demonstrably reduces depression and anxiety, which must be mediated through the serotonin (5-HT2A) system — the same family psychedelics hit. So that piece genuinely overlaps. And advanced esoteric practice reaches some of the same destinations by other roads:

  • Vairagya (cultivated non-attachment) primes the 5-HT2A / oceanic-boundlessness machinery and downregulates dopamine sensitivity.
  • Trataka (a gazing practice) can activate the visual cortex (V1) — meditation's route to "seeing with eyes closed."
  • Shunya (emptiness) practice disables the default mode network — dissolving identity.
  • Slow breathing raises CO₂, and high CO₂ has a whole-brain effect, touching essentially every receptor system at once — something no psychedelic does.
  • The mantra Aham Brahmasmi ("I am the universe"), chanted at high CO₂ with primed 5-HT2A, can directly trigger oceanic boundlessness.

But here is the decisive difference:

"Meditation changes the landscape of your brain. Psychedelics turn on an existing landscape."

A psychedelic binds your existing receptors and switches on what's already there. Meditation, done over years, alters the number of receptors and reshapes your physiology — your kidneys adapt to chronic low respiratory rate, your dopamine receptors upregulate so ordinary life becomes more joyful (the opposite of what stimulants do, which shrink those receptors and make life duller). When you finally reach oceanic boundlessness with hypersensitive dopamine circuitry, you get bliss — samadhi — which is precisely what's absent in psilocybin (and why psilocybin has low addictive potential: no dopamine reward).

And there's a brutal piece of basic chemistry. Your brain makes its own DMT from serotonin. Any chemical reaction obeys feedback: flood the product from outside and the body stops producing it.

"If I ever take DMT, what will happen to my endogenous DMT production? It'll shut down."

So meditating for seven years and taking DMT for seven years aren't the same — they're opposites. Daily exogenous psychedelics build tolerance, suppress your own production, and ramp up liver metabolism. Daily meditation does the reverse, training the brain to generate these states endogenously. This is likely why contemplative traditions insist on abstaining from psychoactive substances: a sattvic diet and abstinence keep the endogenous machinery running.

The other difference is control. On a psychedelic you're strapped into the roller coaster — you have some input, but you're being taken for a ride. In meditation you're driving: you cultivate the state, you know how to reach it and how to back off, which allows a depth of exploration that being dropped on the mountaintop by helicopter doesn't.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "psychedelics" as one thing. LSD ≠ psilocybin ≠ MDMA ≠ ayahuasca ≠ ketamine. Different receptors, different circuits, different — sometimes opposite — effects.
  • "Natural is safe, synthetic is dangerous." LSD's higher risk comes from its dopamine (D2) activity, not from being lab-made. Ayahuasca's distinctiveness comes from an MAOI, not from being "pure."
  • Using a psychedelic to fix yourself while acutely unwell. Edit mode plus a sick brain plus a bad mindset can carve in new damage — panic disorder, PTSD, a permanently shifted baseline.
  • Believing the trip itself is the healing. For most substances, the experience is only useful when bracketed by preparation, expert shepherding, and consolidation afterward. No Ctrl-S, no change.
  • Misreading "low toxicity" as "safe." Psilocybin's huge therapeutic index (you'd need an enormous dose to die) says nothing about the dose that psychologically wrecks you. "Safe" in a pharmacology paper ≠ safe in a basement.
  • Thinking microdosing is harmless. The best available meta-analysis suggests it's largely ineffective and possibly harmful — and a hyperactive default-mode-network mind can get psychologically hooked on the steady oceanic-boundlessness bump.
  • Assuming meditation and psychedelics are interchangeable, or that doing both daily compounds the benefit. They share a region of overlap, but their mechanisms are different, and chronic psychedelic use is the opposite of contemplative training, not a shortcut to it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reframe the question before you ever consider a substance. Stop asking "are psychedelics good or bad?" Ask: "Which neurotransmitters and brain regions does this specific compound move, and is that the change I actually need?"
  2. Audit your starting state honestly. Your current mental health is the single biggest predictor of a harmful experience. If you're acutely depressed, anxious, or in crisis, that is precisely when edit mode is most likely to wire in something worse — not better.
  3. If you ever pursue this therapeutically, demand set, setting, and shepherding. A controlled environment, a prepared mindset, trained guides, and — critically — integration therapy afterward to "save your changes." Anything less is recreation, not medicine.
  4. Choose the durable path through endogenous practice. To reach the genuinely transformative states, build the foundation that contemplative traditions describe: cultivate vairagya (non-attachment), keep a clean, low-stimulation lifestyle, and practice slow breathing / pranayama consistently over months and years — this upregulates your dopamine receptors so ordinary life becomes more joyful.
  5. Protect your endogenous chemistry. Because your brain manufactures its own DMT from serotonin and shuts production down when flooded from outside, sustained abstinence from psychoactive substances is part of why deep practice works. Reducing stress, anxiety, and depression also keeps that serotonin-to-DMT pathway from being starved.
  6. Whatever the path, install the Ctrl-S habit. Insight without consolidation evaporates. After any meaningful experience — meditative or otherwise — ask: What did I realize? What change does it demand? What concretely gets in the way of living it tomorrow when we're arguing over the dishes? Then actually implement it.
