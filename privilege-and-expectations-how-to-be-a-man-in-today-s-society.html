So expectations aren't the enemy. They're a tool. The problem is carrying expectations you never consciously chose — predictions about who you're "supposed" to be that someone else loaded into you.

Step One: Define Your Expectations Concretely

You can't manage what you haven't named. Good diagnosis precedes good treatment.

Most men carry vague, unfulfillable expectations. "A man is a provider." Provide what? For whom? For how long? "A man is independent." Does that mean doing your laundry, paying your bills, or never leaning on anyone emotionally?

So write them out — every expectation for what it means to be a man. Then sharpen each with business KPIs (key performance indicators) — concrete targets, timelines, measurements. How long am I supporting my siblings? How much more is "more independent"?

Two things become obvious. First, many expectations are unfulfillable by design — "provide, until when, for whom, how much more" — a system you can never satisfy. Second, you'll see a pattern:

If you distill other people's expectations of you, it's that I want you to do something, you're gonna pay for it, and I'm gonna benefit from it.

That's not automatically bad — but you need to see it clearly before you decide.

Step Two: Decide What to Embrace and What to Shed

Go through the list and ask honestly of each item:

  1. Where does this expectation come from?
  2. What do I actually get from living up to it? (Benefits needn't be material — "my kids have a better life" counts.)
  3. Which of these will make me happy?
  4. Who do I become if I live up to it — and is that who I want to be?

The goal isn't to shed everything. You can keep every item — but the moment you genuinely choose it, your relationship to it transforms. Being a provider, sacrificing for family, doing excellent work — these stay hard, but now your heart is in them.

Be ruthlessly honest, because some things you do purely for others' approval. And the people you sacrifice for often grow dependent rather than reciprocating. What have you done for me lately?

One caveat: honor your genuine obligations — child support, alimony, the actual duties of your job. The estimate is maybe 90% of what men try to live up to is not mandated; the only "punishment" for skipping it is someone being upset. That 90% is where almost all the suffering lives.

And don't half-ass what you keep:

Don't sign up for shit that you're not willing to whole-ass.

Step Three: Grieve What You Shed (Internally)

Shedding an expectation hurts — guilt, shame, feeling like a failure. This is conditioning, and the pain is real but temporary.

When you create a positive expectation — I'll get swole, everyone will be impressed, I'll get dates — you collect a hit of hypothetical positive emotion before doing any work. Shedding it requires paying that emotion back. That's what grief is. The more you attach, the harder it hurts to let go.

So prepare for the torrent — you're not a real man, the shame, the disappointment — and then do the counterintuitive thing:

Once you shed an expectation, all you have to do is nothing.

The worst move is caving, because then your brain learns you're powerless against feeling — exactly the lever others use to control you. Let the grief run its course. You'll be alive on the other side, with your life back from shame and guilt.

Step Four: Set Boundaries With the People Who Benefited

The internal grief is the easy part. The hard part is telling people you're no longer their doormat:

  • Start small. A low-stakes boundary first: "I'm not available to help you move." Period.
  • Speak respectfully and stay grounded. No resentment, no bitterness — just a calm, centered no.
  • Expect the explosion — and stay tranquil. Explosions are an attempt to induce a behavior; they've learned tantrums work. Let the energy peter out instead of rewarding it.
  • Do not over-justify. You feel guilty, so you explain until they "understand" — but until they do, you have to keep talking, and you've handed them control again. ("Don't justify your actions" tops the advice given to people navigating abusers.)
  • Listen and validate instead. "It sounds really hard. I'm sorry you're in that situation." Disarm the emotion without caving.
  • Name the loop. "We're talking in circles. I've said my piece. Let's stop here. Good luck with your move." Then step away — you always can.
  • Be transparent about the relationship. "If our friendship depends on me helping you move, that's not a friendship I want to be part of. Friendships are about reciprocityThe mutual exchange of care, effort, and goodwill between friends — not as an equal trade, but as each person contributing what they genuinely can.Full definition →."

Then the payoff. As you set limits, some people will leave — and that's a good thing. Some will never lift their own 50%, always looking for someone to do 99%. They aren't friends; they're a leak.

Here's the cruel irony: the two or three draining people you're terrified to lose ("if I lose them I'll have no one") are the very reason you have no one else. They consume all your energy and empathy, so you arrive at a happy hour already burnt out. Cut the leak, tolerate the middle stretch of guilt, and you bloom like a starved flower finally watered.

Privilege, Gratitude, and the Guilt Trap

This is where privilege actually fits — not as a stick, but as a doorway to gratitude.

Depression involves a perception bias (credited to Aaron Beck). When you can't name a single thing going right, that doesn't mean nothing is — it means your mind has filtered it out. A selection bias decides what you notice.

Privilege has been weaponized into guilt: you should be ashamed because you're privileged. Reject that. Feel guilt for your actions, not for being born with a body. But you can absolutely be grateful — and gratitude benefits you. It changes how your mind functions.

The honest version of "acknowledging privilege" is gratitude: a cup of tea, a glass of water, a breath of fresh air. Atop a freezing mountain, peeing standing up while others had to strip and squat, was a genuine flash of gratitude for being a man. That's the spirit — not self-flagellation, just noticing what you've got.

And don't try to make the feelings disappear. If gratitude brings a pang that others don't have it, good. The question is never "how do I avoid feeling bad?" — it's "how do I tolerate this?" Sadness can become compassion, and compassion can become action that makes the world better. Negative emotions are powerful tools, not things to flee.

  • Treating advantage as a debt you must pay with silence. "Privileged" is often just invalidation. You can have advantages and still struggle.
  • Playing the "who has it worse" game. Reframing your pain as the other gender's pain settles nothing.
  • Leaving expectations vague. "A man provides" with no what / for whom / how long is unfulfillable by design.
  • Assuming some expectations are inviolate. That's the conditioning talking. You decide which matter.
  • Caving to the emotion when you shed something. It teaches your brain you're powerless against feelings — handing the remote to anyone who can make you feel guilty.
  • Over-justifying a boundary. You need them to hear you, not agree.
  • Half-assing things you actually chose. If you've embraced it, whole-ass it.
  • Clinging to draining relationships out of fear of loneliness. Those energy vampires are usually why no one else can get close.
  • Demanding "how do I not feel bad?" The skill is tolerating negative emotion, not eliminating it.

  1. Write your expectation inventory. List every expectation about being a man — others' and your own. If you get stuck at four, start with four.
  2. Sharpen each into a KPI. Add targets, timelines, measurements. "Provide" becomes for whom, what, until when. This exposes which are impossible by design.
  3. Trace each expectation's origin. Where did I learn this? Who benefits? What does it cost me?
  4. Run the volition test. Is this the life I want? Who do I become — and do I want to be that person? Mark each embrace or shed. Be honest about ones you do only for approval.

Most men carry vague, unfulfillable expectations. "A man is a provider." Provide what? For whom? For how long? Food, clothing, money? Your sibling, your cousin, your cousin's aunt you're not even related to? "A man is independent." Does that mean doing your own laundry, paying your own bills, or never leaning on anyone for emotional support?

So write them out. List every expectation you can think of for what it means to be a man — what people expect of you. Then sharpen each one with something like business KPIs (key performance indicators) — concrete targets, timelines, and measurements. How long am I supporting my younger siblings? How much more independent is "independent"?

When you do this, two things become obvious. First, many of your expectations are unfulfillable by design — "provide, until when, for whom, how much more" — you've built a system you can never satisfy. Second, you'll start to see a pattern in what others expect of you:

If you distill other people's expectations of you, it's that I want you to do something, you're gonna pay for it, and I'm gonna benefit from it.

That's not automatically bad — but you need to see it clearly before you decide.

Step Two: Decide What to Embrace and What to Shed

Now go through the list and ask, honestly, of each item:

  1. Where does this expectation come from? Where did I learn to expect this of myself?
  2. What do I actually get from living up to it? (Benefits don't have to be material. "My kids have a better life" is a real, chosen benefit.)
  3. Which of these will make me happy?
  4. Who do I become if I live up to it — and is that who I want to be?

The goal isn't to shed everything. You can keep every item on the list. But the moment you genuinely choose it, your relationship to it transforms — now it's yours. Being a provider, sacrificing for family, doing excellent work — these can be fully embraced and they'll still be hard, but your heart will be in them.

Be ruthlessly honest, because some things you're doing purely to make others happy, and you don't actually enjoy them. Career choice is the classic example — people grind deep into a profession they chose for others' approval, then can't find a shred of motivation and don't understand why. And the people you're sacrificing for often don't reciprocate; they grow dependent on your sacrifice. What have you done for me lately?

One important caveat: honor your genuine obligations. Child support, alimony, the actual duties of your job — do those, and do them well. The estimate here is that maybe 90% of what men try to live up to is not legally or practically mandated. It's merely expected, and the only "punishment" for not doing it is someone being upset. That 90% is where almost all of the suffering lives — it's "the shit we carry around up here."

And don't half-ass what you keep:

Don't sign up for shit that you're not willing to whole-ass.

Step Three: Grieve What You Shed (Internally)

Shedding an expectation hurts. You'll feel guilt, shame, like a failure, like you've let yourself and everyone else down. This is conditioning, and the pain is real but temporary.

There's a clean way to understand it. When you create a positive expectation of yourself — I'm going to get swole, everyone will be impressed, I'll be sexy, I'll get all these dates — you collect a hit of hypothetical positive emotion before doing any work. Shedding the expectation requires paying that emotion back. That's what grief is. The more you love or attach, the harder it hurts to let go — it's the same mechanism.

So prepare for the torrent: you're not a real man, the shame, the disappointment. And then do the counterintuitive thing:

Once you shed an expectation, all you have to do is nothing.

This is hard, but your limbic system and amygdala will equilibrate over time on their own. The worst thing you can do is cave to the emotion, because then your brain learns that you're powerless in the face of feeling — and that's exactly the lever other people use to control you. Let the grief run its course. You'll be alive on the other side, and you'll have taken control of your life back from shame and guilt.

Step Four: Set Boundaries With the People Who Benefited

The internal grief is the easy part. The hard part is telling other people you're no longer their doormat. Here's how to do it without causing maximum damage:

  • Start small. Pick a low-stakes boundary with the person first. "I'm not available to help you move." Period.
  • Speak respectfully and stay grounded. No resentment, no bitterness, no "you women get everything for free." Just a calm, centered no.
  • Expect the explosion — and stay tranquil. When someone explodes, they're trying to induce a behavior; they've learned that tantrums get them what they want. Let their energy peter out instead of rewarding it.
  • Do not over-justify. This is where most people trip. You feel guilty, so you convince, explain, and justify — because if they understand, they won't be mad, and then you won't feel guilty. Notice the trap: you've handed them control again, because until they "understand," you have to keep talking. (Notably, "don't justify your actions" tops the list of advice given to victims navigating abusers.)
  • Listen and validate instead. "It sounds really hard. It sounds like you really need help with this. I'm sorry you're in that situation." Disarm the negative emotion without caving.
  • Name the loop when it circles. "I'm noticing we're talking in circles. I've said my piece but it seems hard to hear. Let's stop here. Good luck with your move." Then step away. You can always step away.
  • Be transparent about the relationship. "If our friendship depends on me helping you move, I understand, but that's not a friendship I want to be part of. Friendships are about reciprocity, and I don't see that here."

Then comes the payoff. As you set limits, some people will leave your life — and that's a good thing. There are people who'll never lift their own 50%, who are always looking for someone to do 99%, who become dependent on your sacrifice and drain you dry. They aren't friends; they're a leak.

Here's the cruel irony many men miss: those two or three draining people you're terrified to lose ("if I lose them I'll have no one") are the very reason you have no one else. They consume all your energy, bandwidth, and empathy, so you go to a happy hour already burnt out and can't enjoy it — dreading the lecture waiting at home. Cut the leak, tolerate the middle stretch of guilt and loneliness, and you bloom like a flower that was being starved.

Privilege, Gratitude, and the Guilt Trap

This is where privilege actually fits — not as a stick to beat yourself with, but as a doorway to gratitude.

Depression, at root, involves a perception bias (a discovery credited to Aaron Beck). When you're resentful and can't name a single thing going right in your life, that doesn't mean nothing's going right — it means your mind has filtered it out. You'll point to 5,000 negatives, but there's a selection bias deciding what you notice.

Privilege has been weaponized into guilt: you should be ashamed because you're privileged. Reject that. You should feel guilt for your actions, not for being born with a body. But you can absolutely be grateful for what you have — and the entire point is that gratitude benefits you. It literally changes how your mind functions.

The honest version of "acknowledging privilege" is gratitude: enjoy a cup of tea, a glass of water, a breath of fresh air. Atop a freezing mountain at 20,000 feet, peeing standing up while women had to strip off every layer and squat, was a genuine 10-out-of-10 flash of gratitude for being a man. That's the spirit — not sociological self-flagellation, just noticing what you've got.

And don't try to make the resulting feelings disappear. If gratitude brings a pang of sadness that others don't have it, good. The question is never "how do I avoid feeling bad?" — it's "how do I tolerate this?" Sadness can become compassion, and compassion can become action that makes the world better. Negative emotions are older and more fundamental to life than positive ones; they're powerful tools, not things to flee.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating advantage as a debt you must pay with silence. Being told you're "privileged" is often just a way to invalidate you. You can have advantages and still struggle.
  • Playing the "who has it worse" game. Reframing your pain as the other gender's pain settles nothing and helps no one.
  • Leaving expectations vague. "A man provides" with no what / for whom / how long is a setup you can never satisfy. Undefined expectations are unfulfillable by design.
  • Assuming some expectations are inviolate. This is the conditioning talking. You decide which expectations matter — not wanting to do something is different from deciding it's important to you.
  • Caving to the emotion when you shed something. This teaches your brain you're powerless against feelings, which hands the remote control to anyone who can make you feel guilty.
  • Over-justifying a boundary. Explaining yourself until the other person "understands" surrenders control. You need them to hear you, not agree with you.
  • Half-assing things you actually chose. If you've embraced an expectation, whole-ass it. Reserve "no" for what you haven't signed up for.
  • Clinging to draining relationships out of fear of loneliness. Those few energy vampires are usually the reason no one else can get close to you.
  • Demanding "how do I not feel bad?" Life is good and bad. The skill is tolerating and integrating negative emotion, not eliminating it.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write your expectation inventory. List every expectation you carry about being a man — what others expect of you and what you expect of yourself. Don't aim for completeness; if you get stuck at four, start with four.
  2. Sharpen each one into a KPI. Add concrete targets, timelines, and measurements. "Provide" becomes for whom, what, and until when. This alone exposes which expectations are impossible by design.
  3. Trace each expectation's origin. Ask: Where did I learn this? Who benefits when I fulfill it? What does it cost me?
  4. Run the volition test. For each item ask: Is this the life I want? Who do I become if I live up to it — and do I want to be that person? Mark each as embrace or shed. Be honest about the ones you're only doing for others' approval.
  5. Separate genuine obligations from mere expectations. Keep what's legally or practically mandated and do it well. Be suspicious of the rest — it's likely most of your load.
  6. Grieve a shed expectation by doing nothing. When the shame and guilt surge, don't cave and don't act. Let the emotion crest and equilibrate. Remind yourself: this is temporary, and this is me taking my life back.
  7. Practice one small boundary. Choose a low-stakes request. Say a calm, respectful "no." Don't justify. If pressed, validate their feelings ("that sounds really hard") and, if it loops, name the loop and step away.
  8. Run a daily gratitude practice. Name a few specific things you have — including small ones. Let it shift your perception bias, and let any sadness it brings turn into compassion and action, not guilt.
  9. Audit your draining relationships. Identify anyone who consistently takes 99% and gives nothing back. Notice the energy they cost you, and consider what would open up if that leak were sealed.
  10. Reframe sacrifices made for you. If someone sacrificed so you could achieve something you don't want, have the direct conversation: express genuine gratitude, then ask what they truly want for you — your happiness, or specific obedience. A real gift carries no debt; a "loan" disguised as a gift is worth naming.

Expect all of this to fail catastrophically the first several times. That's normal. It's a skill, not a switch — and tolerating other people's disappointment gets dramatically easier once you've built up emotional regulation and a sense of yourself you're genuinely grounded in.
