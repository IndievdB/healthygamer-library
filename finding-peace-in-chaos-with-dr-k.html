Summary

TL;DR — In a world flooded with engineered pleasure, the old order of building a good life no longer works: put spiritual grounding first, protect a little daily pleasure second, and money and usefulness to others follow far more easily than if you grind for them directly.

Why the Order Has to Flip

The core argument: pleasure has become hyper-concentrated. Modern games, THC, processed food, porn, and apps deliver a far larger dopaminergic signal than anything humans evolved with.

"It's not just access, it is the size of the dopaminergic signal."

So if you engage pleasure before you're grounded, you get swallowed by the binge. The new sequence:

  1. Moksha (spiritual grounding) — first.
  2. Kama (a protected daily dose of pleasure) — second.
  3. Artha (wealth) — third, and much easier once the first two are set.
  4. Dharma (service) — last, once your own house is in order.

The neuroscience fits: whether you become an addict depends on the strength of your frontal lobes versus your dopaminergic circuitry, and meditative practice strengthens the frontal lobes. "Spirituality" here means the internal work — mastering yourself. Notice that what blocks you from updating your resume and what blocks you from a good date is the same thing: you.

Protecting Pleasure, Then Letting Wealth Follow

Do everything "right" — commute, work, cook, exercise, errands — and you become a burnt husk. Many people respond by rejecting the whole system; that rarely produces peace. The healthier move: refuse to sacrifice all joy. Meditate ~an hour, carve out ~an hour of genuine pleasure daily — about 4% of your day. And structured, intentional pleasure beats the binge: one savored episode beats inhaling a season.

Show up to work un-burnt and your mood lifts performance. More importantly, a grounded person stops avoidance coping — retreating from anxiety-inducing situations until they rot. Lift your baseline and you face the resume, the interview, the hard conversation. Artha starts to feel easy. And dharma comes last because you can't pour into others from an empty self — serving from overflow is sustainable; serving from depletion is martyrdom.

Detachment Is a Trainable Skill

Detachment is the engine underneath all of this, and it's built through small acts — the yamas and niyamas that classically precede meditation (cleanliness, truthfulness, non-grasping). These aren't morality; they're a training ground for detachment.

  • Cleanliness: The dropped wrapper you'll "get later" reveals a tiny, uncharactered suffering induced by attachment — to comfort, to not moving. Pick it up anyway, every time, and you train the muscle of not being ruled by impulse.
  • Truthfulness: You lie because you're attached to an outcome. Telling the truth means saying "so be it" to the consequence and letting it land. That capacity is detachment — building consequence resistance from paper cuts up to gut punches.

Desire is fine; attachment is the problem. A desire is just a direction; attachment is how much you suffer when it goes unmet. When rejected, the clean pain is unavoidable — but the second, multiplying layer of suffering ("I'm unlovable") is optional. Detachment fully feels the pain and declines the spiral; suppression denies the pain itself. And detachment makes you more engaged, not less: loose about the outcome, fully in the action — which is also when you perform best.

It also lets you support others without drowning: care about the action that's yours, release the outcome that isn't. Lay out the situation neutrally, divide responsibility, name consequences, hand them the ball.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing wealth or "finding your dharma" first — an anxious, scattered mind has no compass to read.
  • Pursuing only moksha — usually spiritual bypassing, using spiritual language to dodge the life you're avoiding. Ask honestly what you're running from.
  • Confusing detachment with suppression — they look similar and are opposites.
  • Thinking detachment kills desire and joy — it only removes the suffering layer.
  • "I have zero time for pleasure" — usually fostered dependence and unspoken attachments, not iron necessity.
  • Over-functioning as love — repeated bailouts manufacture dependence.

  2. One hour of internal practice daily — meditation, yoga, or philosophy.
  3. One hour of intentional pleasure daily — scheduled and savored, not a numbing binge.
  4. Train on cleanliness — when "I'll do it later" appears, do it now.
  5. Train on truthfulness — catch small lies, tell the truth, practice "so be it."
  6. Audit envy — separate the want from the suffering; act on it, and if it doesn't come, so be it.
  7. Have the responsibility conversation with anyone you over-support; hand them the ball.

Detaching From Family Without Abandoning Them

Detachment Is a Trainable Skill: The Yamas and Niyamas

You build detachment through practice — specifically the yamas and niyamas, the personal and social observances that are supposed to precede meditation in classical yoga. When meditation came West, these got cut as religious morality: non-violence, non-stealing, cleanliness, truthfulness, non-grasping. But they aren't morality. They're a training ground for detachment. Live by them, consequences pile up, and learning to let those consequences come is how you level up.

Cleanliness. You miss the trash can; the wrapper lands on the floor. I'll get it later. Why? The reasons — tiring, more efficient later, not my problem — aren't real emotions. They're a tiny, uncharactered suffering induced by an attachment: to comfort, to efficiency, to not moving.

"It's just suck. A kind of uncharactered suck, in a tiny little way."

The cost is microscopic and falls only on you — which makes it perfect practice. Pick it up anyway, every time the little "I don't wanna" appears. Knock out small impulses, level by level, and eventually you can knock out a big one.

Truthfulness. Why do you lie? Because you're attached to an outcome — dodging a consequence or grabbing a reward. Stop lying and consequences arrive. To tell the truth willingly you must develop the capacity to say "so be it" and let it land. That capacity is detachment. Truth comes in tiers — white lie, bigger lie, the lie protecting something huge — and the consequences escalate from paper cut to gut punch. What you're building is consequence resistance:

"A pain? No problem. I can be happy anyway, even if things don't go well."

Once suffering no longer dictates your behavior, the game changes. Skip the drug and feel withdrawal? "So be it."

Desire Is Fine. Attachment Is the Problem.

Detachment scares people who think it means wanting nothing and feeling nothing. The distinction:

  • A desire is just a direction your brain wants to move on a given input. Wanting a hot dog, first class, the thing someone else has — all fine.
  • An attachment is how much you suffer when the desire goes unmet.

Watch the layers when you ask someone out: (1) the event — yes or no; (2) the natural feeling — pleasure or pain, normal and unavoidable; (3) the optional layer — how butthurt you get. That third layer is attachment, and it's the part you can change. Without removing it, one day of pain over a rejection metastasizes into a year of "I'm unlovable."

Proof that disappointment isn't automatic: a six-year-old offered the yellow piece or the brown sometimes shrugs and takes brown. The desire was real; the attachment was near zero. That shrug is the whole skill.

The trap runs both ways: people get the raise they craved and want another by morning. You can fulfill every desire and stay miserable — because you're attached. That's the engine of modern unhappiness.

Detachment vs. Suppression — and Why It Makes You More Engaged

Suppression denies the pain: "I wasn't that into her anyway." You shove the feeling underground. Detachment fully feels the pain and simply doesn't add the suffering on top. They look similar and are opposites.

And detachment makes you more engaged, not less. When you're highly attached, you avoid and disengage because it matters so much. Fail a test while attached to being a doctor and you collapse and hide. Fail it while detached and you stay in the action: the failure is information; what's the next move?

"I can be fully engaged with this person in this moment... The attachment is what takes us away from that."

Detachment frees you to throw yourself fully into the action while staying loose about the outcome — also the state in which you perform best.

Supporting Someone Without Drowning

Detachment is how you help a struggling sibling, partner, or patient without being consumed: care about the action that's reasonably yours; release the outcome that isn't.

Run an explicit conversation instead of silently over-functioning:

  1. Lay out the situation, neutrally. State the facts; ask them to describe it too.
  2. Divide the responsibility. "What's mine here? What's yours? What are you going to do?"
  3. Name the consequences — for your part and theirs.

If they shut down, put the ball in their court: "I don't know what to do either. I need us to figure this out together. You tell me when and where." Whether it gets solved isn't on you alone — and that fact is your detachment.

If you're the family's designated reliable person, your reliability quietly manufactures their dependence. The remedy is to become deliberately, calculatingly unreliable — not cruel, just neutral: "I'm sorry, I had to get to work." Expect guilt-trips; they intensify because guilt-tripping has worked before. Stay neutral. When someone insists "she needs help," agree warmly, then ask: "I agree completely. So who's going to help? I can't right now."

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing wealth or "finding your dharma" first. With an anxious, scattered mind you have no internal compass to read. Ground yourself first.
  • Pursuing only moksha. Going all-in on spirituality usually means you genuinely become a monk (rare) or you start spiritually bypassing — using spiritual language to dodge life ("getting high is part of my practice, man"). Ask honestly: why don't I want pleasure, wealth, or service? What am I running from?
  • Mistaking rejection of the system for liberation. Disengagement isn't detachment.
  • Confusing detachment with suppression. Detachment feels the pain and skips the spiral; suppression denies the pain.
  • Thinking detachment kills desire and joy. It only removes the suffering layer.
  • "I have zero time for pleasure." Usually fostered dependence and unspoken attachments, not iron necessity. A temporary crunch is fine; a permanent life with no spare 4% means looking at who you can't bear to disappoint.
  • Over-functioning as love. Bailing people out repeatedly engenders dependence.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reverse your sequence. For one month, run it as: spiritual practice → protected pleasure → work/money → helping others. Notice whether the later items get easier.
  2. One hour of spiritual practice, daily. Meditation, yoga, or philosophy — the internal work. Skip only for a serious reason.
  3. One hour of intentional pleasure, daily. Schedule it. One episode, one match, one real break — not a numbing binge. Seven days a week.
  4. Train detachment on cleanliness. Every time the tiny "I'll do it later" appears, do it now. You're practicing not being ruled by your impulses.
  5. Train detachment on truthfulness. Catch the small lies you tell to dodge minor consequences and tell the truth instead. Let it land; practice "so be it." Work up to harder ones.
  6. Run the desire/attachment audit on envy. Separate the want from the suffering: Do I want it? Am I willing to act? Then act, and if it doesn't come, so be it. Want freely; refuse to be butthurt.
  7. Have the responsibility conversation. With anyone you over-support, lay out the situation, split responsibilities, name consequences, and hand them the ball.
  8. Practice being calculatingly unreliable. Decline one request you genuinely can't accommodate, stay neutral through the guilt-trip, and watch dependence loosen.
  9. At work, catch one avoidance. Identify a situation you've been retreating from. Lift your baseline, then walk back into it instead of around it.
  10. Distinguish your feeling from your suffering, in real time. Feel the clean pain — then watch for the second story ("I'm unlovable / a failure"). That story is the attachment. Drop it and stay in the action.

So how do you actually build detachment? Not through insight alone. Through practice — specifically the yamas and niyamas, the personal and social observances that are supposed to precede meditation and breathwork in classical yoga.

When meditation traveled West, these got cut, because they looked like religious morality — non-violence (ahimsa), non-stealing (asteya), cleanliness (shaucha), truthfulness (satya), non-grasping (aparigraha). But they aren't really morality. They're a training ground for detachment. Live by them and consequences pile up, and learning to let those consequences come is exactly how you level up.

Cleanliness: Defeating the Tiny "I Don't Wanna"

Take the simplest case. You miss the trash can; the wrapper lands on the floor. You think, I'll get it later. Why?

When you examine it honestly, the reasons — "tiring," "more efficient to do it all at once," "the cleaners will handle it," "not my problem" — aren't really emotions. You won't feel grief or rage either way. What you feel is a tiny, uncharactered suffering induced by an attachment: attachment to comfort, to efficiency, to not moving.

"It's just suck. A kind of uncharactered suck, in a tiny little way."

The cost of picking it up is microscopic and falls only on you. Which is exactly what makes it perfect practice. Pick it up anyway, every time the little "I don't wanna" appears, and you train the muscle of not being ruled by what's inside your head. Knock out the small impulses, level by level, and eventually you can knock out a big one.

Truthfulness: Choosing the Consequence on Purpose

Truthfulness is the clearer teacher. Why do you lie? Because you're attached to a particular outcome — avoiding a consequence or grabbing a reward. Each lie strengthens the attachment.

Stop lying and the consequences arrive: this person gets mad, that plan falls apart. To willingly tell the truth, you have to develop the capacity to say "so be it" to a negative consequence and let it land. That capacity is detachment.

And truth comes in tiers — the white lie, the bigger lie, the lie that protects something huge. As you practice, the consequences escalate from paper cut, to gut punch, to kick in the nuts. Each level requires more detachment to absorb. What you're really building is consequence resistance:

"A pain? No problem. I can be happy anyway, even if things don't go well."

Once suffering no longer dictates your behavior, the whole game changes. Skip the drug and feel withdrawal? "So be it." You've already walked the gauntlet of telling hard truths and cleaning up after yourself.

Desire Is Fine. Attachment Is the Problem.

A crucial distinction, because detachment scares people who think it means wanting nothing and feeling nothing.

  • A desire is just a direction your brain wants to move when it gets a sensory input. Wanting a hot dog, wanting to fly first class, wanting the cosmetic someone else has — all fine.
  • An attachment is how much you suffer when the desire goes unmet.

Watch the layers when you ask someone out:

  1. The event: they say yes or no.
  2. The natural feeling: pleasure if yes, pain if no. This is normal and unavoidable. Pain is part of life.
  3. The optional layer — suffering: how butthurt you get. This is attachment, and it's the part you can change.

Detachment doesn't suppress the want and it doesn't erase the pain. It removes the second, multiplying layer of suffering. Without it, one day of pain over a rejection metastasizes into a year of "I'm unlovable." That spread is attachment doing its work.

And the proof that disappointment isn't automatic: watch a six-year-old offered the yellow piece or the brown piece. Sometimes they shrug and take brown. The desire was real; the attachment was near zero. That shrug is the whole skill.

The trap of attachment runs the other way too. People get the raise they craved and want another by morning. You can fulfill every desire and stay miserable — because you're attached. That's the engine of modern unhappiness: a world built to hand you exactly what you want, instantly, forever — and a mental health crisis riding right alongside it.

Detachment vs. Suppression — and Why It Makes You More Engaged

A sharp question deserves a sharp answer: how is detachment different from just suppressing how upset you are?

Suppression denies the pain itself: "I wasn't that into her anyway. Doesn't even matter. She doesn't deserve me." You shove the natural feeling underground.

Detachment fully feels the pain — and simply doesn't add the layer of suffering on top. You let yourself hurt, and you decline to spiral.

The biggest misconception is that detachment makes life flat or worse. The opposite is true. Detachment makes you more engaged, not less. When you're highly attached, you avoid, cope, and disengage because it matters so much. Lying is the least engaged act there is. Fail a test while attached to being a doctor and you collapse, lose motivation, hide. Fail it while detached and you stay in the action: the failure is information; what's the next move?

"I can be fully engaged with this person in this moment... I can focus on learning the material. The attachment is what takes us away from that."

Detachment frees you to throw yourself completely into the action while staying loose about the outcome — which, not coincidentally, is also the state in which you perform best.

Supporting Someone Without Drowning: Detachment in Practice

Detachment is also how you help a struggling sibling, partner, or patient without being consumed. The principle: care about the action that's reasonably yours; release the outcome that isn't.

When you're supporting someone who's sliding the wrong way, run an explicit conversation instead of silently over-functioning:

  1. Lay out the situation, neutrally. "You quit your job, you've been out of work eight months, here's what I've been doing." Ask them to describe it too.
  2. Divide the responsibility. "What's mine to do here? What's yours? How can I help — and what are you going to do?"
  3. Name the consequences. "If I don't do my part, here's what happens. If you don't do yours, here's what happens."

If they shut down or walk away — common with depression and avoidance — put the ball firmly in their court: "I don't know what to do either. I need us to figure this out together. You tell me when and where." Whether it gets solved isn't on you alone, and that fact is your detachment. You did your part.

This connects to a hard truth about being the dependable one. If you're the family's designated reliable person, everyone defaults to you, and your reliability quietly manufactures their dependence. The remedy is to become, deliberately, calculatingly unreliable — not cruel, not estranged, just neutral. "I'm sorry, I had to get to work, I wasn't available." Expect the guilt-trips and the family pile-on; they intensify precisely because guilt-tripping has worked before. Stay neutral, don't cave and don't go scorched-earth, and people slowly grow more independent. When someone insists "she needs help" — agree warmly, then ask the disarming question: "I agree completely. So who's going to help? I can't right now."

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing wealth or "finding your dharma" first. With an undisciplined, anxious, scattered mind, you have no internal compass to even read. Ground yourself first; the answers get clearer.
  • Pursuing only moksha. Going all-in on spirituality usually means one of two things: you genuinely become a monk (rare), or you start spiritually bypassing — using spiritual language to dodge the very life you're avoiding ("getting high is part of my practice, man"). When avoidance is the root of your spirituality, you'll just keep finding clever techniques to not face your problems. Ask yourself honestly: why don't I want pleasure, wealth, or service? What am I running from?
  • Mistaking rejection of the system for liberation. Dropping out and getting high is a real response to a real problem, but it rarely produces peace. Disengagement isn't detachment.
  • Confusing detachment with suppression. Detachment feels the pain and skips the spiral. Suppression denies the pain. They look similar and are opposites.
  • Thinking detachment kills desire and joy. You're allowed to want things and to be happy when you get them. Detachment only removes the suffering layer.
  • "I have zero time for pleasure." Usually this is fostered dependence and unspoken attachments, not iron necessity. For a genuine crunch (a brutal work stretch), temporary is fine — but a permanent life with no spare 4% means it's time to look at who you can't bear to disappoint.
  • Over-functioning as love. Bailing people out repeatedly engenders dependence. Real support divides responsibility and lets natural consequences exist.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Reverse your sequence. For the next month, deliberately run it as: spiritual practice → protected pleasure → work/money → helping others. Notice whether the later items get easier.
  2. One hour of spiritual practice, daily. Meditation, yoga, or reading philosophy — anything that does the internal work. Skip only for a genuinely serious reason. (If you're a "night person," consider that you may be an early-morning person; experiment with rising very early to practice, and watch your sleep need possibly drop.)
  3. One hour of intentional pleasure, daily. Schedule it. Make it deliberate — one episode, one match, one real break — rather than a numbing binge. Seven days a week.
  4. Train detachment on cleanliness. Every time the tiny "I'll do it later" appears, do it now. Pick up the dropped wrapper. You're not cleaning a room; you're practicing not being ruled by your own impulses.
  5. Train detachment on truthfulness. Catch the small lies you tell to dodge minor consequences and tell the truth instead. Let the consequence land and practice "so be it." Work up from white lies to harder ones.
  6. Run the desire/attachment audit on envy. When you envy something, separate the want from the suffering. Ask: Do I want it? Am I willing to take real action toward it? Yes — then act, and if it doesn't come, so be it. Let yourself want; refuse to be butthurt.
  7. Have the responsibility conversation. With anyone you're over-supporting, lay out the situation, split the responsibilities explicitly, name the consequences, and hand them the ball. Then let the outcome be theirs.
  8. Practice being calculatingly unreliable. If you're the family's dependable one, decline one request you genuinely can't accommodate, stay neutral through the guilt-trip, and observe how dependence slowly loosens.
  9. At work, catch one avoidance. Identify a situation you've been retreating from because of the feeling it triggers. Lift your baseline (practice + a bit of joy), then walk back into it instead of around it.
  10. Distinguish your feeling from your suffering, in real time. Next time something disappoints you, let yourself feel the clean pain — then watch for the second story you start telling ("this means I'm unlovable / a failure"). That story is the attachment. Drop it and stay in the action.
