Style, Politics, and the Results-Oriented Default

The hardest part of any job isn't doing the report — it's presenting it and following up. Because that's harder, it's compensated more. So cultivate a results-oriented style:

  • Underpromise and overdeliver. If a task takes three weeks, say three weeks, then deliver in two.   • Then ask for more: "How can I keep doing this kind of work? Is there a team-lead role here?"

Real Networking: Friends With Jobs

Most people network badly because they network too late — at the career fair, the moment they desperately need a job. That desperation reads as shallow because it is.

The best networking is when you don't need anything.

Reframe what a network even is:

A professional network isn't people who can do you favors. It's people who genuinely like you, trust you, think you're good at what you do, and are friendly. A network is basically friends with jobs.

In practice it looks like a chief product officer texting you: "I've got a great director who got laid off — are you hiring?" That only happens after years of mutual like and trust.

Two practical notes:

  • Do both internal and external networking — relationships inside your company and outside it.
  • Awkwardness usually isn't about you. Everyone has weird energy because they're all networking too late. Give yourself a second, third, fourth chance.

Social skills are learnable — and best learned with feedback, which is why practicing in groups beats white-knuckling "be more likable" alone.

How to Leave a Job Well

You are not stuck. People stay too long fearing they'll let others down — but there's a clean exit that serves you.

  • Take the network, experience, and skills — and keep nurturing them. That company is on your résumé; if its star rises, yours rises. Stay in touch.
  • Position your exit as helping them. Offer a transition plan and a handoff doc. They may call you back later — at which point you can charge twice as much.
  • Resign in writing, calmly. "I've enjoyed working here. Thank you for the experience. I'm writing to resign, effective [date per contract]. Happy to help with a transition plan." Never emotionally unload on the person you're resigning to.
  • If you carry institutional knowledge, there's a premium to it: "I love working here, but I'm looking to make this much. If you can't, I understand, and we'll part ways nicely." Sometimes they can't afford you — that's its own signal.

One filter for online career advice worth keeping: meaning beats passion. Purpose-rich industries are full of burned-out people, while a "meaningless" job on a fun team can be more than enough. Doing what you're good at, with people you like, without doing harm — that can carry you.

  • Doing great work invisibly. If it can't be forwarded, it won't promote you.

  • Robotic, kiss-ass networking. "Tell me about your biggest accomplishment" feels hollow and people sense it.

  • Emotionally unloading when you resign. Burns a bridge that's on your résumé.
  • Swinging your stick in a new job. Spend the first weeks asking questions and building trust, not declaring everything dumb.

And when someone swoops in to steal credit that was yours to deliver, call it out — privately, not publicly: "I was the team lead on this; next time, please let the lead share the team's news."

Real Networking: Friends With Jobs

Most people network badly because they network too late — at the career fair, the moment they desperately need a job. That desperation makes it feel shallow and artificial, because it is.

The best networking is when you don't need anything.

Reframe what a network even is:

A professional network isn't people who can do you favors. It's people who genuinely like you, trust you, think you're good at what you do, and are friendly. A network is basically friends with jobs.

They're not your best friends, but you understand each other's strengths and weaknesses and know who to call. In practice it looks like a chief product officer texting you: "I've got a great director who just got laid off elsewhere — are you hiring?" That only happens after years of mutual like and trust.

Two practical notes:

  • There's internal and external networking. Build relationships inside your company and outside it. Do both.
  • Awkwardness usually isn't about you. Everyone at these events has weird energy, partly because they're all networking too late. Give yourself a second, third, fourth chance. Cut your losses with the one person you keep fumbling in front of, and move on.

Social skills are learnable — and they're best learned with feedback, which is why practicing in groups beats trying to white-knuckle "be more likable" alone.

How to Leave a Job Well

You are not stuck. People stay too long because they fear letting others down — but there's a clean way out that actually serves you.

  • Take the network, experience, and skills — and keep nurturing them. That company is on your résumé now; if its star rises, yours rises. You want them to do well, so stay in touch (unless they suck — and even then, there's a strategic way to stay in touch).
  • Position your exit as helping them handle their business. Offer a transition plan; document everything; build the handoff doc. You'll feel good, and they may call you back later — at which point you can charge twice as much.
  • Resign in writing, calmly. "I've enjoyed working here. Thank you for the experience. I'm writing to resign, effective [date per your contract]. I'm happy to help with a transition plan." Never emotionally unload on the person you're resigning to — take that to someone else.
  • If you carry institutional knowledge, there's a premium to it. The graceful ultimatum: "I love working here, but I'm looking to make this much. If you can't, I understand, and we'll part ways nicely while I look elsewhere." (Sometimes they can't afford you — that's its own signal.)

Good Advice, Bad Advice

A quick filter for the career content flooding your feed:

  • "Everything is your fault" / "You're the CEO of your life." Cheesy, but largely true. It's brutal to hear when a coworker is sandbagging you — but the mindset of taking full accountability for your circumstances reveals how much influence you actually have. Worth the hard couple of months.
  • "You're there anyway, so be all in." Solid. Mailing in half your waking hours is a waste. If you have to do the boring work regardless, do it excellently — get the best coffee, learn the orders, build a reputation. Run a side project in parallel, but don't half-ass the thing you're already standing inside of.
  • "Don't quit the nine-to-five — build the side hustle in the overlap." Good. Don't leave a business to start one unless you're very comfortable. Constraints force you to prioritize the single most impactful task, which is actually great for building.
  • "Stack skills instead of waiting for the perfect plan." Half-right. Endless five-year planning with no action goes nowhere — so start. But "just learn random skills and see what happens" can waste years. Always be refining skills by doing, not by agonizing over which course to take. Start the course; if it's not for you, ask for a refund and move on.
  • "Transcribe every meeting and email to mirror corporate language." The underlying instinct — reflecting language back to show understanding — is right. But the brute-force version produces a try-hard who can only do what they're told. The lower-effort, higher-ceiling skill is reflective listening.
  • "You don't need passion or purpose — meaning is enough." Strongly true. Purpose-rich industries are full of burned-out people (the refugee-saving nonprofit staff, the therapists drowning in emotional labor), while a "meaningless" job on a fun team can be more than enough. Doing what you're good at, with people you like, without doing harm — that can carry you.
  • "You're stuck because you're not honest about what you really want." Mostly bad. Stuckness is rarely a self-honesty problem; it's a skills-and-situation problem — your network, your industry-by-skill position, your ability to negotiate and manage expectations. And people say they want "time," but time is a luxury that follows financial security. Reframe accordingly.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating self-employment as escape. Quitting out of frustration without building your manager and employee skills just relocates the problem.
  • Confusing feeling undervalued with being undervalued. The first is yours to own; the second must be argued in dollars. Mixing them makes employers shut down.
  • Chasing salary numbers and online comps. They're starting positions, not facts, and the company paying unsustainable rates may not exist in a year.
  • Always saying yes. Easy-to-work-with people get quietly punished with more work. Make your manager prioritize instead of absorbing everything.
  • Doing great work invisibly. If no one sees it and it can't be forwarded, it won't promote you.
  • Networking only when you need a job. Desperation reads as desperation. Build relationships before you need anything.
  • Robotic, kiss-ass networking. "Tell me about your biggest accomplishment" feels like an alien who read that humans like talking about themselves. It's hollow and people sense it.
  • Working for free. If they won't pay you now, they won't pay you later. Offer a paid trial contract instead.
  • Emotionally unloading when you resign. Burns a bridge that's literally on your résumé. Stay gracious.
  • Swinging your stick in a new job. Don't open by declaring everything dumb. Spend the first weeks asking questions and building trust.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your career as industry × skill. Write down the three industry factors (macro trends, your network, your experience) and your honest answer to: What am I better, faster, or cheaper at than other people? Use an AI tool to summarize your industry's macro trends.
  2. Run the magic loop this week. Ask your manager for the single most valuable thing you could do, finish it, then say "It's done — what's next?" Repeat.
  3. Clickbait your next win. Put a concrete, forwardable result in an email subject line or team message — or intercept your manager right before they meet their boss.
  4. Replace "I feel undervalued" with numbers. List what you've generated, saved, or created in dollars, then request a two-year growth plan tied to those metrics.
  5. Audit yourself as manager and employee separately. Score your priority-setting, resourcing, budgeting, and strategy (manager) against your execution, expectation-clarifying, and troubleshooting (employee). Note which side is weaker.
  6. Network when you don't need anything. Go to one webinar, conference, or small group; connect with a presenter or moderator with genuine curiosity, not an ask. Treat it as making "friends with jobs."
  7. Practice the boundary-without-a-boundary script. When handed extra work: "Happy to help — which one first?" or "Happy to pick up that shift — what did you have budgeted for the extra time?"
  8. Set up a clean-exit template (even before you need it): a gracious resignation note, a transition document, and a list of your institutional knowledge.
  9. Learn reflective listening. It's the low-effort, high-ceiling replacement for transcribing every meeting — look it up and practice mirroring understanding back to colleagues.
  10. Decide your meaning source honestly. If your work lacks "purpose," ask whether a fun team, a skill you enjoy, and not doing harm could be enough — and pursue purpose elsewhere in your life if needed.
