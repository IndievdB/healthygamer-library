Why You Can't Just "Check the Studies"

A thin evidence base misleads in both directions:

  • Practitioner skill varies wildly. A weekend-course "energy healer" and a 30-year master share a label and nothing else; many CAM fields have no quality control over training.
  • Few practitioners know research methods, and few researchers know the traditions — there's no pipeline producing both.
  • Most literature is invisible to us — Ayurvedic and TCM databases exist (cataloguing herbs that act on genes like BDNF), mostly not in English. "No studies" often means "none we found."

Why You Can't Just "Check the Studies"

If evidence is what matters, why not simply trust the literature? Because studying CAM honestly is genuinely hard.

1. Wild variability in practitioner skill. If someone's cancer went into remission after an energy healer, the question for you is: are all energy healers equal? Some did a weekend course, some practiced a decade — and many forms of CAM have no quality control over education at all. The same label can mean ten different things.

2. Most CAM practitioners aren't trained in research methodology. Expert scientists don't understand the traditions; expert practitioners often don't understand study design. CAM rarely has allopathic medicine's pipeline of people who are both deep tradition-experts and fluent in rigorous research.

3. We can't even see most of the literature. The English-speaking research world indexes places like PubMed. But there are entire research indices Western physicians don't know exist — Ayurvedic portals, large TCM databases cataloguing which herbs act on which genes (e.g., herbs that interact with BDNF, the same growth factor SSRIsA neurotransmitter responsible for contentment and peace — distinct from the excitement and pleasure driven by dopamine — and for regulating your brain's perception of how hard something is to do.Full definition → increase). Much isn't in English. "There are no studies" often means "none we bothered to find."

4. The standard study design may be the wrong tool. A randomized controlled trial (RCT) deliberately strips out individuality: isolate one disease, one treatment, measure the average. But Ayurveda and TCM are fundamentally individualized — there's no generic "depressionA state of persistently low mood that goes beyond a normal emotional response to difficulty — it becomes clinical when symptoms last at least two weeks, impair functioning, and include at least five of nine recognized…Full definition →," just a particular configuration out of balance.

An RCT is antithetical to the theoretical basis of Ayurveda and traditional Chinese medicine.

A better fit is a cohort study: give 100 people the same condition, half individualized TCM/Ayurveda and half standard treatment, then compare effect sizes. The same nuance appears in acupuncture, where debates over "sham acupuncture" (a collapsible needle) suggest a study assuming skin penetration is the active ingredient may be testing the wrong thing entirely.

The takeaway: a negative study isn't automatically final, and a missing study isn't proof of failure. Be critical of your beliefs in favor of something, and equally critical of a negative result.

Why You Can't Just "Check the Studies"

If evidence is what matters, why not simply trust the literature? Because studying CAM honestly is genuinely hard, and a thin evidence base can be misleading in either direction.

1. Wild variability in practitioner skill. Suppose someone tells you their cancer went into remission after seeing an energy healer. Even if that's true for them, the questions that decide whether you should go are: Are all energy healers equally effective? How do you even rate one? What training did they have? Some did a weekend course, some a week-long retreat, some have practiced for a decade — and many forms of CAM have no quality control over education at all. The same label can mean ten completely different things.

2. Most CAM practitioners aren't trained in research methodology. Expert scientists don't understand these traditions, and expert practitioners often don't understand study design. In allopathic medicine the most competitive, "try-hard" doctors become researchers at top academic centers. CAM rarely has an equivalent pipeline of people who are both deep experts in the tradition and fluent in rigorous research.

3. We can't even see most of the literature. The English-speaking research world covers maybe a billion people and indexes on places like PubMed and Google Scholar. But there are entire research indices Western physicians don't know exist — Ayurvedic research portals, and large traditional Chinese medicine databases cataloguing which herbs act on which genes (one example: herbs shown to interact with BDNF, the same growth factor SSRIsA neurotransmitter responsible for contentment and peace — distinct from the excitement and pleasure driven by dopamine — and for regulating your brain's perception of how hard something is to do.Full definition → increase). Much of this work isn't in English. So "there are no studies" often means "there are no studies we bothered to find."

4. The standard study design may be the wrong tool. This is the deepest point. A randomized controlled trial (RCT) deliberately strips out individuality. You isolate one disease and one treatment, control everything else, and measure the average. But Ayurveda and traditional Chinese medicine are fundamentally individualized — there's no generic "depressionA state of persistently low mood that goes beyond a normal emotional response to difficulty — it becomes clinical when symptoms last at least two weeks, impair functioning, and include at least five of nine recognized…Full definition →," there's a particular configuration that's out of balance, and the treatment is tailored to it. Forcing that into an RCT is almost self-defeating.

An RCT is antithetical to the theoretical basis of Ayurveda and traditional Chinese medicine.

A better fit is a cohort study: take 100 people with the same condition, give half individualized Ayurvedic or TCM treatment and half standard psychiatric treatment, then compare the relative effect sizes. The same nuance shows up in acupuncture research, where debates rage over "sham acupuncture" (a collapsible needle that touches but doesn't pierce the skin). If acupressure works without piercing, and thick Korean needles work as well as thin Japanese ones, then a study assuming skin penetration is the active ingredient may be testing the wrong thing entirely.

The takeaway: a negative study isn't automatically the final word, and a missing study isn't proof of failure. Be critical of your beliefs in favor of something, and equally critical of a negative result.

Does Qi Even Exist? The Power of a Good Heuristic

How can a system built on a non-biological concept produce a treatment as effective as one built on biology? Here's the resolution: a concept doesn't have to be physically real to be useful.

It is a system that we develop to increase our understanding of a situation even if it's not real.

Consider a family. You cannot biopsy, X-ray, or scientifically detect a "family."
