How Raises and Promotions Actually Get Made

People get frustrated — "I've been here three years and haven't gotten a raise" — and aim it at the wrong moment. Company budgeting is inherently slow. Budgets are set a year in advance. The time to ask is during the budget cycle, roughly the end of Q3 / start of Q4.

A raise doesn't materialize because it's deserved in the abstract. Your job is to get budgeted for, and that requires your manager to make this chain of argument upward:

  1. This person will grow into a bigger title,
  2. because they'll take on more responsibility,
  3. because they can deliver results,
  4. and here's the proof — look at what they've already done.

So have the conversation now, framed around delivery:

"I'm really looking to grow in my role here. In 12 months I'd like to show you the right role for me is this. What do you think I should do to make my case?"

Never argue "other people in my role get paid more" — the employer will note those people carry responsibilities you don't yet. Make it about what you deliver. And if what you actually want is time, not money, the same directness works: tell your manager you're happy and effective, then ask about shorter days. It's a more approachable request than you'd expect.

Know Your Own Motivation

Job satisfaction comes from one of two sources, sometimes both:

Intrinsic satisfaction — from the work itself: - The job itself — "I love helping people in acute stress; I hate the follow-up charts." - Achievement — "I want the VP title. Six figures by 25." The goal matters more than the specifics. - Recognition — being the thought leader, the conference speaker; wanting your skill recognized publicly.

Extrinsic satisfaction — from the conditions around the work: - Working conditions — the nice office, control, time away. - Supervision — the chance to mentor and lead. - Social climate — working on things and with people you care about.

This sits on a self-determination continuum:

  • Amotivation — "I don't care about anything." A real slice of people land here, often after trying repeatedly with nothing working, with moral injury locking it in.
  • Intrinsic motivation — "I know exactly what I want to be; I want to be inherently satisfied with how I spend my time."
  • Everything between is extrinsic motivation in shades.

Where you sit isn't fixed. People in the internally-motivated zone often slide back toward money-based extrinsic motivation, because:

"It is expensive to be internally motivated. You're giving up a lot of income potential."

There's no shame in needing the title and the salary. The point is to know which forces actually drive you, so you stop chasing rewards that won't satisfy you.

This is also a superpower in managing others. Spot the colleague motivated by recognition and shout them out in the meeting; spot the one who wants their time back and save them effort. Understanding each person's rewards builds relationships that last years.

Reclaiming Agency in Small Ways

You don't need a job change to recover purpose. Small, deliberate acts restore agency:

  • Mentorship. Choosing how you show up for others is a direct way to reclaim meaning.
  • Small invitations. "Anyone want to join a gaming lobby after work?" Facilitating relationships goes a long way.
  • Negotiating for your values. Pitch an employee-sponsored tree-planting project: "A few of us really wanted to do this — would the company consider it?" Ask first, ask nicely, stay on the side of good morale.

Rich or King, Networks, and AI

For aspiring entrepreneurs, the founding question: do you want to be rich, or do you want to be king?

  • Rich means building something big — but you must delegate, accept others' ideas, and build beyond yourself.
  • King means control — your way, your decisions. You can make a great living but rarely a giant company.

These are almost always mutually exclusive, and purpose and fulfillment tend to live with the king path. If you notice you say yes where others would say no, you may be a king who needs to recognize it. Balance it with a strong co-founder or an entrepreneur community.

On networks: a network is just people who like you, trust you professionally, and whom you don't need anything from. That last part is the secret.

"I always recommend networking when you have nothing to ask."

The reliable on-ramp is regular social activities — a hobby, a sport, volunteering. Show up for tennis and you'll find yourself surrounded by a network you never went looking for.

On AI: process-oriented, QA, and customer-service roles are most exposed. Programming stays valuable, but the edge shifts to what you build and how fast. Data and statistics are becoming baseline skills — the real differentiator is leveraging AI well, since most companies are still figuring it out. For tech people, the future edge increasingly lies in working for yourself.

  • Calling moral injury "just burnout." If your exhaustion comes from work violating your values, more rest won't fix it. Name it correctly.
  • Chasing a "meaningful" field without checking the day-to-day. A noble mission can have soul-killing hours. Separate macro purpose from micro fulfillment before you leap.
  • Walking in with an ideological identity. "I'm anti-capitalist" makes you an opponent to be defeated. Be pro the success of people in the organization, and bring problems with proposed solutions.
  • Blaming your immediate boss for systemic forces. Layoffs and frozen raises are often driven by tax law, budgets, and macroeconomics — not malice. Misdirected anger costs you relationships.
  • Burning bridges on the way out. Torching relationships throws away your network, learnings, and references.
  • Asking for a raise at the wrong time or with the wrong argument. Comparing yourself to others fails; tying your ask to results, timed to Q3/Q4, works.
  • Chasing rewards that don't match your motivation. Know your real driver first.
  • Wanting to be both rich and king. Refusing to choose stalls many founders.
  • Networking only when you need something. Desperation is obvious. Build connections when you have nothing to ask.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Diagnose your moral injury. Decide which of the three forms you're experiencing (action against your values, failure to act, betrayal of trust) and which level it lives at (job, career/system, or boss). The level tells you whether to change tasks, fields, or managers.
  2. Run the macro/micro test. List what your work gives you in the big picture versus how your daily hours feel. If a "meaningful" move just trades one empty day-to-day for another, reconsider — or get the meaning through volunteering.
  3. Convert one fight into a problem. Rewrite a grievance as a collaborative proposal: name the inefficiency, offer ideas, ask permission to share them.
  4. Find the macro force. For one thing you blame your employer for, ask what external pressure (budget cycle, tax change, market shift, fear) might sit behind it.
  5. Have the budget-cycle conversation now. Ask your manager: "In 12 months I'd like to grow into [role]. What results would make my case to you?"
  6. Map your motivation. Sort yourself across intrinsic vs. extrinsic, and locate yourself on the amotivation–intrinsic continuum. Be honest about how much money pulls you back — that's normal.
  7. Name your category and narrow your priority. Billpayer, Soul-Feeder, or Core? If Core, pick the one thing that matters most — mission, people, or daily work.
  8. Do one small agency act this week. Mentor someone, extend a casual invitation, or pitch a values-based project — framed around good morale.
  9. If building your own thing, choose rich or king. Write down which you actually want, notice whether you say yes where others say no, and recruit a co-founder or community to balance your bias.
  10. Network with nothing to ask. While employed and not job-hunting, reconnect with people or join a hobby purely for the hobby. Let the network form as a byproduct.

How Raises and Promotions Actually Get Made

People get frustrated — "I've been here three years and haven't gotten a raise" — and the frustration isn't wrong, but it's aimed at the wrong moment. Company budgeting is inherently slow. Budgets are set a year in advance. The time to ask is not when you're already overdue; it's during the budget cycle, roughly the end of Q3 / start of Q4, when next year's numbers are being planned.

A raise or title doesn't materialize because it's deserved in the abstract. Your job is to get budgeted for, and that requires your manager to be able to make this chain of argument to their bosses:

  1. This person will grow into a bigger title,
  2. because they'll take on more responsibility,
  3. because they can deliver results,
  4. and here's the proof — look at what they've already done.

So have this conversation now, framed around delivery, not comparison:

"I'm really looking to grow in my role here. In 12 months I'd like to show you the right role for me is this. What do you think I should do to make my case that this should be my title and salary?"

Never argue "other people in my role get paid more." The employer will simply note that those people have responsibilities you don't yet carry. Make it about what you deliver to the company, and time the ask to the budget cycle.

And if you've discovered you don't even want the raise — you want time — the same directness applies. Tell your manager you're happy and effective, then ask: "What do you think about me taking a few hours back, or shorter days Thursday and Friday? I can still get everything done." That's a far more approachable request than you'd expect.

Know Your Own Motivation

Job satisfaction comes from one of two sources, and if you work hard and engineer your luck, sometimes both:

Intrinsic satisfaction — from the work itself: - The job itself — "I'm an ER doctor; I love helping people in acute stress, I hate the follow-up charts." - Achievement — "I want the VP title. I want six figures by 25." The goal matters more than the specifics. - Recognition — being the thought leader, the conference speaker, the ambassador; wanting your intellect and skill recognized publicly.

Extrinsic satisfaction — from the conditions around the work: - Working conditions — the nice office, control over how you work, time away. - Supervision — the chance to mentor and lead a team. - Social climate — getting to work on things and with people you care about.

This sits on a self-determination continuum running between two poles:

  • Amotivation — "I don't care about anything." A meaningful slice of people land here, facing total lack of purpose. Often they've tried and tried, nothing worked, the macro world feels too chaotic to plug into, and moral injury locks it in: "Anything I do to create purpose just puts me on the wrong side of things."
  • Intrinsic motivation — "I know exactly what I want to be, I'm chasing fulfillment, I want to be inherently satisfied with how I spend my time."
  • Everything between is extrinsic motivation in shades — how externally or internally regulated you are.

Crucially, where you sit isn't fixed. People in that internally-motivated green zone often slide back toward extrinsic motivation based on money, because:

"It is expensive to be internally motivated. You're giving up a lot of income potential."

There's no shame in needing the title and the salary. The economic reality is real. The point is to know which forces are actually driving you, so you stop chasing rewards that won't satisfy you.

Billpayer, Soul-Feeder, or Core

Underneath all of it, your relationship to work tends to be one of three things — and most people graduate through them:

  1. The Billpayer — "My job is fine. Good enough. It funds the life I want, and that life is the point." Most people start here: I don't care exactly what I do, I need something that pays the bills.
  2. The Soul-Feeder — "I have my life handled; now I want to add soul." Purpose comes from side projects, volunteering, art, community roles — at work or outside it.
  3. The Core — living your purpose day-to-day is non-negotiable. This can come from the work itself, the larger mission, or simply being around inspiring people getting good things done.

For the Core, here's the hard part: you can't have all three at once. You have to narrow it down to what matters most — the organization/mission, the people around you, or the daily work itself.

How to prioritize when they conflict? Treat it like a hierarchy of needs. You can rescue kittens all day, but if you can't pay your bills, you have to start with financial stability, then layer in outside meaning, then optimize.

The one priority worth weighting heavily is the people:

"You can work at a hospital or an animal shelter, and if the people you work with suck, it doesn't matter how many kittens you rescue or babies you deliver — you're going to get beaten down."

This is why people keep working with the same cluster of colleagues across jobs: they know they'll do good work and not have to deal with a lot of nonsense. And don't underrate the value of simply not having terrible people and terrible managers — sometimes the absence of a negative is a perfectly good building block.

Reclaiming Agency in Small Ways

You don't need a job change to recover a sense of purpose. Small, deliberate acts restore agency:

  • Mentorship. If you can mentor someone, do it. Choosing how you show up for others is a direct way to reclaim meaning.
  • Small invitations. "Anyone want to join a gaming lobby after work?" "Anyone up for a walk?" Facilitating relationships at work goes a long way.
  • Negotiating for your values. If you care about the environment, look for a brand angle or an employee-sponsored tree-planting project. Frame it as: "A few of us really wanted to do this — would the company consider getting involved?" Ask first, ask nicely, and always stay on the side of good morale.

If You Want to Start Your Own Thing: Rich or King?

For aspiring entrepreneurs, the foundational question is one founders are asked from day one: do you want to be rich, or do you want to be king?

  • Rich means building something big — possibly millions in value. But to get there you must delegate, accept other people's ideas, and build something bigger than yourself.
  • King means control. Your autonomy, your decisions, your way. You can still make a great living, but it's very unlikely to become a giant company.

These are almost always mutually exclusive. Most people instinctively say "both — I want to call the shots and build something huge," which usually isn't possible. And here's the key tie-back: purpose and fulfillment tend to live with the king path — doing things on your own terms.

Making this a conscious choice reveals your biases. If you notice you say yes where others would say no — that you instinctively want control — you may be a king who needs to recognize it. The healthy counterbalance is a strong co-founder or side-by-side partner, or joining a community of other entrepreneurs as a sounding board. Creators struggle here especially: everything is built on their personal brand, and it's genuinely hard to get others to care about your brand as much as you do.

How to Build a Network (Without Trying To)

A network is simpler than people think: people who like you, trust you professionally, and whom you don't need anything from. That last part is the secret.

"I always recommend networking when you have nothing to ask. If you have a job and you're not looking for one right now, now is the time to network."

Job fairs and "best of the bunch" applicant funnels are bad places to start a relationship. The reliable on-ramp is regular social activities — a hobby, a sport, volunteering. Show up at the tennis court for tennis and you'll find yourself surrounded by wildly successful people, homemakers, retirees — a network you never went looking for.

"If you go into anything willing to meet people, a network is just people you know who like you and trust you. That's it."

On AI and Career Stability

A few forward-looking notes for choosing where to invest:

  • Highly process-oriented, QA, and customer-service roles are most exposed to automation.
  • Programming and tech stay valuable — we'll keep building more software — but the edge shifts to what you build and how fast.
  • Data and statistics are becoming a baseline skill everyone needs. The real differentiator is leveraging AI well — if you can make data faster, more meaningful, and more insightful with AI, you're valuable, especially since most companies are still figuring out how to use it.
  • For tech and programming people, the future edge increasingly lies in working for yourself — finding your niche or side hustle in the new sub-economies (selling on Amazon, etc.) — rather than being one of many doing a function for a big company.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling moral injury "just burnout." If your exhaustion is about your work violating your values, more rest won't fix it. Name it correctly.
  • Chasing a "meaningful" field without checking the day-to-day. The mission can be noble while the actual hours are soul-killing (e.g., endless donor solicitation). Separate macro purpose from micro fulfillment before you leap.
  • Walking in with an ideological identity. "I'm anti-capitalist" / "I'm here to hold management accountable" makes you an opponent to be defeated. Be pro the success of the people in the organization, and bring problems with proposed solutions.
  • Blaming your immediate boss for systemic forces. Layoffs and frozen raises are often driven by tax law, budget cycles, and macroeconomics — not personal malice. Misdirected anger costs you relationships and offers nothing back.
  • Burning bridges on the way out. Torching relationships throws away your network, your learnings, and your future references. Whoever is hiring can tell who scorches the earth.
  • Asking for a raise at the wrong time or with the wrong argument. Comparing yourself to others fails; tying your ask to delivered results, timed to the Q3/Q4 budget cycle, works.
  • Chasing rewards that don't match your motivation. Demanding more time off when what you actually crave keeps shifting; pursuing a promotion when you're intrinsically motivated. Know your real driver first.
  • Wanting to be both rich and king. Refusing to choose stalls many entrepreneurs. Pick one consciously.
  • Networking only when you need something. Desperation is obvious and ineffective. Build connections when you have nothing to ask.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Diagnose your moral injury. Decide which of the three forms you're experiencing (action against your values, failure to act, betrayal of trust) and which level it lives at (the job, the career/system, or the boss). The level tells you whether to change tasks, change fields, or change managers.
  2. Run the macro/micro test. List what your work gives you in the big picture versus how your actual daily hours feel. If a "meaningful" move would just trade one empty day-to-day for another, reconsider — or get the meaning through volunteering instead.
  3. Convert one fight into a problem. Take a grievance you're carrying and rewrite it as a collaborative proposal: name the inefficiency, offer ideas, ask permission to share them.
  4. Find the macro force. For one thing you blame your employer for, ask what external pressure (budget cycle, tax/legal change, market shift, fear) might sit behind it. Notice how it changes your stance.
  5. Have the budget-cycle conversation now. This quarter, ask your manager: "In 12 months I'd like to grow into [role]. What results would make my case to you?" Build the delivery plan together.
  6. Map your motivation. Sort yourself across intrinsic (the work, achievement, recognition) vs. extrinsic (conditions, supervision, social climate), and locate yourself on the amotivation–intrinsic continuum. Be honest about how much money pulls you back toward extrinsic — that's normal.
  7. Name your category and narrow your priority. Are you a Billpayer, Soul-Feeder, or Core person right now? If Core, pick the one thing that matters most — organization, people, or daily work — since you can't have all three at once.
  8. Do one small agency act this week. Mentor someone, extend a casual social invitation, or pitch a values-based project — asked nicely, framed around good morale.
  9. If building your own thing, choose rich or king. Write down which one you actually want, notice whether you say yes where others say no, and recruit a co-founder or community to balance your bias.
  10. Network with nothing to ask. While you're employed and not job-hunting, reconnect with people, or join a hobby purely for the hobby. Let the network form as a byproduct.
