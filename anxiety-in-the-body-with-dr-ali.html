You can't think your way out — the way through is through. Anxiety is a covered pot: escaping (removing it) feeds fear; boiling over means snapping at others; the goal is to let the steam out.

A — Assess Your Thoughts

Treat your thoughts as the background noise of your mind — most pop up and float away meaninglessly. Anxious people give anxious thoughts enormous weight, and when anxiety lives in the body, they fixate on the racing heart and breathlessness. The whole game is to get less focused on your internal universe and more focused on the present moment.

  • "I'm excited." Fear and excitement are physiologically nearly identical — two people in a roller-coaster line have the same racing heart; one calls it terror, one calls it thrill. V — View the Bigger Picture

Whatever you focus on inside your body grows louder. This is true of chronic pain and chronic anxiety, and they respond to the same strategies: care for and rest your body, and occupy your mind.

Picture Mario Kart, when ink splatters across your screen. Stare at the ink and you crash. The skill is to look past it and keep racing. Distraction skills (from dialectical behavior therapy) help — and they aren't avoidance; they're giving your over-dramatic friend a short break so you can come back and cope better.

The most powerful distractions are sensory, because they grab attention fast:

  • Smell — the fastest sense; lavender or a strong scent.
  • Sound — loud or absorbing music.
  • Touch — a soft or textured object.
  • A funny video — laughter creates a competing emotional state that quickly shifts your body out of fear.

E — Expand Through Experience

You cannot think your way out of body-based anxiety. The way through is through.

Imagine anxiety as a covered pot of boiling water. Remove the pot (escape and avoid — temporary relief that feeds the fear). Let it boil over (snapping at family, getting angry at yourself, like a caged lion that can only roar). Or lift the lid a little and let the steam out — what emergency skills do.

Emergency anxiety skills

Your body is a fueled rocket on the launchpad with nowhere to go. Give the fuel something to do:

  • Big physical movements — a walk, jumping jacks, gardening, vacuuming. The worst thing you can do with an activated body is sit still.
  • Progressive muscle relaxation — tense a muscle group, hold a few seconds, release, and notice the relaxation flood in. Squeeze an imaginary lemon in your fist; scrunch your face at an imaginary fly. You can do it invisibly in a meeting.
  • Grounding — pour yourself into something specific: the five-senses scan, a mental top-five list (favorite bands or spaceships), or vividly walking through a place you know. Getting past three or four forces real focus.
  • The dive reflex — submerge the "ski-mask" area (eyes and nose) in cold water for 15–30 seconds and your heart rate drops as the parasympathetic system kicks in. Fast and reliable — but check with a doctor first if you have a heart condition.
  • Sensory overload — head in the freezer, a cold shower, a peppermint, a Sour Patch Kid.
  • Slowed breathing — last deliberately, because for some it helps and for others it sparks more anxiety. Skip the min-maxing; just breathe in for five, hold, out for five.

Interoceptive exposure

When ready, deliberately bring on the very sensations you fear so you become less afraid of them: run in place, breathe through a straw with your nose pinched, spin in a chair for dizziness, wear a heavy jacket in a warm room. The structure: write down what you fear will happen and rate how strongly you believe it (0–100%), do the exercise, then revisit — did it come true? what did you learn? You're fast-tracking the lesson that you can handle these sensations.

Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviors: The SCAMP Model

Hair pulling (trichotillomania), skin picking, and nail biting are body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs) — more common than people think (roughly one to two in fifty). They're best understood as normal grooming behaviors gone awry; stressed birds pull feathers. They work — they rapidly change how you feel — but at the cost of lost time, shame, and visible damage. They're driven not just by anxiety but by boredom (the number one trigger), anger, and even excitement.

You're not damaging yourself permanently — hair grows back, skin heals. We just need to find what meets that need in a different way.

(One genuine warning: if you swallow hair, it can form blockages — trichobezoars — that can be fatal. Get checked.)

The SCAMP model offers substitutes that satisfy the underlying need:

  • S — Sensory: scalp massage, brushing, scratching with a coin, new lotion, a manicure.
  • C — Cognitive: unhook from "I'm harming myself" and reframe toward meeting the need differently.
  • A — Affective: for boredom, build absorbing go-to activities; for excitement, call a friend or journal; for anger, use movement.
  • M — Motor (hands): create barriers — gloves, finger cots, band-aids, silly putty, a Koosh ball, gum for nail biters.
  • P — Place: change the environment — cover mirrors, move tweezers to the back of the fridge.

The same logic helps an urge in the moment: scratching an itch makes you want to itch more, so ice it instead. This is urge surfing: riding the wave until it passes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to destroy or "crush" your anxiety. There's no single root cause to uproot — it's genetics, environment, experience, and skills combined. Fighting it makes it stronger.
  • Suppressing sensations and hiding them. This drains your resources, pulls your focus inward, and intensifies the very thing you're hiding.
  • Treating distraction and grounding as escape. Used to permanently avoid, they backfire; used to give yourself a break so you can re-engage, they help.
  • Doing anxiety work with no values behind it. Without something worth being anxious for, there's no reason to tolerate the discomfort — and progress stalls.
  • Over-relying on mindfulness if you're a ruminator. For chronic worriers, turning attention further inward makes things worse. The need is external focus.
  • Falling for "optimization" culture. Endless solo morning routines, supplements, and cold plunges push you toward isolation and internal focus — the opposite of what anxious people need.
  • Assuming everyone else looks calm. Anxiety is almost entirely invisible. The person presenting "calmly" may be terrified; the room is usually rooting for them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write a letter to your anxiety as if it were an over-dramatic but well-meaning friend. Acknowledge it, thank it, and tell it you'll take one step forward together.
  2. Name it in the moment. When your heart races, say: "This is my anxiety. It means I'm doing something important. What's the one step forward?"
  3. Find your three to five values using the 75th-birthday exercise, value sort cards, or listing the qualities of someone you admire. Use them as your decision-making GPS.
  4. Try the excitement reframe before a stressful event: "My body is getting excited because something important is happening."
  5. Build an emergency-skills shortlist — pick a few that fit you (a walk, jumping jacks, progressive muscle relaxation, the dive reflex, a strong smell, a funny video) and have them ready.
  6. Practice progressive muscle relaxation now: squeeze an imaginary lemon in each fist for several seconds, release, notice the difference. Repeat with face, shoulders, feet.
  7. Test the dive reflex (heart conditions excepted): submerge eyes and nose in cold water for 15–30 seconds and watch your heart rate fall.
  8. Run a curiosity experiment. Write what you fear will happen and your belief (0–100%), do the feared thing or an interoceptive exposure, then revisit what actually happened and what you learned.
  9. For BFRBs, build a SCAMP kit — a sensory substitute, a hand-occupier, an emotion plan for your trigger (often boredom), and one environmental change like relocating your tweezers.
  10. If an urge hits, surf it — ice the itch instead of scratching, occupy your hands, or put a strong scent on your fingertips so you notice the moment your hand rises.

  • Smell is the fastest sense (a short hop from nose to brain) — lavender, a strong scent.
  • Sound — loud or absorbing music.
  • Touch — a soft object, something textured.
  • A funny video — laughter creates a competing emotional state that quickly shifts your body out of fear.

E — Expand Through Experience

You cannot think your way out of anxiety — especially body-based anxiety. The way through is through.

Imagine anxiety as a covered pot of boiling water. You have three options. Remove the pot (escape and avoid — temporary relief that feeds the fear). Let it boil over (we don't usually fight, but we roar — snapping at family, getting angry at ourselves, like a caged lion that can only roar). Or lift the lid a little and let the steam out — which is what emergency skills do.

Emergency anxiety skills

Your body is a fueled rocket on the launchpad with nowhere to go. Give the fuel something to do:

  • Big physical movements — a walk (which also floods your senses with novelty), jumping jacks, gardening, vacuuming. The worst thing you can do with an activated body is sit still.
  • Progressive muscle relaxation — deliberately tense a muscle group, hold a few seconds, release, and notice the relaxation flood in. Squeeze an imaginary lemon in your fist; scrunch your face at an imaginary fly; press your feet into imaginary mud. You can do it invisibly in a meeting.
  • Grounding — pour yourself into something specific: the classic five-senses scan, but also a mental top-five list (favorite bands, or favorite spaceships), or vividly walking through a place you know well. Getting past three or four forces real focus and pulls you out of your body.
  • The dive reflex — every vertebrate has it. Submerge the "ski-mask" area (eyes and nose) in cold water for 15–30 seconds and your heart rate drops as the parasympathetic system kicks in. Fast and reliable — but check with a doctor first if you have a heart condition.
  • Sensory overload — shock the nervous system: head in the freezer, a cold shower, a peppermint, a Sour Patch Kid, loud music.
  • Slowed breathing — last on the list deliberately, because for some it helps and for others it sparks more anxiety. Skip the min-maxing; just slow it down. Breathe in for five, hold, out for five — like smelling a slice of pizza, then cooling it down.

Interoceptive exposure

When you're ready, you can deliberately bring on the very sensations you fear so you become less afraid of them. Run in place, breathe through a straw with your nose pinched, spin in a chair for dizziness, wear a tight collar to mimic choking, wear a heavy jacket in a warm room for heat. The structure: write down what you fear will happen and rate how strongly you believe it (0–100%), do the exercise, then revisit — did it come true? what did you learn? You're fast-tracking the lesson that you can handle these sensations. Pros call this exposure; think of it as a quest to discover what actually happens.

Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviors: The SCAMP Model

Hair pulling (trichotillomania), skin picking, and nail biting are body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs) — far more common than people think (roughly one to two in fifty). They're best understood as normal grooming behaviors gone awry; stressed birds pull feathers, stressed mice pull fur. They work — they rapidly change how you feel — but at the cost of lost time, shame, and visible damage. They're driven not just by anxiety but by boredom (the number one trigger), anger, and even excitement.

You're not damaging yourself permanently — hair grows back, skin heals. We just need to find what meets that need in a different way.

(One genuine medical warning: if you swallow hair, it can form blockages — trichobezoars — that can be fatal. Get checked.)

The SCAMP model offers substitutes that satisfy the underlying need:

  • S — Sensory: scalp massage, brushing/combing, scratching with a coin or pen end, different shampoo, a manicure or nail file, new lotion.
  • C — Cognitive: unhook from the thought "I'm harming myself" and reframe toward meeting the need differently.
  • A — Affective (emotions): for boredom, build absorbing go-to activities; for excitement, call a friend or journal; for anger, use distraction and movement.
  • M — Motor (hands): create barriers and competing activities — gloves, finger cots, band-aids, tape, silly putty, a Koosh ball, gum or a toothpick for nail biters; put pomade in your hair to change its feel.
  • P — Place: change the environment — cover mirrors, move tweezers to the back of the fridge so they're hard to reach.

The same logic helps an urge in the moment: scratching an itch makes you want to itch more, so ice it instead — soothing the urge rather than feeding it. This is urge surfing: riding the wave until it passes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Trying to destroy or "crush" your anxiety. There is no single root cause to uproot — it's genetics, environment, experience, and skills combined. Fighting it makes it stronger.
  • Suppressing sensations and hiding them. This drains your resources, pulls your focus inward, and intensifies the very thing you're hiding.
  • Treating distraction and grounding as escape. Used to permanently avoid, they backfire; used to give yourself a break so you can re-engage, they help.
  • Doing anxiety work with no values behind it. Without something worth being anxious for, there's no reason to tolerate the discomfort — and progress stalls.
  • Over-relying on mindfulness if you're a ruminator. For people who worry and overthink, turning attention further inward often makes things worse. The need is external focus — what your senses reveal, not what your mind imagines.
  • Falling for "optimization" culture. Endless solo morning routines, supplements, and cold plunges tend to push you toward isolation and internal focus — the opposite of what anxious people need.
  • Assuming everyone else looks calm. Anxiety is almost entirely invisible. The person presenting "calmly" may be terrified; the room is usually rooting for them.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Write a letter to your anxiety as if it were an over-dramatic but well-meaning friend. Acknowledge it, thank it, and tell it you'll take one step forward together.
  2. Name it in the moment. When your heart races, say: "This is my anxiety. It means I'm doing something important. What's the one step forward?"
  3. Find your three to five values using any of the methods: the 75th-birthday exercise, value sort cards, or listing the qualities of someone you admire. Use them as your decision-making GPS.
  4. Try the excitement reframe before a stressful event: "My body is getting excited because something important is happening."
  5. Build an emergency-skills shortlist — pick a few that fit you (a walk, jumping jacks, progressive muscle relaxation, the dive reflex, a strong smell, a funny video) and have them ready.
  6. Practice progressive muscle relaxation now: squeeze an imaginary lemon in each fist for several seconds, release, and notice the difference. Repeat with face, shoulders, feet.
  7. Test the dive reflex (heart conditions excepted): submerge eyes and nose in cold water for 15–30 seconds and watch your heart rate fall.
  8. Run a curiosity experiment. Write what you fear will happen and your belief (0–100%), do the feared thing or an interoceptive exposure, then revisit what actually happened and what you learned.
  9. For BFRBs, build a SCAMP kit — a sensory substitute, a hand-occupier, an emotion plan for your specific trigger (often boredom), and one environmental change like relocating your tweezers.
  10. If a urge hits, surf it — ice the itch instead of scratching, occupy your hands, or put a strong scent on your fingertips so you notice the moment your hand rises.
