Common Pitfalls

Two Kinds of Creativity

  2. Divergent thinking — realize you don't need to beat it head-on; go around it and end up somewhere else that works.

The Three Prerequisites

1. A Semantic Apparatus — Something to Manipulate

The single biggest difference between consistently creative people and everyone else is that the creative person has a model — a way to break the thing they're dealing with into parts they can move around.

Consider "lazy." It's binary — you're either lazy or not. No pieces, no levers, no solutions. Now treat laziness as a combination — of motivation, conditioning, emotional activation, physiological state. Suddenly you have knobs to turn.

"If I start to develop an apparatus or a model of what I am dealing with, it fosters solutions."

This is what elite creative training teaches. A writing workshop hands you a vocabulary: character, plot, foreshadowing, dramatic irony. A painter learns shadow, lighting, foreground, background. All that "boring English-class crap" is a toolkit for breaking an abstract whole into manipulable parts.

2. The Ability to Articulate Your Problem

You need language precise enough to capture the real shape of your problem. Good diagnosis precedes good treatment.

When a car won't go from A to B, "it's broken" is useless. Flat tire? Dead engine? Each diagnosis points to a different fix. Doctors spend roughly half their training learning to diagnose, because once a problem is correctly named, the solution often becomes self-evident.

"Most people look for solutions, but they don't understand their problem. And then if you help them understand their problem, the solution becomes more emergent kind of on its own."

This is why coaching and therapy work even when the helper "doesn't fix anything." The client finally understands their problems. Watch anyone complaining online: "I can't get laid," then an elaborate theory about their jaw or some conspiracy. Those are hypotheses dressed up as diagnoses, never examined critically. Poor articulation guarantees poor solutions.

3. Inputs — The Diet for Creativity

Creativity comes out of you, so what comes out depends on what goes in.

"The solution to writer's block is to read more."

The game you'd make depends on games you've played; the novel you'd write on books you've read. Most stuck people have insufficient or monotonous inputs. One game for 10,000 hours is not the same fuel as studying many games. This works because creativity is largely the patching together of old ideas in a new form. Want more output? Give your brain more building blocks, and more kinds of them.

Two foundational facts:

  1. Creativity comes from everywhere. Brain imaging finds no single "creativity circuit" — essentially the whole brain is active.
  2. The right hemisphere matters more. More creative people leaned on the right, emotional, associative side. One executes; the other reframes.

Skill 1: Cognitive Flexibility (via Task Switching)

The opposite of flexibility is perseveration — getting stuck on one track. In a test where you alternate 1 → A → 2 → B, a perseverating patient just runs 1, 2, 3, 4 — locked in one mode. You can perseverate without any diagnosis: "I want friends, so I'll buy people gifts," and you keep buying gifts forever though it never works. Lose a match and you can't see the mistake; watch the replay two days later and the blunders are glaring. Stepping away and coming back refreshes your mode of thinking — effortlessly.

Skill 2: Working Memory

Think of working memory as your brain's RAM — how much you can juggle at once while assembling a solution. Two ways to expand it:

  • External tools. J.K. Rowling used a hand-drawn grid tracking each chapter against the plot, the prophecy, and what each character was doing. Offload what you can't hold in your head.
  • Cleansing debuffs. If part of your mind is worried about rent or a relationship, those background processes eat your RAM. Picture drawing with both hands full of bags. Unburden your mind and its resources free up for creative work.

Skill 3: Sustained Attention

There's a gulf between a creative impulse and creative expression. The bridge is holding attention on one problem for a long stretch.

"If you can stare at a blank page until you start writing, and if that staring involves eight hours, you will be creative."

This is why so many have ideas and produce nothing — when work gets hard, attention flees to Reddit. "Get dressed for work" or use a dedicated desk works because a habit and a place facilitate sustained attention — your brain auto-associates that spot with that task. Meditation strengthens this directly.

Skill 4: Judgment of Propriety

Your frontal lobes provide context-dependent motivation — knowing what's appropriate where. Creativity is saturated with this judgment. Does this scene belong here or twenty minutes later? Three people in the ad or four? In music, when does the bass drop?

"If you push it the right amount, then it's gonna be satisfying... But if I wait too long... it ruins the music."

Deciding what fits is central to creative expression, and it lives in the frontal lobes.

The Subconscious: Incubation Does the Heavy Lifting

A huge amount of creativity happens off-stage. King's drawer trick is incubation — your brain runs combinations unconsciously, then floats results up.

Incubation needs two ingredients: substrate and time. Substrate is your inputs. Time is room to process: walk after you read; protect your sleep (short-term memory is consolidated into long-term while you sleep); spend time alone. Incubation also requires empty working memory — if you're stressed, your brain has no idle cycles to run the assembly. Relatedly, mind-wandering aids creativity — the trick is to wander and come back, alternating bursts of attention with breaks.

Four Networks: A Map of Creativity

Cross two axes — deliberate vs. spontaneous and cognitive vs. emotional:

  • Deliberate + Cognitive: linear problem-solving — working out an equation.
  • Deliberate + Emotional: structured emotional work — psychotherapy, a writer's retreat aimed at a breakthrough.
  • Spontaneous + Cognitive: the "ton of bricks" aha — holy crap, he was a spy the whole time — after incubation.
  • Spontaneous + Emotional: inspired, dreamlike art (think Kubla Khan).

Knowing which quadrant you need helps you pick your approach. Good inputs and models power deliberate work; enough reading and walks set up the spontaneous strike.

Expression: The Unavoidable Grind

Here's where most creatives die. There's a vast difference between a great idea for a game and an actual game. The brain needs a system of expression — high skill to translate impulse into reality.

"It's not enough to have an idea. Your brain literally needs the capacity to translate that idea into a reality."

This part is pure work — no hack, just reps. The 10,000-hour ballpark is a reasonable target. The better you code, the better your game; the better you paint, the better your art. Attack these concrete skills directly.

The Spiritual Layer: Removing Conditioning and Surrendering Ego

Spontaneous creativity can't be done on command — which is why "genius visits you" has survived for millennia.

A powerful reframe: true creativity is not creating something — it's seeing things as they truly are. Nobel-winning experiments often come down to one person perceiving reality as it actually is, not as everyone assumes it is.

In yogic terms, conditioning is klishta — "coloring," everything you add to a situation. If your abusive parents gave gifts out of guilt, a partner's gift may trip false alarms: gifts are dangerous. That's your projection, not reality. Dating is a mess because everyone projects so much you get judged for a phantom version of yourself.

"Conditioning and cognitive flexibility are opposites."

Stripping klishta is maximizing cognitive flexibility. Approach each moment as genuinely new and creative perception flows.

Practical levers for the "caught not created" kind:

  • Reduce klishta through meditation — maps directly onto flexibility.
  • Third-eye practices like trataka and breath work — cultivate intuition (spontaneous knowledge arriving without a deliberate process).
  • Worship / surrender — the most extreme and, surprisingly, potent step. The mechanism is ego dissolution. Saying "I can't do this alone, please help me make this" removes ego — and your ego is the biggest klishta. The instant you judge your draft as "trash," you stop. Surrender loosens that grip and shifts you into a growth mindset. This is likely why worship appears in every culture: it does something genuinely useful for the brain.

You don't have to adopt any religion. Direct the request wherever feels right — the universe, entropy, RNG, a specific tradition. The act of asking — admitting "I can't do this on my own, but I'll try anyway" — is the healthiest, most growth-oriented stance available.

Manifestation: Concept + Energy

To make anything real you need two things: a concept (blueprint — "Shiva") and energy/material (action — "Shakti"). A house needs both an architect's plan and builders.

Most failure isn't a shortage of energy — you're tired every day. It's that your energy isn't concentrated into a sustained concept. You spend yourself on scattered directions: work today, AFK tomorrow. Energy expended rarely correlates with anything manifested, because it has no focused vector. Fix the concept, and your existing energy starts producing results.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating problems as binary. Labels with no parts can't generate solutions.
  • Mistaking hypotheses for diagnosis. Elaborate theories ("it's my jaw / it's forced 50% win rate") feel like understanding but aren't.
  • Starving on monotonous inputs. Variety of input is the diet; one source repeated gives your subconscious nothing new to recombine.
  • Perseverating. Repeating one failed approach harder instead of switching modes.
  • Confusing impulse with expression. Loving the idea, abandoning it the moment it gets hard. Sustained attention, not inspiration, is the bottleneck.
  • Trying to force spontaneous creativity. You can only set conditions — inputs, incubation, sleep, fresh eyes — and let it strike.
  • Skipping the grind. Expecting a brilliant idea to excuse you from the 10,000 hours of craft.
  • Assuming the advice-giver is wrong. Your life improves in proportion to what you realize you're wrong about — not what others are wrong about.
  • Letting exhaustion auto-convert into inaction. Exhaustion is a feeling — "I don't feel like it" — not "I can't."

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Build a model of your problem. Take something binary ("I'm lazy") and break it into at least four levers you could adjust independently.
  2. Diagnose before you solve. Spend a full session articulating why a problem exists before reaching for any fix. Notice if the solution becomes obvious once it's named precisely.
  3. Upgrade your inputs. Diversify your creative diet: several kinds — primary works, plus creators discussing their craft.
  4. Use the drawer. Finish a draft, walk away for days, return with fresh eyes, and note what you now see.
  5. Offload to external tools. Build a grid, outline, or board so you stop burning working memory.
  6. Unburden your RAM. Resolve, schedule, or park background worries before creative work.
  7. Train sustained attention with a fixed ritual. One place, one trigger; sit and work a set amount — no alt-tabbing.
  8. Meditate daily. It strengthens attention, working memory, and flexibility at once.
  9. Protect incubation. Walk after you input. Guard your sleep. Allow mind-wandering — then come back.
  10. Pick your quadrant. Ask whether you need deliberate-cognitive, deliberate-emotional, spontaneous-cognitive, or spontaneous-emotional, and choose your method.
  11. Grind the craft. Name the concrete expressive skill your idea requires and put serious reps toward it.
  12. Strip klishta. Meet one recurring situation as if for the first time. Notice your projections and set them down.
  13. Try surrender. Before creative work, sincerely ask something beyond yourself for help, letting go of the outcome and your ego.
  14. Audit your exhaustion. Open your screen-time and Steam hours. Ask of each: does this give me more energy tomorrow or less? Move energy toward what compounds — and act even when you don't feel like it.

Four Networks: A Map of Creativity

Researchers describe four creativity networks formed by crossing two axes — deliberate vs. spontaneous and cognitive vs. emotional:

  • Deliberate + Cognitive: linear problem-solving — working out an equation.
  • Deliberate + Emotional: structured emotional work — psychotherapy, trauma processing, a writer's or artist's retreat aimed at a breakthrough.
  • Spontaneous + Cognitive: the "hit by a ton of bricks" aha — holy crap, he was a spy the whole time — usually arriving after subconscious incubation.
  • Spontaneous + Emotional: inspired, sometimes dreamlike art (think of the strange revelation behind a poem like Kubla Khan).

Knowing which quadrant you need helps you pick your approach. The good news: everything above feeds all four. Good inputs and good models power deliberate problem-solving; enough reading and enough walks set up the spontaneous strike.

Expression: The Unavoidable Grind

Here's where most creatives die. There's a vast difference between a great idea for a game and an actual game. The brain needs a system of expression — high skill to translate impulse into reality. A novelist needs wordsmithing; a painter needs technical craft; an entrepreneur needs to turn an idea into an operating business.

"It's not enough to have an idea. Your brain literally needs the capacity to translate that idea into a reality."

This is the part that's pure work — no neuroscience hack, just reps. The 10,000-hour ballpark is a reasonable target (medical residency runs roughly 10,000–15,000 hours and reliably produces competence). The better you code, the better your game. The better you paint, the better your art. If you're serious about creating, attack these concrete skills directly.

The Spiritual Layer: Removing Conditioning and Surrendering Ego

Spontaneous creativity is, by definition, not something you can do on command — which is exactly why the "genius visits you" idea has survived for millennia.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating problems as binary. "I'm lazy," "I can't get laid" — labels with no parts can't generate solutions. If there are no components to adjust, you're stuck by definition.
  • Mistaking hypotheses for diagnosis. Spinning elaborate theories ("it's my jaw / it's forced 50% win rate") feels like understanding your problem but isn't. Without honest, critical diagnosis, solutions can't emerge.
  • Starving on monotonous inputs. The same game for 10,000 hours, or zero reading, gives your subconscious nothing new to recombine. Variety of input is the diet.
  • Perseverating. Repeating one failed approach harder instead of switching modes. The hard-stuck gamer and the gift-buying friendless person make the same error.
  • Confusing impulse with expression. Loving the idea, abandoning it the moment it gets hard. Sustained attention, not inspiration, is the bottleneck.
  • Trying to force spontaneous creativity. You can't sit down and command an "aha." You can only set the conditions — inputs, incubation, sleep, fresh eyes — and let it strike.
  • Skipping the grind. Expecting a brilliant idea to excuse you from the 10,000 hours of craft needed to express it.
  • Assuming the advice-giver is wrong. When you hear a suggestion and your mind instantly builds reasons it won't work, that is the trap. Your life improves in proportion to what you realize you're wrong about — not what others are wrong about.
  • Letting exhaustion auto-convert into inaction. Exhaustion is a feeling — "I don't feel like it" — not "I can't." Most modern tiredness isn't from work; check the four-plus hours a day on your phone and the Steam hours first.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Build a model of your problem. Take something you've framed as binary ("I'm lazy," "I'm unlovable") and break it into at least four components you could adjust independently. Write each as a separate lever.
  2. Diagnose before you solve. Spend a full session articulating why a problem exists — distinguishing genuine diagnosis from hypotheses — before reaching for any fix. Notice if the solution becomes obvious once the problem is named precisely.
  3. Upgrade your inputs. Deliberately diversify your creative diet: not one source repeated, but several kinds — primary works, plus creators discussing their craft. Treat inputs as a daily diet you're responsible for.
  4. Use the drawer. Finish a draft or attempt, then walk away for days (or, Stephen King–style, longer). Return with fresh eyes and note what you now see that was invisible before.
  5. Offload to external tools. Build a grid, outline, or board (à la Rowling) so you stop burning working memory holding pieces in your head.
  6. Unburden your RAM. Identify the background worries eating your working memory and deliberately resolve, schedule, or park them before creative work.
  7. Train sustained attention with a fixed ritual. Pick one place and one trigger. Sit and stare at the blank page/canvas/file until you've done a set amount — no alt-tabbing. Build the habit that anchors attention.
  8. Meditate daily. It strengthens attention, working memory, and flexibility all at once. When you're too tired, that's precisely when a short session may pay back energy tomorrow.
  9. Protect incubation. Walk after you input. Guard your sleep as non-negotiable creative infrastructure. Allow mind-wandering during work — then come back.
  10. Pick your quadrant. Before tackling a creative block, ask whether you need deliberate-cognitive, deliberate-emotional, spontaneous-cognitive, or spontaneous-emotional creativity, and choose your method accordingly.
  11. Grind the craft. Name the concrete expressive skill your idea requires and put serious, deliberate reps toward it — accept the 10,000-hour reality.
  12. Strip klishta. Practice meeting one recurring situation (a person, a task, a draft) as if for the first time, bringing nothing from the past. Notice your projections and set them down.
  13. Try surrender. Before creative work, sincerely ask something beyond yourself — universe, RNG, or a tradition of your choosing — for help, explicitly letting go of the outcome and your ego. Observe the shift in how the work feels.
  14. Audit your exhaustion. Open your phone's screen-time and your Steam hours. Ask of each activity: does this give me more energy tomorrow or less? Then move energy toward the things that compound — and act even when you don't feel like it.
