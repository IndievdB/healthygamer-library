Tiredness is also a way of protecting your ego. When you "don't feel like" applying for the promotion, entering the competition, or taking the hard class, dig into the feeling. Often it's fear of falling short — because if you try and fail, you might not be as gifted as you've told yourself you are.

It's why gifted kids don't take hard courses. Because if you challenge yourself, you could fall short. And if you fall short, you may not be as gifted as you think.

Fear of failure feeds the same machinery. Your brain estimates cost as likelihood of failure × consequence of failure. The more failure-prone you believe something is, the more tired you'll feel about doing it. This becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy: job hunting feels impossibly heavy because any single application has near-zero odds, so your brain disincentivizes it, so you don't do it, so you stay unemployed — all driven by a feeling of tiredness rather than reality.

Your perception is the variable, not the facts

People object: "But Dr. K, my job is a dead end and dating is a waste of time — that's just reality." Yes and no. You are not a neutral perceiver of truth. Two people in identical situations respond completely differently. One person who gets a B throws in the towel; another treats it as a signal to step up.

Your conception of a thing determines how much energy you have for it. Reframe the dead-end job as a stepping stone — two years of experience that gets you the next, better job — and motivation returns. The facts didn't change; the calculation did.

Technology Is Hijacking Your Curiosity

Here's the modern engine driving mass tiredness. Curiosity is your primary fuel for trying new things — acquiring skills, exploring, getting those first few positive experiences that teach your brain "this is worth doing." Curiosity is the approach emotion that carries you from no experience to a few good experiences, after which other positive emotions can take over.

But curiosity is governed by dopamine, and your devices have captured it. Every Reddit title, every thumbnail, every TikTok is engineered to trigger a curious impulse — and the action that satisfies it is a tap or a click. Do that a thousand times, ten thousand times, and your brain learns one lesson: when curious, tap a link.

Our curiosity is limited to browsing the internet now.

The result: your single most valuable engine for genuine growth — reading real books, learning real skills, meeting real people — gets rerouted into a behavior that produces nothing. When curiosity can no longer pull you toward new experiences, your world shrinks, your wins dry up, and you sink back into that baseline of tiredness.

The Gunas: Building Sustainable Energy

Now the spiritual layer — a different and, in practice, extremely powerful lens. Where science works from the outside in (a surgeon operates on you, a therapist applies techniques to you), the karmic traditions are do-it-yourself: built entirely around what works for you, discovered by people sitting in meditation observing their own minds.

These traditions describe three fundamental qualities, the gunas, woven through all of existence:

  • Tamas — inertia, heaviness, dullness. The tendency of things to stay as they are. In you, it's sluggishness, hard-to-motivate heaviness.
  • Rajas — activity, drive, fierce pride, accomplishment. This is the grind set our society worships.
  • Sattva — balance, harmony, clarity. The state that emerges when activity and inertia are in equilibrium.

Both extremes are unhealthy, and they oscillate — exactly like bipolar disorder, where a manic high (rajas) is paid for by a depressive crash (tamas). You see this in ordinary life too: weeks of inertia, then a burst of frantic work, then collapse, then inertia again.

Rajas is unsustainable energy. Tamas is a lack of energy. What we want is to cultivate sattva.

Sattva is what allows seemingly superhuman endurance — the calm capacity to work seven days a week for years without burning out. The crucial claim: burnout only happens when what you're doing is unsustainable. A weekend is not a natural concept — lions, ants, and trees don't take days off. You buy the need for a day off by living each day in a way that exhausts you.

The day you buy the karma of needing a day off is the moment you sign up for a job or attitude that is overbearing. If your capacity to handle life is sufficient, you don't need one.

Daily Karma: You Are Building Tomorrow Right Now

The practical heart of the gunas: every action today has a determinate effect on your energy tomorrow. This is karma in the most concrete, mechanical sense — cause and effect.

Trace one ordinary mistake:

  1. You turn on the TV Monday at 10:30 p.m. and go to bed at 12:30.
  2. Tuesday your frontal lobes are tired, so your impulse control drops, you procrastinate, and work piles up.
  3. By Wednesday the backlog is overwhelming. You hyper-caffeinate, swing into rajas, ignore a friend's text — creating another problem.
  4. Thursday you're exhausted but force yourself through. Friday you're burnt out and "just need to get through today."
  5. The weekend you veg out, eat junk, party — and Sunday you're hungover, behind on chores, dreading Monday.

Is this what life is? All of this is because of one mistake. You turned on the TV Monday at 10:30.

This is why people feel perpetually tired: they're sowing terrible karma, one small choice at a time. And it compounds — after five years of weeks like this, your very attitude toward life curdles, which then poisons all those subconscious cost-benefit calculations we discussed earlier. (Notice that Sunday feels worse than Saturday despite being identical — the only difference is your dread of tomorrow. Worry about tomorrow ruins today, and a ruined today makes tomorrow worse.)

So the master question for energy becomes: "What can I do today that will give me more energy tomorrow?" Will this drink, this hour of scrolling, this 15th argument with my brother, this late night, this meditation session, this healthy meal — what will it do to my tamas, my rajas, my sattva by morning?

Diet as the foundation

The food you eat today determines your energy tomorrow. Period.

As long as you eat highly processed food, your energy stays low — partly through insulin spikes (high-glycemic carbs spike insulin, and more insulin means more fatigue, so favor complex carbs with smoother effects). Simple, fresh, plain meals — oats and berries, lean proteins or tofu, rice and vegetables — build sattva.

And there's a hidden bonus. Your pleasure receptors recalibrate to whatever you regularly eat. Cut your salt by half and keep it there; within weeks normal food tastes properly salty again. Eat plain food consistently and it becomes genuinely delicious. The modern problem is that we keep dulling our pleasure receptors with intensity; the fix is to let them grow more sensitive by eating simply.

Stop Running From Your Karma

Beyond diet and daily choices sits the most powerful lever of all: your attitude toward suffering.

If you feel tired about your life, there's a 90% chance the reason is that you're avoidant instead of embracing.

Avoidance is the trap. The treatment for OCD isn't avoiding the feared thing — it's exposure, leaning in until the fear loses its grip. The same principle (Viktor Frankl's paradoxical intention) applies to your whole life: problems don't fix themselves, and the avoidance of negativity is exactly what keeps you cycling through negativity.

So adopt the mantra: "I have to get through this before better days come." When hardship arrives, treat it as your karma bearing fruit — no point running, because running only sows more negative karma. Face the bad job, the draining relationship, the mess you've let accumulate. (None of this means it's your fault — there's a difference between cause and responsibility — but your life today is largely a consequence of how you've spent your recent days, months, and years.)

A radical corollary: getting lucky can be the worst thing that happens to you. When a problem solves itself, your brain learns "if I ignore it long enough, it goes away" — propagating bad decisions. Most lottery winners go broke. Luck rewards the very behaviors that will sink you.

And here's the liberating part: most of what exhausts us isn't the doing, it's the dreading.

Writing a dissertation is not hard. Thinking about writing a dissertation is hard. What is writing a dissertation? It's reading and typing. That's all.

You read and type all day arguing with strangers online. The actions are identical; only your idea of them differs. The tiredness comes from the perception of the problem, not the problem itself.

Sever Action From Motivation

The deepest move of all: stop tying your actions to how motivated or tired you feel.

Right now almost everyone's actions are hostage to their motivation, which is why everyone is endlessly trying to "boost motivation." But that's backwards:

Increasing your motivation is the best way to lose control of your life. Because that means the motivation controls your action.

If your action only fires when motivation is high, then a dip in motivation kills your action. You're at the mercy of a feeling. Instead, act independently of the feeling. Tired today? Do it anyway. Energized today? Do it anyway. "This is my karma; I have to do this today. Done."

When you stop chasing motivation and simply respond to what genuinely needs doing, a calmer, more sustainable energy (shakti) arises naturally — not forced, but called out of you by your environment. And "your environment" includes your own body and mind. You learn to look at your own impulses the way you'd look at a friend asking for help that won't actually help them, and calmly decline:

Your body says, "Give me chicken tendies," and you say, "Sorry, I can't help you today." Then life becomes easy.

This isn't self-deprivation — it's self-compassion. When your child wants cookies for dinner, love is what lets you say no. Treating yourself with that same firm care — recognizing the body and mind as instruments to be maintained well rather than gratified on demand — is where genuine, durable energy comes from.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating tiredness as one thing. It has physical, psychological, and karmic layers. A supplement won't fix a motivational problem, and reframing won't fix sleep apnea.
  • Assuming feeling tired means low energy. Often the tank is fine; your brain has just judged the action "not worth it." Investigate the judgment.
  • Using caffeine as energy. It only blocks your fatigue detector. The underlying deficit remains.
  • Trying to argue yourself into motivation. The cost-benefit machinery is subconscious. Logic helps a little; ego, fear of failure, and avoidance patterns matter more.
  • Living only to remove the negative. You'll feel dead at neutral and only spring to life when something's wrong — never moving toward anything good.
  • Chasing motivation. The more you strengthen the "act only when motivated" circuit, the more you become its slave. Sever action from feeling instead.
  • Overhauling everything overnight. Swing too hard into rajas and you'll exhaust yourself and crash back into tamas. Build gradually but deliberately.
  • Hoping for an easy life and celebrating lucky breaks. Both teach your brain to avoid the work that actually grows you.
  • Trying to help people instead of offering. Pulling someone who doesn't want to move exhausts you both and moves no one.
  • Chasing experiences in meditation (heat in the spine, rocking). These are side effects, not the goal. Grasping for them keeps you stuck.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Get a medical workup if you're chronically tired and haven't. Specifically rule out anemia, vitamin D deficiency, thyroid issues, and sleep apnea — all common and treatable.
  2. Run the energy budget. Honestly estimate your daily maintenance cost and your daily energy. How many surplus hours do you actually have? This is the number you're trying to grow.
  3. When you feel "too tired" for something good, interrogate the feeling. Ask: Is this avoidance? Ego protection — am I afraid of how I'll look if I try and fail? A bad cost-benefit framing I could reframe? Don't just push through; find the source.
  4. Don't just think about a daunting task — actually do it for one day and observe how you feel. Real experience updates your brain's calculation far more than imagining the cost ever will.
  5. Reframe one "dead-end" situation as a stepping stone toward something you value. Write down concretely how it serves a future goal.
  6. Reclaim your curiosity from your phone. The world average is over 4.5 hours of cell-phone use a day. Next time you feel a curious itch, point it at a book or a real skill instead of a feed. Reclaim even one of those hours.
  7. Keep an energy-awareness log. For each significant daily action, note its effect on your energy the next day. Ask before each choice: What tomorrow am I creating?
  8. Overhaul your diet toward simple and fresh — whole grains, lean proteins or tofu, vegetables; cut processed food and high-glycemic carbs. Cut salt by half and let your taste recalibrate over a few weeks.
  9. Adopt one daily energy-building practice and do it regardless of mood. A basic breathing practice (like alternate-nostril breathing or kapalbhati) or a short meditation, done every day — not when you feel like it.
  10. Pick one problem you've been avoiding and lean into it this week. Repeat the mantra: "I have to get through this before better days come." Face it; don't wait for it to disappear.
  11. Practice acting independent of motivation. Choose one important task and do it whether you feel energized or exhausted. Notice that the world doesn't end — and that a steadier energy appears once you stop waiting to "feel like it."
  12. With people you want to help: offer, don't push. Try the script — "I've noticed you've been tired. Would you like my help? Where would you like to start?" — then let them lead, and offer again later. Support their actions; don't take actions for them.
