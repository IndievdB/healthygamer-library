1. Pray for strength, not for ease. When something is hard, the instinct is to wish the difficulty away. Instead, ask for the strength to handle it.

"When I pray for strength to handle this... I'm always praying in the same direction."

The State You Meditate From

Most people obsess over meditation techniques — which method, whether they're doing it "right." But there's a deeper variable that explains why the same technique works powerfully one day and does nothing the next. It's not the technique. It's the state of consciousness you're operating from.

"Everyone's focused on the technique, but you can cultivate a mental state, and then you can do any technique."

The "zoom out" instruction

Sit upright — head, neck, and back straight — and close your eyes. Pick any sense to anchor to: a voice, your breath, the feeling of your body. Now stop attending to the content and instead notice the faculty itself — notice that your ears can hear, the bare capacity of hearing.

Then zoom out. Most of the time you meditate "at the front" of your awareness, straining forward, trying to make your mind do the thing. That front-position is full of effort. Instead, retract your awareness backward, as if a large eye toward the back of your head is simply watching — your body, your thoughts, the faculty of hearing. With eyes closed you may notice a dark screen in front of your mind. This is the chid akash — the inner space. Settle behind the one who is trying, and observe from that primal awareness.

What it feels like

This isn't relaxing. Common descriptions: wavy, dizzy, a retraction or sucking-out sensation, suffocating and thrilling at once, like walking a tightrope, intense, exhausting to maintain.

"It's like the edge of a tunnel where you feel like you're getting sucked in, and it's scary — and the world is out here. That's the gate."

You don't need to hold it long. Even moments count. The point isn't duration; it's to do your practice from there. Whatever technique you know — candle gazing, breath, mantra — becomes dramatically more potent when run from the zoomed-out state. A nitro boost for any meditation.

A few clarifications:

  • It's a state, not a technique. Don't try to label it (Is this shunya? dhyana?) — just do it.
  • You don't strictly need a separate technique. The state is the heart of it; a technique simply gives it something to work on.
  • Don't force it. If it's uncomfortable, ease off. Its potency doesn't require strain or long sessions.

This is also why your practice works some days and not others. On the good days you've stumbled into this backed-out state. Once you can find it deliberately, you stop being at the mercy of luck.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking intellectual understanding for change. If you can explain detachment, ego, or Vasana but your life looks identical, you've collected information, not made the shift.
  • Thinking detachment means wanting nothing. It's what lets fulfilled desires actually satisfy you — not a vow of emotional poverty.
  • Trying to lower your standards until you meet them. Keep the ambition and fix the internal mechanism that spoils the reward.
  • Outsourcing your inner signals. Letting algorithms, productivity culture, or other people decide what you should want, while never checking against your own felt reaction.
  • Chasing the next technique instead of the right state. Collecting methods while never developing the zoomed-out consciousness that makes any of them work.
  • Forcing the meditative state. It's potent precisely because you back off and observe — and brief moments are enough.
  • Treating a single bad day as proof of failure. One mediocre session or rejection is just a low point in an average that can still be very good.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the Vasana audit. For one day, when a strong thought grips you ("I'll always be alone," "I'm lazy"), pause and label it: this feels real, but it may just be mental habit. Don't argue with it — just downgrade it from fact to habit.

  2. Pick one global-buff skill and train it. Choose a single emotional-regulation scenario — receiving feedback, feeling guilt, feeling left behind — and practice staying with the feeling instead of reacting. Watch the same skill pay off across unrelated areas.

  3. Consult your internal instrument once a day. Before defaulting to what you "should" want, pause and ask: what's my actual reaction to this? Notice the difference between a path you've talked yourself into and one your body genuinely responds to.

  4. Reframe one setback in real time. Next time something goes wrong, force the experience interpretation: "Here's what I learned and how I'm better prepared," instead of the catastrophe interpretation.

  5. Switch your intention from ease to strength. When facing something hard, stop wishing the difficulty away. Ask for the capacity to handle it — it always points you toward growth.

  6. Practice meditating from the zoomed-out state. Sit upright, close your eyes, anchor to one sense, then notice the faculty rather than its content. Retract your awareness backward and observe from behind "the one who's trying." Don't force it, don't extend it, don't label it. Bring whatever technique you use into that state — even for a few moments.

  7. Track your good vs. bad meditation days. Instead of blaming the technique, ask what state you were in. Over time you'll learn to deliberately cultivate the backed-out, observing state — turning a lucky day into a repeatable one.

  8. Let your average carry you. Drop the demand to perform perfectly every time. Commit to showing up and doing what you can, and trust that a string of "good enough" efforts adds up to something genuinely good.

Try this. Sit upright — head, neck, and back straight — and close your eyes. Pick any sense to anchor to: the sound of a voice, your breath, the feeling of your body.

Now stop attending to the content (the words, the sensation) and instead notice the faculty itself — notice that your ears can hear, the bare capacity of hearing.

Then zoom out. Most of the time when you meditate, you sit "at the front" of your awareness, straining forward, trying to make your mind do the thing — trying to hold an image, trying to focus. That front-position is full of effort. Instead, retract your awareness backward, as if there's a large eye toward the back of your head simply watching — watching your body, your thoughts, the faculty of hearing.

When you do this with eyes closed, you may notice a dark screen in the front of your mind. This is the chid akash — the inner space. The instruction is to settle behind the one who is trying, and observe from that primal awareness.

What it feels like

This is not described as relaxing. Different people experience it differently, but common descriptions include: wavy, dizzy, a retraction or sucking-out sensation, suffocating and thrilling at once, like walking a tightrope, intense, exhausting to maintain. It can feel like standing at the edge of a tunnel that's pulling you in.

"It's like the edge of a tunnel where you feel like you're getting sucked in, and it's scary — and the world is out here. That's the gate."

You don't need to hold it for long. Even moments count. The point isn't duration; the point is to do your practice from there. Whatever technique you already know — candle gazing, breath, mantra — becomes dramatically more potent when run from the zoomed-out state. Think of it as a nitro boost for any meditation.

A few clarifications worth keeping:

  • It's a state, not a technique. Don't try to label it (Is this shunya? Is this dhyana?) or understand it intellectually — just do it.
  • You don't strictly need a separate technique. The state itself is the heart of it; a technique simply gives the state something to work on.
  • Don't force it. If it's uncomfortable, ease off. Do what you can tolerate. Its potency doesn't require strain or long sessions.

This is also the answer to "why does my practice work some days and not others." On the good days, you've stumbled into this backed-out state. Once you can find it deliberately, you stop being at the mercy of luck.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistaking intellectual understanding for change. Knowing the concept of detachment, ego, or Vasana changes nothing by itself. If you can explain it but your life looks identical, you've collected information, not made the shift.
  • Thinking detachment means wanting nothing. People hear "detachment" and conclude they should suppress ambition and desire. Wrong direction. Detachment is what lets fulfilled desires actually satisfy you — it's not a vow of emotional poverty.
  • Trying to lower your standards until you meet them. "Satisfaction" isn't shrinking your dreams down to something easy. It's keeping the ambition and fixing the internal mechanism that spoils the reward.
  • Outsourcing your inner signals. Letting the external world — algorithms, productivity culture, other people — decide what you should want and how hard you should grind, while never checking against your own felt reaction.
  • Chasing the next technique instead of the right state. Collecting meditation methods while never developing the zoomed-out consciousness that makes any of them work.
  • Forcing the meditative state. Gripping hard, straining at the "front" of your awareness, or trying to extend the intense state for as long as possible. It's potent precisely because you back off and observe — and brief moments are enough.
  • Treating a single bad day as proof of failure. One mediocre session, one rejection, one off-performance is just a low point in an average that can still be very good.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the Vasana audit. For one day, when a strong thought or prediction grips you ("I'll always be alone," "everyone's judging me," "I'm lazy"), pause and label it: this feels real, but it may just be mental habit. You're not arguing with it — just downgrading it from fact to habit.

  2. Pick one global-buff skill and train it. Choose a single emotional-regulation scenario — receiving feedback, feeling guilt, feeling left behind when someone else succeeds — and practice staying with the feeling instead of reacting. Watch how the same skill pays off across unrelated areas of your life.

  3. Consult your internal instrument once a day. Before defaulting to what you "should" want or do, pause and ask: what's my actual reaction to this? Notice the difference between a path you've talked yourself into and one your body genuinely responds to.

  4. Reframe one setback in real time. Next time something goes wrong, force the experience interpretation: "Here's what I learned and how I'm better prepared," instead of the catastrophe interpretation. Same event, deliberately chosen meaning.

  5. Switch your prayer/intention from ease to strength. When facing something hard, stop wishing the difficulty away. Instead, ask for the capacity to handle it. Notice that this always points you in the same direction: growth.

  6. Practice meditating from the zoomed-out state. Sit upright, close your eyes, anchor to one sense, then notice the faculty (that you can hear/feel) rather than its content. Retract your awareness backward and observe from behind "the one who's trying." Don't force it, don't extend it, don't label it. Bring whatever technique you already use into that state — even for a few moments.

  7. Track your good vs. bad meditation days. Instead of blaming the technique, ask what state you were in. Over time you'll learn to deliberately cultivate the backed-out, observing state that makes practice work — turning a lucky day into a repeatable one.

  8. Let your average carry you. Drop the demand to perform perfectly every time. Commit to showing up and doing what you can on a given day, and trust that a string of "good enough" efforts adds up to something genuinely good.
