The Ego Trap: Why Trying to Destroy It Feeds It

The more you set your ego aside, the more you feel good for being humble — which feeds the ego again.

There is the regular ego, and then there is the ego of having no ego. Look at how humble I am. I am the most humble.

The mistake is thinking the solution is to remove the ego. We assume fixing a problem means eliminating it, but sometimes solving a problem just feeds the pattern that causes it. Feel ugly, keep asking for reassurance, and every "you're beautiful" makes your self-worth more dependent on others — deepening the insecurity. Addiction works the same way.

So how do you break the cycle?

1. Awareness itself shrinks the ego. Ego only operates when you're unaware — ask someone with narcissistic personality disorder if they're narcissistic and they'll say, "No, I'm normal." The move isn't to fight the ego but to notice it: oh, now my ego is taking credit. Awareness deflates it automatically.

2. Give up credit — replace it with gratitude. Keep achieving; work as hard as you can. But recognize how much of your success was never yours:

It's not my work that gave me the IQ that I have. It's not my hard work that gave me the circumstances that I have.

You didn't choose the year you were born, your brain, your family, the chain of mentors who appeared. Effort matters — but so much of the outcome was outside your control that there's no point claiming all of it.

As long as you're taking credit, it is hard to have gratitude. If someone else is helping you and they're responsible, then you can have gratitude.

The payoff is threefold and simultaneous: lower ego, higher confidence, and more willingness to work hard. Ego and confidence sit at opposite ends of a spectrum — when you're secure, you don't need to defend yourself when someone calls you an idiot.

Someone struggling with the addiction you've overcome isn't worse — they're at a different point in their journey, dealt a different hand.

This isn't moral blindness. Seeing without ego means perceiving clearly. You can look at a sick person and know they need help, or a hole in your roof and know it should be fixed, without ego entering at all. In fact, ego is what stops you from acting: I deserve better, it's not fair. Ego is a tool — fine when you control it, dangerous when it controls you.

If you are living a life of avoidance, this means that your actions are controlled by your emotions.

You avoid because acting produces a bad feeling — embarrassment, anxiety, shame, fear of failure — and avoiding manages it. Try to do the thing and watch what happens inside you: a heavy weight pins you in place. At bottom, what you're avoiding is spending energy and having nothing to show for it.

Why "fixing your life" feels terrible

Taking control of your life means going through the mail, emptying out your closet, paying all the debts that you owe.

Catching up doesn't feel good — you expend effort just to remove negativity, with no visible gain. That effort is incredibly hard to reinforce, which is exactly why people stay stuck.

So decouple action from feeling good — in both directions. Don't wait for motivation, and don't make winning the fuel either:

If I'm motivated by wins, the moment I lose, my motivation decreases. My motivation should be independent of wins or losses.

Action should be independent of outcome. It's not about how it makes you feel; it's about doing it.

The mind loves complexity because complexity gives it room to stall.

The most people in life are paralyzed because they're waiting for an efficient start. Efficiency is the source of paralysis.

I'll start once I figure out the optimal way. Every added variable is another place to hide and another excuse to delay. The antidote is radical simplicity: when there's nothing to optimize, the mind can't trick you. This is also why consumed content never fixes anything — outside knowledge has to be downloaded and run, and we've built a society of people who download endlessly and never open the file.

Creativity and Visualization

Creativity is learnable. It's two things, both trainable.

First, it's a state of mind, not an action — the ancient muse that inhabits you, lets you produce, then leaves. The learnable skill is inducing that state, which is why creative people are drawn to anything that alters consciousness, including meditation.

Second, it's reorderable building blocks — recombination of what you already have. So: feed it more inputs (the cure for writer's block is to read more); build a ritual and space (one writer showers, dresses in work clothes, and sits at his desk to cue the state); and force the connections with constraints — draw with your non-dominant hand, write sentences without the letter S. Workshops demand you produce on command, without waiting to feel inspired. Because creative work lets you iterate, you can produce uninspired and let inspiration arrive in revision.

When positive visualization turns negative: normally attention faces outward, and external stimulus suppresses what's inside (absorbed in a game, your brain mutes hunger for hours). Visualization reverses this — and if your inner world is full of unprocessed material, that's what surfaces.

If my cup is full of sewage water, I can't add milk to it. I have to empty my cup.

So when negativity floods in, don't force the positive image and don't shove the negativity away. Let it come up and out — journal, speak it aloud, make art, move. (With a trauma history, do this with a professional; silent mindfulness can unleash the flood.) But venting alone isn't the cure. The common mistake is venting the same sad thoughts for years and wondering why they never leave. After letting it out, look at it critically:

Why do the sad thoughts never disappear? Where are they coming from? When did I first start to feel this way?

Subconscious "negative-energy generators" keep producing these thoughts. Until you trace them to the source and examine them — much of what therapy provides — the generator keeps running. Only once the cup is emptied and the source addressed can a positive visualization take hold.

  • Reading causation into cross-sectional correlation, and trusting headlines over methods sections.
  • Letting your beliefs filter the evidence — sharing studies that confirm you, rejecting ones that don't.
  • Trying to destroy your ego, then feeling proud of your humility. The fight is the ego.
  • Taking full credit for your wins, which blocks gratitude and weakens real confidence.
  • Waiting to feel motivated or to find the efficient start — avoidance dressed up as planning.
  • Mistaking consumption for change.
  • Forcing positivity onto a mind full of unprocessed negativity, then concluding visualization "doesn't work for you."

Try the experiment: attempt to do the thing and watch what happens inside you. You'll feel a heavy weight, a strong discomfort pinning you in place. At bottom, what you're avoiding is spending energy and having nothing to show for it.

Why "fixing your life" feels terrible

We romanticize taking control of our lives. The reality is brutal:

Taking control of your life means going through the mail, emptying out your closet, paying all the debts that you owe.

Catching up doesn't feel good — you expend effort just to remove negativity, with no visible gain. Paying off a debt costs energy and gives you nothing back, because you already spent the thing. That kind of effort is incredibly hard to reinforce, which is exactly why people stay stuck.

So the core move is to decouple action from feeling good — in both directions. Don't wait for motivation, and don't make winning the fuel either:

If I'm motivated by wins, the moment I lose, my motivation decreases. My motivation should be independent of wins or losses.

Be tranquil even in the face of your accomplishments. Action should be independent of outcome. It's not about how it makes you feel; it's about doing it.

Efficiency is the source of paralysis

The mind loves complexity because complexity gives it room to stall.

The most people in life are paralyzed because they're waiting for an efficient start. Efficiency is the source of paralysis.

I'll start once I figure out the optimal way. I'll send the email Tuesday because Tuesdays get better response rates. Every added variable is another place for the mind to hide and another excuse to delay. The antidote is radical simplicity: when there's nothing to optimize, the mind can't trick you.

This is also why no amount of consumed content fixes anything. If motivation gurus were enough, everyone watching them would already be happy and successful. Outside knowledge has to be downloaded and run — and we've built a society of people who download endlessly and never open the file.

Is Creativity Learnable?

Yes. Creativity is roughly two things, and both can be trained.

It's a state of mind, not an action. The ancient idea of the muse or genius — a creative force that inhabits you, lets you produce intensely, then leaves — captures something real. Creativity is a state in which things click together and connections form, and when you exit that state you can't reach it the same way. The learnable skill is inducing it. This is why genuinely creative people are drawn to anything that alters consciousness — substances, yes, but also meditation. The first skill is the intentional creation of certain states of mind.

It's reorderable building blocks. From a neuroscience angle, creativity is the recombination of things you already have. So:

  • Feed it more inputs. The standard cure for writer's block is to read more. Given enough material and enough excitement, the mind starts assembling things on its own.
  • Build a ritual and a space. One writer can't work in his pajamas — he showers, shaves, dresses in work clothes, and sits at his desk. The routine and environment cue the creative state. Artists need workspaces conducive to it.
  • Force the connections. Constrain yourself deliberately — draw with your non-dominant hand, write sentences without the letter S. The artificial restriction forces your brain to manipulate familiar material in unfamiliar ways. Writers' workshops do this by demanding you produce on command, without waiting to feel inspired — which is itself a crucial part of the craft.

And because creative work almost always lets you iterate, you can produce uninspired and let the inspiration arrive later in revision. Forcible production in the absence of the muse is a real, trainable skill.

When Positive Visualization Turns Negative

A reader tries to visualize something positive and the scene keeps curdling into something negative. Here's why.

Normally your attention faces outward — TV, work, the live stream, whatever — and external stimulus suppresses what's inside. This is literal: absorbed in a game, your brain mutes hunger and thirst signals, which is why you can play for eight hours and only feel famished afterward. Visualization reverses this. You turn away from the outside world to build an inner scene. But if your inner world is already full of unprocessed material, that's what surfaces.

If my cup is full of sewage water, I can't add milk to it. I have to empty my cup.

So when negativity floods in, don't force the positive image and don't shove the negativity away. Let it come up and, crucially, let it out — journal it, speak it aloud, make art, move your body. (If there's a history of trauma, do this with a professional; classic silent mindfulness can be the wrong tool, because watching the inner mind just unleashes the flood. People with trauma often need external stimulus precisely to stay away from what's buried.)

But venting alone isn't the cure. The most common mistake is venting the same sad thoughts every day for years and wondering why they never leave. After you let the negativity out, you have to look at it critically:

Why do the sad thoughts never disappear? Where are they coming from? When did I first start to feel this way?

There are subconscious "negative-energy generators" producing these thoughts. You can vent each thought individually forever, but until you trace it to its source and examine it critically — which is much of what therapy provides — the generator keeps running. Only once the cup is emptied and the source addressed can a positive visualization actually take hold. If all you see when you try is negativity, your trash can is simply overflowing and needs to be emptied first.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing momentary suppression with healthy emotional regulation. Holding anger back in the moment is fine — but if you never process it later, you're just stockpiling repression that leaks out as chronic tilt or anxiety.
  • Believing endless venting equals healing. Journaling or ranting the same feelings for years without ever looking critically at their source keeps you exactly where you started.
  • Reading causation into cross-sectional correlation, and trusting headlines over methods sections.
  • Letting your beliefs filter the evidence — eagerly sharing studies that confirm you, rejecting quality studies that don't.
  • Trying to destroy your ego, then feeling proud of your humility. The fight is the ego.
  • Taking full credit for your wins, which blocks gratitude, inflates ego, and paradoxically weakens real confidence.
  • Waiting to feel motivated or to find the efficient start. Both are forms of avoidance dressed up as planning.
  • Mistaking consumption for change — downloading insight after insight and never running any of it.
  • Forcing positivity onto a mind that's full of unprocessed negativity, then concluding visualization "doesn't work for you."

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Take out the emotional trash. After you suppress a feeling in the moment, schedule the processing later: (a) name it precisely — fear, shame, despair, not just "stressed"; (b) vent it through the body (exercise, a hike) or through language (speak it aloud or write it). Saying it out loud recruits problem-solving circuits that silent rumination never touches.

  2. Run the science checklist. For any study or headline: assume the headline has its own agenda → go to the source → read the methods section first. Ask: Is this cross-sectional (a snapshot) or could it actually show causation? Is the control group a real control? Am I judging this study by its quality, or by whether it confirms what I already believe?

  3. Catch the ego, then hand back the credit. When you accomplish something, notice the thought "I did this." Then list how much of the outcome came from circumstances you never chose — your brain, your upbringing, the mentors who happened to appear. Replace "I deserve credit" with "I'm grateful." Watch ego drop and confidence rise together.

  4. Drop the good/bad grading. When you feel superior or inferior to someone, practice seeing it as different point in the journey, different hand dealt — neither morally better nor worse. The ego loses its fuel when the ranking disappears.

  5. The 15-to-60-minute action drill. Make a list of everything you've been avoiding. Use a random number generator to pick one — don't deliberate. Do something on it for 15 to 60 minutes. The action is the goal; right, wrong, and efficient don't matter. Repeat daily, increasing time. As you go, watch the internal resistance — it's not the right one, I'll do it later, what if it's not enough — and notice that giving in once is what produces inaction.

  6. Build a creativity ritual. Pick a fixed workspace and a pre-work routine (get dressed, sit at the desk) to cue the creative state. Feed your inputs by reading more. Force recombination with constraints — non-dominant hand, no-letter-S sentences, an assigned prompt. Produce even when uninspired; iterate later.

  7. Empty the cup before you visualize. Close your eyes and let whatever negativity arises come up — don't force positivity, don't push it down. Vent it out (journal, speak, art, movement). Then look at it critically: Where is this coming from? When did it start? Only after emptying and examining will positive visualization hold.
