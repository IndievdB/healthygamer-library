Holding Space, Healing, and the Mind's Echoes

Holding space means doing almost nothing while staying present. The urge to "fix" comes from feeling responsible and from not tolerating your own discomfort. Instead: stay attuned, offer presence, reflect rather than reassure ("tell me more"), and tolerate your own powerlessness. Most people who feel they're "barely holding on" need only a small nudge.

The body runs in two modes — survival or building — and rewiring requires a calm nervous system. "It's hard to heal when you're being cut."

Two related traps: the freeze response runs deeper than conscious will (lower your stress so you can "afford the conflict"), and internal conflict — every "but" pushing both ways — paralyzes you. Examine each belief; one side is what you truly believe, the other what you want to believe.

Fantasy regulates emotion but never processes it. It puts feelings dormant, which is why daydreaming for years heals no one. Treat recurring fantasies as breadcrumbs to the real lack; build that thing in life, and the fantasies stop.

Finally, tiredness and consciousness are created moment to moment, not fixed levels you carry. Tiredness discourages behavior; much chronic exhaustion is suppression or living the right life for everyone else. The mind holds echoes that take months to subside — which is why true stillness is so hard to reach.

Holding Space Means Being "Irresponsible"

When someone we care about suffers, we feel a powerful urge to fix it, from two sources: (1) they came to us, so we feel responsible for their situation; and (2) we can't tolerate our own discomfort — their pain triggers empathic sadness and helplessness that feel unbearable, so we try to make them stop feeling, which makes us stop feeling. That's why we say "calm down" — we're shoving the emotion back down because we have no space for it.

Holding space is the opposite. It mostly means doing nothing — but not being empty. Being present without acting. In practice:

  • Be attuned. Don't mind-wander; stay with the person.
  • A hug or physical presence, if the relationship allows. A child who climbs into your lap and cries needs to be held while the emotion runs its course. Held emotions calm down on their own.
  • Reflect, don't reassure. "It sounds like you're at your wit's end." "I'm with you — tell me more." Avoid "don't worry, it'll all be okay," which shuts the emotion down.
  • Tolerate your own powerlessness. You see someone one hour a week; they live the other 111 waking hours without you. You can't fix their life — only help them fix it themselves.

A liberating reframe: if you're hanging off a cliff and barely holding on, the downward and upward forces are equal — so it takes almost nothing, a finger of force from a spotter, to get you back up. Most hopeless people need far less help than they think. And the minimum viable version of support is genuine, engaged acknowledgment:

"Brah. Damn. Whoa. No way. Oh my god. That's all you need to show engagement."

The Freeze Response and the Civil War in Your Head

2. Internal conflict paralyzes you. Notice the structure: "I know conflict is healthy, but I'm terrified. But I know that's dumb. And yet..." Every "but" pushes the opposite way. Pushing yourself in both directions at once, you don't move at all. That's the freeze.

Holding Space for Emotions Means Being "Irresponsible"

When someone we care about is suffering, we feel a powerful urge to fix it. This urge comes from two places:

  1. They came to us for help, so we feel responsible for their situation. Let me hire the lawyer, find you a rebound, send you to camp, solve the problem.
  2. We can't tolerate our own discomfort. Their pain triggers our empathy, and our empathic sadness, helplessness, and powerlessness feel unbearable — so we try to make them stop feeling, which makes us stop feeling.

So when someone's emotion overflows, we tell them to "calm down" — which is really us shoving the emotion back down because we have no space for it. We bottle our own feelings, and we have no room for anyone else's.

Holding space is the opposite. It mostly means doing nothing — but not being empty. Being present without acting.

What it looks like in practice:

  • Be attuned. Don't mind-wander. Stay with the person.
  • A hug or physical presence, if the relationship allows. A child who climbs into your lap and cries doesn't need reassurance — they need to be held while the emotion runs its course. Held emotions calm down on their own.
  • Reflect, don't reassure. "It sounds like you're at your wit's end." "I'm so sorry you're going through this." "I'm with you — tell me more." Avoid "don't worry, it'll all be okay," because that's another way of shutting the emotion down.
  • Tolerate your own powerlessness. This is the hardest part for any therapist, coach, or parent: you want to fix it, and you usually can't. You see someone one hour a week; they live the other 111 waking hours without you. You can't fix their life — you can only help them fix it themselves.

A liberating reframe on "barely holding on": if you're hanging off a cliff and barely holding, it means the downward and upward forces are equal. How much does it take to get you back up? Almost nothing — a finger of force from a spotter. Most people who feel hopeless need far less help than they think. Holding space is often exactly that small nudge.

And if reflective listening feels like too much, the minimum viable version is genuine, engaged acknowledgment:

"Brah. Damn. Whoa. No way. Oh my god. That's all you need to show engagement."
