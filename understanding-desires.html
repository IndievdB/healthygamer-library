The Hedonic Treadmill and the Mechanics of Desire

You want, you're unhappy until you get it, you're briefly happy, then you want again. This feels unwinnable because we rely on desire to motivate us, yet indulging it breeds compulsion — and maxing out conscientiousness (postponing all enjoyment) makes people less happy too.

There's no way to win, bluntly. You can eat a cookie today and you'll want another cookie tomorrow.

The dopamine system (nucleus accumbens) creates craving, pleasure, and reinforcement; the limbic system drives avoidance of negative emotion; the endocannabinoid system builds habit. Whatever you give into gains more influence over future decisions. The Eastern contemplatives were doing practical, DIY neuroscience — running these techniques on themselves — and their maps line up cleanly with what we now know.

Karma: What You're Signing Up For

The first law: anything you do, your brain will repeat. Each gratification plants a seed.

Each time you gratify a desire, you are creating a karma. If I eat a cookie today, I'm signing myself up for a craving tomorrow.

So never ask "should I indulge?" Ask: when I do this, what am I signing up for tomorrow? This reframes substances: drugs don't make you happy — they're a loan against future happiness. Caffeine doesn't give energy; it blocks fatigue. It's all cause and effect.

Pray for Strength, Not Outcomes

Wishing for a million dollars closes the loop — nothing left to reinforce. Wishing for strength reinforces a circuit pointed inward, at your own capability.

Don't wish for your life to be easier. Wish for the strength to handle a hard life.

The science: visualization works only when you're the subject — seniors who merely visualized their own hands getting stronger gained up to 60% grip strength. And aiming at strength keeps the cost in view, so difficulty doesn't ambush you and make you quit.

Dopamine, Serotonin, and Perceived Effort

Dopamine sets the reward; serotonin governs your perception of the effort required. Serotonin doesn't enlarge the prize — it makes the climb feel smaller. Depression (low serotonin) makes getting out of bed feel impossible though the reward is unchanged. Gratifying dopamine repeatedly lowers serotonin, making everything feel harder; accomplishment raises serotonin, making the next hard task feel cheap.

The tool: separate the formless need from the claimed form. Craving a burger is really hunger — a salad resolves it. Eat the salad and your brain learns I didn't need the burger; I can be content with less. You conquer the desire once, instead of fighting it daily.

The Real Root: You Cherish Desire Itself

We cherish desire. That cherishing is the root of all the problems.

Drop it and a desire becomes just a desire — it loses its grip. This is not anhedonia.

The less you want, the more pleasurable things can become.

A burger you weren't craving still tastes great; your kid's badly made sandwich still delights you. What vanishes is the attachment — getting bent out of shape when it isn't perfect. Distinguish something mattering from something being enjoyable.

Awareness Does the Work

When you burn yourself on a hot stove, you don't have to work hard to avoid touching it. Awareness feeds the subconscious — and the brain makes the right choices automatically.

Your brain calculates motivation like your stomach digests food — but only on the inputs it gets. Biases and propaganda feed it corrupted data. Awareness purifies the inputs, and the rest happens by itself.

Common Pitfalls

  • Sliding into asceticism. The goal isn't grim denial; it's clear awareness of what each choice costs. You can still enjoy the cookie — just stop owning the craving.
  • Believing detachment kills joy. Backwards. Dropping the wanting makes pleasure purer, like a child seeing snow.
  • Avoidance masquerading as courage. "Courage to give up wanting a relationship" is often black-pilled fatalism. Real courage is striving fully while knowing it may not work.
  • Resisting desire out of cherishing desire. A "desire to be desireless" is the same trap in a holy costume.
  • Acting from emotion and calling it motivation. Socializing only when lonely reinforces the emotional circuit — and the moment anxiety outweighs loneliness, you'll bail.

  1. Run the karma question first. Before any impulse, ask: What am I signing up for tomorrow? Let the answer decide.

The Inverse Relationship Between Desire and Happiness

The central, paradoxical insight: gratifying a desire doesn't technically produce happiness.

Our baseline state is happy. A desire arises and our happiness disappears. Desire is what stands in the way of happiness. We gratify the desire, the desire disappears, and we are left with happiness again.

Contentment is defined by the absence of desire. Even the most driven optimizers chase a state of being done — which is just "no remaining desire." So there's an inverse relationship between the strength of your desires and your happiness. The worker thrilled by a $50,000 bonus becomes miserable the instant he learns a colleague got $75,000 — a fresh desire wiped out his contentment.

The yogis found the loophole. There are two ways to want nothing: satisfy all your desires, or satisfy none. One is impossible; the other is available now. Picture bliss (ananda) as your baseline with desire as a wall. Gratifying the desire shrinks the wall — but so does simply letting it go. The point was never the gratification; it was always shrinking the wall.

Most of Your Desires Aren't Even Yours

How can someone want sex "more than anything" having never experienced it — never had a single dopamine release from it? Because the ego (ahamkar) takes a raw sensory impulse and stamps it yours.

Most desires are not yours. They are thalamic inputs that you then claim as your own. Advertisers understand this perfectly — they evoke a desire and trick you into thinking you want it.

We even build identities from what we lack and want ("I want freedom, therefore I'm a hustle guy"). But what you actually are is your experience now, not your projections of what you want.

The tool is to separate the formless need from the claimed form. Crave a burger and the real signal is hunger — formless. A salad would resolve it; the "burger" is the form your mind attached. Unhappiness sets in when you believe only that form satisfies. A crush works the same: the real signals — loneliness, lack of contact — come from inside; the fixation on this specific person is projection.

So when desire grips you, ask: Why do I want this? When did it become mine? Trace it to the formless need, then ask how else it could be met. Eat the salad when craving the burger and something powerful happens — hunger is the ultimate seasoning, so it tastes great, and your brain learns I didn't need the burger. The Real Root: You Cherish Desire Itself

The master key: the problem isn't this desire or that one. It's the silent background belief almost nobody notices — the desire to have your desires fulfilled.

We cherish desire. We don't want to let go of it. We think: if I have no desires, I'll have no fun, I'll accomplish nothing. That cherishing is the root of all the problems.

Inside a system where "gratifying desires = happiness," every desire traps you. Remove the cherishing and a desire that arises is just a desire — it loses its grip. (This loops back only once: even "I desire to have no desires" assumes fulfilling that brings happiness — same trap.)

And the fear people get wrong: dropping cherishing does not make you numb. This is not anhedonia.

The less you want, the more pleasurable things can become. The wanting is a coloring, an expectation. If you get it, fine; if you don't, fine — but the intrinsic pleasure is still there.

Hand someone an uncraved burger and it's still delicious. What disappears isn't enjoyment — it's the attachment, getting bent out of shape when the sandwich isn't perfect. Hold the distinction: something mattering and something being enjoyable are different. You can unfuse them.

Avoidance-Desires Versus Moving-Toward Desires

The most corrosive desires are desires for the absence of something. One person moves toward goals; the avoidant person (common after trauma) phrases everything as "I don't want": I don't want to work, to be lonely, to get up. These only ever move you to neutral — and once there, motivation evaporates. Worse, every avoidance strengthens the pipeline from emotion to behavior until feelings run your life.

If loneliness motivates you to socialize, be careful. The moment your social anxiety outweighs your loneliness, that's the circuit driving your behavior — and you'll leave, reinforcing avoidance.

The fix: socialize when you're not lonely — act from your frontal lobes (goals) or a dopaminergic channel (a shared hobby), not emotional desperation. Lean on the other conduits and the emotional one shrinks.

Calling: The Desires That Come From Inside

Clear out the implanted desires and something subtler surfaces. In the karmic framework you carry a storehouse of past karma (sanchit), a portion activates this life (prarabdha), and you generate new karma as you act (agami). Some impulses arise from prarabdha — deeper in. These are your dharma, your calling.

You can't find dharma by searching harder, because the mind you'd use is clouded by noise. Purify the mind and the calling floats up on its own. You can tell it by quality: a thalamic desire feels like craving claimed by ego; a calling feels like hunger or a task you're meant to complete — executed with tranquility, not obsession.

As you move toward your dharma, things won't get easy — they'll often get harder. But your strength grows and your contentment grows. Your stress goes down even as the work goes up.

Then the right motivation and the dropping of cherished desires happen by themselves.

  • After a month of pure indulgence — gaming instead of studying — you feel low, and the moment you think about working, it feels enormous. That's serotonin dropping.
  • After a week of working hard followed by a weekend of games, you feel content and accomplished — serotonin rises, the next hard task feels easier.

Serotonin comes from a sense of accomplishment. The more you gratify your dopamine over and over, the lower your serotonin tends to go — and the harder everything starts to feel.

This is how some people work brutally hard and end up happier: high serotonin makes high effort feel cheap.

The Inverse Relationship Between Desire and Happiness

Now the central, almost paradoxical insight. We assume gratifying a desire produces happiness. Technically, it doesn't.

Our baseline state is happy. A desire arises and our happiness disappears. Desire is what stands in the way of happiness — it's not what gives us happiness. We gratify the desire, the desire disappears, and we are left with happiness again.

Happiness comes from the desire going away. Contentment is defined by the absence of desire. Notice that even the most driven optimizers are chasing a state of being done — total freedom, no constraints, all desires satisfied — which is just another way of saying "no remaining desire."

There is therefore an inverse relationship between the strength of your desires and the amount of your happiness. The finance worker thrilled by a $50,000 bonus becomes miserable the instant he learns a colleague got $75,000 — a fresh desire wiped out his contentment.

The yogis found the loophole. There are two ways to reach the state of wanting nothing: satisfy all your desires, or satisfy none of them. One is practically impossible. The other is available right now. Picture bliss (ananda) as your baseline, with desire as a wall between you and it. Gratifying the desire shrinks the wall — but so does simply letting the desire go. The point was never the gratification. It was always the shrinking of the wall.

Most of Your Desires Aren't Even Yours

Consider the person who has never had sex but wants it more than anything in the world. How can you want something more than anything when you've never experienced it — when you've never had a single dopamine release from it?

The answer reveals the trick: the ego (ahamkar) takes a raw sensory impulse and stamps it yours.

Most desires are not yours. They are thalamic inputs that you then claim as your own. Advertisers understand this perfectly — they evoke a desire in you and trick you into thinking you want it.

And notice: we build our identities not out of what we have but out of what we lack and want. "I want sex more than anything, therefore I'm an incel." "I want freedom and money, therefore I'm a hustle guy." But what you actually are is the experience you're having now and the experiences you've had — not your projections of what you want.

The practical tool is to separate the formless need from the claimed form:

  • When you crave a burger, the real signal from your body is hunger — formless. Any food resolves it; a salad would make the hunger vanish. The "burger" is the form your mind attached from the outside. Unhappiness sets in when you believe only that form will satisfy you.
  • A crush works the same way. The real signals — loneliness, lack of human contact, horniness — come from inside you. The fixation on this specific person being the one is your mind's projection, claimed by ego.

So when a desire grips you, ask: Why do I want this? When did it become mine? Trace it back to the formless need underneath, then ask how else that need could be met. Eat the salad when you're craving the burger and something quietly powerful happens — hunger is the ultimate seasoning, so the salad tastes great, and your brain wires in the lesson: I didn't need the burger; I can be content with less. That's how eating healthy becomes effortless instead of a daily war. You conquer the desire once, and then it's conquered. The people who struggle are the ones fighting the same battle every single day because they never actually let it go.

The same logic exposes toxic relationships. The healthy ones leave you content — they "encourage sloth," reducing your need for other things. Toxic relationships manufacture discontent and then relieve it: love-bomb today, ignore tomorrow. They generate the desire that destroys your peace, then sell you the cure.

The Real Root: You Cherish Desire Itself

Here is the master key. The problem isn't this desire or that desire. It's the silent, background belief almost nobody notices they hold: the desire to have your desires fulfilled.

We cherish desire. We don't want to let go of it. We think: if I have no desires, I'll have no fun, I'll accomplish nothing. That cherishing is the root of all the problems.

If you live inside a system where "gratifying desires = happiness," then every time a desire arises you're trapped — your own physics demands you chase it. Remove the cherishing, and watch what happens when a desire shows up: it's just a desire. It loses its grip. Importantly, this only loops back once — even "I desire to have no desires" still assumes that fulfilling that desire will bring happiness, so it's the same trap, not a separate one.

And here's the part people fear and get wrong: dropping the cherishing does not make you apathetic or numb. This is not anhedonia. The inability to feel pleasure has nothing to do with how much you want things. In fact:

The less you want, the more pleasurable things can become. The wanting is a coloring, an expectation. If you get it, fine; if you don't, fine — but the intrinsic pleasure is still there.

If someone hands you a burger you weren't craving, you bite in and it's delicious — the sensory pleasure is untouched. Your kids hand you a badly made sandwich and your enjoyment can be enormous. What disappears isn't enjoyment — it's the attachment: the getting bent out of shape when the sandwich isn't perfect. You become free to receive what comes. The distinction to hold: there is a difference between something mattering and something being enjoyable. We fuse the two; you can unfuse them.

Avoidance-Desires Versus Moving-Toward Desires

Not all desires are created equal, and the most corrosive ones are the desires for the absence of something.

There are two motivational modes. One person moves toward goals; the other is wired for avoidance — extremely common after trauma. The avoidant person's desires are all phrased as "I don't want": I don't want to work, I don't want to be lonely, I don't want to deal with people, I don't want to get up. These are real desires — but they only ever move you to neutral, and once you hit neutral the motivation evaporates.

Worse, every act of avoidance strengthens the pipeline from emotion to behavior, until your feelings run your life and discipline becomes impossible (emotions come and go, so the fuel is never reliable). The loneliness example makes it vivid:

If loneliness motivates you to socialize, be careful. The moment your social anxiety outweighs your loneliness, that's the circuit driving your behavior — and you'll leave, reinforcing avoidance.

The fix is to socialize when you're not lonely — to act from your frontal lobes (long-term goals) or through a dopaminergic channel (a Magic: The Gathering tournament, a shared hobby) rather than from emotional desperation. Lean on the other conduits to behavior and the emotional one shrinks.

Calling: The Desires That Come From Inside

Once you start clearing out the thalamic, externally-implanted desires, something subtler surfaces. In the karmic framework, you carry a storehouse of past karma (sanchit); a portion of it activates in this life (prarabdha); and you generate new karma as you act (agami). Some impulses don't come from advertising or sensory bait — they arise from prarabdha, from deeper in. These are your dharma, your calling.

You can't find your dharma by searching harder, because the instrument you'd use to detect it — your mind — is clouded by all the noise. Purify the mind and the calling floats to the surface on its own. And you can tell the difference by quality:

  • A thalamic desire feels like craving and gets claimed by ego.
  • A calling feels more like hunger or a task you're simply meant to complete — you execute it with tranquility, not obsession.

The diagnostic is your overall contentment as you move toward it:

As you move in the direction of your dharma, things won't get easy — they'll often get harder. But your strength grows and your contentment grows. Your stress goes down even as the work goes up.

This is the opposite of the burnt-out striver chasing thalamic desires, dumping dopamine, draining serotonin, finding every task heavier than the last.

Awareness Does the Work for You

The whole method rests on one quietly radical claim: you don't have to force change. You have to feed your brain accurate information, and it changes on its own.

When you burn yourself on a hot stove, you don't have to work hard to avoid touching it. When you enjoy a cookie, you don't have to work hard to want another. Awareness feeds the subconscious — and then the brain makes the right choices automatically.

Your body runs digestion, heartbeat, and temperature regulation without your effort. Your brain calculates motivation the same way — but only on the inputs it receives. Cognitive biases, propaganda, echo chambers, and unexamined assumptions feed it corrupted data, so it produces corrupted outputs. Awareness purifies the inputs. That's it. Then the right motivation, the dropping of cherished desires, the redirection toward dharma — all of it happens by itself, the way your stomach knows how to digest whatever you put in it.

Common Pitfalls

  • Sliding into asceticism. "Karma means I should never eat the cookie" misses the point. The goal isn't grim self-denial; it's clear-eyed awareness of what each choice signs you up for. You can still enjoy the cookie — you just stop owning the craving.
  • Believing detachment kills joy. The single biggest fear, and it's backwards. Detachment isn't anhedonia. Dropping the wanting makes pleasure purer, not absent — like a child seeing snow, free of expectation and ego.
  • Avoidance masquerading as courage. "I need the courage to give up wanting a relationship and accept I may be alone forever" sounds brave but is often fatalistic, black-pilled avoidance. Real courage isn't for giving things up — it's for striving with everything you have while knowing it may not work. It's chemotherapy energy. So the courage you need isn't to renounce a partner; it's to go to the speed-dating night, ask for honest feedback, change your style, keep showing up.
  • Confusing fearlessness with courage. Fearlessness (think a mother bear — oxytocin suppressing the fear of retaliation) is the absence of fear. Courage is your willpower (anterior cingulate) overriding a fear that's fully present. You only need courage at the start; awareness dissolves the desire over time, so you don't have to muscle through forever.
  • Resisting desire out of cherishing desire. If you fight your desires endlessly and it never gets easier, you're probably reinforcing the meta-craving — clinging to a desire to be desireless, which is the same trap wearing a holy costume.
  • Visualizing the prize instead of yourself. Picturing the perfect partner or the marriage does nothing. Picturing your own actions and your own body actually rewires you.
  • Acting from emotion and calling it motivation. Socializing because you're lonely, masturbating to manage stress — these reinforce emotional dependence. The relief feels like progress; it's actually deepening the cage.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the karma question before indulging. Before giving in to any impulse — a game, a snack, a text back to your ex — pause and ask: What am I signing up for tomorrow? Let the answer, not the urge, decide.
  2. Switch your prayers from outcomes to strength. Stop wishing for the result. Each day, ask for the strength to do the hard thing in front of you. You'll reinforce your own agency instead of outsourcing your life.
  3. Visualize yourself, never the prize. Spend a minute picturing your own body doing the thing — getting up, walking out the door, sitting down to study, lifting the weight. Keep the camera on you.
  4. Trace any craving back to its formless need. When desire grips you, ask: Why do I want this? When did it become mine? What's the actual need underneath? Then meet that need a different way — eat the salad when you crave the burger, and let your brain learn it didn't need the burger after all.
  5. Catch the meta-desire. Ask yourself plainly: What do I want more than anything? Notice the honest answer is often "to get the things I want." That's the cherishing. Practice holding desires as a part of me wants this rather than I want this — and see how the grip loosens.
  6. Audit your motivation: toward or away? When you act, check whether you're moving toward something or just escaping discomfort to reach neutral. Socialize, work, and reach out when you're not desperate — act from goals or genuine interest, not from emotional emergency.
  7. Do the things that actually serve the goal — with courage to fail. If you want a relationship, the work isn't renouncing the desire; it's the small brave acts: the blind date, deleting the apps that aren't working, changing your style, asking for feedback — done at 100% while accepting it may not pan out.
  8. (Advanced) The brahmacharya experiment. If you have the opportunity, engage in sexual activity without reaching climax, then within 15–60 minutes sit and meditate with full focus. Observe what happens to the quality of your attention.
  9. Make awareness the whole practice. Don't grind to change yourself. Simply observe your desires, their origins, and their effects honestly — give your brain clean data and let it recalculate. As you go deeper (especially in meditation), start following the small impulses that surface: to eat better, sleep on time, drop a draining friendship. Stop sabotaging the process, and the change runs itself.
