The Bias That Makes Everything Easier: Detachment and Fluidity

The whole approach rests on one disposition: be as tranquil as possible about how work goes. It's good to want things — progress, money, title. But the moment all your eggs are in one basket — this promotion, this boss — you're in for a rough ride, because you cannot control another person. Losing a promotion often means little: you can frequently get the same title and more money at a competing firm.

"When you get overly caught up in a particular thing, that's when things become very difficult, because fundamentally you can't control someone else."

If your circumstances make you un-fluid (caretaking, few lateral options), that immobility is the first problem to solve.

This isn't indifference. The model is medicine: a doctor shows up focused, driven, compassionate, owns mistakes, improves — but doesn't let their value as a person hang on the outcome. You pay attention to outcomes. You just don't become them.

External Skill #1: Understand Your Real Value (ROI)

Ask honestly: what is my value at work? People give defensible answers — team player, solving problems. All real. But too few think in terms of return on investment:

"Your value, and what most companies value, is people who will generate more money than you pay them. Bluntly. Capitalism, baby."

Companies that pay people more than they generate go under. So the work appreciated most visibly moves the bottom line. People make two mistakes: they go the extra mile on things that don't affect the bottom line (planning office parties) and then feel betrayed; or they do high-value work but never translate it into money.

That second one is fixable. If you're an admin paid $40k supporting a boss paid $150k, you free up expensive hours for revenue work. When asking for a raise, don't say "I work hard." Say: "Here's whose time I support, here's what it's worth, here's how I let them close four deals instead of two." Quantify it. Use comparative data.

One caution: some valuable work is structurally invisible (you run 12 interviews to make one hire; the company sees the hire, not the 11 you weeded out). You have to narrate that value. And don't go black-and-white — ROI is one tool in the belt, not the meaning of work.

External Skill #2: Be Responsible — Then Guard Against What That Causes

The best single thing you can do is be someone people want to work with, and at the core is responsibility — meaning owning the outcome, making sure the task gets done, not just doing your rote part and shrugging at obstacles.

"There are some people who, if they get assigned a task, will make sure it gets done. And there are some people that will basically do what they think they should do, but they won't take responsibility for the task."

When you hit an obstacle, run this rubric:

  1. Flag the problem early. Don't disappear and resurface four days later. Try for a few hours, then say: "I'm running into this."
  2. Propose a plan. "I've called twice, checked corporate, I'll try X and Y next."
  3. Ask for help and set a tether. "Can we touch base tomorrow EOD?"

This demonstrates competence, raises awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, and mentally prepares people for bad news so you never go zero-to-a-hundred. Be a smoke detector, not the moment someone notices the house is already on fire. There's an emotional payoff: when you visibly own a problem, others relax — like the Jeff Bezos story, where the executive scrambled over an upside-down demo and Bezos stayed calm: "I'm not worried, because clearly you are."

The trap inside responsibility

Here's the cruel twist:

"If you are responsible, other people will become lazy and they'll start shunting responsibility onto you. The more of a team player you are, the more people start to rely on you."

So manage the pattern: help once freely; on the second request stay warm but raise awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition → ("What's going on? Are you having trouble?"); on the third, "I've got a lot on my plate — if you're stuck, maybe we go to the boss about support you need."

With your boss, be subtler, since helping them is your job: "I've been asked this a few times — happy to. Is this a new responsibility? Which priority does it move down?" If they say "everything's a priority, be a team player," they're crunched. That's your opening for compassion: "I can see everyone's stepping up. How are you holding up?"

"You can demonstrate compassion without turning into a doormat."

You can push back, ensure the work is acknowledged, plan to ask for a raise later — and be genuinely kind. People remember who showed them grace during the crunch.

Spread help wide, not deep

There's a concept: eigenvector centrality — how central you are in a network. Doing favors for one person repeatedly makes you a peripheral node, an asset they own; your standing doesn't rise.

"Do favors for a lot of people once. You don't become one person's crutch — you become a lot of people's eleventh-hour person."

A bonus: many people will want to work with you, but you don't have to work with all. Pick the responsible, pleasant ones and tell your boss: "We gel — pair us on projects." (Dark edge case: sometimes a boss blocks your promotion to keep you under them. Common in finance.)

External Skill #3: Never Be a One-Trick Pony — Say It Once

This is the quiet rule behind half of all friction. The mistake usually isn't saying the wrong thing — it's saying the right thing too many times.

  • Apologies. Apologize once, twice at most, then demonstrate a fix. Apologize five times and you've created five reminders that you screw up. Over-apologizing soothes your guilt — take that to a therapist, not your team.
  • Jokes. A joke once is funny. The same joke 10,000 times — a nickname, a running bit at someone's expense — is bullying.
  • Praise. Give it when authentic, then spread it around. Thank someone five times and you become sycophantic.
  • Reminders. Communicate once, twice, maybe a gentle third. If they didn't listen, stepping back beats nagging. (Still keep a paper trail — that's different.)

A clever variant: when someone takes credit for your work, claim it by giving it. "Total team effort — that piece you did really got it over the line." Passing credit downward quietly establishes you were central.

Repetition feels comfortable — being "the apology guy," "the donuts-every-Friday guy." A genuinely appreciated ritual is fine, but default to variety.

External Skill #4: Attitude — Flag, Don't Complain; Withhold Criticism

The difference between flagging and complaining is your level of responsibility. A complainer states a negative and expects someone else to fix it: "This tea is cold." A flagger states it clinically and owns a piece of the solution: "Invoices are going out late — here's the impact, can I help?"

The harder lesson: don't give feedback or criticism unless asked — and even then, pause and gauge how it lands. The biggest misread is listening to what people say instead of watching what they do. A boss announcing "I'm an open book, tell me anything" usually wants reassurance, not criticism.

"They absolutely want to know all the negative things you think about them — but there's a really good chance, because they're human, they're going to kill the messenger."

So buy a beat: "Can I think for a second?" Run the options, don't lie, but calibrate to what they can metabolize. Never deliver feedback you don't think the person can act on.

The Social Layer: Mentorship and Meetings

The internet loves "colleagues aren't your friends, keep boundaries." Good defensive advice — but it collides with reality:

"Many friendships form in the workplace. Many people who get married met in the workplace."

Much of modern lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → comes from work changing — remote, warehouse, delivery — drying up the organic channels humans used to form relationships through. Both paths work; just don't pretend boundary-purism is cost-free.

Mentorship comes from your job, a professional organization, or organically (often the best, including former bosses). Don't cold-email "Will you be my mentor?" — that's a huge undefined commitment. Instead, make the lift low, targeted, curious:

"Hey, I noticed you do X. I'd love to learn more about this specific thing. Do you have time for a short conversation?"

Then let it grow. After a good talk, thank them, ask to meet again in six months. And ask good questions — not Google-able or generic, but requiring critical thinking and specific to their expertise. The deeper point: to be mentored, be someone worth mentoring — already solid, exceptional with a little targeted help.

Meetings exist even when they could be an email, because meetings are about feelings — coordination, everyone feeling on the same page. Walk in honoring that someone with more power believes it matters. Meetings are also about visibility. Being quiet is fine, but:

  • Every time you speak, you take an opportunity from someone else.
  • Every time you speak, you dilute the quality of what you say — a 9-out-of-10 point watered down by your 2s.

When you can't get a word in, raise your hand — Zoom and Meet both built the button because it maps to how humans coordinate. And use meetings to connect: a joke, a shout-out for good work.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the two-person split. Write two columns for your current frustration — my contribution / theirs — then ask the buried question: "Why do I want this thing?" Name how much is self-worth.
  2. Translate your work into dollars. Rewrite your three biggest contributions in money or comparative terms. Use that language next raise conversation.
  3. Adopt the obstacle rubric. Flag early, propose a plan that shows your thinking, ask for help and set a tether ("Check in tomorrow EOD?").
  4. Track the favor pattern. Help freely once; on the second ask get curious; on the third, route to the boss or a real conversation about support.
  5. Spread help wide, not deep. Small favors for many people, then ask your boss to pair you with those you work well with.
  6. Apply the "say it once" filter. Before apologizing, repeating a joke, thanking, or reminding — check if you've already said it. If yes, switch to demonstrating a fix.
  7. Pause before any feedback. "Can I think for a second?" Predict the reaction, calibrate to what they can act on, watch behavior over words.
  8. Reframe one meeting. Ask "Who wants this and why?" Contribute only your highest-value point; if shut out, raise your hand.
  9. Find a mentor organically. Send one short, specific, low-commitment message about one concrete thing. Prepare one genuinely good question.
  10. Separate want from prediction in writing. What do I want? What will honestly happen? What are the real odds? Then list how to close the gap.
  11. Collect external data before setting expectations. Gather numbers about the world — salaries, hire rates, timelines — not about yourself.
  12. Audit your value sources. If work carries too much, build grounding elsewhere; aim to feel peaceful after wins, not hungrier.
  13. If blending work with friendship or dating, run the end-case first. "If this falls apart, can I handle it at work?" Check policy, keep the two relationships explicitly separate.

One caution: some valuable work is structurally invisible. If you run 12 interviews to make one hire, the company sees the hire, not the 11 you weeded out. If you do that kind of work, you have to narrate its value, because no one will see it on their own.

And don't go black-and-white on this. ROI is one tool in the belt, not the meaning of work. A good relationship — like a good marriage — comes from fluidly responding to the humans in front of you, not from running one play.

External Skill #2: Be Responsible — Then Guard Against What That Causes

The best single thing you can do for your work relationships is be someone people want to work with. And at the core of that is responsibility, which here has a specific meaning that contradicts a lot of Healthy Gamer advice.

Normally the teaching is "focus on actions, not outcomes." But in workplace relationships, responsibility means owning the outcome — making sure the task actually gets done, not just doing your rote part and shrugging at obstacles.

"There are some people who, if they get assigned a task, will make sure it gets done to the best of their ability. And there are some people that will basically do what they think they should do, but they won't take responsibility for the task."

When you hit an obstacle, run this simple rubric:

  1. Flag the problem early. Don't disappear and resurface four days later with "they never answered." Try to solve it for a few hours or a day, then say: "Hey, I'm running into this."
  2. Propose a plan. Show your thinking: "I've called twice, I checked corporate, I'm going to try X and Y next."
  3. Ask for help or suggestions, and set a tether. "Can we touch base tomorrow at end of day in case I haven't cracked it?"

This does three things at once: it demonstrates competence, raises awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →, and mentally prepares people for bad news so you never go zero-to-a-hundred on them. You want to be a smoke detector, not the moment someone notices the house is already on fire.

There's an emotional payoff too. When you visibly own a problem, other people relax. The Jeff Bezos story captures it: an executive's launch demo projected upside-down in front of Bezos, and the executive started scrambling to fix it. Bezos stayed calm and said, in effect, "I'm not worried about it, because clearly you are." When you carry the responsibility, others don't have to — and they love you for it.

The trap inside responsibility

Here is the cruel twist, and it's one of the most common ways doing everything right lands you somewhere wrong:

"If you are responsible, other people will become lazy and they'll start shunting responsibility onto you. The more of a team player you are, the more people start to rely on you for stuff."

And the emotional arc is brutal: a favor starts as appreciation, becomes expectation, curdles into entitlementA personality pattern — not an identity — rooted in low internal self-worth, where the gap between how little you feel you're worth on the inside gets filled by constant external validation.Full definition → — and the day you finally say no, the person you've been rescuing is disappointed and angry at you.

So manage the pattern, not just the moment:

  • Help once freely. Help should be the exception, not the rule.
  • Second request: stay warm but raise awarenessThe capacity to accurately receive and process information about what is happening — in your environment, your body, or your mind — so that your brain can respond appropriately.Full definition →. "Happy to help again — but what's going on? Are you having trouble with this?"
  • Third request: "I've got a lot on my plate. If you're really stuck, maybe we should go to the boss about what support you need."

With your boss, be subtler, because helping them is your job: "I've been asked to do this a few times — happy to. Is this becoming a new responsibility? If so, which of my current priorities does it move down the list?" If they say "everything's a priority, just be a team player," they don't actually have a good answer — they're crunched. That's your opening for the next-level move: compassion. "I can see everyone's stepping up. How are you holding up? What are you dealing with?"

"You can demonstrate compassion without turning into a doormat."

You can push back, make sure the extra work is acknowledged, plan to ask for a raise later — and be genuinely kind in the moment. People remember who showed them grace during the crunch. When the company turns the corner, that's the relationship that pays off.

Why spreading help around beats being one person's crutch

There's a concept worth knowing: eigenvector centrality — how central you are in a social network. When you do favors for one person over and over, you become a peripheral node — an asset owned by them. Your standing in the wider organization doesn't rise.

"Do favors for a lot of people once. That's how you actually increase your respect within an organization. You don't become one person's crutch — you become a lot of people's eleventh-hour person."

A bonus most people miss: being good to work with means many people want to work with you — but you don't have to work with all of them. Pick the ones who are equally responsible and pleasant, and tell your boss explicitly: "We gel well, can you put us on projects together?"

Common in finance. Watch for it.)

External Skill #3: Never Be a One-Trick Pony — Say It Once

This is the quiet rule behind half of all workplace friction. The mistake usually isn't saying the wrong thing — it's saying the right thing too many times until it becomes wrong.

  • Apologies. Screw up? Apologize once, twice at most — then demonstrate responsibility and a fix. Apologize five times and you've created five fresh reminders that you screw up. People conclude you can't handle anything. The urge to over-apologize is about soothing your guilt. Take the guilt to a therapist, not your team.
  • Jokes. A joke once is funny. The same joke 10,000 times — a nickname, a running bit at someone's expense — is bullying.
  • Praise. Give it when it's authentic and deserved, then spread it around. Thank someone effusively three, four, five times and you've become sycophantic.
  • Reminders and communication. Communicate once, twice, maybe a gentle third. If they didn't listen the first two times, stepping back works better than nagging. (Do still cover yourself with a paper trail — that's different from over-communicating.)

A clever variant on credit: when someone takes credit for your work, you can claim it by giving it. "Yeah, the deal closed — total team effort, and that piece you did at the end really got it over the line." Passing credit downward, in most non-narcissistic environments, quietly establishes that you were central to it.

The reason one-trick-pony behavior feels so tempting is that repetition is where we feel comfortable — being "the apology guy," "the joke guy," "the donuts-every-Friday guy." A genuinely appreciated ritual is fine to continue. But default to variety; rigid patterns degrade relationships.

External Skill #4: Attitude — Flag, Don't Complain; Withhold Criticism

The difference between flagging a problem and complaining is your level of responsibility. A complainer states something negative and expects someone else to fix it: "This tea is cold." A flagger states the problem clinically and owns a piece of the solution: "Our invoices are going out late — here's the impact, is there something I can do to help?"

"We don't want to be complainers. We just want to say: there's a problem, and problems are designed to be solved."

The harder lesson — learned the hard way — is about feedback:

Don't give people feedback or criticism unless they ask for it. And even when they ask, pause before answering and gauge how it will land.

This is the single biggest misread people make: they listen to what people say instead of watching what they do. A boss who walks in announcing "I'm an open book, tell me anything I'm doing wrong" usually doesn't actually want the criticism. They want reassurance — they're anxious about what you secretly think.

"They absolutely want to know all the negative things you think about them — but there's a really good chance, because they're human, they're going to kill the messenger."

So when asked, buy yourself a beat: "Can I think for a second?" Use it to run the options — if I say this, how will they react? If I say that? Don't lie, but calibrate to what the person can actually metabolize: "Most of what you're doing seems fine. It looks like you're under a lot of pressure — and when I can't reach you, that's hard for me to work around, though I can see you're juggling a lot." Never deliver feedback you don't think the person can act on.

The Social Layer: Mentorship, Meetings, and the Truth About Boundaries

The internet loves a clean rule: work colleagues are not your friends; keep boundaries; don't get burned. That's good defensive advice, and boundaries matter. But it collides with reality:

"Many friendships form in the workplace. Many people who get married met in the workplace."

A lot of the modern lonelinessA state of painful disconnection that arises not simply from being alone, but from lacking meaningful human connection — even when other people are physically present.Full definition → epidemic comes from work changing — warehouse and delivery jobs, fully remote setups — so the organic channels through which humans used to form relationships have dried up. You have a genuine choice: capitalize on the muddy waters of work and let real friendships form, or chant "boundaries, boundaries" and possibly struggle socially. Both can work. Just don't pretend the boundary-purist path is cost-free.

Mentorship is worth what you invest in it

Mentorship can come from your job, a professional organization (you can often get assigned a mentor as a junior member), or organically — and the organic ones are frequently the best, including former bosses after you leave a job (no conflict of interest).

How to not ask for mentorship: a cold email saying "Will you be my mentor?" That's a huge, undefined commitment, and the honest answer is usually no, because the person has no idea what they're signing up for.

How to actually do it — make the lift low, targeted, and curious:

"Hey, I noticed you do X. I'd love to learn more about this specific thing. Do you have time for a short conversation?"

Then let it grow organically. Organic is the opposite of a dating app's checkbox-checkbox structure — it's one small thing, then a second, then six months later a real relationship. After a good first conversation: thank them, and ask if you can meet again in six months. Over time it becomes mentorship without anyone ever having to formally "commit."

And ask good questions. A bad question asks for information you could Google ("How do I find a therapist?") or is generic ("What's one thing you wish you knew ten years ago?"). A good question requires critical thinking and is specific to the person's expertise ("What are the real obstacles to starting a digital mental health company?"). When someone thinks "wow, good question," it's almost always one they haven't heard a hundred times. (And don't outsource it to ChatGPT — people can spot it.)

The deeper point: to be mentored, be someone worth mentoring. Mentors get most excited about people who are already solid and would become exceptional with a little targeted help: "I'm pretty good on my own — with your specific guidance I could be even better."

Meetings are about feelings, not just information

Half the meetings on earth could be an email. So why do they exist? Because just because you don't see a meeting's value doesn't mean it has none.

"Meetings are about feelings. They're about coordination, about everyone feeling like they're on the same page."

A boss who doesn't read fifteen emails holds a meeting to feel oriented — and they're the boss, so they get to. Walk in with that frame: someone with more power than me believes this matters, so I'm honoring it. You can even gently ask, "What are the objectives here?" — but assume the meeting is serving a real human function.

Meetings are also about visibility. It's fine to be quiet — you don't have to talk for the sake of talking. But if you have something worth saying, say it, because:

  • Every time you speak, you take an opportunity away from someone else. People who respond to everything are exhausting and crowd others out.
  • Every time you speak, you dilute the quality of what you say. A 9-out-of-10 point gets watered down by your 2s and 4s. Share when it matters.

A genuinely useful trick when you can't get a word in: raise your hand. It feels childish, but it works — and the fact that Zoom and Google Meet both built a "raise hand" button tells you it maps onto how humans actually coordinate. If no one's looking, find the meeting's organizer, hold eye contact until they see you, nod, and lower your hand — they'll give you the floor.

And use meetings for what they organically are: a place humans connect. Crack the occasional joke (not as the designated joke guy), shout people out for good work. People who struggle at work often waste these organic moments because they've decided meetings are beneath them.

Internal Factors: Where People Quietly Sabotage Themselves

Don't look for justice at work

Workplaces are flawed. People play favorites, take credit, have higher EQ than you, panic, get overpaid and underpaid. You can advocate for fairness — that's not wrong — but if you go to work expecting justice, you'll be perpetually wounded.

A priest once said marriage is 60–40, not 50–50: you do 60% of the work and feel like you get 40% of the benefit, and your spouse feels exactly the same. There's a built-in human bias toward feeling shortchanged. Know that, advocate for yourself anyway, and don't get hung up on it.

Don't confuse wants with predictions

A very common error: "I want a promotion" silently becomes "I think I'll get it / I deserve it / I should get it." Separate the two cleanly. The questions are simple:

  1. What do you want?
  2. What do you think will actually happen — independent of what you want?
  3. What are the real odds?

Both an over-negative ego ("I'm a loser, they're all better") and an over-inflated one distort the prediction. Strip out the bias, make an honest estimate, and where you find a weakness, ask the only useful question: how do I correct it?

Compare for direction, not for worth

Comparison isn't the thief of joy — your brain compares for a reason, and noticing someone is better at presentations than you is genuinely useful information. It becomes poison only when you attach your fundamental value to the comparison.

"When you notice someone is better than you, that is a path forward."

In a team game, a support player doesn't melt down because the carry does more damage — different roles, different strengths. Same at work. Let comparison be a roadmap for progress, never a verdict on your worth.

Don't make work the source of your value

This is the deepest trap, and it shows up two ways:

  • Outsourcing esteem to work. Needing your boss's validation, your coworkers' respect, to feel okay as a person. The burnt-out programmer who works the whole weekend to walk in Monday with "I solved it" — chasing the pat on the back — is feeding self-worth, not solving a problem. His own boss told him he didn't need to. Work should be a source of pride, never a substitute for value you couldn't find elsewhere ("my parents didn't love me, so I need my boss to").
  • Intellectualized avoidanceAvoidance is the largely subconscious impulse to move away from a negative emotion or the situation that triggers it, rather than moving toward something positive.Full definition →. Elaborate "capitalism is evil / I'm an anarchist" gymnastics that, underneath, are really "I don't feel good at this stuff, so I'll devalue it." You don't have to love or hate capitalism — just notice when a worldview is secretly protecting a wound.

Here's why this matters even if you succeed:

"Even when you win, you lose. If you become the best because you don't feel like you're the best in here, you've programmed your self-esteemSelf-esteem is the degree to which you accept yourself as you are — not how you measure up against others.Full definition → to be tied to your work. Now you have the threat of losing it the moment someone else becomes the best."

That's a double whammy — the professional loss and the ego collapse. So work hard, challenge yourself, win awards, make money — let maybe 5–10% of your self-worth ride on work that you genuinely care about. The rest should be grounded in things outside it: meditationMeditation is the practice of focusing consciousness completely — until thinking itself falls away and you exist in a state of pure awareness with no mental activity.Full definition →, hikes, hugging people, taking the dog to the vet, learning to make a good matcha. A healthy amount of ego-feeding leaves you peaceful; an unhealthy amount leaves you craving more — that's the tell.

Have realistic expectations — built from outside data, not your ego

When you want something, go collect data on the world, not on yourself. Watching Twitch, the realistic targets were roughly 5 concurrent viewers at one month, 20 at three months, 100 at a year — because that's what's statistically probable. Companies move slowly: a 2026 budget is set before 2026 arrives, so a raise request in spring runs into forecasts made last year. Change that takes other companies 3–5 years might take a fast org 6–18 months.

"Realistic expectations don't come from in here. They come from outside of you. They come from data. And the ego doesn't like realistic expectations."

The reason to do this is to protect your morale. An unrealistic expectation sets up a double hit: you don't get the thing and you drown in disappointment, which kills the motivationThe internal mental state of holding a thought steady at the center of your mind — not a feeling, a behavior, or a special fire, but simply the capacity to keep one thought present without it being displaced.Full definition → you'd need to try harder.

You see it in cancer patients too — facing how bleak things truly are, free of ego, something inside can become inspired rather than defeated. But you have to look honestly first.

Common Pitfalls

  • Asking "how do I get X from my boss?" without ever asking "why do I need X?" Skipping the internal question means you'll over-function for approval and never feel satisfied.
  • Tying your self-worth to work. Even winning then becomes a setup for a devastating loss the moment someone outperforms you.
  • Confusing what you want with what's likely. Wanting a promotion is not predicting one, and certainly not deserving one.
  • Doing high-value work but never translating it into money. If no one can see your impact on the bottom line, it barely counts in their eyes.
  • Grinding on the extra mile that doesn't move the bottom line — and then feeling betrayed when it's invisible.
  • Becoming one person's permanent crutch. Responsibility attracts other people's laziness; favors curdle from appreciation into entitlementA personality pattern — not an identity — rooted in low internal self-worth, where the gap between how little you feel you're worth on the inside gets filled by constant external validation.Full definition →.
  • The one-trick-pony reflex: apologizing five times, repeating a joke until it's bullying, thanking people into sycophancy, nagging past the second reminder.
  • Taking "I'm an open book, criticize me" literally. Believing words over behavior is how you get punished for honesty you were invited to give.
  • Complaining instead of flagging. The difference is whether you carry any responsibility for the fix.
  • Hiding problems and quietly trying to fix them alone until they explode into a zero-to-a-hundred disaster.
  • Dismissing meetings as a waste of time — and missing that they're about coordination, visibility, and feelings you can't see from your seat.
  • Expecting justice, or rigidly preaching "work colleagues aren't friends" — both leave you either chronically wounded or needlessly lonely.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the two-person split. Take your current workplace frustration and write two columns: my contribution and the other person's contribution. Then ask the buried question — "Why do I actually want this thing (credit, approval, the promotion)?" Name how much of it is self-worth.
  2. Translate your work into dollars. List your three biggest contributions this quarter and rewrite each in money or comparative terms (whose expensive time you freed up, what got closed, how you beat a comparable benchmark). Use this language next time you ask for credit or a raise.
  3. Adopt the obstacle rubric. Next time you hit a problem, don't disappear: (a) flag it early, (b) propose a plan that shows your thinking, (c) ask for help and set a tether — "Can we check in tomorrow EOD?"
  4. Track the favor pattern. When someone asks you to take on their work, note the count. Help freely once. On the second ask, get curious ("What's going on?"). On the third, route it to the boss or to a real conversation about support.
  5. Spread help wide, not deep. Deliberately do small favors for many people rather than becoming one person's go-to. Then ask your boss to pair you with the colleagues you actually work well with.
  6. Apply the "say it once" filter. Before you apologize, repeat a joke, thank someone, or send another reminder — check whether you've already said it. If yes, stop and switch to demonstrating a fix instead.
  7. Pause before any feedback. When asked for criticism, say "Can I think for a second?" and use it to predict how the person will react. Calibrate to what they can act on; never dump feedback that isn't actionable. Practice watching behavior over words.
  8. Reframe one meeting. Walk into your next pointless-seeming meeting asking, "Who wants this and why?" Find the function it serves, contribute only your highest-value point, and if you can't get a word in, literally raise your hand.
  9. Find a mentor the organic way. Identify one person whose expertise you want. Send a short, specific, low-commitment message asking about one concrete thing. Prepare one genuinely good question — something that requires thinking, not Googling, and is specific to them.
  10. Separate want from prediction in writing. For any goal, answer three questions on paper: What do I want? What do I honestly think will happen? What are the real odds (based on outside data, not my ego)? Then list how you'd close the gap.
  11. Collect external data before setting expectations. Researching a job, a raise, or a launch? Gather numbers about the world (typical salaries, hire rates, timelines, what successful applicants did), not about yourself. Set your targets from that.
  12. Audit your value sources. Write down where your sense of worth comes from.
