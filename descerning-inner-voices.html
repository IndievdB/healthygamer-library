Unblending: the U-turn

When a part takes over and fills your whole inner space — flooding you with this is true, do this now — you are blended with it. Unblending is stepping back: the same as mindful awareness, mentalizing, dis-identification.

The shift is in language. Instead of "I'm an insecure person," say "A part of me feels insecure, and it's activated right now." Now you're the one noticing it.

I'm not the issue, I'm the one that's working with the issue.

The most powerful unblending question: "How am I feeling toward this part?" If the answer is judgment, that's just another part jumping in — unblend from that one too. Keep going until you feel curiosity, ideally compassion. That curious, compassionate witnessing is the self-led state.

The Two Kinds of Parts: Protectors and Exiles

There are really only two categories.

Protector parts have a job. They're your defenses, designed to keep you safe from humiliation, rejection, or danger. Even the part that shames you is a protector — it figures if it tears you down first, you'll change before someone else rejects you. Two flavors:

  • Managers are proactive — they plan ahead so pain never gets triggered: perfectionism, intellectualizing, people-pleasing, control. Often socially rewarded.
  • Firefighters show up after the alarm is going off, when you're flooded. Desperate measures, no concern for collateral damage: raging, drinking, bingeing, risky behavior, even suicidality. Like a real firefighter breaking a window to save the baby — damage is real, intent is rescue.

So instead of being filed as "When I was five, I wasn't safe," the memory gets read as a map of reality. When triggered, it doesn't feel like remembering — it feels like "I'm not safe right now," and you feel five again. That's an emotional flashback.

If we're ever overwhelmed, it's probably a flashback.

Safety note: work with protectors on your own; approach exiles with a therapist. Your protectors won't even let you reach exiles at first. But if one surfaces, unblending ("this is a young part having a living memory; I'm not actually five") is what helps.

There Are No Bad Parts

This is the heart of the approach: there are no bad parts. Every part, however destructive, exists for a protective reason and comes from a good intention.

This was learned from clients. The founder of IFS once argued a self-harming client's part out of cutting — even got it to agree. The next week she came in with a worse injury. He said, "I give up, I can't win." She replied that she didn't want him to win or lose — she wanted to be understood. When he started asking parts why they did what they did, every horrible part had a valid reason underneath.

It always comes from a good intention. If we dig deep enough, it's pure. We're just trying to survive.

Every protector's mantra is "never again." Some piece of your brain works feverishly, overtime, without thanks — often while you abuse it for being "lazy." When you finally drop the judgment and sit down to understand it, the relief can be profound. Many parts know their methods aren't working but don't know what else to do.

Working With Inner Conflict (Polarization)

When two voices fight inside, IFS calls it a polarization. A neural network is simple — one part has one concern ("never feel small again, so yell"). The rest of your system notices the fallout, so a second part comes online with the opposite concern ("stop yelling, you're ruining things"). Now they're at war. Neither is bad; each holds a grain of truth; neither is the whole you.

Example: a woman calls, furious at her husband. Challenge her slightly and she flips instantly to "It's all my fault," spiraling into shame. Which is the truth? Neither — two polarized parts. The blaming part exists to protect her from the shame underneath.

The way through: name both parts, honor both, and hear them one at a time. Parts try to talk simultaneously — "I should leave, but I made a commitment, but I deserve better" — and that overlap is what makes you feel crazy. Slow it down. Ask one not to interrupt; hear the other completely; then switch. The image: all your parts get a seat at the table, but you make the decision, with their input. Shut them up and they revolt; listen, then choose with common sense.

Common Pitfalls

  • Privileging your neocortex. Assuming your logical mind is enlightened and your emotional brain is just "irrational." Your subcortical brain is equally complex and often more trustworthy — it goes off what life actually proved.
  • Judging parts instead of getting curious. The instant you call a behavior "stupid" or "lazy," you've blended with a critical part and slammed the door on understanding.
  • Coming in to fix. Approaching a part with an agenda to change it backfires, like trying to fix a teenager. Only when you genuinely set down that agenda does change become possible.
  • Over-reifying parts into a cult. Hold it lightly — a useful frame, not a religion.
  • Diving into exiles alone. Excavating deep wounds without support can re-traumatize. Work with protectors solo; bring exiles to a therapist. The moment anything feels too stimulating, stop.
  • Treating self-defeating behavior as evidence you're bad. It's evidence of a protector running an outdated program. The behavior may need to change, but the intention is protective.

  1. Pick your word. Choose one term — schema, mental model, part — and use it consistently when you go inside.

  2. Reframe a "crazy" behavior. Take something that frustrates you. Instead of "What's wrong with me?" ask: "What might my deeper brain believe that makes this the wise response?"

  3. Practice unblending in language. Catch yourself mid-reaction: not "I am anxious," but "A part of me feels anxious, and it's activated right now."

  4. Ask the unblending question. When a part is active, ask: "How am I feeling toward it?" If you find judgment, ask that part to step back, until you reach curiosity.

  5. Have a conversation with a protector. Breathe, bring the part to mind, notice how it shows up in your body. Put a hand there and say, "I'm here and I'm listening." Then ask: "Tell me about the job you do for me," and the golden question: "What are you afraid would happen if you didn't do this?"

  6. Replay a moment in slow motion. Recall the last time a pattern happened and run it frame by frame. What happened just before? What did I think, feel, assume?

  7. Use sentence completion. Finish the same stem 5–10 times: "I'd better [behavior] because if I don't, ______." Let each ending surprise you.

  8. Map a polarization. Name both warring voices, write down each one's genuine concern, let each speak fully without interruption. Then decide from your calm center, with their input.

When a part takes over and fills your whole inner space — flooding you with its reality, telling you this is true, do this now — you are blended with it. You've shrunk yourself down into one learned response, as if that's all you are.

Unblending is stepping back from that. It's the same thing as mindful awareness, mentalizing, mind-sight, dis-identification — using your mind to notice your mind.

The shift is in the language. Instead of "I'm an insecure person" (no wiggle room, dead end), you say "A part of me feels insecure, and it's activated right now." Now you're not the insecurity — you're the one noticing it and able to show up for it.

I'm not the issue, I'm the one that's working with the issue.

And the single most powerful unblending question is: "How am I feeling toward this part?" If the answer is judgment, that just means another part has jumped in, and you gently unblend from that one too. You keep going until what you feel toward the part is curiosity, and ideally compassion. That curious, compassionate witnessing is the unblended, self-led state.

The Two Kinds of Parts: Protectors and Exiles

In parts work there are really only two categories — it's much simpler than people make it.

Protector parts have a job. They are your defenses — roles, stances, behavioral patterns designed to keep you safe from humiliation, rejection, suffering, or danger. Crucially, even the part that shames you is a protector: it figures that if it tears you down first, you'll change before someone else rejects you. Protectors come in two flavors:

  • Managers are proactive and preemptive. They plan ahead so the pain never gets triggered in the first place — perfectionism, intellectualizing, people-pleasing, control, rigid boundaries. These tend to be socially acceptable, even rewarded.
  • Firefighters show up after the alarm is already going off, when you're flooded and overwhelmed. Desperate times, desperate measures — they don't care about collateral damage. Raging, drinking, cutting, bingeing, risky sex, gambling, even suicidality. A firefighter is like a real firefighter who breaks a window to save the baby; the damage is real but the intent is rescue.

Exiles are different — they don't have a job, they're just wounded. They're the parts that hold old pain, fear, grief, and shameThe emotion triggered by a perceived gap between where you are and where you think you should be.Full definition →. Your system tends to "exile" them — push them out of awareness — because their feelings are unbearable.

Here's why exiles are so disruptive. Trauma memories often get stored implicitly rather than as a normal explicit memory (because the hippocampusThe brain structure responsible for memory consolidation — taking information from short-term storage and transferring it into long-term understanding.Full definition → goes offline under threat). So instead of being filed as "When I was five, I wasn't safe," the memory gets read by the brain as a map of reality. When it gets triggered, it doesn't feel like remembering — it feels like "I'm not safe right now," and you feel five years old again. That's an emotional flashback. Many of your overwhelming feelings are actually flashbacks.

If we're ever overwhelmed, it's probably a flashback.

A note on self-work: it's generally safer to work with protector parts on your own, and to approach exiles with a therapist. You don't usually need to go hunting for exiles — your protectors won't even let you reach them at first. But if an exile surfaces on its own, knowing how to unblend ("this is a young part of me having a living memory; I'm not actually five") is exactly what helps.

There Are No Bad Parts

This is the heart of the whole approach: there are no bad parts. Every part, no matter how destructive its behavior, exists for a protective reason and comes from a good intention.

This wasn't a theory imposed from above — it was learned from clients. The founder of IFS once tried to argue a self-harming client's part out of cutting, even got the part to agree it was a bad idea. The next week the client came in with a worse injury. He collapsed and said, "I give up, I can't win." The client replied that she didn't want him to win or lose — she just wanted to be understood. When he finally started asking parts why they did what they did, every seemingly horrible part turned out to have a valid reason underneath.

It comes from not — it always comes from a good intention. If we dig deep enough, it's pure. We're just trying to survive.

The mantra of every protector is "never again." Some piece of your brain got dedicated to making sure a specific suffering never happens again, and it will work feverishly, overtime, without thanks — often while you abuse it for being "stupid" or "lazy." When you finally drop the judgment and sit down to understand it, the relief can be profound. These parts are often little heroes, and many of them know their methods aren't working but don't know what else to do.

Mindfulness for Self-Exploration (Not Just Self-Regulation)

Most mindfulness teaching aims at self-regulationThe ability to identify and modify emotional states in ways that support adaptive, goal-directed behavior.Full definition → — calming down, refocusing, bringing the neocortex back online when you've flipped your lid. That's genuinely valuable. But there's a different use: self-exploration, and through it, transformation.

Both work by strengthening the connection between your neocortex and your subcortical brain. The difference is what you do with that open channel:

  • Self-regulation uses your thinking brain to soothe the deeper brain: "It's okay, we're not in danger, you're safe."
  • Self-exploration uses that same open line to listen to the deeper brain: "What's feeling so scary? What did you learn? What are you protecting me from?"

Working With Inner Conflict (Polarization)

What about when two voices fight inside you? IFS calls this a polarization, and it's completely logical once you understand parts.

A neural network is simple. One part has one concern — say, "never feel small again, so yell." It can't weigh that against other concerns; it just knows its one job. But the rest of your system notices the fallout ("everyone hates us now"), so a second part comes online with the opposite concern — "stop yelling, you're ruining things." Now they're at war, and it's exhausting and confusing. Neither part is bad. Each holds a grain of truth. Neither is the whole you.

Consider a real example: someone calls, furious at her husband, listing every reason he's wrong. Challenge that even slightly and she flips instantly to "Oh my god, it's all my fault," spiraling into shame. A moment ago she was the victim; now she's the villain. Which one is the truth? Neither — they're two polarized parts. The blaming part exists precisely to protect her from the shame part underneath. Disarm the blame, and the shame floods in.

The way through is to name both parts, honor both, and hear them one at a time. Parts often try to talk simultaneously — "I should leave, but I made a commitment, but I deserve better, but I'll be alone" — and that overlap is exactly what makes you feel crazy. Slow it down. Tell one part you'll come back to it and ask it not to interrupt. Hear out the other part's concerns completely. Then switch. You can even use two chairs.

The guiding image: all your parts get a seat at the table, but you — your fresh, intelligent self — are the one who ultimately makes the decision, with their input. Shut your parts up and they revolt; listen to them, then choose with common sense.

Common Pitfalls

  • Privileging your neocortex. Assuming your logical mind is enlightened and your emotional brain is just "irrational, crazy, false." Your subcortical brain is equally complex and, in many ways, more trustworthy — it goes off what life actually proved to you.
  • Judging your parts instead of getting curious. The instant you call a behavior "stupid," "lazy," or "broken," you've blended with a critical part and slammed the door on understanding. Drop the judgment first.
  • Coming in to manage and fix. Approaching a part with an agenda to change it is like trying to fix a teenager — it backfires. Paradoxically, only when you genuinely set down the agenda to change it ("I just want to understand you") does change become possible.
  • Over-reifying parts into a cult. You don't have to make parts literally real or force parts-language onto everything. It can become heavy-handed and annoying. Hold it lightly — it's a useful frame, not a religion.
  • Diving into exiles alone. Deliberately excavating deep wounds without support can be re-traumatizing. Meditation and therapy can both temporarily destabilize you. Work with protectors on your own; bring exiles to a therapist. And the moment anything feels too stimulating, stop.
  • Treating self-defeating behavior as evidence you're bad. It's evidence of a protector running an outdated program. The behavior may genuinely need to change, but the intention underneath is always protective.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Pick your word. Choose one term — schema, unconscious belief, mental model, prediction, part — that feels natural to you, and use it consistently when you go looking inside.

  2. Reframe a "crazy" behavior with coherence. Take one thing you do that frustrates you (procrastinating, snapping, numbing out). Instead of "What's wrong with me?" ask: "What might my deeper brain believe that makes this the wise response?" Just sit with the question.

  3. Practice unblending in language. Catch yourself mid-reaction and rephrase: not "I am anxious/insecure/angry," but "A part of me feels anxious, and it's activated right now." Notice how that creates space.

  4. Ask the unblending question. When a part is active, ask: "How am I feeling toward it?" If you find judgment, recognize that's another part, and gently ask that one to step back, until you reach curiosity.

  5. Have a conversation with a protector. Get comfortable, take a few breaths. Bring to mind the part you're curious about. Notice how it shows up — a sensation in your body, an image, a felt energy. Put a hand there and say, "I'm here and I'm listening." Then ask: "Tell me about the job you do for me," and the golden question: "What are you afraid would happen if you didn't do this?" Let it answer fully.

  6. Replay a specific moment in slow motion. For a recurring pattern, recall the last time it happened and run it like a movie frame by frame. What happened just before? What did I think, feel, sense, assume right there? You couldn't track it in the heat of the moment; give it time now.

  7. Use sentence completion if parts work feels awkward. Finish the same stem 5–10 times: "I'd better [your behavior] because if I don't, ______." Let each ending surprise you — this helps the implicit mind speak.

  8. Map a polarization. When two voices war inside you, name them both, write down each one's genuine concern, and let each speak fully without interruption — one at a time. Then, from your calm center, decide with their input rather than letting either one hijack you.

  9. Try the relational mindfulness shift. Next time something distressing surfaces in meditation, don't let it float away. Turn toward it: "You're what I'll focus on. What do you want me to understand?"
