Telling a Real Vision From a Trick of the Mind

Start with a counterintuitive fact: the mind can never have a vision. The mind is not an organ of perception — you perceive through your senses, and the sensory process actually precedes the mental one. What the mind does is analyze, doubt, interpret, frame, and claim.

So the mind can't create a vision — but it can construct an imagination of a vision and then claim it. The difference is enormous in density: a song stuck in your head is nothing compared to actually hearing it. The same gap separates a mind-built "vision" from a genuine one.

Two reliable tells:

  1. Emotional flavor. A genuine mystical experience is often negative, scary, or foreign at the start — brief, and you snap out of it. The mind's counterfeit feels triumphant and pleasurable: "I achieved ego deathThe temporary or permanent falling away of the "you-ness" wrapped around every experience — the state where there is no observer, only the experience itself.Full definition →, I saw the dimensions." If you love it and feel accomplished, be suspicious.

  2. The mind's reflex toward doubt. Faced with a real vision it can't understand, the mind's instinct is to doubt and analyze. That's the danger — the more you swim in analysis, the further you drift from the source into a "circle jerk" of interpretation the mind adores.

Whatever vision — just sit down and meditate again. Whatever happened, happened. Don't get caught up.

This is why the traditional advice is to let visions go: attaching activates the analytical machinery that pulls you away from the state where they arose. (The exception: if an experience is genuinely painful or dangerous, see a professional.)

Telling a Real Vision From a Trick of the Mind

Start with a counterintuitive fact: the mind can never have a vision. The mind is not an organ of perception. It doesn't see or hear — you do, through your senses, and the sensory process actually precedes the mental one (you see the poop on the sidewalk before your mind labels it "poop"). What the mind does is analyze, doubt, interpret, frame, reframe, and claim.

So the mind can't create a vision — but it can do something sneaky: it can construct an imagination of a vision and then claim it. The difference between imagination and a real perception is enormous in density. A song stuck in your head is nothing compared to actually hearing the song; a fantasy about your crush is nothing compared to being with them. The same gap separates a mind-built "vision" from a genuine one.

Two reliable tells:

  1. Emotional flavor. A genuine mystical experience is often negative, scary, or foreign at the start — you snap out of it, it's brief. The mind's counterfeit feels triumphant and pleasurable: "I achieved ego deathThe temporary or permanent falling away of the "you-ness" wrapped around every experience — the state where there is no observer, only the experience itself.Full definition →, I saw the dimensions, I descended into the abyss."   2. The mind's reflex toward doubt. Faced with a real vision — something it can't understand — the mind's instinct is to doubt and analyze it. And that's the danger. The more you swim in analysis, the further you drift from the actual source of the vision into a "circle jerk" of interpretation the mind adores.

Whatever vision — just sit down and meditate again. Whatever happened, happened. Don't get caught up.

This is why the traditional advice is to let visions go. Not because they're worthless, but because attaching to them activates the analytical machinery that pulls you permanently away from the state where they arose. (The exception: if an experience is genuinely painful or dangerous, see a professional.)
