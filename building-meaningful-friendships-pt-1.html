Karma as Cause and Effect That Never Stops

Start with your parents. What caused you to have them? Biologically there's a clean chain — two people, a sperm and egg, a zygote, you. Obvious cause and effect, and you had zero input. You didn't choose it.

Zoom out. Why is your childhood best friend your best friend? Because you grew up in the same neighborhood and your parents were friends. Every relationship has circumstances that created the opportunity — we just dismiss them as coincidence.

But is there really any such thing as coincidence? In a physics lab there are none — a billiard ball moves because another struck it. Physics becomes chemistry: no coincidence in how hydrogen bonds form. Chemistry becomes biology, then neurobiology, then psychology, where experiences shape behavior and behavior shapes your life. The chain is unbroken.

As science expands, God disappears. And in the same way, randomness and coincidence are starting to live within the gaps.

The more we discover, the more "random" shrinks and "caused" grows. So does causation just stop at the edge of your relationships and hand the rest to luck? The argument here is no. The same unbroken cause and effect that runs through gravity and chemistry runs through who shows up in your life. There's a whole layer of causation determining who ends up in your box — and if you act in harmony with it, relationships get dramatically easier. You don't have to believe this. Treat it as an axiom and experiment.

Souls Reincarnate in Groups

Extend cause and effect to its end. If every action has an equal and opposite reaction — Newton applied to relationships — debts don't vanish. If I loan you a hundred dollars and die before you repay, the debt has to settle somewhere. The framework here (from texts like Many Lives, Many Masters and the Upanishads) says the only place left is a future life.

The practical consequence: souls reincarnate in groups. As a child I rack up enormous debt with my parents — they fed me, paid my bills. The cleanest way to balance the ledger is role reversal across lifetimes: this life I'm their child; a future one, I'm their parent. Scale that up and you have roughly a thousand karmic bonds, each life partly about discharging a batch. The soul "chooses" an incarnation that lets it settle certain debts — which is why you and certain people end up born in the same city, meeting at the same school, drawn together as if by destiny.

That destiny feeling is universal. People describe love at first sight, an instant connection out of thousands of strangers. It's why Romeo and Juliet still lands — two people conditioned from birth to hate each other, yet a pull overrides all of it. The trope persists because something in us recognizes a force that transcends circumstance.

The Premise Problem: Why Needing a Reason Kills Friendship

Human bonds evolved through repeated, unplanned interactions — the tribe ran into each other constantly, so trust formed organically without anyone trying. If you only saw someone once, you were skeptical. And you were especially skeptical of an outsider who showed up with an agenda — someone there to sell you something.

That instinct is still wired in. When someone interacts with you for a reason, it triggers suspicion and blocks the organic bond. Which traps us, because modern life forces us to manufacture reasons:

At two ends of the spectrum: "I want something from you," which means I can't really be your friend — or "I want nothing from you," which is what makes the best friend.

You can't just tell someone you'd like to hang out, so you reach for a pretext — let's study, let's network, let's grind this game. Paradoxically that's wise, as long as the pretext is the genuine goal. If the real goal is "study," there's empty space around it for friendship to grow. That's why "we were friends first" works. The error is making the connection itself the explicit, pressured objective — that re-triggers the agenda alarm and unplugs the very machinery that forms bonds.

The fix: stop manufacturing relationships directly; enter situations where they form as a byproduct.
