The Practical Playbook

  • Dinacharya (daily routine). How you spend your days is the most reliable predictor of financial security. Two highest-leverage anchors: prepare in the morning and reflect at night — in writing. Anything you add requires consciously sacrificing something else.
  • Collect information. Information determines the value of your hour — and its value is inversely proportional to how many people have it. Invest in yourself — the only investment you carry everywhere. Grades, bosses, and circumstance-bound relationships evaporate; body, mind, and skill stay. For memory, lean on salience (give information gut-level purpose before absorbing it) and systems (copious notes, compiled).

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the symptom as the cause — thinking the problem is the amount, not the meaning.
  • Using money as a proxy — buying a gift instead of actually loving someone.
  • Sacrificing artha for dharma carelessly — helping no one while wrecking yourself.
  • Hedonism with no ceiling, or wealth with no spending — empty either way.
  • Spiritual bypassing — declaring yourself above money to dodge feeling like a failure.
  • Shrinking your wants to avoid effort — laziness in a spiritual mask.
  • Transactional projection — treat relationships as transactions and you'll attract only transactors.

Reaction Formation: The Anti-Money Disguise

Some people hate money, and that's just as tangled. Reaction formation is a defense mechanism where something painful inside gets projected outward and attacked — the classic example being the closeted person who becomes a vocal homophobe. With money this looks like spiritual bypassing: "I tried to make money and failed, so rather than feel like a failure, I'll decide money is beneath me and call myself enlightened." A lot of intense anti-money energy turns out to be a personal scar dressed up as a worldview. The point isn't whether any view is right; it's that an unexamined emotional charge is steering the ship.

Surfacing Your Associations

The first move toward a healthy relationship with money is not deciding it's good or bad. It's understanding what money already means to you. You don't get to choose whether you have a relationship with money — you already do. Your only choice is whether it runs in the dark.

Ask yourself, honestly and at length:

  • What does money mean to me? Generate as many answers as you can.
  • When I see someone wealthy or poor, what do I assume? The judgments reveal your wiring.
  • When did I learn this? What household or moment taught you money equals worth, safety, or care?

The key thing here is this is not about right or wrong. This is about understanding.

People get into trouble not because of their associations but because they're unaware of them. Once you see the wire, you can decide whether to cut it.

The Practical Playbook: How to Actually Make Money

Drawing on the Arthashastra — not guaranteed, but consistently useful.

1. Dinacharya — your daily routine. Across a decade of working with everyone from homeless people to executives, the single most reliable predictor of long-term financial security is how you spend your days. Most people exchange time for money, so making the most of your time is the most direct lever you have. The two highest-leverage habits for most people are preparation at the start (map the day: what's hard, what am I excited about) and reflection at the end (what went well, what to improve, what I enjoyed) — and writing them down, because writing recruits your visual cortex and hippocampus far more than thinking alone. The honest caveat: this routine was written for kings with servants, so adapt it. The deeper principle survives: your day should be thoughtfully planned, and anything you add requires consciously sacrificing something else.

2. Collect information — wealth is born of information.

If you want to make every hour of effort worth ten times more than it's worth now, information is what transforms that.

The same content gets 10,000 or 100,000 views depending on the information behind it. Information determines the value of your hour. The crucial twist: information's value is inversely proportional to how many people have it. Google and AI hand everyone the same answers — which is exactly the information that won't make you money. The valuable information lives outside Google and AI: in subreddits, conversations, things others ignore. As critical thinking declines and AI output grows derivative, being genuinely critical and curious becomes a superpower — being early to something before it's cool.

3. Avoid unnecessary conflicts.

Eighty to ninety percent of the conflicts people engage in aren't even worth winning.

Conflict is a resource drain — a contested resource costs far more than an uncontested one. Before entering any conflict, ask: What will it take to win? What will it cost? What's the reward? Often the move is to leave. And you can't argue an unwilling person into therapy — offer, set boundaries, and let the rest go. That last part is moksha woven in: detachment instead of exhausting yourself controlling others.

4. Find the right allies — and the right elders. The people you build with should combine competence and care — and these don't have to live in the same person. Many friendships and partnerships fail because they're based on mutual affection without competence. Be intentional about who you surround yourself with. Also, spend time with older people — there's wisdom there you can't get elsewhere, including the unknown unknowns.

5. Beware the six inner enemies. The biggest opponent to your wealth is you — specifically lust, anger, greed, vanity, haughtiness, and excessive joy. Anger drags you into costly conflicts. The surprising one is excessive joy — getting so over-excited (quitting your job over a half-baked startup idea) that you deviate from your path.

Invest in Yourself First

If you remember one thing: invest in yourself — it's the only investment you carry wherever you go. Most people study to get an A, not to own the calculus for life; they work to please a boss they'll leave. Those are investments in external results, which evaporate the moment your circumstances change. Investing in your body, mind, knowledge, and skills is permanent. When an unpaid project appears, ask: Will I learn something?

Collecting information doesn't mean hoarding facts (you'll forget those) — it means building understanding of trends and what's moving in your field. If your memory is bad, two fixes:

  • Salience. Your brain remembers what matters to you, not what's merely important. Before absorbing anything, give it a real, gut-level purpose — this could help someone — and recall jumps.
  • Systems. Take copious notes across notebooks or apps, then periodically compile them. A bad memory just needs a good external system.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the symptom as the cause. Believing your problem is the amount of money, when it's the meaning attached to it.
  • Using money as a proxy. Buying a gift instead of actually loving someone; spending instead of working through a relationship problem.
  • Sacrificing artha for dharma carelessly. Wrecking your own future "for family" in ways that ultimately help no one.
  • Hedonism with no ceiling, or wealth with no spending. All play and no growth leaves you empty; all earning and no enjoyment leaves you a frozen miser.
  • Reaction formation / spiritual bypassing. Declaring yourself above money to avoid feeling like a failure at it.
  • Shrinking your wants to dodge effort. Trying to "want less" so you don't have to work isn't detachment — it's laziness wearing a spiritual mask.
  • Mistaking grind for the point. It's not about working 80 hours; it's about being willing to work when work is the right move, and amplifying each hour through information, allies, and routine.
  • Transactional projection. Treat relationships transactionally and you'll attract only people willing to transact, driving away anyone who wanted something real.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your money symbolism. Write out at length: What does money mean to me? Push past the first answer — value, freedom, security, love, control, status.
  2. Trace the origin. For your strongest association, ask when did I learn this?
  3. Audit your judgments. Notice what you automatically assume about people based on whether they're rich or poor.
  4. Find the real need and pursue it directly. If money stands in for self-esteem or love, name it — and work on that need on its own terms.
  5. Run the four-aims balance check. Look at your life through dharma–artha–kama–moksha. Which one is starving the others? Rebalance toward it.
  6. Add two anchors to your day. Tomorrow morning, write down what the day's about and what's hard; tonight, write what went well, what to improve, what you enjoyed.
  7. Do a spending meditation. Buy something small — an ice cream cone — and enjoy it fully. Notice any guilt, and ask where it comes from.
  8. Practice information leverage. Pick something you care about and gather information from outside Google and AI. Think critically about what you find.
  9. Set your salience before learning. Define a concrete, gut-level purpose first, and build an external note system so your memory doesn't carry the load.
  10. Pick one conflict to drop. Calculate the cost, odds, and reward. If it doesn't add up, set a boundary or walk away and reclaim the energy.

1. Dinacharya — your daily routine

Across a decade of working with everyone from homeless people to C-suite executives, the single most reliable predictor of long-term financial security is how you spend your days. Most people exchange time for money, so making the most of your time is the most direct lever you have.

A loose template — adjust to your life — divides the day into roughly eight 90-minute blocks:

  • Prepare for the day. Before diving in, take a few minutes to map it: What's today about? What's hard? What am I excited about? Give your frontal lobes a chance to plan.
  • Handle family responsibilities (precedence, not strict order) — the dishwasher, the kids' lunches, whatever must get done.
  • Study or learn in the morning, working with your circadian rhythm. If your job involves zero learning, that's a sign to reconsider it.
  • Appointments, meetings, or creative work.
  • Administrative time — a dedicated block for the BS of life.
  • Reflect on the day. What went well? What could I do better? What did I enjoy? Daily, not monthly.
  • Recreation and outdoor time.
  • Prayer/meditation and prep for bed (60–90 minutes), including getting ready for tomorrow.

The two highest-leverage additions for most people are preparation at the start and reflection at the end — and writing them down, because writing recruits your visual cortex and hippocampus far more robustly than thinking alone, so you actually learn from your own days.

The honest caveat: this routine was written for kings with servants. You have a commute and an eight-hour job, so something has to give. The deeper principle survives intact — your day should be thoughtfully planned, and anything you add requires consciously sacrificing something else. Often the early grind is brutal and social-life-free, like an ashram of your own making — but as you become more independent, wiggle room and resources grow, the way a StarCraft economy compounds from a few workers into many.

2. Collect information — wealth is born of information

If you want to make every hour of effort worth ten times more than it's worth now, information is what transforms that.

The same content can get 10,000 or 100,000 views depending on the information behind it. A startup lives or dies on whether the founders sat down and gathered the facts about their costs ahead of time. Information determines the value of your hour.

The crucial twist: information's value is inversely proportional to how many people have it. Google and AI hand everyone the same answers — which means that's exactly the information that won't make you money. Ask AI "what jobs are vulnerable to AI?" and you'll apply for the same jobs as everyone else, chasing the pack into the thickest part of it. The valuable information lives outside Google and AI: in subreddits, in conversations, in what people around you are actually saying, in the things others ignore.

And here's the edge: as critical thinking declines and AI output grows increasingly derivative (even AI art is degrading as it feeds on itself), being genuinely critical and curious becomes a superpower. Real success comes from seeing the world in a way others don't yet — being early to something before it's cool.

3. Avoid unnecessary conflicts

Eighty to ninety percent of the conflicts people engage in aren't even worth winning.

Conflict is a resource drain. A contested resource costs far more to secure than an uncontested one — four people fighting hard for one promotion, when there may be an easier, overlooked opportunity beside them. Before entering any conflict, ask: What will it take to win? What will it cost? What's the reward? Usually it's not worth it. Sometimes the move is simply to leave, or to kick the disruptive player out of the D&D group rather than spend weeks reforming them. And you can't argue an unwilling person into therapy — you can offer, set boundaries ("I'll come home for one day"), and let the rest go. That last part is moksha woven in: a little detachment instead of exhausting yourself trying to control others.

4. Find the right allies — and the right elders

The people you build with should combine competence and care — and these don't have to live in the same person. Your mom cares but may not know a good college essay; your advisor knows but may not be invested in you. Many friendships and even business partnerships fail because they're based on circumstance and mutual affection without competence ("we like each other but neither of us has a clue"). Be intentional about who you surround yourself with.

Also: spend time with older people. Modern society quietly devalues elders, but there's wisdom there you can't get elsewhere — including the unknown unknowns, the things you don't even realize you're missing.

5. Beware the six inner enemies

The biggest opponent to your wealth is you — specifically six emotions: lust, anger, greed, vanity, haughtiness, and excessive joy. Anger drags you into conflicts you win at catastrophic cost. The surprising one is excessive joy — getting so swept up and over-excited (quitting your job over a half-baked startup idea) that you deviate from your path.

Invest in Yourself First

If you remember one piece of advice: invest in yourself — it's the only investment you carry wherever you go.

Most people study to get an A, not to actually learn the calculus they could own for life. They work to please a boss they'll leave, cultivate relationships tied to a company they'll move on from. Those are investments in external results, which is why they evaporate the moment your circumstances change. Investing in your body, mind, knowledge, and skills is permanent. When an unpaid project appears, ask: Will I learn something? If yes, take it.

A note on memory and "collecting information"

Collecting information doesn't mean hoarding facts (you'll forget those). It means building understanding — trends, what's moving in your field, what's been forgotten — which sticks far longer.

If your memory is bad, two fixes:

  • Salience. Your brain remembers what matters to you, not what's merely important in your life. (People never forget where their bong is.) Before absorbing anything, give it a real, gut-level purpose: there could be a video in here, this could help someone. Orienting yourself toward why you're receiving information dramatically boosts recall. Reading a passage's questions first works for the same reason.
  • Systems. Take copious notes. Use notebooks, Google Keep, OneNote, documents — then periodically compile them. A bad memory just needs a good external system.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the symptom as the cause. Believing your problem is the amount of money, when it's the meaning you've attached to it. More money won't fix it.
  • Using money as a proxy. Buying a gift instead of actually loving someone; spending instead of working through a relationship problem. Money should be a means, never a substitute for the real thing.
  • Sacrificing artha for dharma carelessly. Wrecking your own future "for family" in ways that ultimately help no one — both of you end up struggling.
  • Hedonism with no ceiling, or wealth with no spending. All play and no growth leaves you empty; all earning and no enjoyment leaves you a frozen miser guilt-ridden over an ice cream cone.
  • Reaction formation / spiritual bypassing. Declaring yourself above money to avoid feeling like a failure at it.
  • Shrinking your wants to dodge effort. Trying to "want less" so you don't have to work more isn't detachment — it's laziness wearing a spiritual mask. Either become hardworking and pursue what you want, or genuinely detach — but don't lie to yourself about which you're doing.
  • Mistaking grind for the point. It's not about working 80 hours a week; it's about being willing to work when work is the right move, and amplifying each hour through information, allies, and routine.
  • Outsourcing your relationship with money to "have as little as possible." You already have a relationship with money. The goal isn't to minimize it but to understand it. The world can value money however it wants; you only need enough to buy what you actually want from the world — without being attached to it.
  • Transactional projection. Treat relationships transactionally (because "everyone just wants money") and you'll attract only the people willing to transact, driving away anyone who wanted something real. What you put out is what comes back.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Map your money symbolism. Write out, at length: What does money mean to me? Push past the first answer — value, freedom, security, love, control, status. The goal is conscious awareness, not judgment.
  2. Trace the origin. For your strongest association, ask when did I learn this? What household or moment taught you that money equals worth, or safety, or care?
  3. Audit your judgments. Notice what you automatically assume about people based on whether they're rich or poor. Those reflexes expose your wiring.
  4. Find the real need and pursue it directly. If money is standing in for self-esteem, respect, or love, name it — and start working on that need on its own terms. Watch how your relationship with money changes.
  5. Run the four-aims balance check. Look at your life through dharma–artha–kama–moksha. Which one is starving the others? Rebalance toward the one being damaged.
  6. Add two anchors to your day. Tomorrow morning, spend a few minutes preparing (write down what the day's about and what's hard); tonight, spend a few minutes reflecting in writing (what went well, what to improve, what you enjoyed).
  7. Do a spending meditation. Buy yourself something small — an ice cream cone — and just enjoy it fully for five minutes. Notice any guilt that surfaces, and ask where it comes from.
  8. Practice information leverage. Pick something you care about and gather information from outside Google and AI — subreddits, real conversations, overlooked sources. Spend time thinking critically about what you find, not just collecting it.
  9. Set your salience before learning. Next time you read or study, define a concrete, gut-level purpose first — how will this specifically help me or someone I care about? — and build an external note system so your memory doesn't have to carry the load.
  10. Pick one conflict to drop. Identify a contested fight you're currently in. Calculate the cost, the odds, and the reward. If it doesn't add up, set a boundary or walk away and reclaim the energy.
