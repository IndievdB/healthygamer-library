The Two Reasons Any Medicine Exists

Strip away the jargon and there are only two reasons to prescribe anything:

  1. To improve symptoms — make you more comfortable.
  2. To alter the course of the disease — help you live longer or heal.

Some drugs do only the first: cough syrup makes a cold bearable but doesn't cure it; painkillers don't speed wound healing. Some do only the second, brutally: chemotherapy makes you feel worse while trying to cure you. Some do both: antibiotics relieve you and kill the bacteria. An ACE inhibitor lowers your blood pressure today (symptom) and prevents your heart from enlarging over years of strain (disease course).

So the first question to ask of any psychiatric drug is simple: which job is it doing for me? Most do the first. A few, in the right circumstances, do the second.

How Much Do Psychiatric Meds Actually Work?

A useful back-of-the-napkin number for almost any psychiatric medication: expect 30–70% improvement. Not a cure, not zero. A more humbling picture is thirds: a third get a significant benefit, a third get a mild to moderate benefit, and a third get basically nothing.

Then there's placebo. Meta-analyses of antidepressant trials suggest up to 81% of the response can be attributed to non-pharmacologic factors.

The more that you believe it works, the more likely it will be to work. That doesn't mean it doesn't work.

Placebo is astonishingly real — people told they're getting a sugar pill still improve, and there are cases of placebo "overdoses" landing people in the ICU with genuine physiological reactions. It's your nervous system responding to belief and context.

  • Publication bias is enormous. Among published antidepressant trials, 94% appeared positive. Include the unpublished studies the FDA saw, and only 51% were positive.
  • Severity changes everything. ECT and TMS: Turning It Off and On Again

Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) no longer matches its horror-movie reputation. Done under anesthesia, it's safe and remarkably effective. The mechanism is almost comically simple: like restarting a frozen computer, a controlled seizure triggers a brain-wide reset. Counterintuitively, it's often the safest choice for the most fragile patients — the elderly with weak hearts and pregnant women (essentially zero effect on the fetus). It works fast for severe mood disorders, especially with psychotic features. Its main downside is amnesia around the time of treatment.

The Two Reasons Any Medicine Exists

Strip away the jargon and there are only two reasons to prescribe anything, in any field of medicine:

  1. To improve symptoms — make you more comfortable.
  2. To alter the course of the disease — help you live longer or heal.

Some drugs do only the first. Cough syrup and dextromethorphan make a cold more bearable but don't cure it one minute faster. Painkillers after surgery don't make the wound heal quicker. Some drugs do only the second, brutally: chemotherapy makes you feel worse while it tries to cure you. And some do both at once — antibiotics relieve you and kill the bacteria.

A clean non-psychiatric example: an ACE inhibitor for high blood pressure lowers your pressure today (symptom) and prevents your heart from slowly enlarging into "cardiomegaly" over years of strain (disease course). Ten years of lower pressure literally buys you lifespan.

So the first question to ask of any psychiatric drug is simple: which of these two jobs is it doing for me? Most do the first. A few, in the right circumstances, do the second.

  • A third get a mild to moderate benefit.
  • A third get basically nothing.

The more that you believe it works, the more likely it will be to work. That doesn't mean it doesn't work.

Placebo is astonishingly real. People given sugar pills and told it's a placebo still improve. There are cases of people "overdosing" on placebo tablets and landing in the ICU with genuine physiological reactions. Placebo isn't "fake" — it's your nervous system responding to belief and context.

  • Publication bias is enormous. Looking only at published antidepressant trials, 94% appeared positive. Once you include the unpublished studies the FDA had access to, only 51% were positive. Drug companies aren't obligated to publish the failures.
