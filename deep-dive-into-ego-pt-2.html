Ego: A Tool That Started Driving

You have an ego because you live among humans — you need a sense of "me" and "mine" to function. It becomes a problem when self-perception gets treated as 100% true: when "I am a loser" stops being an attribute of my mind and becomes a fact about reality.

Our goal is to have an ego that is functional, but not independently functional. It should defer to you.

The same person who failed out of college later graduated Harvard Medical School — same body, same mind, only the ego's verdict changed. Your ego forms conclusions about you that aren't true, and you live as if they are.

When you weaken the ego, you can look at life dispassionately (the Stoic insight). The agony over quitting a job or staying in a marriage usually isn't logistical — it's attachment. Remove that, and you can ask the real question and simply choose. Imagine applying for the 99th job with none of the 98 rejections touching you emotionally. Life starts to feel like operating a character: you send the command, and body and mind follow.

Weakening doesn't mean deleting. Keep the ego leashed and call it out deliberately when respect is truly on the line — then step back. When your discernment (buddhi) is strong, ego stays in service.

The Three Layers That Make You "You"

Who you are in the world is built from three layers that cascade:

  1. How you interpret information (what an event means)
  2. Your internal reaction (the emotion/pattern triggered)
  3. Your external behavior (what you do)

Text someone, get no reply for four hours: one person reads "they're busy," another "they're cheating." Same silence, manufactured meaning. Even people with the same interpretation feel different things. An F is literally just a paper saying you missed some calculus answers — everything else ("I'm a failure") is something you add. We call this added freight baggage — and baggage is klishta.

Klishta is the coloring your mind paints onto a raw experience. Practicing aklishta ("uncoloring") means peeling away the additions until a missed call is only a missed call.

Vasana is a groove — a habitual pattern that runs automatically once triggered. A trigger fires and rant.exe runs to completion without you choosing it. Watch dueling vasanas: one person nags on autopilot, the other tunes out, neither able to stop. Or the soda craving after fries — order water and notice the dip in enjoyment is tiny.

What you will find is that the majority of your unhappiness comes from being a spoiled little bitch.

It's your attachment to the idea of soda — the vasana — that creates suffering, not the experience.

How to Reshape Yourself

Step 1 — Change your interpretation. Notice the klishta, uncolor it, and build a differential diagnosis: force out three other explanations that fit the same facts. Most beliefs you think are carefully reasoned were formed automatically.

Step 2 — Manage your internal reaction. Notice the vasana. Underneath sits a negative emotion with identity laced in. Regulate the emotion directly — breathe, journal, walk, meditate. Once it settles, the ego calms and you see clearly.

Step 3 — Don't worry about the action.

The actions will come naturally if you can fix step one and fix step two. Because the input determines the output.

People who make life look effortless aren't more disciplined — they interpret and react differently, and the action falls out. You can wake up tomorrow and be what you want. The only thing in the way is you — specifically the vasana that fires "it won't work, I've tried before."

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating self-image as fact. "I am a loser" is an attribute of your mind, not reality.
  • Trying to kill the ego instead of leashing it. You still need it to function and guard the door.
  • Confusing fast thinking for careful thinking. Most "well-reasoned" beliefs were assembled automatically.
  • Jumping straight to behavior. Action is the last layer; it follows the first two.
  • Getting frustrated when a vasana vanishes the instant you notice it. That disappearance is the result. The frustration is just another vasana.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the three-layer drill. Write the bare facts, your interpretation, your reaction, your default behavior — then generate an alternative interpretation and behavior.
  2. Build a differential diagnosis. When your mind snaps to one conclusion, force out three other explanations that fit the same facts.

  7. Practice the laugh. When you catch autopilot in yourself or others, choose lightness: GG, let's do a little better today.

How to Actually Reshape Yourself

Step 1 — Change your interpretation. Notice the klishta and uncolor it. Then build a differential diagnosis the way a doctor refuses to leap to one conclusion: they didn't text back — what are all the other explanations? This is how a real treatment for narcissism works: in mentalization-based therapy, patients pause and imagine the other person's inner world — maybe they're late because they stopped to buy you flowers.

This matters because of two modes of thinking — fast, automatic, conclusive thinking and slow, laborious, analytical thinking.

Everyone thinks that type 1 reasoning is type 2 reasoning. They believe all of their beliefs have been well thought out. But most human beings have beliefs that are formed automatically.

Cognitive reframing is a genuine, underrated coping skill. Use it.

Step 2 — Manage your internal reaction. Notice the vasana. Underneath most reactions sits a negative emotion with some identity laced in. Fear arises, ego whispers "you're weak, run." So regulate the emotion directly — breathe, journal, walk, meditate. Once the emotion settles, the ego calms on its own and you can see clearly.

Step 3 — Don't worry about the action.

The actions will come naturally if you can fix step number one and fix step number two.

This is why people stay stuck asking how do I go to the gym, how do I start dating — hammering on behavior while ignoring the two layers that produce it. The person who makes it look effortless isn't more disciplined; they interpret information differently and react internally differently, and the action just falls out.

Change the way that you interpret the information. Because the input determines the output.

What the Brain Confirms About the Self

This isn't only ancient theory. The default mode network (DMN) is the brain's sense-of-self machinery, and it's hyperactive in depression — which is, literally, intensely self-focused ("I'm pathetic, people are better off without me"). Both meditation and ketamine quiet the default mode network, and as it quiets, people tend to feel better.

Schizophrenia is at root a disease of misinterpreting benign stimuli and of the self losing its boundary with the world. A delusion often builds logically on meaningless noise: years of a faint electrical buzzing (a prodrome) leads the mind to conclude there must be electronics → who could do that? → the FBI. The logic is sound; the input was noise.

Psychedelics point the opposite way. Therapeutic value can be predicted by how much ego dissolution occurs — the more the boundary softens in a held, intentional context, the more people report interpreting their lives differently afterward. The yogis said dissolve the ego and life gets easier thousands of years before anyone could measure it in a scanner.

You Can Become the Person You Imagine

Picture the ideal version of you. They wake on time, exercise, meditate, drink the green smoothie instead of the sugary frappuccino, go to work content.

It is within your power to wake up tomorrow and drink your yerba maté instead of your cup of coffee. You can be exactly what you want to be.

When you protest "but it's hard," ask precisely what makes it hard. You do — the part running klishta and vasana. To one person, pain is something to avoid; to another, "pain is weakness leaving the body."

And notice what your mind did when told you could start tomorrow: a vasana fired — "it won't work, I've tried before." That reflex is the deepest control of all, because if your mind pre-concludes failure, you won't even try.

The only thing getting in the way is you.

The way to make your life the easiest is to recognize that your life is hard. The moment you realize life is hard, you're not owed anything, and the best you can do is what you can control — then life becomes easy.

When you grasp how much runs on autopilot in everyone, the Zen move is to laugh. Everyone makes mistakes — so you might as well smile and see what happens today.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating your self-image as fact. "I am a loser" is an attribute of your mind, not a description of reality. Forget the distinction and the ego is driving.
  • Trying to kill the ego instead of leashing it. The goal is to sublimate it — weaker, smaller, obedient — not destroy it. You still need it to guard the door when respect is truly on the line.
  • Confusing fast thinking for careful thinking. Most "type 2" conclusions are actually "type 1," assembled automatically.
  • Jumping straight to behavior. Endlessly asking "how do I make myself do X" while ignoring how you interpret events and react. The action is the last layer.
  • Mistaking the logic of a thought for its grip on you. You can have a hundred valid reasons to feel low self-esteem — the question is whether you need to feel it 16 hours a day or one.
  • Outsourcing your direction. It isn't "out there" — it's drowned out inside you by conditioning, and advertisers and gurus are eager to "find it for you."
  • Getting frustrated when a vasana vanishes the instant you notice it. That disappearance is the result. The frustration is just another vasana.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the three-layer drill. Write down: (a) the bare facts, (b) your default interpretation, (c) your internal reaction, (d) your default behavior. Then deliberately generate an alternative interpretation and, after settling the reaction, an alternative behavior. Practice on small daily events.

  2. Build a differential diagnosis. Whenever your mind snaps to one conclusion ("they're mad at me"), force out at least three other explanations that fit the same facts. Treat your first read as one hypothesis, not the verdict.

  3. Spot vasana by frequency. The more often a thought recurs — especially your most troubling ones — the more likely it's a habitual groove. Repetition is the tell.

  4. Run the soda experiment. When a small craving hits, choose the plain alternative and watch your enjoyment. Notice how small the drop is — and locate the real unhappiness in the attachment to the idea, not the experience.

  5. Notice the "it won't work" reflex. When told you could start a better habit tomorrow, catch the automatic objection. Name it as a vasana before deciding whether to act.

  6. Regulate first, then act. When a strong reaction fires, don't push for the "right behavior" yet. Use one tool — breathing, journaling, a walk — until the charge drops. Watch the ego calm, then choose.

  7. Sit with vasanas, twice daily, 30 minutes, for 1–2 weeks. Observe each pattern as it arises; let it dissolve when noticed; watch the next surface. The noticing is the practice. Treat frustration as just another vasana.

  8. The uncontrolled-breath meditation. Aim for 100% awareness of a 0%-controlled breath — watch your natural breathing without altering it. This trains pure observation, the same muscle that uncolors klishta.

  9. Spend deliberate time alone. Eat a meal by yourself and just taste it. Walk with no agenda. Let external desires quiet so the inner calling can surface.

  10. Practice the laugh. When you catch yourself or others on full autopilot, choose lightness: GG, let's see if we can do a little better today.

Consider the Game of Thrones troll: tell people "Season 8 was the best — I didn't love the first seven seasons, but they ended it on such a high note," and watch fans erupt every single time. The same trick works on entire online communities — take the opposite stance on any heated topic and you can trigger a whole subreddit on command.

These people are not in control of their minds. We are not in control of our minds.

You can watch vasana in real time:

  • Nagging and ignoring — two people locked in dueling vasanas. One nags ("you never do this") on autopilot; the other tunes out on autopilot. Each one thinks, I've done this a thousand times and it never changes anything — and yet neither can stop. It's two swordmasters' grooves clanging against each other, going nowhere forever.
  • Soda with fries. Crispy + salty triggers a craving for something bubbly. It's a mental habit, not a need. Order water instead and watch your enjoyment dip slightly — and notice that the dip isn't really about the soda.

What you will find is that the majority of your unhappiness comes from being a spoiled little bitch.

The fries are still good. The water is still refreshing. It's your attachment to the idea of soda that creates the suffering — the vasana, not the experience.

How to Actually Reshape Yourself

Put the tools to work, in order:

Step 1 — Change your interpretation. Notice the klishta and uncolor it. Then build a differential diagnosis for the event, the way a doctor refuses to jump to one conclusion: This person didn't text back — what are all the other explanations? This is also exactly how a real treatment for narcissistic personality disorder works. In mentalization-based therapy, patients are trained to pause and imagine the other person's inner world: Maybe they're late because they stopped to buy you flowers. You're deliberately rewriting layer one.

This matters because of how the brain naturally runs. There are two modes of thinking — fast, automatic, conclusive thinking and slow, laborious, analytical thinking. The catch:

Everyone thinks that type 1 reasoning is type 2 reasoning. They believe that all of their beliefs have been well thought out. But most human beings have beliefs that are formed automatically.

Cognitive reframing — generating other ways to read the same information — is a genuine, underrated coping skill. Use it.

Step 2 — Manage your internal reaction. Notice the vasana. Underneath most internal reactions sits a negative emotion with some identity laced in. Fear arises, ego whispers "you're weak, run," because it's trying to protect you. So regulate the emotion directly — breathe, journal, walk, meditate, whatever works. Once the emotion settles, the ego calms on its own, and you can finally see clearly.

Step 3 — Don't worry about the action. This is the counterintuitive heart of it:

The actions will come naturally if you can fix step number one and fix step number two.

This is why so many people are stuck asking how do I go to the gym, how do I start dating, how do I get the raise — they're hammering on behavior while ignoring the two layers that produce it. The person you envy who makes it all look effortless isn't more disciplined; they interpret information differently and react internally differently, and the action just falls out. The yogi is the least concerned with action — there are hundreds of texts on the inner life and almost none telling you how to wake up on time, precisely because the inner layers are what determine who you are.

Change the way that you interpret the information. Because the input determines the output.

What the Brain Confirms About the Self

This isn't only ancient theory. The default mode network (DMN) is the brain's sense-of-self machinery, and it's hyperactive in depression. That fits a strange truth: depression is, in a literal sense, intensely self-focused — the mind won't stop generating thoughts about me ("I'm pathetic, people are better off without me, I'm a loser"). Both meditation and ketamine quiet the default mode network, and as it quiets, people tend to feel better.

Two more windows onto the self:

  • Schizophrenia is, at root, a disease of misinterpreting benign stimuli — and of the self losing its boundary with the outside world. An artist's self-portraits, drawn while coming off medication, show the self becoming invaded and dissolved by the environment. A delusion often builds logically on a real but meaningless sensation: years of hearing a faint electrical "buzzing" (a prodrome) leads the reasoning mind to conclude there must be electronics around me → who could do that? → the FBI, the mafia. The logic is sound; the input was noise.
  • Psychedelics point the opposite direction. The therapeutic value of a psychedelic experience can be predicted by how much ego dissolution occurs — the more the boundary softens in a held, intentional context, the more people report interpreting their lives differently afterward ("it's part of my path; things will work out"). Without that container, the same dissolution can become a traumatic invasion that doesn't fully recede.

The throughline: the yogis said dissolve the ego and life gets easier thousands of years before anyone could measure ego dissolution in a scanner. Done well — the yogic way — you bend toward connection rather than invasion.

Picture the ideal version of you. Follow that person for 24 hours and follow yourself for 24 — what's the difference? They wake up on time, exercise first thing, meditate, drink the green smoothie instead of the sugary frappuccino, go to work content. And here's the thing:

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating your self-image as fact. "I am a loser" is an attribute of your mind, not a description of reality. The moment you forget that distinction, the ego is driving.
  • Trying to kill the ego instead of leashing it. The goal is to sublimate it — make it weaker, smaller, and obedient — not to destroy it. You still need it to function in a communal world and to "guard the door" when respect is truly on the line.
  • Confusing fast thinking for careful thinking. You'll insist your beliefs are well-reasoned when they were assembled automatically. Most "type 2" conclusions are actually "type 1."
  • Jumping straight to behavior. Endlessly asking "how do I make myself do X" while ignoring how you interpret events and how you internally react. The action is the last layer, and it follows the first two.
  • Mistaking the logic of a thought for its grip on you. You can have a hundred valid reasons to feel low self-esteem — that's not the point. The question is whether you need to feel it 16 hours a day or whether you can compress it to one and let your mind run freer the rest of the time.
  • Outsourcing your direction. Believing direction is "out there" to be found. It isn't — it's drowned out inside you by external conditioning, and advertisers, designers, and gurus are all eager to "find it for you."
  • Getting frustrated when a vasana vanishes the instant you notice it. That disappearance is the result. The frustration is just another vasana. You had the answer and mistook it for the problem.
  • Chasing the "wisdom" instead of applying it. Admiring an insight and wishing it were shared with the masses is itself a vasana — a way of absolving yourself. The point of the teaching is you, transformed.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. Run the three-layer drill on real events. When something happens, write down: (a) the situation, stripped to bare facts, (b) your default interpretation, (c) your internal reaction, (d) your default behavior. Then deliberately generate an alternative interpretation and, after settling the reaction, an alternative behavior. Practice this on small daily events.

  2. Build a differential diagnosis for upsetting events. Whenever your mind snaps to one conclusion ("they're mad at me," "I'm a failure"), force out at least three other explanations that fit the same facts. Treat your first read as one hypothesis, not the verdict.

  3. Spot vasana by frequency. Notice which thoughts repeat. The more often a thought recurs — especially your most troubling ones — the more likely it's a habitual groove rather than a fresh response. Repetition is the tell.

  4. Run the soda experiment. Next time the craving hits (soda with fries, or any small habitual want), choose the plain alternative and watch your enjoyment carefully. Observe how small the actual drop is — and locate the real unhappiness in the attachment to the idea, not the experience.

  5. Notice the "it won't work" reflex. When you're told you could just start a better habit tomorrow, catch the automatic objection your mind throws up. Name it as a vasana before deciding whether to act.

  6. Regulate first, then act. When a strong internal reaction fires, don't push for the "right behavior" yet. Use one emotional-regulation tool — deep breathing, journaling, a walk, meditation — until the charge drops. Watch the ego calm on its own, then choose your action.

  7. Sit with vasanas, twice daily, 30 minutes, for 1–2 weeks. Observe each pattern as it arises; let it dissolve when noticed; watch the next one surface. Don't fight to "fix" anything — the noticing is the practice. Treat the frustration that arises as just another vasana to observe.

  8. The uncontrolled-breath meditation. Aim for 100% awareness of a 0%-controlled breath: watch your natural breathing without altering it at all. This trains pure observation — noticing without interfering — which is the same muscle that uncolors klishta.

  9. Spend deliberate time alone to hear your own direction. Eat a meal by yourself and just taste it. Take a walk with no agenda. Let the external desires quiet down so the inner calling — the one drowned out by conditioning — can surface.

  10. Practice the laugh. When you catch yourself or others running on full autopilot, instead of tilting, choose lightness: GG, let's see if we can do a little better today. It's the move that lets the vasanas fall away.
