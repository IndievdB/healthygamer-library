Common Pitfalls

  • Believing a thought is true because it's frequent. Repetition is the signature of a vasana, not evidence.
  • Trusting your reasons while ego runs your logic. When ahamkara controls buddhi, distorted arguments feel airtight. No narcissist thinks I'm being narcissistic.
  • Starting with your strongest compulsion and concluding the method is broken when you fail.
  • Confusing action with identity. "I failed a test" ≠ "I am a failure." The "I am ___" version carries far more ego and risk.
  • Owning wins or losses too hard. It's the ownership — taking full credit or full blame — that inflates the ego either way.

Why Killing the Ego Makes You More Effective

This isn't only inner peace. Removing ego improves real-world outcomes, because it's what lets you see accurately and choose well.

Miss a promotion. Strip out the ego and let your full buddhi work: Is there real bias? Will I ever get promoted? Should I look elsewhere? You can answer those. But let ego in — That manager's biased, I'm amazing, they play favorites — and you might quit when the real issue is that you're not yet effective at the work, which no new job will fix.

The correct interpretation of the real world, in removing your ego from the equation, is critical to your success in life.

Klishta: The Coloring Your Mind Paints Over Reality

Klishta means coloring. The world simply is — and your mind adds meaning that isn't there.

A pen is plastic, metal, ink — not good or bad. But the mind says: great pen, shitty pen, look at that wobbly tip. You're handed two hunks of plastic, one slightly flimsier (a fact), and your mind builds an entire emotional verdict (the coloring).

The most relatable example: someone doesn't text back. The reality is precisely one thing — an absence of a text. The mind colors it instantly: They don't like me. This is one-way. I should never speak to them again. None of that is in the silence.

The Dalai Lama says the hardest thing to do is to see the world as it is. And the job of a monk is simply to see the world as it is. That's it.

Someone calls you a loser. Strip the coloring and a human being said some words. The negative-ego script: I knew it, it's true. The inquiring script: How so? Help me understand. Even with real consequences — a big creator brigading you — you can meet it objectively: "Let me contact my mods," without adding I am a loser, it's happening again.

Klishta poisons experiences before they begin. Seeing a teammate in a game and thinking this guy's going to feed ruins the match before a single move. The mind manufactured the suffering preemptively.

The Internet as Industrial-Strength Klishta

The clearest place to watch all this is online. The internet is unregulated, steroidal vasana and klishta. Almost no one is responding to what anyone says; it's coloring vomited against coloring. The party you dislike speaks — boo. The party you like speaks — yay.

And the platforms select for it. What makes you upvote? Your emotional reaction. A blank white image triggers nothing, so it dies. A loaded political statement triggers maximum coloring, so it rises. The system rewards whatever provokes the most klishta — which is why posting feels frustrating: what comes back isn't engagement but a spray of projections, and you fire back with your own.

  • Owning your wins (or losses) too hard. It's the ownership that breeds unhealthiness — inflating the ego with credit or feeding the negative ego with blame.
  • Imposter syndrome's hidden double standard. You credit your own wins to luck but others' identical wins to talent. So your wins never count.

Vasana: Your Thoughts Are Mostly Habit, Not Truth

Now the advanced material. They aren't fresh, they aren't necessarily true. They're just repetitions.

Just because you think something a thousand times does not make it true. And yet we go on believing that our identity is correct — but it's not correct, it's just a habit.

Watch for it. After dinner, you "want" dessert — but pay attention and you'll see it's a vasana, the same craving firing on schedule. Walking the school hallway, you run the same checks: appearance, nothing on your back, the same anxious script. Talk to someone caught in an identity (the example used is an incel) and you'll get a script — a near-constant, repeating interpretation of everything around them. The reasons your mind gives come from buddhi — and if your ego is in control of buddhi, the reasons are distorted. This is exactly why narcissism correlates with being more divorced from reality, not less: when ego is active, it feels perfectly objective from the inside. Klishta: The Coloring Your Mind Paints Over Reality

Klishta means coloring. The world simply is — and your mind adds layers of meaning that aren't actually in it.

You're handed two hunks of plastic, one slightly flimsier (a fact), and your mind builds an entire emotional verdict on top (the coloring). Get the "shitty" one and you feel unhappy — over plastic.

The Internet as Industrial-Strength Klishta

The clearest place to watch all of this is online. The internet is unregulated, steroidal vasana and klishta. Common Pitfalls

  • Believing a thought is true because it's frequent. Repetition is the signature of a vasana, not evidence of accuracy. "I think this constantly, therefore it's real" is exactly backwards.
  • Trusting your reasons while your ego runs your logic. When ahamkara controls buddhi, your arguments are distorted — yet they feel airtight and objective. The more active the ego, the more confident and wrong you become.
  • Dismissing the practice with the practice's own enemy. "That won't work, I've tried it" is itself a vasana. The objection is part of the disease.
  • Starting with your strongest compulsion. Try to observe your way out of a massive addictionThe use of a chemical (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, caffeine, etc.) that produces neurological effects your brain then adapts to — leading to tolerance, withdrawal, and, potentially, addiction.Full definition → on day one and you'll fail and conclude the method is broken. Build the skill on small habits first.
  • Confusing action statements with identity statements. "I don't smoke" and "I am not a smoker" are not the same level of claim. "I swim" versus "I am a swimmer"; "I failed a test" versus "I am a failure." The "I am ___" version carries far more ego and far more risk.
  • Stopping at a positive ego. "I am lovable, everyone should love me" can make you swat away legitimate feedback ("you were a dick to me") because it threatens the identity. A positive ego is a useful waypoint, not the destination.
  • Owning your wins (or losses) too hard. It's the ownership — "this is mine," whether it's success or failure — that breeds unhealthiness. Taking full credit inflates the ego; taking full blame ("it's all my fault, I'm a loser") feeds the negative ego. In both cases, the sin is in the owning.
  • Imposter syndrome's hidden double standard. People with imposter syndrome credit their own wins to luck and circumstance, but credit others' identical wins to talent and effort. So their wins never count, and they feel like frauds among the "deserving."
  • Trying to "prove the teacher wrong" for an audience. Genuinely thinking for yourself is the goal; performing your disagreement to show off is just ego wearing a clever disguise.

Action Items & Exercises

  1. List five daily vasanas. Name five mental habits you run every day. Then sort them: which are hard to overcome, and which (like a useful cognitive reframe) you'd actually want to cultivate. Just naming them as habits already loosens their grip.

  2. Sit and do nothing for an hour — then do it again tomorrow. This is the single best way to see your vasanas. Watch how much of your mind is on pure autopilot, recycling the same thoughts. The realization "my mind is just repeating itself all the time" is the doorway.

  3. Run the observer drill on a small craving. Pick something minor — coffee first thing, dessert after dinner. When the urge fires, label it: this is a vasana, an echo, not a real need right now. Then watch it like a spectator and ask with curiosity, "Will I win today or lose?" Don't fight on the same floor; observe from above.

  4. Separate the fact from the coloring. Several times a day, catch a reaction and split it: what objectively happened (an absence of a text; some words were said; one pen is flimsier) versus what your mind added. Make the bare-fact statement first, deliberately.

  5. Audit your "I am ___" statements. Notice how often you define your identity rather than describe an action or event. Practice downgrading: "I am a failure" → "I failed a test" → "I didn't learn this material yet, so let me go back and study it."

  6. Re-credit your wins (the imposter-syndrome fix). After any accomplishment, instead of "I got lucky," list what you actually contributed — you studied, you showed up, you asked questions. Then for someone else's identical win, list the circumstances that helped them. Apply the same lens to both.

  7. Use the internet as a klishta lab. Notice how little anyone is actually responding to content versus projecting their own coloring — including you. When you feel the urge to fire back, recognize it as your klishta meeting theirs.
